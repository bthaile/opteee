# You CAN'T AFFORD It #investing #shorts

## Summary

A short educational piece on the distinction between affording an asset outright versus financing it strategically. The core principle: you cannot afford to buy a depreciating asset unless you can pay cash for it; however, leverage itself is neither good nor bad—it depends entirely on the interest rate relative to your expected return and whether it forces you into financial constraint.

## Key takeaways

- **Affording vs. financing [00:00]**: You can only truly afford a depreciating asset if you can buy it in cash. Financing is a separate decision based on rate arbitrage, not necessity.
- **Good leverage [00:00–00:22]**: When interest rates are low (e.g., 2% mortgage), financing allows you to invest the cash elsewhere at a higher expected return—a positive carry trade.
- **Bad leverage [00:22–00:41]**: Paying 8.5% to finance a car you otherwise couldn't afford locks you into forced income generation; you lose optionality and become trapped by payment obligations.
- **Leverage is neutral [00:41]**: The morality of leverage depends on the math (rate vs. return) and whether it constrains your freedom; use it only when the spread works in your favor.

## Notable quotes

> "If you cannot afford to buy a depreciating asset in cash, you can't afford it."

> "Good leverage is when you're paying two percent for a house and making excess return by investing that money. Bad leverage is when you're paying eight and a half percent for a car that you otherwise couldn't afford."
