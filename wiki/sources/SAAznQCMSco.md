# The Ultimate Guide to Buying vs Selling Options (Long vs Short Options)

## Summary

This video systematically compares buying premium (long options) versus selling premium (short options) across risk profile, probability of profit, capital requirements, Greeks exposure, and volatility sensitivity. Eric argues that neither approach is inherently superior; the choice depends on your risk tolerance, profit mechanism preference, and market outlook. The key insight is that buying premium offers defined risk but requires directional conviction and larger moves, while selling premium offers higher probability of profit but undefined risk and requires active management.

## Key takeaways

- **Entry/exit mechanics** [01:04]: Buying premium = buy to open, sell to close; selling premium = sell to open, buy to close.
- **Risk profile is the primary differentiator** [02:13]: Buying defines your max loss upfront (the premium paid); selling caps profit but leaves loss undefined (except for puts, which bottom out at zero).
- **Probability of profit trade-off** [03:24]: Selling premium has higher probability of profit due to built-in buffer; buying premium requires an accommodating directional move.
- **The buffer illusion** [04:36]: A wide break-even buffer on a short put feels safe until the stock approaches it—then unrealized losses mount quickly, creating psychological pressure and undefined-risk exposure.
- **Volatility relationship** [07:55]: Buying premium benefits from rising volatility (long vega); selling premium benefits from falling volatility (short vega). Volatility changes affect margin requirements for short positions but not for long.
- **Greeks summary** [08:52]: Long options = short theta, long gamma, long vega; short options = long theta, short gamma, short vega. Gamma preference: long gamma wants big moves; short gamma wants small moves.
- **Use both, don't default** [11:08]: Neither buying nor selling is inherently better. Choose based on your profit mechanism and risk preference for the specific setup.
- **Fit the structure to the outlook** [12:14]: If you expect a big move, prefer undefined profit potential (long options); if you expect range-bound action, selling can offset theta decay from long positions.
- **Don't overweight volatility alone** [13:24]: IV percentile is important but not the sole decision factor. Delta and directional conviction matter more unless you're running a pure volatility trade. A short strangle in a range-bound stock profits from theta decay even if IV temporarily expands.
- **Decision framework** [15:43]: (1) What risk profile fits my account and psychology? (2) Do I have conviction in direction and timing (buy) or do I prefer theta decay and range-bound profit (sell)?

## Notable quotes

> "Theta in and of itself is unequivocally not an edge."

> "The built-in buffer for selling premium comes at a cost: undefined risk, and sometimes that can sneak up on you a little faster than you think."

> "Volatility is a massively important consideration, but it should not be the decision factor—unless you're deploying a specific volatility trade."
