# Deep Dive Into My Trading Approach: Profit Mechanisms & Strategy Process

## Summary

Eric walks through the foundational framework that underpins his trading approach: identifying and trading profit mechanisms rather than chasing specific strategies or structures. Using real chart examples (Palantir, Apple, T), he demonstrates how post-earnings drift, breakouts, and momentum represent repeatable, quantifiable patterns. The core thesis is that consistent 15% annual returns require understanding *why* a trade works (the profit mechanism) before deciding *how* to structure it (the strategy), and that deep knowledge of options Greeks allows traders to match position structure to their edge.

## Key takeaways

### Profit mechanisms over strategies [18:15]
- The outlier strategy process begins with identifying a **profit mechanism** (e.g., post-earnings announcement drift, breakout, momentum), not with picking a strategy structure.
- Most traders waste time debating delta selection (30 vs. 35) or indicator choice (MACD vs. RSI) when they should focus on whether the underlying profit mechanism is sound.
- If you sell a 30-delta put and the stock drops, you lose money—a "low-class problem." If you sell a 30-delta and could have made more with a 35-delta, that's a "high-class problem" (you still made money).

### Post-earnings announcement drift (PEAD) as a case study [19:34]–[21:20]
- Palantir exhibited 5-for-6 profitable moves in the period following earnings releases, demonstrating a quantifiable, repeatable profit mechanism.
- PEAD is well-researched; you can measure average post-earnings moves across a basket of stocks with specific criteria.
- The key is not worrying about MACD or RSI—focus on whether the profit mechanism itself is sound.

### Breakout vs. momentum distinction [30:15]–[32:55]
- A **breakout** has specific entry criteria (strong volume, catalyst, price acceleration) and a defined consolidation endpoint.
- Once the breakout consolidates, if price continues higher, it transitions into **momentum** (drift).
- Apple's 57% move over ~1 month from entry was a breakout that evolved into momentum; T's 14.5% move over 30 days did not meet the same breakout criteria, so it would not have been a candidate in Eric's framework.

### Chart reading for profit mechanisms [34:17]–[35:32]
- Flip through thousands of charts, looking at **price and volume**, not candlestick patterns.
- Ask: "Why did volume spike?" (e.g., T on 25 Nov: volume 64.7M vs. 33M average). Understand what information moves stocks.
- Build contextual understanding of how things move; this is the foundation for identifying repeatable patterns.

### Greeks as inputs to strategy selection [23:00]–[25:45]
- Profit mechanisms fall into two buckets: **price direction** and **volatility**.
- If you buy a straddle and gamma-scalp (delta-hedge), you mute price exposure and isolate the volatility profit mechanism.
- The Greeks (delta, gamma, theta, vega) are tools to match your position structure to your profit mechanism, not the starting point.

### Strike and expiration selection via Greeks [37:25]–[43:12]
- Using T's 12.6% move (27 Jan to 7 Mar) as a cherry-picked example, lower-delta calls sized proportionally outperformed higher-delta calls.
- The optimal strike depends on matching your expected move to the Greeks; this requires deep options knowledge, not guesswork.
- Expiration selection matters: near-term options capture more of the volatility surface impact than far-term options with higher Vega.

### Core allocation vs. speculative allocation [57:53]–[01:03:02]
- **Core allocation**: covered strangles on index ETFs (e.g., SPY), which profit from the market's upward bias most of the time.
- **Speculative allocation**: trades in pivots, flow, breakouts, breakdowns, momentum—designed to generate returns in *any* market condition.
- When the core allocation is working, lean into it; when it's not, deploy speculative tools.

### Consistency via quarterly milestones [01:04:48]–[01:06:14]
- Don't wait until year-end to check if you hit 15% annual return; break it into quarterly targets.
- Even if Q1 is +2% (behind pace), as long as the direction is correct, you can adjust in subsequent quarters.
- This approach allows you to rotate between core and speculative allocations based on market regime.

### Trade management: idea alignment [01:10:11]–[01:11:26]
- If a trade stops doing what you expected, exit it—regardless of P&L.
- Example: short calls on SATS that rallied into support; even though the calls had room, the trade wasn't behaving as expected, so Eric closed it at a small loss.
- Frequent unexpected behavior signals you don't understand the profit mechanism well enough; use that feedback to refine your strategy.

### Fundamentals and timeframe dependency [01:13:38]–[01:19:59]
- **Short-term ideas** (e.g., earnings volatility trades): fundamentals matter less; focus on implied volatility pricing.
- **Long-term ideas** (e.g., Palantir 2027 position): fundamentals matter more—revenue growth, operating efficiency, debt, solvency.
- For some volatility trades, you may not care about fundamentals at all; you're trading the vol surface, not the company.

### Volatility surface and term structure [01:25:10]–[01:28:45]
- Higher Vega in longer-dated options does not mean they move more in absolute terms; the volatility surface is not flat.
- Near-term options often have higher implied volatility and experience larger percentage moves when realized volatility spikes.
- Example: VIX spike in mid-December; 30-day vol surges, but 221-day vol (tied to far-dated options) may barely move.

## Notable quotes

> "If I sell a 30 delta and I say to myself, 'Ah, shit, I could have made more money with a 35 delta'—that's a high-class problem. Get over it. But if the underlying goes down, I lose. That's a low-class problem."

> "Most people don't think like this. It's not because I'm super unique. It's just because I've looked at this shit long enough that I've found ways to categorize stuff in my mind. That structure adds so much efficiency to what I'm doing."
