# Position Adjustments: Defending vs. Repairing vs. Adjusting

## Summary

This session deconstructs the terminology and psychology of position adjustments in options trading, arguing that "adjusting" is the correct mental frame rather than "defending" or "repairing." The host walks through the primary reasons positions require intervention (P&L targets, duration, Greek profile drift, portfolio fit), then demonstrates adjustment mechanics across vertical spreads, strangles, and diagonals using live examples.

## Key takeaways

- **Terminology matters for subconscious processing** [00:00–09:55]: "Defending" and "repairing" carry negative connotations that bias decision-making; "adjusting" is neutral and agnostic. Repairing is impossible (you cannot return to the original state); this framing sets up failure. Language shapes how you process information under stress.

- **Four primary reasons to adjust a position** [11:08–23:37]:
  - P&L targets hit (profit-taking or loss-cutting thresholds)
  - Duration / time in trade (trade didn't hit targets within your timeframe)
  - Greek profile changes (delta, gamma, theta, vega drift due to market moves, volatility shifts, or time decay)
  - Portfolio fit (other positions change, requiring rebalancing of systematic risk)

- **Vertical spreads: narrow widths are directional bets, not adjustable** [25:55–39:47]: A $1–$5 wide vertical is a binary directional hypothesis. Rolling is impractical due to liquidity constraints and bid–ask spread widening during stress. Sophisticated traders close the entire spread and open a new one rather than attempt a roll. Wider verticals ($20+) may be rolled by adjusting individual legs (short leg first, to avoid undefined risk).

- **Rolling the untested side in strangles** [43:23–49:02]: When a short strangle's put side is tested (delta rising from 19 to 30), the call side (untested) loses delta. To rebalance and maintain neutrality, roll the call side (untested) to a higher strike and higher delta. This regains Greek sensitivity without chasing the tested leg.

- **Diagonals to calendars: avoid the label trap** [50:24–01:04:10]: Converting a diagonal to a calendar is a mental constraint. Instead, adjust individual legs (long and short) to whatever strikes reflect your current idea. Most adjustments follow profit moves on the short leg; the long leg naturally gains delta as the trade moves in your favor. Don't be bound by structure names.

- **Portfolio-level Greek monitoring is essential** [22:10–23:37]: With multiple trades (T1–T30), systematic risk can drift unintentionally. Monitor delta, gamma, vega, and vomma across the entire portfolio. Adjust or close existing positions to make room for new opportunities without skewing overall exposure.

- **Palantir sentiment snapshot** [01:06:52–01:13:08]: Put–call ratio ~2:1 favoring calls; IV at 95% (front week) and 113% (21 Mar). Market positioning for higher probability of small upside move with moderate downside tail risk; overwhelmingly bullish near-term.
