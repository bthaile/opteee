# GME Call Volatility Erased

## Summary

This video analyzes the dramatic collapse in implied volatility across GameStop's options chain on June 21, 2024, following a period of extreme IV expansion. Eric walks through how to read volatility surfaces, interpret the volatility smile, and understand why far out-of-the-money calls command outsized premiums relative to puts—a "puncher's chance" pricing model that reflects tail-risk hedging by market makers.

## Key takeaways

- **Volatility contraction across the chain** [00:47–02:27]: GME's near-term IV collapsed from ~247% to much lower levels by close; the 28 June expiration dropped from 189% to 155%, with the entire volatility surface flattening significantly. This is typical as expiration approaches unless a large move is expected.

- **Time-adjusted expected move** [01:12–01:35]: Volatility is annualized and adjusted for days-to-expiration. A 247% IV with only one day left yields a ~$22.70 expected move; the same 95% IV 100 days out yields a ~$40 expected move. Shorter time = smaller expected range.

- **Volatility surface flattening** [02:27–03:21]: The term structure has compressed dramatically. Expirations from 28 June through mid-August now cluster around 139–155% IV, indicating the market no longer prices in a sustained elevated-volatility regime.

- **Call-side skew inversion on GME** [04:10–04:50]: Unlike typical equity skew, GME's 28 June expiration shows calls with higher IV than puts at far out-of-the-money strikes—the opposite of Apple's fear-skew. This reflects "puncher's chance" pricing: market makers jack up call IV to hedge against a parabolic squeeze.

- **Puncher's chance mechanics** [05:30–07:02]: Far OTM calls (e.g., 125 strike) trade at 6,600%+ IV because a single catalyst (Roaring Kitty post, Cohen announcement) could trigger a parabolic move that runs over market makers. The put side is even higher (11,900% IV), but the relative call-to-put skew is inverted vs. Apple.

- **Lottery-ticket decay** [08:19–11:00]: These ultra-cheap far OTM options (pennies) decay rapidly via theta. A 7-cent option with 4 cents of daily theta decay becomes worthless in days. Buying them is a low-probability lottery; the math favors sellers despite the tail risk.

- **Open interest concentration** [09:16–10:20]: The 30 strike shows 17,500 OI with 14,600 transacted today. Most depth on the put side; call-side OI is sparse except at extreme strikes (120+), where 32,000 contracts sit at 7 pennies each.

## Notable quotes

> "If you kind of like martial arts, I'm a martial artist myself—there's always a puncher's chance. You can be against somebody that's grossly skilled compared to you, but if they have enough power, one shot literally can knock you out."

> "These are insanely cheap options that are going to decay away essentially to nothing as they do far more often than not. This is a lottery and then some—this is playing the lottery with the expectation of also getting struck by lightning in the same day."
