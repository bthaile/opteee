# Selling the tails

## Summary

Selling extremely far out-of-the-money options (delta ~0.001) appears safe because of high win probability, but the asymmetric payoff structure creates account-blowing risk: tiny credits per trade force traders to layer multiple positions, and when the tail event occurs, losses can erase many winning trades. Position sizing and account management are critical when pursuing this high-probability, low-premium strategy.

## Key takeaways

- **The tail-selling trap** [00:00–00:27]: Selling puts at 0.001 delta (e.g., $4,100 strike on SPY) collects only $0.15–$0.16 per contract—a high-probability win that masks asymmetric downside.
- **Forced over-leverage** [00:27–00:55]: To reach meaningful income (e.g., $100 per trade), traders must layer 10+ identical positions on a small account, concentrating tail risk.
- **Margin call and ruin** [00:55–01:12]: A single large move while holding 10 short puts can trigger margin calls or account liquidation, erasing months of small wins in one trade.
- **Requires discipline** [01:12]: Tail-selling *can* work, but demands rigorous position sizing, account-size alignment, and acceptance that occasional losses will be severe.

## Notable quotes

> "You are collecting such a tiny little bit of money that you are likely going to win the overwhelming majority of times, but sometimes it will lose, and when this loses, those losses can undo multiples of the winning trades."
