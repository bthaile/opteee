# 2 Powerful Trading Psychology Techniques - Rich Friesen | TOP Ep77

## Summary

Richard Newton returns to discuss two foundational trading psychology techniques: the practice of wearing distinct "hats" for strategy development, risk management, and execution (keeping these functions separate), and the deeper work of identifying and resolving identity-based self-sabotage through visualization and agency-building. The conversation emphasizes that most traders fail not from lack of technical skill but from psychological barriers—particularly the inability to execute consistently and the unconscious belief that they don't deserve sustained profitability.

## Key takeaways

### Easy exercise: The three hats [04:45–05:37]
- Assign distinct roles: **red hat** (risk management), **blue hat** (execution), **green hat** (strategy development).
- Execute only one function at a time; once strategy and risk are locked, *just execute* without second-guessing.
- This separation prevents strategy doubt and risk anxiety from contaminating execution decisions.
- Physical or visceral anchors (actual hats, environment changes) reinforce the mental shift.

### Hidden framework: Identity and worthiness [02:14–03:24]
- Most traders never examine whether they believe they *deserve* sustained profitability.
- Self-sabotage typically emerges when traders reach a profit level that conflicts with deep beliefs about their own worth (often inherited from family narratives about struggle and hard work).
- Visualization exercises (imagining a successful trader's house, walking toward it, identifying who objects) surface these hidden objections.
- Once resolved, traders can anchor that state of deserving and step into it intentionally during trading.

### Market mood detection and strategy adaptation [23:30–26:48]
- All strategies eventually fail as market conditions shift; the primary skill is detecting when the market "mood" changes.
- Define "Market Heaven" (conditions where your strategy thrives) and "Market Hell" (conditions where it repeatedly stops you out).
- Use volume, implied volatility, bar length, and other criteria to identify which mood is present.
- Rotate strategies based on market mood rather than endlessly tweaking a single system.

### Reframing loss: Execution vs. outcome [52:47–56:30]
- Stop measuring success by P&L; measure it by **execution quality** (did you follow your plan?).
- A "lucrative trade" is one executed well according to your rules, regardless of profit or loss.
- This removes ego and shame from the equation and shifts focus to controllable inputs.
- Review trades afterward to assess whether the strategy fits the market mood, not whether you failed.

### Agency and third-party perspective [11:09–13:56]
- Agency is the ability to reframe events so you remain in control of your emotional response, rather than letting external events (market moves, other traders) determine your state.
- Use a "computer game" mindset: your job is to navigate obstacles skillfully, not to prevent them from existing.
- Humor can develop agency (by creating distance from the event) but can also become avoidance if it slides into "I don't care."
- True agency requires awareness, acceptance, and intentional choice—not detachment.

### The trader's lifecycle and confidence loss [20:37–22:49]
- **Beginner stage**: Trades tied to dreams (wealth, status, respect); every tick is emotional.
- **Strategy stage**: Develop and backtest; gain confidence in the system.
- **Execution stage**: Follow the plan consistently—most traders fail here.
- **Doubt stage**: Strategy stops working (market mood shifted); trader loses confidence and begins tweaking.
- **Adaptation stage**: Learn to identify market mood and rotate strategies; accept that all strategies have an expiration date.

### Visualization and identity work [36:10–39:17]
- Guided visualization: design a successful trader's house, approach it, notice resistance (family voices, beliefs about worthiness).
- Have conversations with objecting family members; usually they want the best for you but carry their own trauma about struggle.
- Anchor the feeling of deserving success viscerally so you can step into it intentionally during trading.
- Until you can *feel* success, parts of yourself will sabotage it.

### Execution consistency and presence [58:18–58:50]
- Traders almost never violate their strategy when a mentor is present (on Zoom, observing).
- This is not willpower; it's the activation of higher-level agency through external presence.
- The goal is to internalize that presence so traders carry their own higher-level observer.

### Analysis paralysis and the wave model [44:42–46:30]
- Life is a wave: analysis phase, execution phase, review phase, creativity phase.
- Excessive analysis locks you out of intuition and execution; excessive execution without review prevents learning.
- Intentionally shift between phases; know which phase you're in.
- 70–80% analysis, then execute; review and adjust; repeat.

### The discipline myth [57:26–58:18]
- There is no such thing as willpower or discipline; there is only the experience of stepping into a state that *feels better*.
- Set up your environment and mindset so that following your strategy feels more rewarding than deviating from it.
- Invite yourself to your higher-level agency; the desire to execute will follow.
