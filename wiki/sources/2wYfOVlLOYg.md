# Doomed from the Start

## Summary

A trader with 20 years of experience claims to have learned a lesson about greed after blowing up their account, but Eric demonstrates that the real problem is a fundamental misunderstanding of realistic return expectations and a complete absence of planning. The trader's stated goal of $500/day (2% on 25K) compounds to impossible annual returns, revealing that they never did basic math before risking capital.

## Key takeaways

- **False attribution of failure** [00:26–01:20]: The trader blames "greed" for their blow-up, but the actual problem is unrealistic expectations—$500/day on 25K compounds to ~14,600% annualized, or 180% if traded weekly. This is mathematically impossible in real markets.
- **Lack of planning precedes failure** [01:20–01:47]: No requisite research, no position-sizing framework, no realistic goal-setting. The trader set themselves up for failure before they ever placed a trade.
- **Confusing outcome with lesson** [00:00–00:26]: Saying "greed taught me to stop when I hit my goal" misses the point entirely—the issue is that the goal itself was never grounded in reality.
- **Quick math check reveals the gap** [01:47]: A 20-second calculator check exposes the delusion; most traders skip this step and wonder why they fail.

## Notable quotes

> "This person has smoked crack cocaine and has the most contorted, skewed perception of reality that I could conceive of."

> "This isn't actually about greed at all. This is about having completely false expectations of what trading is, what's possible."

This is a timeless critique of retail trader psychology: the conflation of outcome (account blow-up) with root cause (unrealistic expectations). The trader's 20-year tenure makes the gap even more striking—experience without deliberate planning compounds overconfidence. No specific market regime applies; the lesson is structural.
