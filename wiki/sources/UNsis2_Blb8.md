# Chris from ProjectOption Pt1 | Money Talks

## Summary

Chris Butler, founder of ProjectOption, discusses his evolution from computer science to finance, his early options trading journey at tastytrade, and the critical role of trader psychology in sustainable profitability. He emphasizes that active trading requires strict rules-based systems, proper position sizing, and realistic expectations—and that most traders benefit from a hybrid approach combining passive index investing with disciplined options strategies rather than relying solely on active trading for wealth building.

## Key takeaways

- **Career pivot to finance** [02:26–03:32]: Switched from computer science to finance at DePaul after realizing the importance of understanding money management; cold-emailed tastytrade founder Tom Sosnoff, leading to a three-year internship (2013–2016) that launched ProjectOption.

- **YouTube strategy over written content** [06:04–06:46]: Recognized YouTube had fewer competitors than blogs for options education; made a deliberate 2019 New Year's resolution to focus 100% on video production and quality, abandoning the website.

- **Early trading mistakes and volatility lessons** [42:09–44:38]: In August 2015, lost all year's profits (50% gain) in 36 hours selling naked strangles during a market shock; took a month off to reset mentally, learning that volatile markets expose psychological vulnerabilities in active traders.

- **Three-trade framework** [01:00:02–01:02:01]: Now structures trading around (1) leveraged long-term call options on conviction plays, (2) directionally neutral index butterflies (iron butterflies on SPY/SPX), and (3) hedging existing stock positions with puts when they've appreciated significantly.

- **Position sizing and capital allocation** [47:50–49:14]: Recommends allocating only 5% of portfolio to speculative options; the leverage means small allocations can generate outsized returns while limiting catastrophic loss if wrong; majority of wealth should be in passive index funds.

- **Stock price path vs. probability of profit** [01:08:17–01:11:23]: High-probability trades can still cause severe drawdowns before expiration if the stock path is unfavorable; understanding what can happen *between entry and expiration*—not just at expiration—is critical to avoiding emotional exits.

- **Rules-based systems essential for active trading** [01:16:40–01:17:22]: If trading actively, must have predetermined entry/exit rules, consistent position sizing, and backtesting; random strategy selection with different sizing across trades makes it impossible to attribute success or failure to skill vs. luck.

- **Passive investing as wealth foundation** [51:22–52:07]: Longer holding periods dramatically increase probability of profit (50% at 1 day, ~68% at 1 year, 100% at 20 years historically); structure majority of savings in passive index funds to avoid the stress of needing to be right on short-term predictions.

- **Active trading experience improves passive investing** [01:40:35–01:41:47]: Experience with options and active trading teaches emotional discipline and the value of inaction; helps long-term investors resist panic selling and stay committed to their plan during drawdowns.

- **Dunning-Kruger effect in options learning** [01:25:24–01:26:04]: New traders feel overconfident early, then hit a valley of despair as they realize complexity; only after sustained learning do they reach competence—but even then, knowledge doesn't guarantee profitability.

## Notable quotes

> "I need to know how money works. I need to figure out how investing works, because it doesn't matter what kind of money I make in my life—if I don't know what to do with it or the things I can do with it to grow it and preserve it, then I'm going to be screwed." [03:32]

> "The more active I am, the worse I do... I was never letting a prediction play out. I was just changing my predictions with each day with each new movement in the market." [34:53]

> "Fortune favors the prepared mind." [01:34:08]

## Regime / context

Recorded October 2020, in the aftermath of the March 2020 COVID crash and subsequent V-shaped recovery. Chris reflects on his experience during that volatile period—both the initial hedge gains from long VXX calls and the subsequent losses from betting on a second leg lower that never materialized. The conversation emphasizes lessons learned from the 2015 August volatility shock and the 2020 market regime, making it particularly relevant to traders navigating high-volatility environments. This is Part 1 of a multi-part interview series.
