# What You Want vs What Is in Options Trading | Outlier Options Trading Beginner Lab

## Summary

This episode dissects the gap between trader preferences and market reality, using community comments on a podcast with Augustine LeBron as case studies. Eric demonstrates how traders often demand incompatible outcomes (high returns + low volatility + cheap trades + high win rates), then explores why simpler, naked strategies outperform complex spreads—and why capital efficiency, while appealing, is not the same as optimal performance. The session includes a live backtesting example in SLV comparing short puts, put spreads, short calls, and call spreads to illustrate real trade-offs.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2025-12-13)
- SLV rallied from ~37.15 to ~47.19 between early September and mid-October expiration [49:09–57:14]
- MSOS experienced an upside catalyst (Trump cannabis comments) that exceeded the expected move of the short straddle [38:06–42:16]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **The wants vs. reality gap** [03:34–07:21]: Traders typically demand 30%+ returns, low volatility, cheap trades, high win rates, and simplicity simultaneously—an incompatible set of constraints.
- **Naked strategies outperform spreads** [12:25–17:12]: If a credit spread is profitable, at least one leg is profitable; trading only the profitable leg (naked) is mathematically superior, though more expensive in margin.
- **Fees and commissions worsen with complexity** [19:53–21:23]: A single naked option has a tighter bid-ask; a spread requires a more nuanced counterparty and two-leg routing, increasing total cost.
- **Capital efficiency ≠ optimal performance** [43:51–45:15]: Prioritizing capital efficiency is a valid preference, but it is sub-optimal for risk-adjusted returns; traders must acknowledge this trade-off honestly.
- **Small accounts face hard constraints** [24:05–31:50]: A $5K account with 1% risk per trade can only risk ~$50 per position, forcing oversizing or avoidance of low-edge strategies like earnings plays (which require large sample sizes to overcome ~1–2% casino-like edge).
- **Positioning includes more than Greeks** [36:47–38:06]: Total Greek exposure, correlations, alignment to profit mechanisms, and catalyst exposure all matter; hedging (e.g., buying shares to offset delta) is about isolating desired risk, not predicting specific events.
- **Backtesting example: SLV short put vs. spreads** [49:09–57:14]: Short put (63¢ credit) outperformed put spread (30¢ credit) by 2× on a down move; short call (56¢) was crushed; call spread (19¢) hit max loss. Management at inflection points (e.g., closing short calls at $1.58 vs. $0.79 at expiration) can recover ~$630 per contract.
- **Edge is thinner and less glamorous than expected** [59:00–01:00:18]: Real edge is typically 1–5% alpha, not overnight riches; it often looks boring and doesn't match trader preferences, which is why many reject it.
- **Confidence and ignorance** [10:50–11:30]: Traders often express strong opinions on topics they don't fully understand (e.g., "spreads waste buying power"); this confidence-ignorance mismatch is dangerous.
