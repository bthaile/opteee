# High Win Rate Trading Strategies

## Summary

Win rate—the frequency with which trades end profitably—is a psychologically appealing metric that drives much retail trading marketing, but it is a dangerously incomplete measure of strategy quality. A strategy can have a 95% win rate and still lose money if average losses far exceed average wins. True edge lies in expected return, not win rate; the speaker demonstrates this through backtested short-premium strategies (selling puts in indices, short straddles around earnings) that achieve high win rates (~89%) but require careful risk management to handle fat-tail losses.

## Key takeaways

- **Win rate is a psychological trap** [00:23–01:25]: High win rates appeal to our preference for winning, but this preference drives the disposition effect—selling winners too early while holding losers—which undermines profitability.

- **Expected return, not win rate, determines profitability** [06:19–06:51]: A portfolio with 95 winning trades averaging $100 gain and 5 losing trades averaging $2,000 loss yields negative $500 total P&L. Win rate alone tells you nothing; you need average win, average loss, and sample size.

- **Short premium strategies naturally produce high win rates** [02:47–04:05]: Selling options (puts, calls, straddles) creates a capped profit with unlimited risk—the mathematical tradeoff that generates high probability of small wins but occasional catastrophic losses.

- **Delta as probability proxy** [04:38–05:27]: A 30-delta option has ~30% probability of being in-the-money at expiration and ~65% probability of touching that strike. Delta is a useful shortcut but diverges from true probability, especially in high-volatility or longer-dated names.

- **Earnings straddle example** [07:48–09:05]: Selling at-the-money straddles into earnings (trading implied volatility overpricing vs. realized volatility) produced 4 wins and 2 losses across 6 names in a single day—illustrating the strategy's high win rate but requiring many samples to validate edge.

- **Put skew and variance risk premium** [10:00–11:02]: Puts trade at higher implied volatility than calls (e.g., 27.66% IV on 30-delta put vs. 18.23% on 30-delta call in SPY) because of demand for crash protection. This skew creates a structural edge for put sellers, especially in uptrending markets.

- **Backtested 25-delta put-selling strategy (2013–present)** [11:29–13:09]: 139 trades, 89% win rate (124 wins), average win $2,700, average loss $13,613, max loss $87,770. Compound annual growth rate modest (~broad-market returns), but strategy demonstrates that high win rate + fat tails can still be profitable if paired with filters and position sizing.

- **The fix: focus on expected return, not win rate** [14:25]: Ignore win-rate marketing. Evaluate strategies by expected value (average win × win probability − average loss × loss probability). Low-win-rate strategies with high expected returns can outperform high-win-rate strategies with poor risk-reward.
