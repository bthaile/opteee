# Market Inflection Point with David Hunter | The Outlier Pod Ep48

## Summary

David Hunter discusses multiple inflection points across markets and the economy in late August 2023, forecasting a "melt-up" rally to 6,000–7,000 on the S&P 500 and 20,000 on the Nasdaq before a severe deflationary bust and financial crisis in 2024. He argues that despite recent pullbacks, excess liquidity from pandemic stimulus remains, the Fed is likely near the end of its tightening cycle, and gold and silver are poised for significant rallies to $3,000 and $60 respectively before the downturn.

## Key takeaways

- **Short-term pullback vs. longer-term melt-up** [00:40–01:23]: Hunter made a Friday call for market lows; today's weakness and potential head-and-shoulders pattern could test 4,200, but he maintains conviction in a melt-up to 6,000–7,000 on the S&P and 20,000 on the Nasdaq.
- **Sentiment as fuel** [02:09–03:18]: A 5% pullback has reversed sentiment sharply; many who fought the rally from October are now bearish again, showing lack of conviction. This skepticism is healthy—it's the "wall of worry" that fuels rallies.
- **Fed pause likely in September; tightening cycle ending** [04:49–05:33]: Powell speaks tomorrow; Hunter expects a "steady as she goes" message. Retail and consumer weakness are visible, but the Fed will balance inflation progress against recession risk. The narrative shift to "tightening is ending" is the key catalyst.
- **Oil and gasoline rolling over** [07:02–07:52]: WTI crude heading toward low 60s; gasoline prices falling. This supports the inflation-expectations story and removes a headwind for Fed policy.
- **Three catalysts for the rally** [08:15–09:24]: (1) Fed tightening cycle perceived as over; (2) dollar rolling over (from ~104 toward 80); (3) interest rates peaking (10-year to 2.5% by year-end). Together, these drive FOMO and sustained upside.
- **Street still skeptical on November hike** [09:47–10:28]: Futures show ~47.5% probability of a 25bp hike in November, but Hunter believes Fed members will signal a pause. Street is more bearish on economy than the Fed itself.
- **Fed Dot Plot unreliable** [13:46–14:37]: Hunter dismisses the Dot Plot as a consensus of academics; the Fed is always late to tighten and late to ease. Markets are better leading indicators than Fed forecasts.
- **Balance sheet unwind in context** [16:16–17:08]: ~$1 trillion pulled from the system, but this is only 20% of the $5 trillion injected in 2020–21. Excess liquidity remains, explaining economic resilience despite tight policy.
- **Melt-up timeline: months, not years** [19:42–20:21]: The rally will be steep and fast—a matter of months. The bust follows in 2024, but Hunter cannot pinpoint exact timing; the market will signal when to turn.
- **2024 bust: depression-speed recession** [36:44–37:35]: Hunter expects the bulk of 2024 to be negative GDP and negative financial environment. The bust will "feel like a depression but at the speed of a recession" (12–18 months), with major banks failing globally.
- **Global leverage and systemic risk** [39:17–39:42]: Global debt at ~$300 trillion vs. $875 billion Fed balance sheet in 2008; today's Fed balance sheet is $9 trillion. Leverage is massively greater, making downside moves faster and more severe.
- **Housing: builder subsidies masking secondary-market collapse** [26:01–28:45]: Home builders are subsidizing mortgage rates (5% vs. 7.5% market) to attract buyers. Secondary market is frozen (17% YoY decline in sales); builders capture demand. In the bust, secondary supply will flood back, and Hunter forecasts a potential 30% drop in home prices.
- **Consumer bifurcation: haves vs. have-nots** [31:59–34:26]: Wealthy homeowners with equity and pensions spend freely; lower-income consumers are strapped, using credit cards for necessities. This split explains resilience now but masks fragility.
- **Gold at inflection point** [40:34–42:46]: Gold near all-time highs (~$1,950); triple-top pattern at $2,100 is bullish (third time's the charm). Hunter targets $3,000 pre-bust within 6–8 months; sentiment is rock-bottom, a contrarian signal. Breakout above $2,100 should run hard.
- **Silver consolidation base** [47:08–49:05]: Silver in long consolidation since 2011; Hunter targets $60 pre-bust. Double-bottom forming; must clear $30, then $35, then old highs near $50 before breakout. Sentiment worse than gold; vertical move likely within 6 months.
- **Dollar and rates as drivers** [49:29–51:45]: Dollar rolling over (104 → 80); rates peaking (10-year to 2.5% by year-end, then to zero or negative during bust). These shifts, plus Fed narrative, will propel equities and metals together—unusual but powerful.
- **Options traders: give yourself time** [55:44–56:01]: Hunter cautions that his forecasts may stretch longer than expected. Options traders should not assume 3-month timeframes; the market is in charge, not the forecast.

## Notable quotes

> "I think an awful lot of people viewed any rally as a bear market rally and now they feel that bear market rallies were on its course and then we're heading back towards... there's still a lot of skepticism about this market and that's healthy. I mean that's a wall of worry that I talk about—you need the fuel comes from people remaining skeptical."

> "If the street starts thinking it's permanent that the tightening cycle is over, that's all you need to get a big rally going."

> "The market is the leader most of the time... the stock market, the bond market are going to lead the Fed. They're going to be leading indicators and the Fed's a follower, so by definition the Fed's going to be kind of late to the recognition."

## Regime / context

Dated 2023-08-29. This is a live market discussion recorded during a pullback in late August 2023, with the S&P 500 near 4,400–4,500 and gold near $1,950. Hunter's forecast is for a melt-up rally through Q4 2023 and into early 2024, followed by a severe deflationary bust and financial crisis dominating 2024. The Fed is expected to pause in September; Powell speaks the day after this recording. Hunter's longer-term thesis (global bust, bank failures, 30% housing decline) is contingent on the Fed's reluctance to re-stimulate aggressively, a key difference from 2008–09 policy response.
