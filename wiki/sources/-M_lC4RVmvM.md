# 2 BIG Problems - Wheel Options Strategy

## Summary

Eric identifies two critical pitfalls in the wheel strategy: (1) traders claim comfort with assignment at a price but fail to mentally model what happens when the stock declines significantly, leaving them holding underwater positions without a management plan; (2) premium hunting—chasing the highest return on invested capital—leads traders into high-volatility securities where they encounter far greater variance than expected, forcing uncomfortable decisions mid-trade. The covered strangle offers a partial solution by maintaining capital flexibility and allowing basis management through additional short puts.

## Key takeaways

- **Assignment comfort is theoretical until tested** [00:42–01:43]: Traders say they're comfortable getting assigned at price X when the stock looks strong, but when it declines 20–30%, that comfort evaporates. The real test is whether you'd still accept assignment at that strike after a significant drawdown.

- **Establish a hard floor, not just a plan to hold** [04:23–05:26]: If selling cash-secured puts, set a sell-stop order 5–10% below your entry. This prevents the trap of "I'll just hold and sell covered calls" when the underlying has collapsed and your thesis has changed.

- **Premium hunting conflates return and risk** [06:05–08:48]: Comparing PG (1.3% return on invested capital, 26% one-year variance) to TSLA (5.5% return, 66% variance) shows that higher premium correlates with higher implied volatility and larger underlying moves. Chasing the highest return means accepting 3× the variance.

- **Implied volatility drives both premium and movement** [08:48–09:38]: TSLA's 81.64% IV vs. PG's 22% IV explains the premium difference. Higher IV means you'll deal with the trade more frequently—assignments, rolls, and larger adverse moves—not just higher income.

- **Think through the entire scenario before entry** [03:37–03:56]: Most traders imagine a small dip and recovery. Reality often brings sustained declines. Pre-plan your exit or basis-management protocol so you don't abandon your plan mid-crisis.

- **Covered strangle as an alternative** [02:53–03:15]: By not deploying all capital on the initial short put, you retain dry powder to sell additional puts after assignment, maintaining flexibility and avoiding the all-in basis trap of the pure wheel.

## Notable quotes

> "Most traders fail to truly materialize in their mind what is the case that is going to lead them to being assigned." [03:15]

## Regime / context

Recorded 27 December 2022, during a bear market with elevated volatility. Eric notes that 1.3% return on invested capital for PG was still attractive despite the bear regime; in prior bull markets, he hunted for ~1% returns. The AAPL example (trading at $132 after a $179 entry) reflects the 2022 tech selloff. Volatility conditions and IV levels cited are specific to late 2022 and should not be extrapolated to other regimes.
