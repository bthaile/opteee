# Ultimate Trading Blueprint: Trading Plans, Strategy Outlines, & Trade Logs Explained

## Summary

Eric walks through the three foundational documentation tools that transformed his trading from consistent underperformance (first 5 years) to a 24.6% average annual return (2018–2022): **trading plans**, **strategy outlines**, and **trading logs**. These tools enforce organization, pre-decision planning, objective performance measurement, and accountability—eliminating the trader as an uncontrolled variable. The video provides a detailed template for each component and emphasizes that structure, not intelligence, is the primary driver of outperformance.

## Key takeaways

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Purpose of documentation** [00:00–01:27]: Trading plans, strategy outlines, and logs serve five core functions: (1) organization across infinite market variables, (2) pre-decision planning to avoid reactive decision-making under stress, (3) forcing clarity on trading objectives, (4) enabling optimization through data, and (5) creating accountability for controllable factors.

- **The planning paradox** [02:06–03:08]: Plans rarely survive first contact with the market (Mike Tyson: "everybody has a plan until they get punched in the mouth"), but they provide a starting point for adjustment. The Marine Corps planning model: acknowledge the plan will change, but have one anyway.

- **Goal alignment** [03:08–03:45]: Every trading activity must serve the ultimate goal of making money. Vague objectives ("practice this strategy") waste time; goals must be clearly defined and measurable (desired end state, 6-month, 1-year, 5-year, 10-year objectives).

- **Trading plan structure** [06:28–14:19]: Eight main sections: (1) **Purpose & Goals** (desired end state + timebound objectives); (2) **General Market Notes** (structure, studies, observations, mechanisms); (3) **Analysis** (technical/fundamental tools, efficiency filters, tool library); (4) **Systems** (known trading systems reference); (5) **Trade Checklist** (generalized pre/post-trade items); (6) **Portfolio Management** (core/speculative allocation, risk protocols, performance metrics); (7) **Strategy Outlines** (per-strategy detail); (8) **Notes & RFI** (self-assessment, performance reviews, audits).

- **Strategy outline anatomy** [14:19–17:22]: Four core sections per strategy: (1) **Executive Summary** (3–5 sentences explaining how the strategy makes money); (2) **Setup** (scan criteria, technical/fundamental indicators, allocation, scaling); (3) **Management** (time-bound, profit, and loss management); (4) **Analysis** (which metrics matter for this strategy, e.g., IV vs. HV for short premium, irrelevant for ratio diagonals).

- **Self-assessment & controls** [17:22–18:34]: Identify personal weaknesses (e.g., impulsivity) and create explicit controls (e.g., mandatory pre-trade checklist before entry). Performance variance between traders stems from individual psychology, not tool access; controls mitigate this.

- **Trading log implementation** [19:33–21:46]: Use Excel or Google Sheets; manually input trades to stay close to data. Structure: portfolio summary tab (calculators, year-to-date metrics), individual strategy tabs (different metrics per strategy), and studies section (backtesting results, hypothesis validation). Logs force reconciliation between memory and actual performance.

- **Optimization loop** [04:06–05:30]: Without documentation, traders run in circles. Logs create a dataset that eliminates guessing; you know what works best, reducing wasted effort and feeding improvements back into the system.

- **Start simple, evolve** [08:24–08:45]: Do not be overwhelmed by the full template. Begin with Purpose & Goals, then add sections as you learn. The mental hurdle of perfection prevents action; start imperfectly and refine.
