# Top Energy and Geopolitical Trends Shaping 2024 - Doomberg | The Outlier Podcast Ep68

## Summary

Doomberg discusses macroeconomic and geopolitical risks dominating 2024, including U.S.–Russia escalation over Ukraine, the Biden debate fallout, and the structural impossibility of global decarbonization. The core thesis: energy is life; all humans seek higher living standards; fossil fuel demand will continue rising regardless of renewable deployment, because production gates demand and developing nations will not voluntarily stunt growth.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2024-06-28)

- **Biden debate as watershed moment** [00:39–03:26]: The June 27 Biden–Trump debate exposed cognitive decline that was already obvious to observers; Democratic panic and media coordination calling for Biden's withdrawal signal a narrative shift, though Biden's refusal to resign creates a constitutional paradox (unfit to run but still commander-in-chief).
- **U.S.–Russia escalation unfolding** [12:44–22:03]: Russia's Foreign Ministry summoned the U.S. ambassador and formally declared the U.S. an official party to the Ukraine conflict, warning of swift retaliation. This is wall-to-wall coverage in Russia and globally but nearly absent from Western media—a dangerous information asymmetry.
- **Nord Stream pipeline destruction** [18:34–22:03]: Biden publicly stated Nord Stream would not happen; the pipeline was destroyed weeks later. While not conclusively proven, the hypothesis fits all observable data (no credible alternative actor identified, all adversaries believe it was the U.S., and the U.S. has not produced exculpatory evidence).
- **Drone strike on Crimean beach** [13:43–14:59]: Ukraine launched ATACMS missiles into contested territory; Russian air defense partially deflected one, scattering cluster munitions onto a civilian beach. Russia claims U.S. facilitation via guidance systems and drone targeting; the U.S. has not publicly addressed the incident.
- **Energy Institute 2024 data** [39:38–40:28]: Global oil, coal, and natural gas consumption all hit all-time records in 2023. The world added 7.5 exajoules of fossil fuel energy vs. 5 exajoules of renewables—a 50% margin favoring fossil fuels, contradicting decarbonization narratives.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Energy is life; demand is inelastic** [42:05–43:04]: All humans everywhere seek higher living standards. Energy consumption is the primary lever. Seven billion people outside the G7 will develop; they will burn fossil fuels. No amount of Western renewable deployment changes global consumption—it only shifts who benefits.
- **Production gates demand** [55:16–56:04]: The U.S. produces 20% of global oil and 25% of global natural gas. If the U.S. stopped producing coal, oil, or gas, those molecules would be produced elsewhere and burned elsewhere. Unilateral production cuts do not reduce global emissions; they only cede market share and revenue.
- **Decarbonization is not happening** [39:38–43:46]: Despite trillions spent on renewables, fossil fuel consumption grows ~1.5–2% annually. Europe's reversion to coal during the energy crisis proved that when energy security is threatened, climate commitments evaporate. Developing nations watched and learned.
- **Solar is expensive, not cheap** [46:56–48:24]: California has 40%+ solar penetration and is the second-most expensive state for electricity. If solar were cheap, California would have the cheapest power. The conflation of spot-market negative prices (when solar overproduces) with average delivered cost to consumers is a grand fallacy.
- **Grid reliability degrading** [45:14–46:17]: Forced introduction of intermittent renewables degrades grid inertia and power quality, causing manufacturing disruptions. Batteries are too expensive and require materials that don't exist at scale. Thermal batteries and hydroelectric storage are narrow exceptions.
- **Nuclear is the only scalable decarbonization lever** [56:29–57:19]: If the world truly cared about carbon emissions, it would build nuclear plants. Environmentalists' opposition to nuclear makes decarbonization impossible; AI demand will further strain grids, forcing higher energy prices and rationing by price.
- **Democracy incompatible with energy austerity** [58:29–59:17]: Forcing populations to lower living standards is unpopular. Elections are popularity contests. The green energy transition is fundamentally incompatible with democracy; populism is the crowd's response to unpopular central-planning mandates.
- **Geopolitical singularities ahead** [01:04:22–01:05:26]: The November 2024 election and potential Ukraine/Middle East/China escalation are "singularities" where economic laws break down. Contested elections, succession risk (Texas), and nuclear-armed adversaries create unmodellable tail risks.
