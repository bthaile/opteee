# Fed Rate Cut Aftermath - Stock Market Update

## Summary

The Fed delivered a widely-anticipated 25 basis point rate cut on September 18, 2025—the first cut of the year in a cycle expected to see four to six cuts total. The market responded with broad gains, particularly in small caps (Russell 2000), which had been lagging large caps significantly. Eric reviews his trading approach for the event, discusses sector rotation opportunities (real estate, financials, homebuilders), and contextualizes near-term market dynamics against longer-term structural shifts in retail investing behavior.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2025-09-18)

- **Fed cut delivered as expected** [04:08]–[04:36]: 25 bps cut confirmed; first of the year. Some dissent on record (members arguing for 50 bps), signaling potential for more aggressive cuts ahead.
- **Russell 2000 outperformed on the day** [05:04]–[05:35]: Small caps are far more sensitive to Fed rate changes and business credit conditions. Consumer charge-offs declining, but business charge-offs rising—a lag indicator favoring small-cap stimulus.
- **Powell's commentary on inflation and tariffs** [08:08]–[09:07]: Fed expressed concern about tariff-driven inflation but unemployment broadly where they want it. Two competing macro narratives (rate-sensitive growth vs. tariff-driven inflation) create nuance in forward guidance.
- **VIX at 15.75—elevated but not extreme** [30:28]: Market uneasy despite rally; volatility has been trickling up while S&P hovers near all-time highs. Decoupling of VIX and equity direction is normal and nuanced.
- **Probability of two more cuts by year-end** [20:44]–[21:05]: Market pricing suggests ~2 additional cuts through December, which could trigger another leg up in small caps if confirmed in next FOMC statement.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Russell vs. S&P concentration risk** [25:17]–[26:34]: S&P 500 now ~33.8% tech; Russell 2000 offers better diversification. Large-cap dominance (mega-cap tech) is a regime shift; small caps have structural room to run if rate environment supports.
- **Small-cap catch-up thesis** [28:13]–[29:32]: S&P up ~11.8% YTD; Russell up ~7.16%. If small caps match large-cap performance from prior all-time highs (~7–12% move), Russell could reach ~2600 handle. Depends on rate commentary and speed of capital rotation.
- **Real estate sector positioning** [32:09]–[36:44]: CBRE and Welltower outperforming XLRE; suggests informed capital flowing into select names. Two scenarios: (1) leaders consolidate gains, or (2) sector-wide capital inflow lifts all boats. Prefer scenario 2; monitor options market for early signals.
- **Homebuilder lag-and-lead dynamic** [38:46]–[40:36]: Building permits and housing starts slowing; excess inventory headwind. But as inventory dries up, builders will activate. Don't wait for the data release—monitor options positioning and price action for early entry.
- **Financials benefit from rate cuts** [41:52]–[44:06]: Smaller banks (Wells Fargo, Bank of America proxy) move more than mega-banks (JPM, GS) because they gain volume and customer stimulus. Fixed-rate assets don't reprice immediately; volume growth is the primary lever.
- **Options market as leading indicator** [40:36]–[41:22]: Informed traders position in options before equity moves. Monitor unusual activity and IV skew to front-run sector rotations; pot-odds approach to low-conviction trades.

## Regime / context

**Date:** September 18, 2025 — first Fed rate cut of 2025 cycle.

**Market structure:** S&P 500 and Nasdaq near all-time highs; Russell 2000 significantly lagging (7.16% YTD vs. 11.8% for S&P). Tech concentration in S&P at ~33.8% creates regime where small-cap diversification and rate sensitivity offer structural upside if cuts continue.

**Forward catalysts:** Next FOMC statement (implied ~2 weeks out) will signal whether Fed remains accommodative. If commentary supports additional cuts, expect acceleration in small-cap rotation and sector-specific moves (real estate, financials, homebuilders).

**Retail context:** JPMorgan data shows 37% of 25-year-olds now use investment accounts (vs. 6% in 2015); average investment amount ~$470. Lower-income cohorts increasing activity but at risk of pursuing aggressive/speculative strategies. Structural wealth-building (savings + compound returns) remains underappreciated.
