# Using Military Strategies in Options Trading

## Summary

Eric draws seven core military concepts—preparation, planning, ruthless objectivity, emotional regulation, adaptability, risk management, and after-action review—and translates each into actionable trading frameworks. The thesis is that military decision-making structures (tactical decision games, course-of-action analysis, stress inoculation, Bayesian updating, and risk assessment matrices) directly improve trading discipline, process adherence, and long-term edge.

## Key takeaways

### Preparation
- **Tactical Decision Games (TDGs)** [02:20]: Scenario-based training ensures you encounter problems in simulation before live execution. Equivalent: paper trading, studying market structure, and understanding common failure points before risking capital.
- **Intelligence Preparation of the Battlefield (IPB)** [02:20]: Know the terrain, weather, and participants before engagement. Equivalent: understand market participants, volatility regimes, and the specific profit mechanism you're targeting.

### Planning
- **Marine Corps Planning Process (MCPP)** [05:37]: Problem framing → course-of-action (COA) development → COA war-gaming → COA comparison → orders development → execution.
- **COA War-Gaming in Trading** [06:49]: The [[outlier-strategy-process]] step "outline fitting structures, identify profit mechanism, create list of structures, test performance, analyze, identify best fit" is direct COA war-gaming.

### Ruthless Objectivity
- **Trade Logs as Quantitative Feedback** [10:22]: Physical fitness tests and rifle range scores are military trade logs; they force honest assessment. Equivalent: maintain a detailed [[trading-log]] to see the gap between what you think you're doing and what you're actually doing.
- **Standards and Culture** [09:10]: Enforce standards ruthlessly; peer pressure and accountability drive performance.

### Emotional Regulation
- **Stress Inoculation Training** [11:32]: Expose yourself gradually to discomfort so the real event is not your first time. Equivalent: [[paper-trading]] seriously, treating it as real money psychologically.
- **Whole-Life Approach** [13:50]: Discipline in one domain only doesn't work; emotional regulation must be consistent across life to prevent revenge trading and despair-driven decisions.
- **Even Keel Disposition** [13:50]: Temper emotional swings (happy/sad cycles) to maintain measured decision-making.

### Adaptability
- **Commander Intent** [14:53]: Define your ultimate goal (e.g., long-term wealth); decentralized execution means adapting tactics as conditions change.
- **Bayesian Updating** [14:53]: Update your understanding as new information arrives; focus on [[process-over-outcome]] rather than individual trade results.
- **Planning Process Value** [14:53]: The planning itself is as valuable as the plan; it builds mental models that allow rapid adaptation when reality diverges (Mike Tyson: "Everybody's got a plan till they get punched in the mouth").

### Risk Management
- **Risk Assessment Matrix (RAM)** [16:11]: Two columns—probability and severity. A low-probability, account-wipeout event still requires controls. Equivalent: [[trade-checklist]], portfolio-level circuit breakers, and trade management plans.
- **Unwarranted Risk** [16:11]: Ensure risks accepted have commensurate reward; avoid hidden risks (e.g., selling puts on a stock with unrealized earnings).

### After-Action Review (AAR)
- **Structured Reflection Cadence** [18:29]: Conduct post-trade AARs, weekly portfolio mini-AARs, monthly deep AARs (3–4 hours), and annual holistic AARs (~2 weeks). Questions: What was supposed to happen? What actually happened? Why? What can we do better?
