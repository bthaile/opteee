# Tom Sosnoff - Short Vol, Long View | The Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Tom Sosnoff discusses volatility dynamics at market highs, the structural risks of short-vol positioning in a crowded trade, and the transformative impact of AI on retail traders and market structure. He argues that AI creates unprecedented opportunities for self-directed investors—particularly in portfolio monitoring, risk mitigation, and backtesting—while criticizing tech leaders for framing AI narratives around job displacement rather than opportunity. The conversation covers his current short-vol, long-theta, short-delta positioning, interest-rate risks in bonds, and the evolution of prediction markets.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2026-05-27)

- **Record highs + elevated vol = structural risk** [31:33] — VIX at ~20 (10% above mean) while market near all-time highs is historically anomalous; at record highs, VIX should be ~20% below mean. This suggests crowded short-vol positioning that could unwind violently if market turns.
- **Nvidia earnings: expected move ~12, realized ~2** [03:00] — Good day for premium sellers; move was much smaller than anticipated, typical of strong earnings in a momentum environment.
- **Bond yields at 19-year highs; Fed policy contradiction** [45:50] — 30-year bond trading at 19-year yield highs despite administration mandate to lower rates. Bonds "teetering" overnight; if they break, Fed forced to raise rates despite political pressure.
- **VIX futures vs. spot VIX spread signals complacency** [27:50] — VIX futures closed >20 (spot market), but one-month VIX ~10% lower, indicating market pricing near-term risk but expecting significant relief. Sosnoff skeptical of this assumption.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Short-vol positioning at market extremes** [31:55] — When short vol at record highs, the risk is asymmetric: if market reverses, vol can spike 33% overnight (20→30+). Sosnoff manages this by maintaining negative delta hedge (short futures + short ETFs) to offset theta gains.
- **Theta-delta ratio in high-market regimes** [34:29] — At record highs, Sosnoff targets 1:0.5 to 1:0.75 theta-to-delta ratio (e.g., short $1,000 theta, short $500–$1,500 delta). This provides real protection without over-hedging.
- **Call spread construction** [35:56] — Collects 35–50% of width; uses 5–15 point spreads in liquid names (SPY, QQQ); prefers wider spreads before adding contracts.
- **Profit-taking discipline** [37:46] — Takes profits opportunistically (10–30% of max profit); doesn't hold to 50% target if market moves against position. On defined-risk spreads, doesn't adjust; lets them expire or closes at profit.
- **Short futures as capital-efficient delta** [35:35] — Uses ES/NQ futures for pure delta hedge rather than short ETFs; more efficient margin. Sells call spreads + naked calls on top.
- **Hedging vs. adjustment philosophy** [38:48] — Rejects "hedging" terminology; prefers "adjustment." As retail trader, exits positions rather than buying insurance (hedges). Hedging is expensive; liquid markets allow exit instead.
- **Under-hedging principle** [40:23] — If short 100 delta, hedge only 50 (never over-hedge). Avoids being wrong both ways.

- **AI as leveling force for retail traders** [12:51] — With AI, traders with zero coding background can now ingest options data (2007–present), build databases, and run backtests. Removes infrastructure barrier; enables exploration of ideas previously locked behind institutional resources.
- **24/7 trading already here; 24/5 now** [48:42] — Markets already trade 24/5 (Sunday 5pm–Friday 5pm CT); crypto, forex, CFDs trade 24/7. Adding weekend hours is incremental; won't dramatically change volume patterns or zero-DTE dynamics.
- **Prediction markets: disruptive but flawed** [51:32] — Kalshi, Polymarket have challenged SEC/CFTC and opened new markets; Sosnoff credits them as disruptors. However: (1) fee structure 20–100× higher than listed markets; (2) customer-acquisition model (not pure exchange); (3) unsustainable as "dead money" instrument (mathematically impossible for majority to win long-term). Expects disruption of their fee model.
- **Market as prediction engine** [24:01] — Price is the most accurate predictive mechanism in the world because it aggregates massive money flow and emotion. Price peaks long before technology matures; AI price peak likely sooner than expected due to acceleration of all timeframes.
- **Interest-rate risk under-priced** [26:01] — Bonds teetering; if they break, Fed has no choice but to raise rates despite political mandate. Regulatory flip or rate shock could compress AI valuations quickly.

## Regime / context

**Date:** 2026-05-27. S&P 500 ~90% through earnings season; YoY profit growth ~20–23%. Tech strong; Nvidia reported with expected move ~12, realized ~2.

**Volatility regime:** VIX ~20 (10% above historical mean) at market all-time highs—structurally unusual and signals crowded short-vol positioning. One-month VIX ~10% lower, indicating market expects volatility relief but Sosnoff skeptical.

**Interest-rate risk:** 30-year bond yields at 19-year highs overnight; bonds "teetering" despite Fed mandate to lower rates. If bonds break, Fed forced to raise rates. This is a key tail risk to AI/growth valuations.

**AI narrative:** Sosnoff emphasizes disconnect between stock-market enthusiasm for AI and public sentiment (Eric Schmidt booed at commencement). Argues tech leaders frame AI as job-killer rather than opportunity-creator, poisoning narrative. Contrasts with his own experience: AI dramatically lowers barriers for retail traders (backtesting, portfolio monitoring, 24/7 risk management).

**Prediction markets:** Kalshi and Polymarket have disrupted regulatory landscape but face fee-structure vulnerability and unsustainable "dead money" model. Sosnoff bullish on disruptors, skeptical on valuations.
