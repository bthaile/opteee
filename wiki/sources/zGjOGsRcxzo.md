# Michael Burry & GameStop Analysis | Meme Stock Watch Ep57

## Summary

Eric reviews Michael Burry's detailed Substack essay on his GameStop investment thesis from 2018–2021, tracing Burry's fundamental analysis, board engagement, and eventual exit before the January 2021 squeeze. The discussion covers Burry's earlier Avanti investment (2001), his recognition of GameStop's undervaluation and cash generation, the mechanics of the gamma squeeze driven by retail call buying, and current GameStop positioning with elevated implied volatility despite recent price weakness.

## Key takeaways

- **Burry's Avanti thesis (2001)** [14:52–21:14]: Burry identified a company trading below free cash flow after legal scandal, averaged down as it fell, and exited profitably when a competitor agreed to acquire it—illustrating thesis-driven value buying independent of short interest.

- **GameStop entry (2018)** [23:55–26:39]: Burry began accumulating GME in 2018 when console cycle pessimism depressed valuation; he identified catalysts including potential leverage buyout, asset sales (Spring Mobile), and significant cash generation relative to market cap.

- **Board activism and buyback strategy (2019)** [30:45–35:37]: Burry engaged with GameStop's board, advocated for large share buybacks to compress the share count and boost EPS, and noted the unusual opportunity created by high short interest and trading volume.

- **Keith Gill / Roaring Kitty emergence (2020)** [39:33–41:05]: Burry initially avoided contact with Gill to prevent SEC Schedule 13D filing obligations; later spoke with Ryan Cohen for two hours, discovering Cohen's patient, long-term approach aligned with deep-value investing.

- **Exit before squeeze (2020)** [48:25–51:15]: Burry sold his 3 million share position at ~$135 in late 2020, missing the January 2021 gamma squeeze. He cites execution risk, board concerns, and the vague nature of Cohen's technology pivot as reasons for exiting before the retail-driven call buying created the squeeze.

- **Gamma squeeze mechanics** [55:10–57:36]: Retail traders bought massive call volumes; market makers (Citadel, Virtu) sold those calls and became short gamma. As calls fell in-the-money, dealers faced pressure to buy stock to maintain delta neutrality, creating a feedback loop—though Eric notes Burry's explanation oversimplifies the role of existing dealer books and gamma management.

- **Gamma ramp vs. squeeze distinction** [01:07:52–01:09:11]: Eric clarifies that retail call buying created a gamma *ramp* (more options falling in-the-money), not the squeeze itself; the squeeze resulted from subsequent dealer hedging and scarcity of real shares, compounded by naked short exemptions.

- **Current GME positioning (Dec 2025)** [01:14:30–01:21:43]: GameStop trades near $31–32 with 50% implied volatility (1st percentile historically low). Eric maintains a covered strangle, noting strong call skew and elevated premium despite recent weakness; he sees no near-term squeeze catalyst without share scarcity compression.

- **GameStop Vault and collectibles strategy** [01:23:53–01:29:39]: GameStop launched a digital-physical hybrid vault for PSA-graded trading cards (Pokemon, Magic) in partnership with PSA, storing physical cards with NFT ownership. The addressable market (graded cards, sports collectibles) is ~$2–3 billion globally, growing at ~7% CAGR—modest relative to GameStop's current market cap.

- **Volatility premium opportunity** [01:34:33–01:38:22]: Despite lower realized volatility, GME's implied volatility remains ~8.4% premium-per-delta-per-strike vs. ~3.2% for Apple, making short premium strategies (covered strangles, short puts) attractive even as the stock consolidates.
