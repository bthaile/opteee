# Outlier Options Trading for Beginners Live

## Summary
A ~2.5-hour live beginner session where the Outlier host (Eric) teaches foundational options/trading concepts through two live trade case studies (a crude-oil MCL breakout, plus Google, Tesla and short-put examples) and an audience AMA. The core Outlier framing is process over outcome: a trade is "good" or "bad" based on its foundation, not its P&L, and everything must align — thesis, profit mechanism, strategy, and time frame. Beginners learn that markets are probabilistic (not predictable), that risk/position-sizing comes before profit, and that a plan you actually trust can only be built through paper trading, logging, and data.

## Key takeaways
- **Recap the market yourself, daily.** Deliberately synthesize what happened (an "after-action review") rather than just glancing at prices — that is where learning sticks [09:21].
- **Identify trend from the relative position of highs and lows** (higher highs/lower lows, "swings" or "terms"), reinforced by linear-regression / standard-deviation channels. Real charts are messy, so context and multiple indicators matter [11:02–13:56].
- **Time is objective; longer horizons are less predictable.** More time = a bigger window for new information to enter and move price. "The best predictor of the weather tomorrow is the weather today" [16:07–17:14].
- **The probability cone widens with time.** A one-standard-deviation expected-move projection (probability of touch) fans out the further out you go — GME's 90-day expected range was far wider than its one-week range [19:03–21:15].
- **Align thesis → profit mechanism → strategy → time frame.** Match research depth to holding period: a 6-month thesis needs far more analysis than trading a short-term momentum move [22:45, 29:48]. This is Outlier's "Big Four" trade/strategy process [25:47, 40:33].
- **A good trade can lose money and a bad trade can make money** — quality is the foundation of the trade, not the outcome. Over a system, a single trade's result should be almost fully deprioritized in favor of replicable conditions and strategy [47:53–49:46].
- **Don't buy something while it's still falling ("catching a knife")** unless you're sizing for a probabilistic floor; enter on evidence of a turn/breakout, not on impulse to chase a move [50:15, 53:12, 1:00:42].
- **Size on risk first, not reward.** Define your support/floor (zooming out across 3-month/6-month/1-year/3-year charts), then size so the downside is acceptable; a tighter entry-to-support gap lets you size larger [51:51–56:15, 1:12:02].
- **Scale in/out; prefer micro futures (MCL) for granular sizing.** Futures give Delta-1 leverage plus Section 1256 tax treatment, but you pay carry and higher commissions [58:23, 1:06:53–1:10:05].
- **Manage gap risk with sizing, not hedges.** For (near-)24h futures the only real gap window is when they're closed; the answer is always: size the trade correctly [1:03:17–1:04:28].
- **Use two stops:** a mental/soft stop to watch behavior around a level, and a hard stop rested with extra room so only an undeniable move takes you out. Use support/resistance, not arbitrary percentages [1:05:48, 1:10:31, 1:13:41].
- **Read candlesticks for the "flavor" of the day** (who won, buyers vs sellers) rather than named patterns; volume profile shows where volume actually transacted at each price [1:02:29, 1:20:18].
- **Long-term buy-and-hold is a bet on the company growing** — day-to-day price barely matters; this misaligns with covered calls / CSPs (the "wheel"), whose big drawback is never actually owning the stock you wanted [42:47–46:12].
- **The disposition effect is real:** in a live poll on a winning short put, the room split ~60/40 on taking profit — humans over-sell winners. "You don't go broke taking profits" is a broken adage for trading [1:35:55–1:42:46].
- **Whether to take a winning short put off is "it depends" — on expected return.** Taking it early can lock a smaller total profit and lower your strategy's overall expected return, even as win rate rises; decide the rule ahead of time, by strategy (Theta-harvest vs directional) [1:47:35–2:01:08].
- **Emotional discipline = reward the process, not the outcome.** If you feel great on wins you'll feel terrible on losses; focus on executing the skill correctly [2:20:25–2:22:11].
- **The hard part isn't "follow your plan" — it's building a plan you trust.** You naturally deviate from anything you don't believe in (the "directions from someone who's never been there" analogy). Trust comes only from data [2:24:16–2:29:30].
- **Paper trading + a trading log are non-negotiable.** They're the only way to build your own data, know a strategy's real behavior (so a losing streak doesn't shake you out), and decide objectively when to replan (a big deviation from expected win rate) [2:22:33, 2:33:11–2:36:05].
- **Trade your own circumstance — no FOMO.** Risk tolerance and who depends on you dictate your approach; admiring someone like Roaring Kitty doesn't mean copying his risk [2:36:37–2:37:50].
