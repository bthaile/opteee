# Trap Tasks: STOP Making These Options Trading Mistakes That Destroy Your Results

## Summary

This video identifies six fundamental trading activities that, when executed poorly, damage results more than if left undone entirely—termed "trap tasks." Eric walks through risk management, backtesting, trading plans, technical/fundamental analysis, options fundamentals, and strategy optimization, showing how each can become a self-sabotaging exercise and how to execute each correctly using profit mechanisms, robustness checks, and process-based discipline.

## Key takeaways

- **Risk management trap: static rules and over-hedging** [02:36–03:56]
  - Static exit rules (e.g., "always exit at X points") ignore context and volatility regime.
  - Over-hedging to cap downside while outperforming is mathematically impossible; if it worked, everyone would do it.
  - **Fix:** Build portfolio- and strategy-specific plans tied to the underlying's implied volatility and regime (e.g., Tesla at 65% IV vs. GE at 34% IV require different management).

- **Backtesting trap: overfitting and bias** [05:15–08:49]
  - Overfitting creates false precision (e.g., entering at 11:52:30 on Thursdays only).
  - Availability bias: easy-to-access information is widely known and unlikely to contain edge.
  - Desired-outcome bias: entering analysis with a predetermined conclusion poisons all downstream decisions.
  - **Fix:** Build a structured scientific framework; control for biases; run robustness checks (tweak one variable—if performance collapses, it's not robust); include realistic friction, execution costs, and latency assumptions.

- **Trading plan trap: superficial confidence without research** [08:49–11:15]
  - "Trade your plan" sounds smart but fails if the plan lacks empirical foundation.
  - Analogy: a free-throw shooter with thousands of reps vs. one who's never touched a ball—confidence and adherence differ vastly.
  - Abandoning a plan under pressure reveals it was built on paper, not research.
  - **Fix:** Conduct end-to-end analysis and outline; the planning exercise itself is the primary value, not the final plan document.

- **Analysis trap: signals without profit mechanisms** [11:15–12:34]
  - Using Bollinger Bands or moving averages as standalone signals is confirmation bias; traders remember hits, forget misses.
  - Technical and fundamental analysis are tools to identify opportunities, not edges themselves.
  - **Fix:** Start with a well-researched profit mechanism (e.g., momentum); then select analysis tools that directly identify candidates for that mechanism.

- **Options fundamentals trap: structure as edge** [13:45–14:56]
  - Buying options, selling options, or switching from iron condors to verticals is not an edge—it's just learning the cost of entry.
  - Different structures are tools; fit the tool to the job, not the job to the tool you know.
  - **Fix:** Start with profit mechanism; fit the option structure to it (e.g., short iron condor makes no sense for a momentum play expecting upside).

- **Strategy optimization trap: abandoning too early, tweaking without hypothesis** [14:56–16:12]
  - Abandoning a strategy after a few losing trades is a massive mistake.
  - Tweaking inputs without a hypothesis and large-sample validation leads to false improvements.
  - **Fix:** Start with well-researched strategies; form a hypothesis for any improvement; test and validate on a much larger sample than recent trades; focus on robustness and process, not individual trade outcomes.
