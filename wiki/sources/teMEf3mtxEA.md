# Anne-Marie | Derivatives Day Trader & My Dog's Guest Appearance | Money Talks

## Summary

Anne-Marie, an intraday futures day trader with a background in neuroscience, statistics, and recruiting, discusses her evolution from a chaotic early trading career (losing millions in 18 months) to a disciplined, probability-based approach focused on price action and dealer positioning. She emphasizes the role of the OODA loop (observe–orient–decide–act), contingency planning, and emotional discipline as the core edges for retail traders competing against institutional players and algorithms.

## Key takeaways

- **Early losses and the pivot** [04:44–05:46]: Lost millions in first 18 months by overestimating ability and treating the market like a slot machine; took 6 years to break even. The turning point came when her husband told her she didn't know what she was doing, forcing a complete reset.

- **Statistical and behavioral foundation** [05:46–06:35]: Shifted from anecdotal pattern-matching to correlations and probabilities; studied *Thinking, Fast and Slow* and neuroscience (David Eagleman) to understand how the brain fills in gaps and creates false confidence in hindsight.

- **The OODA loop as trading framework** [10:19–11:22]: Adopted John Boyd's OODA loop (observe–orient–decide–act) as a daily discipline; the critical gap is *orientation*—understanding what the market players are actually doing, not just what indicators say.

- **Player-centric storyboarding over indicators** [12:38–13:32]: Stopped relying on technical indicators; instead, reads the "storyboard" of where sellers have hit their targets and where buyers are gaining force. Tracks support/resistance levels and dealer gamma positioning to anticipate reversals.

- **Contingency planning vs. second-guessing** [33:58–34:58]: A trading plan is not just entry/stop/target; it's a series of if-then statements for what to do if the market moves unexpectedly. The difference between contingency planning and second-guessing is whether you're materially re-evaluating the setup or just panicking.

- **Position sizing by trend and support** [43:10–44:22]: Sizes up when trading in the direction of the broad trend and coming off deep support; sizes down (half-size) for counter-trend trades. Avoids cortisol-driven stress by keeping position size manageable enough to stay mentally engaged but not panicked.

- **Intraday cycle approach** [37:09–41:20]: Maps daily/weekly open, high, low, close on regular trading hours only; looks for strength (price above prior close) vs. weakness (price below prior close). Trades from one "stair step" to the next in the direction of trend; reduced from 30–50 trades/day to ~4 trades/day as volatility expanded.

- **Options driving the market (2020 observation)** [52:20–54:27]: Noted a major shift starting June–July 2020: market makers buying massive out-of-the-money calls (200+ delta on AAPL, TSLA) to hedge short gamma, which is now driving price action rather than the reverse. Warns that rapid moves create bubble-like conditions prone to sudden reversals.

- **Illiquidity as crash catalyst** [54:52–55:50]: The only thing that causes a market crash is illiquidity; credit markets are the canary. Cited China loan restructuring and money-printing as warning signs; noted the 2007 crisis had roots in 1985, so unwinding can take 15+ years.

- **Emotional capital and sound-off** [24:36–25:01]: After the ISRG earnings disaster, turned off all audio alerts because the "ding" triggered Pavlov's-dog panic. Still uses silent pop-ups; emotional recovery required breaking the feedback loop.

- **Homework and discipline** [31:43–32:24]: Compares trading to professional athletics: everyone wants the reward, but few do the unglamorous daily review and analysis. Homework is non-negotiable; it's where edge is built.

- **Risk vs. reward thread** [17:37–18:09]: Risk and reward are intimately linked on a thread; expanding reward expectations expands risk even more. Overestimating ability and intellect to overcome emotion is the #1 mistake.

## Notable quotes

> "The brain does not like anything that's not solid… your mind will only connect two or three things and your brain fills in the rest with whatever else it thinks ought to be back there."

> "No plan survives first contact… we think we have a trading plan because we know our stop, we think we know our entry and we think we know our target—that's not a plan at all."

> "The only thing that makes a market crash is illiquidity… the more money they print, the less reliable the whole system becomes."

## Regime / context

Recorded December 2020, during a period of elevated retail options activity and dealer gamma-driven rallies. Anne-Marie notes a structural shift in mid-2020 where options positioning (particularly out-of-the-money calls) began driving underlying price action rather than following it—a regime change she identifies as historically unusual and unsustainable. Her warnings about illiquidity and rapid reversals reflect late-2020 market fragility. The interview captures a moment of transition in market microstructure that would intensify in 2021.
