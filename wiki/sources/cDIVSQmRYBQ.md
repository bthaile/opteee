# Take Risks to Create Wealth - Tom Sosnoff | The Outlier Podcast Ep49

## Summary
A long-form interview with tastytrade/tastylive co-founder Tom Sosnoff (ex-CBOE open-outcry floor trader, builder of thinkorswim and tastytrade) on The Outlier Podcast. Sosnoff's through-line: wealth comes from taking risk at every reasonable opportunity and making the maximum number of decisions, because success is a byproduct of decision volume and longevity, not of waiting for certainty. The usable material is his trading philosophy — a counterparty/order-flow worldview, dismissal of technical analysis and price mean-reversion in favor of forward-looking derivatives pricing, and the claim that the only true pot-odds edge for a premium seller is selling in very high implied volatility.

## Key takeaways
- Take risk at every opportunity. His stated differentiator: "we take risk at every opportunity we get, no matter what... every single chance you have to take risk you should" — with the guardrails that it must be a reasonable, probabilistic play and you are not "giving up odds to the house" (not a casino/roulette bet). [31:18]
- Success is a byproduct of the number of decisions. "The more decisions you make, the more good decisions you make." Get comfortable, then scale decision-making — the same muscle carried tastytrade from tens of thousands of trading decisions into building businesses. [33:19]
- Speed over deliberation, and never wait for certainty. An 80% decision executed now beats a 100% decision later; "there's nobody who creates wealth with 100% decisions." On the floor everything was speed — "don't think about it, there's nothing to think about." [27:22] [28:09]
- Bad decisions are unavoidable, so don't dwell. In an efficient world you will make X bad decisions; the job is to make far more good than bad and build off winners rather than ruminate on losers (host flags the stoicism parallel). [30:31]
- Longevity is the real edge. Between two nearly identical people, the marginal difference is the ability to stay in the game longer — framed via a baseball analogy: 12–15 "bloop single" at-bats separate a .302 hitter from a .256 hitter over a season. [28:32]
- Counterparty mindset, not market-timing. A floor trader doesn't arrive with an opinion; you take the other side of order flow "as long as you give me a little bit of edge." Being a "contrarian" means playing the counterparty role, which is distinct from believing in mean reversion. [24:52]
- Rejects price mean reversion. Something up big still has the same chance of going higher as lower; he does not believe in mean reversion on price. As of recording he had an internal PhD/mathematician debate scheduled specifically on price mean reversion. [23:43] [01:05:39]
- The only real pot-odds advantage is high IV. Probabilistically, strategies tend to perform the same across volatility environments; what changes is that in higher implied volatility "you take more risk, you make more money." Selling price extremes (sell what's up big, buy what's down big) is only a subjective "engagement tool," not a proven edge. [22:52] [24:01]
- Anti-technical-analysis by design. Markets are driven by order flow, not charts ("charting was the dumbest thing ever to me"). thinkorswim launched with no charting because nobody they hired off the floor had ever used a chart; they later licensed Prophet Charts (Tim Knight) and built their own only to appease customers. Everything they do is forward-looking derivatives pricing, treating markets as random and efficient. [16:26] [18:44]
- Derivatives pricing as crowdsourced probability. Using the totality of market money to price outcomes is a law-of-large-numbers bet (the "jelly beans in a jar" analogy) — a better projection of the future than looking at the past. [20:28]
- No exit strategy; "no high-five rule." He gets nervous when a founder has an exit strategy because it means they're focused on the wrong thing — "we're just Traders," so they'll sell only if someone overpays. Both thinkorswim and tastytrade drew zero acquisition interest for ~10 years, then multiple bidders appeared "out of the woodwork" around year 10. [36:21] [37:32]
- Sold tastytrade to IG for ~$1.1B in 2021; took the second-lowest of five bids because he distrusted the SPAC market (and was proven right). Built thinkorswim in his 40s, tastytrade in his 50s — "you never know how your life's going to play out." [38:14] [43:07]
- Content, not static "investor education." He views investor education as doomed like academia (curriculum too expensive to change); instead he built dynamic, quantitative, homegrown content with a deliberate "no news, no guests" rule, pushed through a fire hose and integrated with personalities (Howard Stern parallel). [52:31] [54:27]
- Mission = create outliers by reducing the "pool of mediocrity." He accepts that his content has both lost people money and created more millionaires/success stories than anyone in finance; the goal is to push people off the mean toward outlier outcomes rather than preach "protect the nest egg." [47:56] [48:44]
- Stay open on mechanics, fixed on values. He'll change his view on the "mechanical optimization" of finance whenever new data appears (in-house think tank actively tries to find holes in what they do), while noting he won't change core social/values positions. Twin ideas the host says shaped him: accepting risk over risk-avoidance, and "nobody knows anything." [1:07:39] [50:29]

## Regime / context
- Recorded/published September 2023. The material is largely timeless philosophy rather than a dated market call, so most takeaways are regime-independent.
- Main dated anchors: the ~$1.1B tastytrade→IG sale in 2021 ("two years ago"), and the open-outcry Chicago pit era of the early 1980s–late 1990s that Sosnoff repeatedly calls gone and non-replicable — his counterparty/order-flow instincts are rooted in a market structure that no longer exists.
- The anti-mean-reversion and pot-odds-in-high-IV claims are presented as Sosnoff's live, still-debated positions (he mentions a scheduled internal PhD debate on price mean reversion), not settled conclusions — treat as one expert's stance.
