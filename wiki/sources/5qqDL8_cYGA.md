# When NOT to Use Vertical Spreads

## Summary

This video examines a common mistake in vertical spread construction: using them to trade volatility when the Greeks don't actually provide meaningful exposure to vol changes. The example shows how a short put vertical on a high-IV-percentile stock can leave a trader nearly vega-neutral, defeating the stated thesis of selling premium into elevated implied volatility.

## Key takeaways

- **Vega mismatch in spreads** [00:27]: A short 130 put (17¢ vega) paired with a long 125 call (16¢ vega) nets only ~1¢ of residual vega exposure—effectively making the trader indifferent to volatility moves, despite the high IV percentile setup.
- **Thesis-strategy alignment** [00:00]: High implied volatility percentile (86%) alone is not sufficient reason to deploy a vertical spread; the Greeks must align with your actual directional and volatility thesis.
- **Avoid vol-contraction trades in spreads**: If your core thesis is vol crush, a vertical spread's offsetting Greeks can neutralize that edge entirely.

## Notable quotes

> "You're not getting any real exposure to volatility here... You're effectively indifferent to what vol does."
