# War in Iran, Oil Volatility, and Defense Sector Opportunities | The Options Trench

## Summary

In response to escalating US-Israel-Iran conflict, oil volatility has spiked dramatically with extreme call skew, creating dislocations across energy and defense sectors. The hosts analyze how to identify trading opportunities in elevated volatility regimes by examining volatility surfaces, term structures, and cross-sectional price performance, with particular focus on why delta shifts in high-volatility commodities and how to exploit mispricings between options markets and prediction markets.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2026-03-07)

- **Oil volatility extreme**: Oil up >2 standard deviations; call skew "one of the most extreme" ever seen, with calls repriced rapidly and puts "wrecked" relative to at-the-money [02:28]
- **Implied correlations rising**: 3-month S&P implied correlations exceeded 20%; fixed income (TLT) down ~2 standard deviations; dollar up >1 standard deviation [01:10]
- **Oil futures curve steep**: Front-month WTI ~$91; March 2027 ~$67 (≈25–30% premium); August future ~$75, indicating acute near-term stress [29:46]
- **Defense contractor flows**: Lockheed Martin saw net call premium spike from $17.9M to $270M on Feb 27 at 11:30 AM PT; underlying moved $656→$681 over two days [24:20]
- **Polymarket vs. options dislocation**: Polymarket bidding 60% probability oil >$90 by March 31; WTI options market pricing 87.5–90 call spread at $1 (1.5:1 odds), inverted signals [50:26]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Delta in high-volatility regimes**: When volatility is extreme and asset has zero lower bound (e.g., USO), delta shifts higher on calls because all volatility extends right tail; at-the-money call may be 57 delta instead of 50 [07:41]
- **Volatility-delta coupling**: In oil, realized volatility and price direction move together (both up); this amplifies delta beyond model prediction because gamma gains compound with vega gains [12:11]
- **Skew as risk signal**: Extreme call skew (calls expensive, puts cheap) makes call spreads cheap and put spreads expensive; selling call spreads is inefficient; selling puts or buying put spreads is the asymmetric play [34:59]
- **Put spread vs. outright put**: Selling outright puts risks catastrophic loss if asset drops >30% in one day; ratio put spread (buy 1, sell 2 lower strike) provides delta-neutral short-volatility exposure while capping downside [39:33]
- **Futures curve as compass**: Steep backwardation (front month >> back month) signals near-term stress; flat or contango back months suggest market doesn't expect crisis to persist [29:46]
- **Cross-sectional scanning**: Sort assets by 5-day, 1-month, 52-week price performance; identify ripple trades (e.g., airline vol when oil spikes) and second-order opportunities (e.g., fertilizers, aerospace) [01:02:10]
- **Realized vs. implied volatility**: Lockheed Martin 20-day realized vol ~57%; Raytheon ~80%; both have similar implied vol (~mid-30s), suggesting LMT is underpriced relative to realized move [32:29]
- **Prediction market inefficiency**: Polymarket odds move on small price swings despite unchanged delta; options market is more efficient; retail traders can arbitrage or fade prediction markets [54:22]

## Regime / context

**Date**: March 6–7, 2026. US-Israel-Iran conflict escalating; Trump administration meeting with defense contractors (Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, BAE, Boeing, Honeywell, L3, Northrop Grumman) to discuss replenishment of munitions and supplies.

**Volatility regime**: Extreme event-driven spike in oil and energy; elevated implied correlations across equities; defense sector benefiting from procurement signals. Oil futures curve steeply backwardated, suggesting market expects near-term resolution or supply relief, not sustained crisis.

**Key structural note**: USO (oil ETF) has zero lower bound, causing delta compression at-the-money and amplification of out-of-the-money call deltas in high-vol regimes. This is distinct from equities (e.g., SPX) where skew is typically negative (puts expensive). Oil skew is positive (calls expensive), inverting typical hedging dynamics.
