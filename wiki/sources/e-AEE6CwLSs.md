# How Stocks and Options Reprice Through Earnings | The Options Trench

## Summary

This episode explores the four primary effects that drive stock and options repricing through earnings events: implied volatility expansion into the release (~2 weeks prior), IV contraction through earnings, immediate price reaction gaps, and post-earnings announcement drift (PEAD) over the following quarter. The hosts detail the "renting the straddle" trade—buying a straddle when implied volatility is cheap relative to expected earnings move, gamma-scalping the position, and exiting before earnings—as a practical framework for capturing volatility expansion without directional risk.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2026-03-20)
- Robin Hood (HOOD) example: IV was trading ~60% two weeks before earnings, with historical earnings moves averaging ~8% [08:21]
- At the time, HOOD stock was ~$120, making the expected earnings straddle worth ~$9.60 (8% of $120) [09:27]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **IV expansion into earnings** [01:35–02:17]: Implied volatility typically begins rising ~2 weeks before a known earnings date as traders buy protective puts and uncertainty increases.

- **IV contraction through earnings** [02:39–03:07]: Once the market opens post-earnings, the unknown becomes known; IV in the front contract typically contracts, though realized volatility may exceed implied, creating losses on short premium.

- **Immediate price reaction** [03:27–03:54]: Large information releases often create gaps that can continue, reverse, or define the tenor of the trading day; these moves can be ridden for short-term profit.

- **Post-earnings announcement drift (PEAD)** [03:54–04:36]: Repricing of information is not instantaneous; moves often continue over the following quarter as capital rotates and the market gradually incorporates the new information.

- **Renting the straddle framework** [05:40–06:04]: Buy a straddle when implied volatility is cheap relative to the expected earnings move; gamma-scalp the position to isolate volatility exposure; exit before earnings when IV has expanded to reflect the earnings move.

- **Converting straddle to volatility** [10:35–11:26]: To compare a straddle price to trading-day volatility, divide the straddle by 0.8 (since mean absolute deviation is 80% of standard deviation), then annualize by multiplying by 16 (√251 trading days).

- **Variance averaging, not IV averaging** [15:08–15:48]: When blending trading-day volatility with earnings-day volatility, square both numbers first, average the variances, then take the square root to recover the blended IV. Naive averaging of IV percentages produces incorrect results.

- **Why renting the straddle is not a slam dunk** [23:47–24:33]: If realized volatility comes in lower than the implied volatility you bought, theta decay will exceed gamma scalp profits, resulting in a loss. The trade depends on accurate volatility forecasting.

- **Gamma scalping vs. point-to-point volatility** [25:47–28:47]: Gamma scalping reduces noise and keeps your position aligned with your volatility thesis; however, it is not mandatory to isolate volatility. Alternatively, trading a large sample of names without scalping can also work if your forecasts are better than market pricing.

- **Restriking the straddle** [29:13–31:13]: Restrike when the underlying moves significantly and you want to maintain your target Greeks and volatility exposure, not based on time or price movement alone. Treat each day as "day zero"—would you initiate this trade now?

- **Building a trading universe** [32:34–36:26]: Compare implied and realized volatility both to each name's own history and cross-sectionally (relative value) across the market. Look for intersections where IV is low relative to history AND low relative to peers, or vice versa. Avoid timing volatility globally; instead, construct long/short portfolios within any regime.
