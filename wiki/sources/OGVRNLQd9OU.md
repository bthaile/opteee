# Wednesday Skill Development Stream: Trading Fundamentals & Market Analysis

## Summary

Eric restructures the Wednesday live stream to focus on skill development and practical trading concepts rather than deep market analysis. The session covers critical misconceptions about options trading—particularly that selling options provides inherent edge, that account size determines profitability, and that high probability of profit equals edge—before walking through a CCJ breakout trade and a fundamental/technical analysis of MNMD ahead of an FDA catalyst.

## Key takeaways

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Account size is not a binary switch for profitability** [00:00–13:18]. Large accounts enable portfolio margin and reduce PDT friction, but the core trading skills required remain identical regardless of capital. The belief that "trading becomes easy with more money" is a common failure mode.

- **Selling options has no structural edge over buying** [14:45–24:35]. Both sides of the market have inherent trade-offs: sellers benefit from theta decay but sacrifice gamma and convexity; buyers sacrifice theta but gain convexity. Neither is objectively superior absent additional edge.

- **High probability of profit ≠ edge** [36:13–39:21]. A 90% win rate with $10 wins and 10% loss rate with $100 losses yields negative expected return. Expected return requires integrating four components: probability of win, win size, probability of loss, and loss size. A 35% win rate with $100 wins and 65% loss rate with $10 losses is profitable.

- **Rolling is simply closing and opening a new trade** [10:54–12:10]. This conceptual clarity took years to internalize; it is not a special mechanic but a sequence of two discrete actions.

- **Directional bias exists in both long and short premium strategies** [18:26–19:45]. A cash-secured put payoff diagram shows you still need directional correctness; you merely have a small buffer (typically 1–5%) rather than unlimited downside protection.

- **Theta trade-offs are real** [27:20–30:38]. Selling theta decay comes with defined profit potential and reduced wiggle room; you sacrifice gamma and convexity. There is no free lunch; every component you gain comes with a corresponding cost.

- **Discretionary trading edge is built on experience and intuition** [47:00–48:24]. Automated systems are binary (fit criteria or don't); discretionary traders operate in shades of gray, leveraging decades of subconscious pattern recognition that cannot be fully quantified.

- **Scanning and filtering are learnable skills** [43:34–47:00]. Efficiency with constrained attention—learning which filters narrow the opportunity set meaningfully—is a core edge. Blindly trading scan results without additional analysis is ineffective.

### Dated market read (2024-06-05)

- **CCJ breakout trade** [42:06–49:55]. A uranium play scaled to a $600k model portfolio, based on breakout mechanics. The trade illustrates that finding one winning idea among many non-events is normal; the goal is to position yourself to capture good opportunities, not to predict outcomes with certainty.

- **MNMD fundamental & technical setup** [01:02:14–01:24:04]. Stock is unprofitable, leadership compensation is disproportionately high ($2.67M CEO comp with 76.6% bonus), but price action and options positioning suggest near-term upside catalyst (FDA-related). Technical shows higher lows, consolidation, and 10-day MA above 20-day MA. Options market shows 82% IV in June 20 expiration and bullish skew but small overall OI. Trade approach: play the near-term catalyst or trade vol edge; do not hold post-catalyst.
