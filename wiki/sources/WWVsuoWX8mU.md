# Tariffs, FOMC, Earnings, & Options   - Stock Market Update | Options Trading Live

## Summary
A mid-week (Wednesday, 2025-07-31) live market update from the Outlier Trading host, threading together four dated catalysts — the Aug 1 tariff deadline, that day's FOMC hold, ongoing Q2 earnings season, and options positioning — into repeatable trading mechanics. The evergreen backbone is reading the market as a forward-discounting machine and using the **term structure of implied volatility** to see what the market is pricing around a known event date, then choosing an option structure whose payoff fits the expected move. Along the way it covers sector rotation, deriving the risk-free rate from box spreads, position sizing for earnings, evaluating a strategy statistically, and reading unusual options flow.

### Evergreen mechanics
- **Market as a forward-discounting machine:** "The stock market is forecasting. It's discounting future cash flows into today," looking 6–9 months out; economic prints are lagging, so watch the *trend* of the metrics, not the single number [13:00]–[13:41].
- **Volatility term structure as event pricing:** plot implied vol for business days before/after a known catalyst; a rising, more-pronounced kink around the event date means the market is pricing more expected movement there. This is "the same thing" as reading vol around an earnings event, and it yields the standard earnings playbook — play an **expansion or contraction of post-catalyst vol** [28:15], [31:52]–[32:35]. Unique events have few historical look-backs but useful proxies.
- **Risk-free rate from box spreads:** you can back out the inferred yield from a box spread (example: Oct-17 6350/6400 box at a 49.12 mid → ~4.574%), often a better rate than T-bills/ETFs and usable for cash management [46:31]–[47:54].
- **Rate transmission is not 1:1:** overnight/policy rates don't pass straight through to savings (liquidity-vs-yield tradeoff across checking/savings/CD), and front-end cuts don't map to mortgage rates because mortgages live on the long tail of the yield curve. A 7% mortgage is roughly the historical average [40:11]–[49:56].
- **Position sizing for earnings:** this cycle had ~46 earnings trades with several outsized moves (a 4-SD, plus 2.84 and 2.72 SD), i.e. a bit under 10% exceeding 2 SD. Because names can move 3–5x their expected move, it is "very easy to be oversized" — a robust, correctly-sized system matters more than being right [66:31]–[67:57].
- **Evaluating a strategy statistically:** track the variance/dispersion of trade outcomes over time; you can compute a required sample size via a coefficient-of-variation / confidence-interval calc (often absurdly large), so in practice watch whether dispersion tightens and tail losses stay infrequent [73:38]–[77:57].
- **Backtesting is for elimination, not validation:** "a backtest that looks good is table stakes — you can make any backtest look good"; a bad backtest lets you throw an idea out, a good one only signals potential [78:37]–[79:18].
- **Match structure to the expected move:** for a strong near-term directional expectation, compare long ATM call vs synthetic long vs shares vs short puts on a cash-normalized/leverage-adjusted basis — long calls delivered the most, synthetic long second, short puts far behind. His actual trade was a **ratio synthetic long** (long calls, fewer short puts against them purely to offset theta), avoiding ratio-call-diagonals which suit longer campaigns [82:04]–[91:27].
- **Reading a breakout with volume:** confirm quality by comparing single-day volume to the 5-day and 20-day averages (ETHA on 9 July: 44.5M vs 26M 5-day vs 20M 20-day → near-term buildout of bullish volume) [80:43]–[82:04].
