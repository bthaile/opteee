# trade management decision making | Options Trading Basics

## Summary

This live education stream deconstructs common mistakes in trade management decision-making, emphasizing that management rules must be anchored to the **profit mechanism** of the trade, not arbitrary option value thresholds. Eric walks through real Reddit examples and demonstrates how to model option P&L at different price/volatility scenarios using simple calculators, then applies military planning concepts (commander's intent) to pre-trade scenario planning.

## Key takeaways

- **Profit mechanism first** [04:13–10:18]: Management decisions must be based on whether the underlying price action validates or invalidates your trade thesis, not on whether your option is up/down X%. Arbitrary percentage stops (10%, 50%, etc.) are meaningless without understanding the profit mechanism.

- **Structure doesn't determine management** [09:50–10:18]: Different option structures (long calls, spreads, short puts) require different management approaches, but the *real* decision tree starts with defining the profit mechanism, not the structure.

- **Example: momentum/relative strength breakout** [15:08–18:54]: A swing trade entry on a first higher low after breakout should exit when price violates that higher low, regardless of option P&L. A 17% option loss while the trade setup remains valid is irrelevant to the exit decision.

- **Position sizing to the stop** [26:34–28:24]: Size your position so that the loss at your stop level (defined by price action, not option value) represents an acceptable portfolio drawdown (e.g., 0.5%). If the trade reconstitutes another entry signal, size that separately.

- **Modeling option P&L at different scenarios** [32:36–38:11]: Use free tools (BarChart, Weeble's calculator, or a simple Black-Scholes spreadsheet) to interpolate what your option will be worth at your stop price, accounting for delta, gamma, vega, and theta. This informs position sizing, not exit timing.

- **Volatility changes don't invalidate the trade** [38:45–40:55]: If IV contracts but your profit mechanism is still alive, the trade remains valid. Use historical IV ranges to estimate potential vega P&L and bake that into position sizing.

- **Pre-trade scenario planning (commander's intent)** [47:23–51:36]: Before entering a trade, explicitly decide your intent (e.g., "acquire stock via assignment" vs. "manage as a short put without assignment"). Plan rough responses to likely scenarios so you're not building the plane mid-flight.

- **Deep ITM short puts example** [47:00–49:18]: A trader who says "I'm okay with assignment but hate the cost basis" is contradicting themselves. They should have pre-planned assignment scenarios before selling the put.

## Notable quotes

> "Do not worry about what the value of your option is. Think about what the profit mechanism is. What then validates or invalidates the profit mechanism itself—not the value of your option. That doesn't matter." [19:30]

> "The propensity is to base the decision heavily on the value of an option, which is a massive mistake." [21:48]

> "The purpose of commander's intent is acknowledging the fact that shit is likely going to go sideways. That big awesome beautiful plan is great to have, but shit is likely going to go sideways and you're going to have to call some audibles." [50:45]
