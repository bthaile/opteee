# CANSLIM Trend Trading with Jim Roppel | The Outlier Podcast Ep50

## Summary

Jim Roppel, a 35-year veteran trend-following trader and student of Bill O'Neill's CANSLIM methodology, discusses his evolution from a losing stock broker to a successful hedge fund manager. The conversation covers his approach to identifying market leaders through technical and fundamental screening, the critical role of risk management and emotional discipline, and his current bullish outlook on a potential market inflection point after a prolonged bear market.

## Key takeaways

### Foundational approach
- **Disposition effect as pivot point** [03:03]: Cutting losses and implementing better selection criteria immediately reversed Roppel's trading results from consistent losses to profitability.
- **CANSLIM as resonance, not dogma** [06:16]: The methodology must align with your nature and DNA; many styles work, but only if they agree with your temperament. Roppel became an institutional customer of William O'Neill and attended his seminars regularly starting around 1990.
- **Charts as trend visualization, not prediction** [07:59]: Charts are a visual way to follow trends, not predictive tools. Risk management is critical because most breakouts fail.

### Market leader identification
- **Relative strength as divining rod** [32:13]: RS above 85–90 points directly to the best names in the market; Roppel screens for RS >85, EPS rating 80s–90s (ideally 99), and comp rating >90.
- **Beat-and-raise as serial phenomenon** [22:35]: Earnings beats are not one-off events; they typically repeat over multiple quarters. This is the "cockroach theory"—if you see one beat, expect more.
- **Tight consolidations precede big moves** [34:06]: Historical winners form tight areas (2–5 weeks of tight weekly closes) before breakouts. Example: BKR recently broke out of a five-week tight consolidation.
- **Earnings bring durability and institutions** [36:10]: Stocks with earnings attract institutional buyers and liquidity; no-earnings stocks get pummeled in bear markets because there's no bid when sellers appear.

### Position sizing and hedging
- **"Price will hurt you, but size will kill you"** [26:27]: Roppel learned this lesson by holding 27,000 shares of Siebel Systems and getting stopped out of a stock that later rose 800%. Concentration is necessary for outsized gains, but over-concentration creates unmanageable drawdowns.
- **Hedge 30–50% with in-the-money calls** [30:12]: When a stock extends 20–30% above the 50-day moving average, sell in-the-money calls (typically 80-delta) to hedge without triggering tax events or giving up unlimited upside.
- **Avoid hedging the entire position** [30:12]: If you hedge 100%, you miss the blowoff move. Roppel typically hedges 30–50% to dampen pullbacks while preserving upside.

### Screening and implementation
- **Minimum liquidity threshold: $100M average daily dollar volume** [46:58]: Below $50M is "junk heap"; below $100M introduces slippage and execution risk.
- **Screening is the easy part; implementation is everything** [48:08]: Finding stocks via screens is just "walking out of the dugout in the first inning." The real battle is emotional control when the market is open and firing at you.
- **Sunday research, daily execution** [44:07]: Roppel runs screens on Sunday to identify candidates, then watches for price-up-on-volume signals during the week. He already knows the names before the market opens.

### Market regime and timing
- **Net new highs/lows as bull confirmation** [53:19]: A true bull market requires net new highs exceeding new lows by 150+ per day, often reaching 400–500. Throughout 2023, the market had net negative breadth, signaling a "transitional bull" at best, not a confirmed bull.
- **Buy at breakout, not extended** [55:05]: The third biggest rookie mistake is buying extended. If a stock has already run 20 points from a breakout, wait for the next consolidation and base.
- **Catch the move in size at the right time** [57:32]: Most big winners run 400–1000% post-bear-market. If you catch one at the right point but only allocate 10% of your account, a 100% move only moves your equity 10%. You must size appropriately.

### Current market outlook (October 2023)
- **Bear market duration and inflection risk** [01:06:29]: Roppel believes the bear market began in February 2021 (when the advance-decline line topped), making it ~2.5 years—historically long. A new low would make it the fifth longest bear in history.
- **18-month forward view** [01:07:10]: The Fed will crush inflation and stop raising rates within 18 months. Monetary policy will shift from headwind to neutral. AI's potential $15.9 trillion GDP boost by 2030 (separate from EVs, biotech, digital revolution) is a generational tailwind.
- **Short-term pessimism, long-term optimism** [01:15:03]: Market temperature for the next year is a 2 (very bearish); for 18 months out, a 10 (very bullish). If forced to wait 9–12 months to double money vs. 24 months to triple, the triple is the better bet—but most traders want it now.
- **Russell 2000 as key breadth indicator** [01:09:47]: Until the Russell exceeds 2,000 and net new highs exceed new lows by 150+ daily, a bull market is not confirmed. The indexes remain in a "massive cup and handle."

### Discipline and ego
- **Being right vs. making money** [01:21:06]: You must choose one. Roppel chose to make money, which means reversing positions quickly when the trend contradicts his opinion, even if it bruises his ego.
- **Specialization over diversification** [01:25:02]: Roppel has mastered one method (CANSLIM trend-following) over 35+ years. He is not interested in learning shorting or other approaches; he compounds within his circle of competence.
- **Process over outcome** [01:27:33]: Roppel never expected to run a hedge fund or appear on podcasts; he simply wanted to learn the markets. Thirty years of process-driven discipline and golf (20+ years with a pro, bringing his handicap from 36 to mid-single digits) show that mastery requires decades.
