# Don't Make These Common Trading Mistakes w/ Euan Sinclair

## Summary

Euan Sinclair discusses three critical mistakes retail options traders make: reversing the trilogy of edge, risk management, and psychology; confusing options mechanics with edge; and misunderstanding volatility forecasting. He emphasizes that edge—finding a statistical advantage—must come first, that risk management is straightforward, and that psychology is overrated. The conversation covers FTX's collapse, cryptocurrency's speculative nature, and practical guidance on selling volatility around defined events rather than competing with professional models.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2022-12-09)

- **FTX collapse mechanics** [05:34]: The implosion was driven by a reverse squeeze on FTT (the exchange's own token used as collateral), similar to a bank run on a stock. Alameda was losing money trading while simultaneously propping up FTX's liquidity artificially.
- **Crypto market aftermath** [01:56]: Volatility in major coins dropped back to pre-FTX levels within days; Bitcoin and Ethereum are perceived as surviving independently of the exchange ecosystem.
- **Cryptocurrency's speculative foundation** [14:06]: Bitcoin has no role in the broader economy as a currency; the entire space is built on speculation, comparable to fantasy football companies.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **The trilogy reversed** [23:07]: Traders must prioritize (1) edge first, (2) risk management second, (3) psychology third. Most retail traders invert this, overemphasizing discipline and psychology while neglecting edge discovery.
- **Edge vs. mechanics** [30:04]: Options are merely a vehicle; learning Greeks, structures (iron condors, butterflies), and mechanics does not create edge. All option structures reduce to either long or short volatility.
- **Selling volatility as the retail edge** [01:00:40]: The most accessible edge for retail is selling volatility around defined events (earnings, Fed meetings, elections, FDA announcements) where timing is known but outcome is uncertain.
- **Volatility forecasting for retail** [33:22]: Competing with professional quant models (Jane Street, Susquehanna) via time-series methods (GARCH) is futile. Instead, identify idiosyncratic situations where volatility is mispriced due to event risk.
- **Straddles vs. strangles** [39:15]: Prefer short straddles over strangles because losses provide faster feedback; selling strangles can mask losses until catastrophic blowup. Theta decay alone is not an edge.
- **Delta hedging costs and ranges** [48:23]: Hedging is expensive; retail should widen hedging ranges significantly (e.g., rehedge when straddle price doubles, not at fixed delta intervals). Transaction costs are insidious and often consume 10–15% of annual profits.
- **Hedging gamma dependency** [50:38]: Delta hedging ranges must account for gamma; high gamma means delta changes rapidly, so wider ranges are appropriate. Gamma-neutral positioning simplifies hedging decisions.
- **Directional traders should avoid options** [56:20]: If you have genuine directional edge, trade the underlying or use margin—not options. Options force you to trade volatility whether you intend to or not.
- **Avoid binary thinking** [58:15]: Retail traders often adopt rigid frameworks (long premium vs. short premium, technical vs. fundamental). The best approach is context-dependent: use the simplest instrument that expresses your edge.
- **One edge is enough** [59:29]: Attempting to build multiple uncorrelated strategies is counterproductive. Master one edge deeply rather than spreading effort across many weak strategies.

## Regime / context

Recorded December 9, 2022, in the immediate aftermath of FTX's collapse (November 2022). Crypto volatility had normalized within days; broader equity markets were navigating Fed tightening and recession concerns. The discussion reflects a mature, skeptical view of cryptocurrency's utility and a focus on volatility-selling edge in traditional derivatives markets. Sinclair's emphasis on event-driven volatility selling reflects the post-2008 regulatory environment and the persistence of mispricing around scheduled announcements.
