# INSIDE a $10 Billion Fund - Wes Gray | The Outlier Podcast Ep66

## Summary

Wes Gray, co-founder of Alpha Architect, discusses his unconventional path from a Colorado ranch to leading a ~$10 billion asset management firm. The conversation covers his evolution from fundamental value investing through quantitative research, the critical role of trend-following strategies, and the structural advantages retail traders possess—particularly the ability to hold strategies that institutional managers cannot due to benchmark constraints and fee pressures.

## Key takeaways

### Career & strategy development
- **Childhood motivation** [02:10]: Grew up broke on a Colorado ranch; grandmother's introduction to Warren Buffett and Ben Graham sparked early interest in wealth-building through investing.
- **Academic rigor at University of Chicago** [21:48]: PhD program culture emphasized "violent idea exchange"—challenging ideas rather than validating them—which fostered openness to new approaches despite initial anchoring on value investing.
- **Billionaire client as catalyst** [23:09]: A long-time trend-following client challenged Gray's academic dismissal of momentum strategies, forcing him to investigate empirically rather than rely on ideological bias.
- **Systematic value evolution** [20:21]: Transitioned from fundamental stock-picking to computerized value strategies, eventually publishing *Quantitative Value* (the book became the core strategy deployed at Alpha Architect).
- **Serendipitous infrastructure business** [13:38]: A casual suggestion to rent out internal ETF infrastructure to others evolved into a $10+ billion business—larger than the original asset management operation.

### Retail trader advantages
- **Behavioral edge through long time horizons** [31:00]: Retail investors can hold strategies that underperform 7 out of 10 years if they compound to superior long-term returns—institutional managers cannot due to benchmark pressure and fee collection cycles.
- **Trend-following as retail haven** [37:48]: Trend strategies typically deliver similar long-term returns with half the drawdown but suffer frequent whipsaws; professionals avoid them due to short-term performance drag, making this a natural retail edge.
- **Tax and fee minimization** [34:09]: The primary wealth-building advantage for retail is controlling fees and taxes; a 7% after-tax, low-turnover strategy beats a 10% pre-fee strategy that nets 5% after costs.
- **Weird strategies as edge** [30:44]: Strategies that "look like puke" in the short run and would be unmarketable to institutional clients often contain the most robust edge.

### Academic vs. practitioner gaps
- **Implementation constraints** [26:30]: Academics ignore practical limits (short availability, margin rules, market impact); professionals understand implementation but face incentive misalignment (managing other people's money).
- **Benchmark drag** [28:06]: A strategy that underperforms 7 out of 10 years but compounds to $100 is inferior to one that beats the benchmark 8 out of 10 years and compounds to $95—yet professionals must chase the latter for marketing and fee collection.
- **Trend-following resistance** [22:48]: Academic finance long dismissed trend-following as irrational; Gray initially rejected it despite billionaire client advocacy, only to discover robust empirical support after investigation.

### Behavioral pitfalls for retail
- **Strategy abandonment** [40:23]: Retail traders often test a strategy for 2 weeks, see good results, declare it "cracked," then abandon it after a drawdown—failing to give strategies sufficient sample size.
- **Autonomy paradox** [40:23]: Too much freedom without discipline leads to constant strategy switching rather than committed execution.

## Notable quotes

> "I've just noticed over the long haul that rich people—the reason they're rich is not because they're genius investors, frankly they're usually okay—they're good enough for government work, but they're great at avoiding taxes and they're great at minimizing fees." [34:20]

> "Any opportunity that is high level weird and hard to do and looks ugly in the short run is usually a great strategy and something that we have competitive edge as a retail trader." [33:04]

> "If it's not something I can code it up, can't put it in a system with rules, I ain't doing it." [18:43]
