# How I Overcame 3 TERRIBLE Trading Habits | Options Trading Basics

## Summary

Eric walks through three critical trading habits he overcame during his development as a trader: lack of self-awareness, outcome-focused thinking on individual trades, and absence of process. He illustrates each with concrete examples—including a large drawdown from an iron condor trade—and explains how implementing a trading plan and trading log became transformative. The session emphasizes that behavioral discipline and systematic tracking matter far more than strategy complexity.

## Key takeaways

### Habit 1: Lack of Self-Awareness ("Know Thyself")
- [10:37] Starting young without fully understanding your own traits, risk tolerance, and propensities leads to poor decision-making.
- [13:15] Eric's largest drawdown occurred because he was disciplined in study but lacked self-awareness about his actual comfort with dollar amounts at risk.
- [25:44] Risk aversion stems from a scarcity mindset—psychologically programmed to overvalue losses relative to gains when baseline needs feel threatened.
- [28:47] Mental detachment from trading capital is learnable; Eric used deliberate language ("and it's gone") and rules to separate emotional attachment from account size.
- [30:53] Being dispassionate and cold in decision-making produces better outcomes than emotional attachment to dollar amounts.

### Habit 2: Outcome-Focused on Individual Trades
- [14:32] Hyperfocus on making a few hundred dollars on a "high probability" trade blinded him to the path of returns and intermediate drawdowns.
- [15:45] Example: a short 5-delta iron condor on SPX risking ~$98k to make ~$2k showed unrealized losses of ~$12k mid-trade, still within profit zone—but the dollar magnitude was psychologically unbearable.
- [19:08] He knew theoretically that iron condors could show losses while in-the-money, but did not respect the actual dollar values that could appear.
- [20:35] The fix: close the trade when you realize you haven't mentally prepared for the potential path; don't force yourself to hold something you're unprepared to manage.
- [21:01] Irony: the trade ultimately landed at full profit at expiration, but he was already out.

### Habit 3: Lack of Process (The Worst Habit)
- [34:18] Starting with options, he spent a couple weeks learning basics, then immediately began trading—trying upside buys, downside trades, directionally neutral positions, all while testing different indicators (MACD, moving averages, RSI, Bollinger Bands, Keltner channels).
- [37:27] With no tracking, he fell victim to severe confirmation bias: high win rates on MACD trades were actually just because he was selling puts (higher win rate) instead of buying calls.
- [38:59] The fix: create a trading plan and trading log. These two documents operationalized early changed everything.
- [40:28] A well-defined plan and log eliminate misizing, letting winners run too short, letting losers run too long, and obsessive focus on win rate.
- [41:23] Many Theta Gang traders underperform buy-and-hold S&P 500 because of structural issues in their approach—uncomfortable truths revealed by logging.

### Using a Trading Log Effectively
- [45:48] Structure logs with a C2C (command and control) summary tab, a general "singles" tab, and strategy-specific tabs (covered strangle, ratio call diagonal, earnings, etc.).
- [47:56] Define the profit mechanism first (e.g., "momentum and drift" for long calls).
- [49:03] Quantify and qualify profit mechanisms: what defines momentum? (e.g., RSI, support/resistance, volume nodes, moving averages).
- [50:41] Plot P&L by bucket to see concentration of wins vs. spread of losses; use "cutting the tails" to identify outlier losing trades.
- [52:54] Understand outlier losses: when do they happen, why? Apply rule sets to prevent them going forward; test in-sample and out-of-sample.
- [54:19] The core question: "How can I make this strategy better? What information do I need?"

### Bargain Buying (Bonus Habit)
- [55:13] Waiting for a dip that never comes is a common trap; it's the antithesis of momentum trading.
- [56:02] Fix: buy a portion at current price, then scale in if it pulls back to your target; if it keeps falling, exit until it stabilizes.
- [57:17] "Buy high, sell higher" works for momentum; "buy low, sell high" works for value. Strategy matters.
- [58:14] A well-crafted log will prove to your brain that buying "high" in a momentum strategy works, eliminating the psychological friction.

### Self-Talk and Subconscious Priming
- [31:26] Stopped saying "I'm bad at math" in junior high; didn't replace it with a lie, just stopped the negative self-talk.
- [32:40] Self-deprecating humor and casual negative self-talk have cumulative subconscious effects; be intentional about language.
- [33:02] Small linguistic choices compound over time and affect how you apply yourself as a human.

### Obsessive Personality (Double-Edged Sword)
- [22:12] Obsessive personality can drive deep expertise (trading, gym, shooting, scuba diving) but also leads to premature application.
- [23:20] Learning option structures (verticals, butterflies) is fine; thinking there's "magic" in knowing them and immediately trading them is wrong.
- [23:58] Correct process: start with profit mechanism → scope it with signals → overlay structures → then strategies.

## Notable quotes

> "I knew full well that the trade could show a loss as it's well within this zone. What I did not respect was the dollar amounts that it could show me in this time frame."

> "If you have a well-defined plan and log, all of these other things, it goes away. And it's because you're looking at your actual performance and you now just have to reconcile."

> "Buy high, sell higher works for momentum stuff. It does not work that well for value stuff."
