# Market Melt up into a 80% Global Bust with David Hunter | Money Talks

## Summary

David Hunter, a 49-year veteran market analyst, argues that the Fed is over-tightening despite headline inflation, having misunderstood the magnitude of demand destruction already underway via bond-market repricing and commodity deflation. He forecasts a near-term melt-up (S&P 3650→4200, Nasdaq 20k) driven by Fed pivot expectations and short-covering, followed by a severe global recession and 80% bear market dominating 2023, with triggers originating in Europe, Canada, and emerging-market dollar-debt crises.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2022-07-21)

- **Fed over-tightening despite low headline rates** [01:34–03:31]: The bond market has already done ~200 bps of tightening in months; mortgage rates jumped from 2.75% to 6%, shutting down refi activity and home-buying. This is a massive liquidity drain the Fed hasn't fully accounted for.
- **Consumer is tapped out** [08:04–09:17]: Savings rate collapsed from 35% (pandemic) to 4.4% (below pre-pandemic), credit card balances surging. Consumers are switching from material purchases (homes, yards, equipment) to experiences (vacations, dining) — a temporary seasonal shift masking deeper exhaustion.
- **Recession already underway** [16:11–16:38]: Q1 GDP negative; Q2 likely negative. Housing rolling over hard, autos vulnerable, retail inventory correction imminent. The economy is showing all the signs of a sharp downturn.
- **Melt-up scenario still intact** [27:12–28:20]: Lows likely in at S&P 3650 (June). Probable path: 4100–4200 then pullback to 3800–3900, staying above lows. Targets: S&P 6000, Nasdaq 20k by end of year or into 2023 — "huge historic moves."
- **Catalyst: Fed pivot + bond-market lead** [32:42–34:50]: Bond market leads; Fed follows. Hunter expects 10-year Treasury to 2–2.5% by year-end (from ~3%), signaling lower rates. Fed likely pauses after July or September hike. Market is so bearish (short-heavy) that a pivot triggers violent short-covering and re-entry.
- **Global bust follows melt-up** [36:31–40:05]: Europe in worse shape (Ukraine, energy crisis, negative rates, leverage). ECB hiking into weakness. Canada's housing as leveraged as US 2009. Australia real-estate vulnerable. Emerging markets drowning in dollar-denominated debt as USD strengthens. These are the triggers for the 80% bust.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Leading vs. lagging indicators** [11:09–13:11]: Fed fixates on headline CPI (lagging) instead of leading indicators (lumber futures down 70%, used-car prices falling, retail inventory surges). Greenspan was rare Fed chair who zigged early; Powell is academic, headline-focused, not market-savvy.
- **Rate of change matters more than level** [06:24–07:16]: Even "historically low" rates can shock the system if they move 200 bps in months. The *velocity* of tightening, not the absolute level, drives demand destruction.
- **Inventory recession is the real recession** [14:00–14:27]: Big inventory builds at Target, Walmart signal demand collapse. Recession = inventory correction. Fed should be watching this, not just CPI.
- **Psychology and positioning** [41:34–41:56]: Market psychology is very bearish; this usually precedes a turn. Don't just watch economy/Fed; watch what's already discounted and where sentiment sits.
- **Melt-up ≠ straight up** [28:20–29:04]: Melt-up is a multi-month process with back-and-forth, not a V-recovery. Market reprices from "tightening forever" to "Fed has our back," justifying 6–9 month forward look into better times (even if reality is bad).
