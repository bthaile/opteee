# What Happens to Delta in a Vertical Spread?

## Summary

Vertical spreads are designed to neutralize Greek exposure by offsetting long and short option positions. The narrower the spread width, the smaller the residual delta and the flatter the gamma, vega, and theta curves across the underlying price range.

## Key takeaways

- **Delta cancellation in tight spreads** [00:00–00:23]: A 135/125 short call spread (dollar-wide or five-wide) leaves minimal residual delta—long 29 deltas offset by short 26 deltas—because the two strikes are close enough that their Greeks nearly cancel.
- **Gamma flattening** [00:23]: The gamma exposure across the curve effectively flattens when strikes are tightly grouped, reducing convexity risk.
- **Vega and theta neutralization** [00:23]: Vega and theta also flatten across the curve in a tight vertical, minimizing sensitivity to volatility changes and time decay asymmetry.
- **Width trade-off**: Wider spreads (e.g., $5 wide vs. $1 wide) retain more Greek influence; tighter spreads approach delta-neutral mechanics.

## Notable quotes

> "If you're trading a vertical, really what you're trying to do is get rid of a lot of your Greeks."
