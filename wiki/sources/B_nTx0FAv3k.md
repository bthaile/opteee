# Option Volatility Surface Explained

## Summary

An implied volatility surface plots implied volatilities across all strikes and expirations for a given underlying, revealing structural patterns in how the market prices risk. The surface can be visualized as 2D lines (one per expiration) or as a 3D model, and anomalies—such as elevated put-side IV in large-cap stocks or inverted call-side IV in meme stocks—signal market expectations about directional moves and hedging demand.

## Key takeaways

- **What it is** [00:00–00:30]: An IV surface simply plots implied volatilities for each strike across different expirations; multiple visualization methods exist (2D lines per expiration, 3D surface model).
- **2D visualization** [00:30–01:02]: Thinker Swim displays different lines for each expiration; x-axis = strikes, y-axis = implied volatility; allows comparison within and across expirations.
- **3D visualization** [01:02–01:27]: Python-based 3D models consolidate the same information into a surface; same data, different presentation.
- **Put-side elevation in large-cap stocks** [01:27–01:49]: Out-of-the-money puts often trade at higher IV than calls in large-cap names because holders hedge downside risk; signals lower probability of massive downside, higher probability of smaller upside.
- **Inverted surface in meme stocks** [01:49–02:08]: Names like [[gme]] often show the opposite pattern—out-of-the-money calls trade at much higher IV than puts, reflecting market expectation of large upside potential moves.
- **Practical use** [02:08–02:30]: Read the surface for anomalies, volatility relationships, and asymmetries between put and call sides to infer market expectations.
