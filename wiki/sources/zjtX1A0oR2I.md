# Trading Options in Gamestop Pt1 - Ratio Covered Call Explained

## Summary

Eric introduces the ratio covered call strategy as a practical way to generate premium income on long GameStop holdings while preserving upside profit potential. Unlike a standard covered call (which caps gains), a ratio covered call maintains uncapped upside by selling fewer call contracts than the one-to-one ratio, exploiting GameStop's high implied and realized volatility. The strategy is designed for holders with multi-year positions seeking to accelerate wealth creation through options.

## Key takeaways

- **Covered call basics** [03:19–05:44]: A standard covered call sells one call contract per 100 shares held, collecting premium upfront but capping profit at the strike price. This creates unlimited downside risk with capped upside—the core weakness.

- **Why ratio covered calls fix the problem** [08:08–09:25]: By selling fewer calls than shares (e.g., 4 calls against 500 shares), you preserve uncapped upside. The long deltas from excess shares overwhelm the short call deltas beyond the strike, allowing profit to continue rising.

- **Delta mechanics and strike selection** [06:56–08:08]: At-the-money calls have lower delta (~0.28 for a 42-strike 45 DTE example); as price rises, delta increases toward 1.0. Selling fewer contracts means your long delta exposure remains positive past the short strike.

- **Profit comparison: ratio vs. standard** [10:46–12:11]: At a $40 target, selling 5 calls yields ~$10–15k profit at expiration; selling 3 calls yields ~$9.9k. But at $60, the 3-call ratio generates ~$14.5k vs. ~$11.2k for the 5-call standard—demonstrating the upside advantage of lower ratios.

- **GameStop's volatility profile** [02:06–03:19]: High implied volatility and realized volatility (frequent sharp moves followed by pullbacks) make GameStop ideal for premium-selling strategies. Volatility contraction and theta decay both benefit the short call seller.

- **Risk and management** [13:13–14:14]: Early assignment risk is low but possible. Short calls will show losses as the underlying rises, but long shares overwhelm those losses in aggregate. Strike selection (above cost basis, higher = better max profit) is critical.

- **Capital efficiency** [03:19]: The strategy requires no additional capital beyond the long stock position already held (e.g., 500 shares at $21.39 basis = ~$14k).
