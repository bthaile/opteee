# Why are Vertical Spreads so Common?

## Summary

This video explores the practical reasons traders gravitate toward vertical spreads (put spreads and call spreads) rather than naked short options. The core driver is capital efficiency: selling a naked put requires substantial upfront capital (cash-secured or portfolio-margin backed), while buying the put outright is expensive. Vertical spreads offer a middle ground by capping both risk and capital requirement, though they come with tradeoffs.

## Key takeaways

- **Capital requirement comparison** [00:00–00:22]: Selling a naked put on a ~$149 stock requires ~$13,000 in cash-secured margin (or less with portfolio margin). Buying the put outright costs ~$1,420. Vertical spreads reduce both extremes.
- **Why traders choose verticals** [00:43]: When naked short options are either too capital-intensive or too expensive to buy, traders default to vertical spreads as a compromise structure.
- **Tradeoff acknowledgment** [00:43]: The video hints at "issues" with vertical spreads that will be discussed further, signaling that capital efficiency comes with mechanical or P&L drawbacks.
