# Advice I'd Give My Younger Self as a Trader

## Summary

Eric reflects on lessons learned from starting to trade at age 17, offering practical guidance for younger traders on the pitfalls of early entry without proper discipline. The core message: early capital is not your future wealth; focus instead on developing consistent process, mastering risk management, and building a written trading plan before deploying real money.

## Key takeaways

- **Paper trading as a filter** [02:43] — Take paper trading seriously; it's the only way to work out major flaws in your approach without paying for them. If you can't discipline yourself in paper, you'll struggle with real money.

- **Reframe the account's purpose** [03:59] — Stop treating your initial trading capital as future wealth. The money you'll truly benefit from won't materialize until your mid-20s or later. Focus on developing a *consistent, scalable strategy* rather than chasing dollar returns on a small account.

- **Patience and realistic timelines** [05:19] — Don't rush. Meaningful wealth from trading typically emerges only after years of consistent success. Set realistic expectations (age 25+) to avoid disappointment and reckless behavior.

- **Risk management as the differentiator** [08:05] — The ability to modulate risk at different points in time separates long-term traders from those who blow up. This matters more than finding the "perfect" strategy.

- **External audit and written plans** [09:27] — Verbalize your process with others and write down your trading plan. This forces you to confront your logic objectively and flush out details you'd otherwise miss. Few traders do this; it's a competitive edge.

- **Avoid strategy sprawl** [13:25] — Don't live-trade every strategy you encounter. Test new approaches in paper first. Early experimentation with real money wastes capital; focus on mastering a few core strategies.

- **Diversify income sources** [14:49] — Trading alone won't build wealth quickly if you start with small capital. Pursue other business ventures (real estate, turning hobbies into income) in parallel.

- **Calibrate risk-taking** [16:25] — Balance risk aversion and recklessness. Take *calculated* good risk (favorable risk/reward), not risk for its own sake. Understand your personal risk profile early.

## Notable quotes
