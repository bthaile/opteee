# How to Buy Call Options Smarter: Step by Step Guide

## Summary

A practical framework for buying call options that prioritizes longevity and compounding returns over lottery-ticket plays. The video walks through three core decision points—days to expiration (DTE), delta selection, and gamma exposure—to help traders structure positions that survive theta decay and benefit from favorable price moves rather than evaporating in days.

## Key takeaways

- **Days to expiration (DTE) selection** [01:14–05:46]: Aim for at least 30 days out, ideally 60+ days. Beyond 60 DTE, theta decay accelerates sharply; at 29 DTE, daily theta loss can double (e.g., 3¢ → 7¢). Longer-dated options cost more upfront but lose a smaller *percentage* of premium daily, giving your thesis time to work.

- **Delta as a profitability lever** [09:01–11:48]: Higher-delta options (60+) move more per dollar of underlying movement but cost more. Lower-delta options are cheaper but require larger moves to profit. Buying six 49-delta calls vs. one 64-delta call at the same total cost yields nearly identical P&L on a +2σ move—but that move occurs only ~2.5% of the time. Structure a base position with 60+ delta to capture dollar-for-dollar moves reliably.

- **Gamma: the compounding accelerant** [06:10–08:42]: Gamma is highest for at-the-money options and those closest to expiration. Far OTM options have near-zero gamma and won't accelerate in value even if the stock moves. Use gamma strategically: a base position with moderate delta and time, plus "kicker" options (shorter-dated, further OTM) to amplify gains if the stock rallies sharply.

- **Theta decay as a percentage** [02:08–05:24]: Don't just look at absolute theta (e.g., −$0.07/day); compare it to the option's price. A 70¢ option losing 14¢/day is hemorrhaging 20% of its value daily. Longer-dated, slightly higher-delta positions bleed theta more slowly as a percentage, preserving capital for the trade to develop.

- **Avoid lottery-ticket structures** [05:46–06:10]: Far OTM, 1-DTE options (e.g., 125 strike with 13,000 open interest) have delta so low that a $1 move yields less than a penny of profit. Gamma doesn't activate until you're already profitable, making these positions mathematically hostile to small moves.

- **Position structure example** [12:32–13:59]: Build a base with 60+ delta, 60+ DTE calls (e.g., 120-day, slightly ITM). Layer in shorter-dated, further OTM "kickers" to capture explosive upside if conviction is high. The base position survives theta; the kickers compound if the thesis plays out.

## Notable quotes

> "The further out in time you go, the more expensive extrinsic value gets for options, also the slower gamma will move." [01:35]

> "If you go super close in time and you're out of the money, you are fighting so much theta decay as a percentage of your position that it's going to erode away to nothing." [15:02]
