# Why Most Traders SUCK at Risk Management

## Summary

Risk management is not about stop-losses or emotional reactions—it's a systematic framework that preserves capital and enables compounding. The speaker demonstrates how a portfolio with lower average returns but controlled drawdowns vastly outperforms a higher-return portfolio with a catastrophic loss, and walks through a two-band position-management system paired with portfolio-level delta, gamma, theta, and vega monitoring to align risk-taking with annual return targets.

## Key takeaways

- **Compounding impact of drawdowns** [01:42–02:06]: A portfolio with 8.6% average annual return but a −25% drawdown ends with lower compound annual growth rate (CAGR) than a 7.4% average return portfolio with no major losses. Drawdown severity crushes long-term wealth far more than average return suggests.

- **Disposition effect and loss aversion** [04:50–05:36]: Traders sell winners too fast and hold losers too long due to ego protection and biological loss aversion (losses feel ~2× worse than equivalent gains). These psychological biases run rampant across retail, day traders, and even dividend investors.

- **Goal-driven risk allocation** [06:22–06:57]: Set an annual return target (e.g., 15% per year) and break it into monthly targets to calibrate how much risk you should take. This controls psychological dysfunction and maintains CAGR well above the target.

- **Expected return and tail risk** [07:37–08:19]: Every trade involves a trade-off between win rate, loss rate, average win size, and average loss size. Maximize the relationship between these variables by analyzing historical data to find where big moves occur and set stops only at true tail events.

- **Stop-loss paradox** [09:01–09:25]: Stops increase loss frequency and may decrease average loss size, but if you modulate loss rate and loss size too much, you overwhelm the winning side and create a negatively expected strategy. Use data to identify true outliers, not arbitrary levels.

- **Two-band position management** [11:35–12:58]: First band is a checkpoint—"Is this still working?"—where you decide to stay or exit. Second band is a hard stop that protects severity and prevents the disposition effect from letting losers run or winners get cut short prematurely. This decouples profit-taking from fear.

- **Portfolio-level Greeks** [13:49–14:24]: Track beta-weighted delta (exposure in equivalent SPX shares), gamma, theta, and vega across all positions to understand correlation, directional risk, and how fast the portfolio will move. This gives fidelity over risk profile.

- **Portfolio P&L bands** [15:19–15:59]: Set profit and loss bands to guide allocation, but hitting a profit target does not mean you stop—it means you become more selective. Loss bands are a safety net if individual position management fails. Drawdowns compound negatively, so protect against them.

- **Event awareness** [15:02–15:19]: Monitor FOMC statements and other catalysts at both portfolio and individual position levels. Adjust risk exposure ahead of known events to prevent surprise drawdowns.

- **Expectancy-driven position sizing** [16:34–16:57]: Allocate initial capital based on strategy expectancy, then scale via predefined profit/loss targets. This prevents the common mistake of adding to losing trades and keeps allocation aligned with edge.
