# Edge, Profit Mechanisms, and Position Sizing — Q&A

## Summary

A comprehensive live Q&A addressing foundational trading concepts: how to identify and measure edge through profit mechanisms, the mechanics of ratio writing and position sizing, the critical distinction between return-on-capital and return-on-invested-capital, and why consistency and iteration—not raw returns—drive long-term wealth. The session emphasizes that all meaningful trading work happens during market closure through research and planning, not during live trading.

## Key takeaways

### Foundational concepts
- **Edge is everything** [00:00]. Without a profit mechanism to monetize, there is nothing to trade. All discipline, forecasting, and strategy questions are secondary to identifying edge.
- **Define your goal first** [09:51]. Edge can mean alpha (risk-adjusted outperformance) or simply achieving your personal objective (e.g., income with lower drawdowns). Define what success looks like before building a strategy.
- **Profit mechanisms are the source of edge** [08:23]. A profit mechanism is a market effect you can monetize. If you cannot identify one, you have no foundation to build on.

### Ratio writing and position management
- **Ratio writing preserves upside while generating income** [13:33]. Selling fewer calls than shares held (e.g., 2 calls against 500 shares) caps profit on only part of the position, leaving uncovered shares to participate in rallies.
- **Dynamic lot sizing unlocks flexibility** [23:48]. By adjusting the number of short calls (e.g., moving from 2 to 3 lots), you can increase basis adjustments and total profit potential even when rolling deep ITM calls at a debit.
- **Rolling deep ITM calls becomes difficult** [21:26]. When short calls are far in the money, closing and rolling to higher strikes often requires paying a net debit, making ratio sizing essential to maintain profitability.

### Capital efficiency and position sizing
- **Return-on-invested-capital (ROIC) vs. return-on-capital (ROC)** [35:22]. ROIC measures efficiency of capital deployed in a single trade; ROC measures total portfolio return. Both matter, but for different purposes.
- **Avoid vanity metrics** [36:44]. A 6,900% return on a $5 nickel option that becomes $3.50 is meaningless if you only made $345. Focus on raw dollars and portfolio-level ROC.
- **Reverse-engineer position size from goals** [52:46]. Start with annual return target (e.g., 15%), break into monthly targets, calculate expected return per trade using probabilities, then size accordingly. Never pick utilization percentages arbitrarily.
- **Account utilization depends on strategy performance** [56:43]. A 35% win-rate strategy with $5,000 winners and $300 average losses is highly profitable; size it accordingly. A high-loss-rate strategy requires tighter risk control per trade.

### Trade logging and research
- **Track only what matters to your profit mechanism** [48:13]. Read peer-reviewed research on your market effect first (e.g., SSRN for variance risk premium), identify key variables, then build your trade log around those inputs.
- **The outlier strategy process guides what to measure** [51:31]. Define your profit mechanism → identify inputs that matter → build strategy → log only relevant variables. This prevents over-logging and captures signal.

### Market regimes and adaptation
- **Adapt to market conditions; don't lock into one approach** [59:47]. If you only sell premium and markets rally, you underperform. Build a varied toolkit to trade different regimes (breakouts, mean reversion, earnings vol, etc.).
- **Tech has outperformed since mid-2010s** [01:14:28]. Large-cap growth (especially tech) has dominated small-cap since ~2015. Sector rotation from IWM to leveraged QQQ/SPY reflects this regime shift.
- **Earnings season is time-bound** [01:24:51]. Earnings plays are viable for non-correlated returns, but earnings wind down seasonally. Adjust trading activity accordingly.

### Work allocation and discipline
- **All work is done when markets are closed** [01:27:32]. Research, planning, and decision-making happen Friday evening or before market open. Live trading is just button-clicking; it takes seconds because the homework is done.
- **Consistency and iteration yield higher returns** [01:03:51]. Emphasizing repetition and refinement (like practicing an instrument or martial art) is what ultimately produces the returns people chase. It is not a trade-off; it is the path.
- **Hit your annual target, then become selective** [29:49]. If you exceed your yearly goal by May, do not stop or raise the target. Keep trading but become more selective; the target is a planning minimum, not a ceiling.

### Risk management
- **Size trades before entry, then follow the rule** [01:08:04]. Define max portfolio drawdown per trade (e.g., 8%), size accordingly, and execute. Effective risk management is planning, not improvisation.
- **Wing width is a valid risk definition** [01:11:04]. Using the width of a vertical spread (iron condor, butterfly, etc.) to define max loss is sound, but still size the position so the loss is acceptable as a percentage of portfolio.

### Tools and data
- **YouTube channel search bar finds specific topics** [01:23:45]. Use the channel search to find earnings, zero-DTE, or other keyword-specific content rather than scrolling 600+ videos.
- **Unusual Whales, O'Rats, and BarChart show historical earnings moves** [01:28:45]. These tools display pre- and post-earnings price movement; you can also download market data and calculate moves yourself via ChatGPT or Python.
