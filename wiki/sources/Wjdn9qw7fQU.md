# Trading Near All Time Highs - Stock Market Update

## Summary

When markets trade near or at all-time highs, retail traders often become nervous and constrained in their approach. This update demonstrates that strength at highs creates abundant trading opportunities across sectors and strategies—the key is expanding your universe beyond a handful of familiar names and developing conviction in a plan that works across market regimes. The host walks through sector analysis (healthcare weakness vs. financial strength), scaling protocols, and the psychology of trading expensive stocks with momentum.

## Key takeaways

- **Market spends significant time at highs:** SPY has spent ~60% of its time within 10% of all-time highs and ~44% within 5% since 1979 [29:38]. Sitting out when near highs means missing 44% of trading opportunities.

- **Healthcare sector weakness creates fades:** Healthcare has been persistently weak all year; the host's preferred trade is finding mid-sector names that are overextended relative to sector momentum, then fading via synthetic shorts or long puts [06:30–07:44].

- **ETF flows constrain individual stock strength:** Even strong names like Cardinal Health and Gilead are held back by sector ETF outflows; when the script flips and healthcare inflows resume, these leaders can "absolutely fly" [13:35–13:55].

- **Expand your trading universe:** Standard retail traders see ~20–50 names; professional retail traders see effects across 4,000+ names. The probability of finding opportunities in 4,000 names is far higher [21:20–22:29].

- **Bargains in trading ≠ bargains in investing:** Buying Palanteer at all-time highs (200× forward earnings) is not a bargain by valuation, but momentum and growth (93% YoY commercial revenue growth) justify buying higher [31:05–32:36].

- **Scaling-in protocol reduces basis without blind averaging:** Split capital allocation into tranches; buy more units at lower prices. Example: 100 shares at $125 vs. 100+ shares at $90 with the same capital [08:54–09:22].

- **Implied volatility context matters:** Low volatility can be more profitable to sell than high volatility if realized volatility is even lower; broad platitudes ("sell when IV is high") miss nuance [39:41–40:19].

- **Contrarian identity limits opportunity:** Labeling yourself "contrarian," "trend-follower," or "iron condor trader" mentally filters out suitable trades; be whatever the market requires [43:13–45:17].

- **Plan and conviction prevent panic:** The host managed a covered strangle through a 63% drawdown in TQQQ without fear because he had a logical plan and long-term conviction in tech growth [51:45–52:44].

- **Earnings, momentum, breakouts, and drift remain viable near highs:** Even when the index is flat or near highs, there are always profit mechanisms—earnings vol plays, sector rotation, post-earnings drift [35:28–36:27].

## Notable quotes

> "The market spends 60% of its time within 10% of all-time highs. If I start getting gunshy when we're 5% off all-time highs, I'm going to be sitting out 44% of the time. That's not going to be a very successful trading approach." [29:38]

> "My job as a trader is not to get a bargain. My job is to make money. I know things like a momentum factor—as things are hitting highs, they have a propensity to go higher." [32:36]

> "You should never just be anything. You shouldn't be an option trader, an option buyer, an option seller, an iron condor trader, a spy trader. You should be whatever you need to be in order to capitalize on what the market is giving us." [44:45]
