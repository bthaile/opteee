# Agustin Lebron: Ex-Jane Street Trader on Why You Should Trade | the Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Agustin Lebron, a six-year veteran of Jane Street and current founder of a Prague-based algorithmic trading startup, argues that most retail traders should not trade—but for those who do, success hinges on edge identification, disciplined position sizing, and systematic decision-making rather than emotional control or complex multi-leg strategies. The conversation spans market-making mechanics, the illusion of retail trading courses, the role of reinforcement learning in institutional trading, and the importance of iterative skill development over time.

## Key takeaways

- **The NBA analogy for trading [01:10–02:16]:** Trading is like competing in professional sports without teams, drafts, or salary caps. Institutional players have resources (capital, technology, prime brokers, computational speed) that retail traders cannot match; table selection and niche identification are critical.

- **Market making vs. retail trading [05:21–06:33]:** Market makers profit from spreads and liquidity provision; they don't need an opinion on direction. Retail traders must identify mispricings the market doesn't understand and execute cost-effectively. These are fundamentally different games.

- **The money-tree analogy [25:59–27:03]:** If you have a profitable strategy, you would never sell it—every buyer becomes a competitor. Courses and "systems" sold online are red flags; profitable traders keep their edge private.

- **Position sizing is the #1 mistake [15:00–17:10]:** Retail traders consistently over-leverage. Large institutions bet tiny fractions of capital (e.g., 1% on a $100M position); retail traders with $100k accounts often risk $10k–$50k per trade. Fractional Kelly sizing and absolute position limits are essential.

- **Edge requires quantification and iteration [09:35–11:30]:** You must understand whether your strategy actually makes money. Small peer groups (3–5 trusted traders) accelerate learning. Emotional control cannot turn a losing strategy profitable; edge is prerequisite.

- **Trading is about positions, not trades [39:57–41:00]:** The question is not "when do I exit?" but "what is the correct position for me to hold now?" Entry and exit prices are irrelevant; only current profitability and risk matter. This mindset prevents anchoring to sunk costs.

- **Sample size and trade frequency trade-off [08:34–09:35]:** High-frequency strategies need large sample sizes but exploit small edges; infrequent trades need fewer samples if edges are larger. Both can work; math determines feasibility.

- **Institutional training: Socratic method and post-mortems [13:55–22:34]:** Jane Street and similar firms teach via questioning and structured after-action reviews. Pre-mortems (imagining failure modes) and peer feedback accelerate decision-making skill.

- **Multi-leg option strategies are often suboptimal [46:09–47:19]:** If a complex strategy (iron condor, strangle, etc.) is profitable, at least one leg is profitable and others are losing money. Simplify: trade the profitable leg and manage risk directly.

- **Systematic vs. discretionary trading [45:14–46:09]:** Systematic approaches define strategy, sizing, and risk limits upfront, then run mechanically. This removes daily decision fatigue and emotional bias but requires high confidence in the model.

- **Reinforcement learning and emergent risks [52:18–55:13]:** End-to-end agent training can discover unintended behaviors (e.g., penny wars). Regulatory and competitive safeguards must be built in; multiple agents with similar strategies can create harmful emergent behavior.

- **Consolidation in institutional trading [31:55–33:04]:** Technology costs are rising; smaller options trading firms are being acquired. Retail trading has exploded post-COVID; whether it persists or retracts is uncertain. Gamification continues to expand.

- **Mentorship and first principles [12:51–13:55]:** Talking to junior traders forces you to reconstruct knowledge from first principles, which is valuable for both parties. Small, trusted peer groups are more valuable than large Discord communities.

- **Life is long; serial expertise [01:03:03–01:04:14]:** Skills compound across domains. Agustin changes focus roughly every six years; each "serial PhD" informs the next. Diverse experience is an asset, not a liability.

- **Why he wrote the book [36:46–38:51]:** Wanted to focus on principles and decision-making, not specific strategies. The book emerged from training material; writing forced clarity and permanence that video cannot match.

> "A bet is a tax on bullshit. Anytime you are forced to put your money where your mouth is, you start to think, 'Oh, actually, hang on a sec. Maybe I should figure out whether I believe the words I'm saying.'"

> "Life is long. You have time to dive into something and get really good at it, and then you can go do something else. The thing you were doing 12 years ago somehow informs the thing you're doing now."
