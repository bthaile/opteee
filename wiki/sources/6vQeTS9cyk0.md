# 0DTE Options & Sports Betting w/ Euan Sinclair | The Outlier Podcast Ep62

## Summary
Euan Sinclair walks through the volatility design of the Hull Tactical ETF (HTUS) — an active SPY replacement built from ~20 equity-anomaly / market-timing signals overlaid with a skew-harvesting risk reversal and a small active short-volatility sleeve — then delivers a skeptical, cost-focused take on the 0DTE options boom. His core argument: 0DTE options carry real volatility-terms edge but the dollar transaction costs are enormous relative to that edge, so they are astonishingly hard to trade profitably and offer no advantage over weeklies/monthlies. He closes with lessons carried over from professional sports betting: bet relative value not outcomes, size by Kelly, and exploit parlays when you have positive edge.

## Key takeaways
- HTUS is designed as an active replacement for SPY: a portfolio of market-timing / equity-anomaly strategies (January effect, end-of-month effect, election seasonality, momentum, mean reversion) combined with the best portfolio-construction techniques to beat the index. [00:42] [01:27]
- Volatility overlays are two pieces: a risk-reversal strategy that replaces ~10–15% of the equity delta, plus a small, active short-dated volatility-selling sleeve to collect the variance premium. Both are rules-based, not blindly on all the time. [04:04] [04:30]
- Risk-reversal structure is unbalanced: short ~15-delta puts, long ~25-delta calls, leaving the position net long Vega. A delta-hedged risk reversal has significant alpha — a skewness premium (implied skewness, distinct from the underlying's skew and from the variance premium). Not delta-hedging it lets you replace a long-stock position and pick up that premium. [09:18] [11:17]
- Beta averages ~1 by calibration of the timing model, but can flex (theoretically 50% short to 200% long); the goal is risk-adjusted return, not a fixed beta target. [16:06]
- Total return vs price appreciation: active ETFs must pay out short-term trading profits as dividends, so price-only charts (e.g. Yahoo Finance) understate performance. HTUS beat SPY on 1/2/3-year return and Sharpe. [05:51] [06:13]
- 0DTE edge is real in volatility terms, but costs are in dollars and are large relative to the tiny premium of a same-day option — so 0DTE is astonishingly hard to trade. Recent 0DTE "wins" mostly came from being long deltas in a rising market, not skill. [16:58] [17:26]
- Skeptical of 0DTE income ETFs (e.g. QQQY, ~$310M AUM, launched Sept 2023): high transaction costs (much of it hidden in fills), highish fees, and unlikely to beat buy-and-hold QQQ. Be careful: advertised "income" is option premium sold, which can mask a large loss and is not a dividend. [19:18] [20:31]
- Market structure: market makers welcome 0DTE flow because retail is mostly on the buy side, giving MMs a chance to sell/cover; for every buyer there's a seller, and 0DTE risk resets nightly so it's easy to risk-manage. The real fear is a flash crash — a fast feedback loop (2010-style, ~6–8% in minutes for no clear reason). [22:24] [23:33] [23:51]
- Retail generally shouldn't trade 0DTE: any strategy that works in 0DTE also works in weeklies/monthlies at far lower cost. Don't put essentially the same trade on five times a week. [27:37] [29:14]
- Reducing transaction costs is the single best way to improve results and matters most for retail (highest costs). Analogy: costs are like termites; the biggest improvement most traders can make is to trade less (like waiting in poker). [30:08] [31:19]
- If you're going to gamble in short-dated options, treat it as genuine tuition: trade a one-lot and document everything. Losing money by doing dumb things is not learning. [35:21] [35:47]
- Don't let capital constraints force the trade: if your account is too small to do something safely, you're too small to do it — grow the account rather than half-ass it with 0DTE. [37:22] [37:48]
- Style drift is a real problem: an investor needs a clean, consistent product to use as a portfolio building block. He was annoyed when SVIX added its own hedging — he'd rather buy the clean exposure and decide his own hedges. [12:37] [13:20]
- Sports-betting lessons that transfer: don't try to predict game outcomes, hunt relative-value / prop bets; bet the size Kelly dictates; parlays compound edge (good when your edge is positive); and "take any opportunity that doesn't cost you money to look like an idiot" (from Ed Miller) so bookies don't limit you — an idea that maps to cheap diversifiers/hedges in trading. [43:37] [44:48] [45:37]

## Regime / context
- Recorded 2024-03-20, during a strong tech-led bull market. Sinclair's point that recent 0DTE gains came from "being long deltas" is explicitly regime-dependent — it reflects a rising market, not a repeatable edge.
- HTUS has never been through a 2020-style crash; Sinclair offers no guarantees on crash performance, expects mean-reversion elements and long-Vega positioning to help but stresses "every crash is different — people always plan for the last war."
- Broker/margin mechanics were changing at the time: some brokers had begun charging margin for intraday 0DTE positions that previously required none — a moving cost input to the 0DTE calculus.
- QQQY figures (~$310M AUM, Sept 2023 launch) are as of the recording date and describe a very young fund.
- The dividend/total-return caveat is specific to active ETFs and to a fund reorganization / admin change that made HTUS's prior-year payout unusually small.
