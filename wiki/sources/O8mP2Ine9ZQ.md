# Chat GPT, Leveraged ETFs, and Volatility Regimes with Euan Sinclair | The Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Euan Sinclair discusses the balance between theoretical rigor and practical application in options education, the mechanics of volatility surface regimes and their limited tradability, the mathematical pitfalls of leveraged ETFs as buy-and-hold vehicles, and the appropriate role of AI and ChatGPT in retail trading workflows. The conversation emphasizes that edge comes from clarity about what phenomenon you're monetizing, not from complexity or automation.

## Key takeaways

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Theory-practice balance in options education** [01:25–02:08]: Options are irreducibly mathematically complex, but the edge is not in knowing the math—it's in understanding the phenomena. Avoid both the "fire thousands of orders" mentality and the pure-math-professor approach; find the middle ground.

- **Perfection is the enemy of progress** [10:19–11:31]: Academics and highly analytical traders often get stuck waiting for the perfect trade setup. Professional traders execute when conditions are "pretty good," not perfect. This applies especially to retail traders who need to learn by doing, not by optimizing endlessly before entry.

- **Define your edge first** [13:46–14:55]: Before choosing an instrument, ask: what phenomenon am I trying to monetize? If bonds go up, buy bonds—not call spreads or LEAPs. The simplest, cheapest instrument is almost always the right choice. Options introduce volatility, theta, and vega that may not be part of your hypothesis.

- **Volatility surface regimes are idealized, not actionable** [16:03–17:52]: Sticky delta, sticky strike, sticky local vol, and jumpy vol are educational concepts, not trading regimes. The world is never exactly one; it's always doing all of them simultaneously. Most traders should not hang their hat on parameterizing the surface.

- **Total delta vs. Black-Scholes delta** [19:31–22:25]: Black-Scholes delta is a partial derivative (assuming nothing else changes). Total delta includes the vega term—how volatility changes as the underlying moves. This matters for market makers staying on the market, but retail traders gain little from the effort; the difference between 25 and 28 delta is noise after accounting for model uncertainty.

- **Leveraged ETFs decay is complicated, not simple** [25:14–26:53]: Leveraged ETFs don't always decay; the mean return depends on the underlying process (which is unknown). The real issue is Kelly Criterion: most leveraged ETFs are over-leveraged relative to optimal growth size. A 3× product may be at the point of diminished returns. Check borrow costs—a 1.5× product with 3% borrow may outperform a 2× product with 22% borrow.

- **AI and ChatGPT are tools, not alpha generators** [30:05–31:52]: Use ChatGPT as an intern—great for writing code, explaining concepts, or accelerating non-core tasks. It will not beat the market or predict volatility better than you. Every trade is an implicit forecast; the question is whether you can forecast better than the market. Most people cannot; focus on risk premiums instead.

- **Slow down, don't automate** [34:18–36:47]: Retail traders should slow down, not speed up. Review every step: forecast, strike selection, expiration, re-hedging frequency. The value of keeping records is the *process of making them*, not statistical analysis later. Active learning beats automation. Even a legal pad works.

- **ChatGPT for learning and coding, not trading decisions** [37:36–40:00]: ChatGPT is useful for synthesizing concepts (Pareto principle, etc.) and writing code (Monte Carlo simulations, volatility measurement). It is not useful for deciding how to trade. Think of it as an intern you'd ask to write a simulation, not one you'd ask how to trade an option.

- **AI will likely automate market-making and vanilla facilitation** [41:10–46:14]: AI will be better at matching flows and managing spreads than humans. Delta-one facilitation, vanilla market-making, and similar rule-based tasks will be automated. Ill-defined problems (customer advisory, creative structuring) will remain human. Variance risk premium and put-call skew will persist as long as humans want protection, though spreads may tighten.

- **Debt ceiling volatility is priced correctly** [46:42–50:16]: The market knew the likely outcome (political theater, eventual resolution). Volatility did not spike because the base case was well-understood. Even a default would not be as catastrophic as the 1950s; US credit is no longer risk-free. The real issue is the structural absurdity: Congress authorizes spending, then votes not to pay for it.

## Notable quotes

- "There's no edge in knowing about options, but if you don't know about options and you're trading them, you're going to blow up." [02:49]

- "You're going to waste time looking for an optimal solution to something that doesn't exist. The only thing we know is we've got this broad effect and the best you can do is throw shit at this broad effect and hope over time it wins." [08:01]

- "The action of doing a trade is an implicit forecast. But trying to out-forecast the market is a very difficult problem. You don't have to get alpha—you've got to get money. Beta counts as money too." [32:17–32:41]
