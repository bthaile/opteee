# Variance Risk Premium (VRP) & Market Inefficiencies Explained

## Summary

This video distinguishes between two distinct trading opportunities: the **variance risk premium** (a persistent structural feature where portfolio protection via puts is systematically overpriced) and **market inefficiencies** (temporary mispricings with finite lifespans). Eric demonstrates how to identify and measure VRP by comparing implied volatility to realized volatility, and explains why zero-DTE SPX credit spreads exploit VRP rather than inefficiencies—a critical distinction for position sizing and risk management.

## Key takeaways

- **VRP vs. inefficiency distinction** [01:03–03:27]: The variance risk premium is a persistent, structural phenomenon where puts trade at a premium relative to calls of the same delta due to demand for portfolio insurance. Inefficiencies are temporary mispricings that eventually get arbitraged away.

- **Put skew as VRP indicator** [01:32–02:20]: Compare same-delta puts and calls; a 30-delta put on AAPL traded ~60 cents higher than a 30-delta call, reflecting the structural overpricing of downside protection.

- **Euan Sinclair's analogy** [03:27–05:27]: Finding a $20 bill on a safe sidewalk = inefficiency (one-time, finite). Finding a $20 bill on a New Jersey highway = VRP (many see it, few take the risk; the premium persists because risk-aversion is structural).

- **Measuring VRP: IV vs. realized vol** [06:12–07:00]: Track the gap between implied volatility and 20-day historical volatility. On 11 October, IV was 33.25% vs. realized 24%, a ~10-point premium—the opportunity to sell premium.

- **VRP presence is not guaranteed** [07:44–08:09]: On 27 October, IV (28.1%) nearly matched realized vol (28%), eliminating the premium. On 28 October, realized vol (28.5%) exceeded IV (26.7%), creating a loss scenario. Zero-DTE strategies only work when VRP is present and large enough to justify the risk.

- **Capital intensity of zero-DTE adjustments** [07:24–07:44]: Fighting a losing zero-DTE trade on 28 October required ~$850k in capital committed at peak to adjust and recover—not accessible to all traders.

- **VRP is product-dependent** [08:38–09:00]: VRP is common in indices (SPX, broad markets) but rarer in individual stocks; finding it elsewhere requires significant research.
