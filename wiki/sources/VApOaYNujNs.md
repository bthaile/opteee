# Volatility Clustering in Short Option Strategies

## Summary

This video challenges the common misconception that selling options when implied volatility is high is a reliable edge. While volatility is mean-reverting over long periods, **volatility clustering** — the tendency for volatility to persist in regimes before reverting — can cause short-volatility strategies to suffer significant drawdowns within typical trade durations (e.g., 30-day positions). The key insight is that mean reversion timing is strategy-dependent and cannot be assumed to occur within your holding period.

## Key takeaways

- **Volatility clustering is real and dangerous for short-option traders** [00:27]: High volatility can persist for extended periods before reverting, not immediately. A 30-day short-premium trade may not capture the mean reversion it expects.
- **"Sell when IV is high" is commonly misused** [00:00]: This rule assumes mean reversion will occur within your trade's time frame, which is not guaranteed.
- **Mean reversion is a long-term property, not a short-term guarantee** [00:27]: Volatility clustering means volatility can remain elevated or continue trending before reverting, creating regime persistence that conflicts with short-duration option strategies.

## Notable quotes

> "Volatility does something we refer to as clustering. That means it can go through durations where it looks a lot like what it recently looks like before it goes through some sort of reversion process."
