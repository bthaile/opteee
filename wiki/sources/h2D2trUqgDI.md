# Change Your Trading Psychology with Richard Friesen - The Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Richard Friesen, a veteran options trader and clinical psychologist, discusses the psychological foundations of successful trading. Drawing from decades on the Pacific Stock Exchange and Chicago Mercantile Exchange, Friesen argues that trader success is determined not by intelligence or technical knowledge, but by mindset and emotional regulation. He introduces frameworks like the Confidence Circle, SET scoring (Sensations, Emotions, Thoughts), and the Cocky Clanger exercise to help traders identify and navigate psychological barriers to consistent profitability.

## Key takeaways

### Institutional vs. retail trader psychology
- **Professional traders** already know markets and strategies; coaching focuses on thin layers of belief and identity blocking expansion [10:31]
- **Retail traders** begin with naive confidence, then cycle through profitable-trade euphoria, losing-streak despair, and strategy-hunting before reaching mastery [11:18–13:13]
- Same human brain drives both; expression differs by sophistication level [09:14]

### The Confidence Circle progression
- **Stage 1:** Naive confidence ("I can learn this") [11:18]
- **Stage 2:** Profitable-trade euphoria (dreams of mansions, trophy spouses) [12:17]
- **Stage 3:** Losing-streak collapse (three losses in a row = dream collapse) [12:17]
- **Stage 4:** Strategy confidence (backtest, experiment, find a system that works) [12:46]
- **Stage 5:** Strategy failure (all strategies eventually fail; market tests your belief) [13:13]
- **Stage 6:** Market-regime mastery (know which strategy fits which market type) [14:08]
- **Stage 7:** Ego-free presence (no need to prove anything; fully here with the market) [14:39]

### SET scoring (Sensations, Emotions, Thoughts)
- Score each on a −5 to +5 scale before trading [20:43]
- **Sensations:** Physical state (−5 = hungover/injured; +5 = hyper; 0 = relaxed and present)
- **Emotions:** Mood state (−5 = depressed; +5 = euphoric; 0 = neutral)
- **Thoughts:** Quality of thinking (−5 = self-critical; +5 = euphoric; 0 = neutral)
- Creates centering and awareness before market entry [20:43]

### Daily risk awareness ritual
- Ask traders each morning: "Where's the bullet coming from today?" [21:44]
- Imagine threats: interest rate changes, large market moves, IV crush/explosion, borrow availability [22:05]
- Prevents complacency; assumes you're "just a bump on the twig" extracting value carefully [22:28]

### Dunning-Kruger effect and the 3–6 year trap
- Traders at 3–6 years of success are at highest risk; they think they know what they don't [18:06]
- New trader who made money in a rare retail-vs-pro volatility mismatch took profits and stopped; Friesen reset his account to prevent overconfidence [18:50]
- Winning streaks trigger survival mechanisms; traders believe their dreams are coming true and take excessive risk [22:59]

### Market metrics and lucrative vs. lousy trades
- Track every trade: strategy used, SET scores, outcome [26:36]
- **Lucrative trade:** follows parameters, enters/exits per plan, gets stopped out if needed—profitable or not [26:36]
- **Lousy trade:** violates plan, chases, ignores stops [26:36]
- Reframe losses: "You made $7.50 on a lucrative trade that lost money" (if average win is $750) [27:04]
- Enough lucrative trades + edge = money [26:36]

### Relationship with money and wealth resistance
- ~60–70% of traders have unexamined belief systems about money, wealth, and meaning [29:55]
- Common block: trading feels "icky" or evil; wealth contradicts parental values (hard work, sacrifice) [30:20]
- Exercise: visualize wealth, list who objects, have imaginary conversation with critical parent [31:07]
- Until you can visualize and accept wealth, survival mechanisms block you from getting there [31:34]

### Language and disassociation from capital
- Replace "try" with "step into" (try = internal struggle; step into = agency) [33:54]
- Treat money as a tool/machine, not a life-or-death resource [34:19]
- Pressure to "do it right" triggers survival mechanisms; healthy disassociation reduces that pressure [34:45]
- Eric's example: once he had enough to care for his mother and knew passive index returns would sustain him, he could disassociate and say "no big deal" on losses [32:01–32:45]

### The Cocky Clanger exercise
- Recall a trade where you "just knew" you had it made and canceled stops [44:19]
- Map physiology, emotions, thoughts in that state [44:39]
- Create a mental alarm (Pavlovian bell) [45:02]
- Ring the bell, exaggerate the cocky state, then intentionally move out of it [45:02]
- Repeat until the bell becomes an automatic signal: "I'm in ego-driven mode; do I want to be here?" [45:21]

### Awareness, acceptance, agency (the golden keys)
- **Awareness:** SET scores, alarms, physical cues (jaw tension, leaning forward) [56:38]
- **Acceptance:** "Oh, that's fascinating I have judgmental thoughts" (not "Damn it, stop judging") [57:06]
- **Agency:** Now what do I want? Decide from your higher self, not your primitive brain [57:38]

### Vision and process balance
- Stick your head up periodically to check progress against your 10–20 year vision [48:23]
- Don't set rigid expectations; focus on process improvement [48:23]
- Enjoy the gains when you look back, then return to process [51:08]
- Survival mechanisms won't let you reach a place you haven't conceived of and felt safe in [48:48]

### Combat parallels
- Trading is one of the most challenging things you can do except for combat [36:07]
- Muscle memory in trading (automated responses) mirrors weapon training [36:39]
- Worst-case scenario framing: "I lose money, not a leg" [37:19]
- Civilians without combat reference may feel losing money = losing their life [37:46]
