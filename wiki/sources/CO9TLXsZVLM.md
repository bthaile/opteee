# How to Trade Options When Implied Volatility is Low

## Summary

When broad-market IV (VIX) is low, traders often assume all volatility is suppressed—a dangerous misconception. This video demonstrates four concrete approaches to find and trade volatility even in low-IV regimes: trading implied volatility relative to realized volatility, hunting for catalysts in individual names, exploiting earnings moves, and identifying mispricings across the volatility surface and term structure.

## Key takeaways

- **VIX ≠ all volatility** [00:00–01:17]: VIX measures only S&P 500 implied volatility. Even when VIX is at 14, gold may trade at 56% IV percentile, energy at 98%, and individual sectors (materials, healthcare, staples) show wide dispersion. Always scan across the market for pockets of elevated vol.

- **You trade implied vs. realized, not just IV direction** [01:17–02:40]: Selling a straddle when IV is 30% and watching IV rise to 35% hurts unrealized P&L, but if realized volatility stays range-bound, theta decay still works in your favor. Conversely, selling vol at 35% and watching it drop to 30% can still lose money if realized vol breaches your short strikes. The true edge is the *spread* between implied and realized vol, not the direction of IV alone.

- **Trade implied vol relative to realized vol** [03:55–05:05]: When IV percentile is at 0% (as in SPX), look at the relationship between the yellow line (implied) and realized vol. If implied remains elevated relative to realized, short premium still carries risk premium. Avoid trading the extremes (top/bottom 20% of IV percentile); the bottom 20% typically offers better relative value than the top 20%.

- **Hunt for catalysts in individual names** [05:05–06:20]: When broad vol is low, scan for stocks with unusual moves. Example: SOC gapped up 44% in one day, then another 10%, with IV percentile at 98%. Catalyst: pipeline approval and sector-specific energy tailwinds. High-vol opportunities exist; you must search for them rather than assume the entire market is flat.

- **Earnings season is a reliable vol source** [06:20–07:29]: Even outside peak earnings cycles, upcoming earnings provide natural volatility expansion. Mark the calendar and prepare earnings-vol plays when the broader market is quiet.

- **Exploit relative vol across the surface and term structure** [07:29–08:48]: Compare IV across strikes (the smile) and across expirations (term structure). In SLV, the 16-Jan vol between 60–70 strikes may be higher than 31-Dec vol; this mismatch can signal diagonal, calendar, or term-vol trades. Scan for pockets where near-term vol is contracting while longer-dated vol expands (or vice versa), and trade the normalization.

## Notable quotes

> "VIX does not equal all volatility. VIX is the S&P 500 volatility."

> "What you're actually trading is not the mean reverting component of implied vol, but the relative relationship between implied vol and realized vol."

> "Don't be lazy. Be an outlier."
