# 47 signs you MIGHT be gambling vs trading options | Options Trading 101

## Summary

Eric reviews a Reddit post identifying 47 behavioral and methodological red flags that distinguish gambling from disciplined options trading. The core thesis: these signs don't automatically disqualify a trader, but their presence—especially in combination—indicates a lack of process, self-awareness, and professional rigor. The video emphasizes that trading success requires detailed planning, emotional detachment from individual outcomes, and systematic record-keeping.

## Key takeaways

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Emotional attachment to outcomes [08:48–17:14]**: Feeling happy after wins or sad after losses is a gambling tell. Professional traders focus on process and long-term edge, not individual trade results. The lottery-ticket analogy: if you know a specific sequence of tickets guarantees profit, losing one ticket doesn't upset you because you trust the system.

- **Process over outcome [16:43–20:49]**: Traders must shift focus from whether a single trade is profitable to whether they're executing their strategy within defined bounds. This requires pre-planning both profit-taking and loss-management rules before entry.

- **No management plan = gambling [28:40–30:26]**: Entering a trade without predetermined profit and loss targets is equivalent to roulette. Even during unforeseen events (e.g., COVID), professional traders maintain P&L management metrics on every position.

- **Strategy hopping [31:38–34:40]**: Jumping between strategies based on hope ("maybe this one works better") rather than a coherent playbook is a hallmark of gambling. The guaranteed-10-vs-maybe-12 lottery analogy: don't abandon a known edge for an uncertain alternative.

- **Trade logging is non-negotiable [35:06–38:09]**: Most retail traders lack trade logs—the professional equivalent of business bookkeeping. Without logs, you cannot objectively answer "What strategy is working in this regime?" and cannot allocate capital intelligently.

- **Theta is not an edge [38:42–41:05]**: Premium sellers often mistake theta decay for an edge. Theta is merely compensation for selling convexity (gamma). The real edge lies in volatility mispricing (skew, term structure, surface kinks), not time decay itself.

- **Win rate without expected return is useless [41:05–42:49]**: A 90% win rate can still produce negative expected value (e.g., 9 wins of $10 each, 1 loss of $100). Win rate is only meaningful as an input to expected-value calculations, not in isolation.

- **GEX/dealer-gamma plots are not predictive [42:49–48:59]**: These tools assume dealers are on every trade and ignore hedging complexity, individual-stock offsets, and market-maker discretion in sizing. They may flag interesting areas but are not reliable predictors.

- **Recollection is fallible [49:20–50:29]**: Saying "Bollinger Bands work well for me" without backtesting data is meaningless. Ego and confirmation bias distort memory. Only tracked, logged outcomes matter.

- **Constraints are headwinds, not excuses [51:01–53:41]**: "I don't know how to backtest" or "I can't afford data" are constraints to overcome, not reasons to skip essential steps. Find workarounds (group subscriptions, free tools, off-market research) but do not skip the work.

- **Data and backtesting are table stakes [54:10–58:14]**: If you take trading seriously, you must invest in data and backtesting infrastructure. The opportunity exists precisely because most traders skip this work. Acknowledge the opportunity cost of shortcuts, but do not pretend they are optimal.

- **Long-term thinking and compounding [23:40–25:31]**: A 1% improvement in annual return compounds dramatically over decades (e.g., $50k → $1.1M vs. $1.38M over 20 years at higher returns). Optimal strategies matter far more than convenience-based shortcuts.
