# 10a. Erik's Favorite Strategy - The Covered Strangle (CS) Pt1 | Options Strategies

## Summary

Eric introduces the **cash-secured strangle** (also called covered strangle or "the wheel"), which he describes as his primary trading strategy used in approximately 80–85% of his trades. The strategy combines a short put with a covered call and optional additional short put, rewarding disciplined management over directional accuracy. This is Part 1 of a two-part breakdown covering the mechanics, setup criteria, and allocation framework; Part 2 will demonstrate a live trade example.

## Key takeaways

- **Core structure** [00:24–04:47]: Sell a put; if assigned, sell a call against the shares and optionally sell another put (creating a strangle). If the put stays OTM, close and repeat. If the call is assigned, shares are called away and the position resets.

- **Why management beats direction** [00:52–01:20]: The strategy rewards good trade management over directional prediction, which Eric views as a "fool's errand" with 50/50 odds. Edge comes from active position management, not market timing.

- **Underlying selection criteria** [06:57–08:35]:
  - Prefer dividend-paying stocks or index/sector ETFs to limit risk
  - S&P 500 constituents for individual stocks; index or sector ETFs for diversification
  - Speculative growth plays in smaller allocations
  - Minimum 25% IV rank (higher IV = more premium)
  - Tight bid-ask spreads; at least 1 million shares traded daily
  - Avoid all-time highs; use linear regressions and moving averages to identify price extremes

- **Position sizing and allocation** [12:06–13:44]: Keep capital reserved to add to the trade if assigned at unfavorable prices. Selling multiple puts (e.g., 4+) allows cost-averaging down and faster return to breakeven if the stock declines.

- **Entry criteria and delta selection** [14:04–16:27]:
  - Target at least 1% return on capital per trade
  - Use weekly options only (more management flexibility)
  - Open trades 14–50 days to expiration (DTE)
  - Manage around 20 DTE: if longer, expect to collect theta; at 20 DTE, decide to roll or allow assignment
  - Delta selection tied to distance from all-time highs: 30 delta is a baseline (70% probability of success); lower delta closer to highs; higher delta further from highs
  - Never go below 16 delta unless using a different strategy

- **Expiration and management timing** [10:31–11:40]: Manage trades around 20 DTE because gamma risk accelerates below that threshold. At 20 DTE, make a binary decision: roll or allow assignment.

## Notable quotes

> "This strategy rewards good management approach and it doesn't care quite as much about being directionally correct, which is great because I don't try to pick direction. I always figure it's a fool's errand over the long run—it's a 50/50 chance."

> "I prefer to be able to sell personally at least four puts. The reason for that is if I sell a 20 strike and then I get assigned at 20 and the stock is now trading at 15, I might not be able to sell a call at my basis. So sometimes the best strategy is to wait and just sell another put maybe at the 12 strike, which is below the current price."
