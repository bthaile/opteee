# Choosing the RIGHT Option Strategy

## Summary

Eric walks through a systematic, process-driven framework for selecting option strategies based on portfolio needs and market opportunities. Rather than defaulting to one or two strategies, traders should develop a toolkit and match strategies to specific market conditions by filtering on risk profile, profit structure, time horizon, volatility disposition, and Greek exposure (delta, gamma, theta, vega).

## Key takeaways

- **Process-driven selection reduces variance** [00:59] — Codifying which strategies work in which market conditions prevents random strategy selection and improves consistency.
- **Two entry scenarios: portfolio-driven vs. opportunity-driven** [01:35–02:34] — Portfolio-driven trades identify what the portfolio needs (more delta, less risk, etc.); opportunity-driven trades are tied to events like FOMC meetings or earnings.
- **Define the hypothesis first** [03:15] — Before selecting a strategy, clarify what you're trading (direction, volatility, time frame) and contextualize it against your portfolio.
- **Bullish strategies: covered strangle, long call, short put, ratio call diagonal, long stock** [03:54–04:25] — Each has different risk/reward and Greek profiles; choose based on your specific hypothesis.
- **Bearish strategies: short call, long put, ratio put diagonal** [04:43] — Avoid short stock due to holding costs; use puts or ratio diagonals instead.
- **Neutral trades focus on volatility opinion** [04:43–05:25] — Long straddle if volatility will expand; short straddle if volatility is overstated (e.g., pre-FOMC). Use ratio and skew adjustments to add secondary directional bias.
- **Filter by defined vs. undefined risk** [06:08–06:31] — This is the first critical threshold; defined-risk strategies (ratio diagonals, long calls, covered strangle) naturally narrow your choices.
- **Duration of hypothesis matters** [07:13–07:31] — Short-duration trades (1–2 days, 1 week) rule out ratio diagonals; event-driven trades (FOMC, earnings) focus on the release itself, not long-term volatility.
- **Volatility disposition shapes strategy** [07:31–07:53] — Is volatility cheap, expensive, or transitioning? This determines whether you buy or sell premium.
- **Greek preferences (theta, gamma, delta) filter further** [07:53–08:32] — Want to collect theta? Sell. Want long gamma? Buy. Need short deltas? Sell correlated deltas or buy inverse deltas.
- **Stacking criteria naturally backs you into the right strategy** [08:32–08:56] — Once you outline your risk, profit, duration, volatility, and Greek preferences, only a few strategies will fit; this removes guesswork.

## Notable quotes

> "The market doesn't care about what strategy you like. The market is going to do whatever the market's going to do. So it's on us as traders to develop a toolkit that allows us to take advantage of the current market condition." [00:00–00:23]

> "If you don't figure out which one of those works best in scenario A, we're wasting a lot of time." [01:20–01:35]
