# Finding Your Trading Style | Outlier Options Trading Bootcamp

## Summary

This live bootcamp session addresses how traders should align their trading approach with their personality and constraints. The core thesis: **start by researching objective profit mechanisms and market effects, then layer in personal constraints and strengths—never the reverse.** High win rates are a preference trap; edge is prevalent but unglamorous, and lucrative edge is hard to find. The path forward requires honest self-assessment of structural constraints, baseline skill gaps, and innate proclivities, tested rigorously in paper trading before committing capital.

## Key takeaways

### Foundational framework: the tug-of-war [07:06–11:50]
- **Personality/preference vs. reality**: traders face a tension between what they want (high win rates, low time commitment, large frequent wins) and what markets actually offer.
- **Edge is prevalent but not lucrative**: most accessible edges are boring, low-return, and require discipline. Strong, lucrative edge is rare and hard to find.
- **The market doesn't care about your schedule**: profit mechanisms operate on their own timeline; if you can't access the screen when needed, you cannot execute the strategy, period.

### Phase 1: Research profit mechanisms objectively [13:01–14:51]
- **Do not start with preferences.** Identify market effects and profit mechanisms *without* filtering for high win rates, low drawdown, or personal comfort.
- **Developing edge and process is already hard enough**; adding preference filters before you have a repeatable process wastes time.
- **You will discover multiple profit mechanisms** with different behaviors; only then can you begin integrating personal fit.

### Phase 2: Assess hard constraints [15:27–17:43]
- **Structural constraints** (work schedule, device access, location) are non-negotiable realities. Mitigate them as much as possible, but accept what you cannot change.
- **Example**: on active duty with no cell service, Eric pulled off the highway to trade before work—a constraint, not an improvement, but a reality to navigate.
- **Inaccessible strategies remain inaccessible**, even if you've identified edge in them. Studying them is still valuable for research reps, but you cannot trade them practically.

### Phase 3: Strengths and weaknesses assessment [18:40–23:32]
- **Strengths**: identify what you naturally excel at (e.g., deep research, obsessive focus) to lean into later.
- **Weaknesses**: prioritize fixing baseline deficits—Excel, Python/LLMs, data analysis, discipline, paper-trading seriousness—before worrying about style.
  - *Discipline weakness example*: if you cannot take paper trading seriously without real money at risk, you will lose heavily because you'll only learn by committing capital and making expensive mistakes.
- **You are compensated for leaning into strengths, not fixing weaknesses**—but baseline weaknesses must be addressed first or the game is unplayable.

### Phase 4: Identify innate proclivities through testing [24:30–31:34]
- **Paper-trade different strategies** to discover where you naturally perform better (e.g., Eric trades long-side 5+ days better than short-side; shorts work best ≤5 days).
- **Scarcity mindset example**: Eric grew up lower-income; dollar amounts felt psychologically large even when mathematically correct (1% risk on $100k = $1k, which made him uncomfortable). He had to desensitize himself over years.
- **Do not try to wipe away innate tendencies**; acknowledge them and build controls (automation, guardrails, position sizing) around them.

### Phase 5: Match constraints to profit mechanisms [37:28–45:57]
- **Never start from "I want to trade X"**; start from "What profit mechanisms exist?" and "Which can I actually execute given my constraints?"
- **Example**: HVAC technician can check market ~1× daily → sector rotation rebalancing every 3 weeks fits; intraday scalping does not.
- **Test the optimal structure first**, then make small tweaks for your constraints, accepting that tweaks may degrade edge.
- **Style is defined by the optimal way to trade a profit mechanism**, not by risk tolerance or product choice.

### Detecting fit vs. misfit [46:30–47:55]
- **Paper-trading reveals misalignment**: if you win only 32% with $500 avg win / $700 avg loss, and you know the profit mechanism exists but you cannot execute it, that strategy is not a fit *for you right now*.
- **Two paths**: automate it, or fix your relationship with it (e.g., size down, but then returns become negligible).
- **Acknowledge the trade-off**: a viable profit mechanism may not be viable *for you* if you would bail at 8% drawdown.

### Avoiding over-pickiness [42:20–42:49]
- **Do not cut off entire asset classes or directions** (e.g., "I only go long") without reason; you eliminate massive sources of profit mechanisms.
- **Most edges behave similarly**; if you get too selective, you pick yourself out of having anything that works.

### Current market context & opportunity [52:40–01:02:24]
- **Volatility and uncertainty are *good* for active traders**: complex geopolitical environment levels the playing field; slow grindy markets are harder.
- **Directional opportunities are abundant now**: recent ceasefire announcement drove S&P +2.5%; individual names repricing rapidly (examples: 17% moves in single positions).
- **Vol trading alone is limiting**: if you only look at 30-day IV, you are trading one market effect. Complement with directional, earnings, correlation, mean-reversion, and other vol effects.

### LEAPS strategy critique [01:05:03–01:16:01]
- **Going "as far out as possible" increases bid-ask spread significantly** without proportional benefit (e.g., 35 DTE bid-ask ~0.4%, 980 DTE ~1.5% in SPY).
- **Far-dated options do not capture vol expansion**: vol changes are minimal at the tail of the curve (e.g., 37.4% → 38.9% = 1.5 points).
- **Averaging into a downtrend requires specific profit mechanism and turnaround signals**, not blind scaling. Palantir 40% off 52-week highs is a deep bear market, not a "dip."

### Dunning-Kruger effect & learning [49:39–52:14]
- **"The more I learn, the worse I get"** is normal: competence rises, but confidence drops as you realize how much you don't know.
- **This is healthy**; it means you're past the initial overconfidence phase and into the long climb toward mastery (Eric: 35,000+ hours in markets).
