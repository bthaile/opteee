# Green 16/17 Months Selling Options — The Win-Rate Trap

## Summary

A trader posts 17 consecutive months of profitable option-selling trades, claiming an 8.32% return since 2004. Eric analyzes the account and reveals a critical flaw: while the trader achieved a high win rate through consistent premium selling, the S&P 500 returned ~27.67% over the same period. The core lesson is that obsession with win rate and green months can mask severe underperformance and opportunity cost, especially when buy-and-hold strategies are more tax-efficient and profitable.

## Key takeaways

- **The win-rate illusion [00:00–02:38]**: High consecutive winning months feel psychologically rewarding but can mask underperformance relative to the market. A trader up 8% while the market is up 27.67% over 17 months is leaving significant money on the table.

- **Opportunity cost quantified [05:18–06:44]**: Starting with ~$588k and earning 8.32% yields ~$637k; the same capital in the S&P 500 at 12% long-term average would yield ~$750k—a $163k variance (19.3% return difference) over 18 months.

- **Tax efficiency and simplicity [08:04]**: Option-selling strategies often generate short-term capital gains and frequent taxable events, whereas buy-and-hold is more tax-efficient and typically produces better absolute returns without the operational complexity.

- **Blending vs. dogma [08:04]**: Rather than committing exclusively to selling or buying options, traders should deploy each tactic contextually—sometimes buying, sometimes selling—based on market conditions and edge, not psychological preference.

- **Disposition effect in action [03:58–04:00]**: The seductive feeling of consistent green days and months can lull traders into a false sense of security, causing them to ignore that their strategy is underperforming the market.

## Notable quotes

> "The problem here is the disease of people who are really too wrapped up in win rate and selling to get a high win rate that we sometimes lose the forest for the trees."

> "If you decide to trade options, do your best to avoid getting sucked into one of these two polar camps of buying options that are like zero DTE going to expire or selling options."
