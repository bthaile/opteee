# Trading Options as a Business | The Options Trench

## Summary

This episode explores the operational and psychological foundations of running options trading as a sustainable business. The discussion covers the critical role of competitiveness and decision-making under uncertainty, the importance of divorcing outcomes from process, statistical frameworks for strategy evaluation, emotional regulation, and the strategic expansion of a trading repertoire across market regimes. The hosts emphasize that successful trading is less about genius and more about disciplined execution, honest accounting, and building a diversified toolkit of profit mechanisms.

## Key takeaways

### Competitiveness and decision-making under uncertainty
- **Competitiveness as the common thread** [01:10–04:35]: Across the trading floor—from PhDs to unconventional traders—the unifying trait is hyper-competitiveness and willingness to act despite incomplete information. Talent can offset some lack of competitiveness, but geniuses without competitive drive often fail to execute.
- **Imperfect information is the norm** [06:39–07:30]: Trading requires comfort with perpetual uncertainty. Military backgrounds provide an advantage because they normalize calculated risk-taking with unknowns.
- **Poker as a training ground** [08:01–08:45]: Understanding that a good hand wins only ~55% of the time teaches the fundamental lesson: good decisions can produce bad outcomes, and vice versa. This decoupling of decision quality from outcome is essential.

### Process over outcome, guided by aggregate statistics
- **Moneyball principle** [09:08–09:55]: Billy Beane's refusal to watch games exemplifies outcome detachment. One or two outcomes provide no actionable information; only aggregate patterns matter.
- **Distinguishing bad runs from regime change** [12:24–15:27]: Use statistical frameworks (mean, standard deviation, descriptive stats like consecutive wins/losses, max drawdown) to determine whether a strategy is experiencing normal variance or genuine deterioration. For discretionary traders, this is a lifelong challenge.
- **Sizing down during uncertainty** [18:29–19:31]: When unsure whether a drawdown is noise or signal, reduce position size and observe. This protects capital while gathering evidence.
- **Decomposing strategy performance** [20:20–22:53]: Break down P&L into component KPIs (hit rate, payoff ratio, trend formation frequency). Monitor these "vital signs" separately to troubleshoot which aspect has degraded.

### Emotional regulation and what to be happy/sad about
- **Outcomes are often outside your control** [24:34–26:40]: Being happy because money was made or sad because it was lost conflates luck with skill. Happiness should attach to conditions that support future profitability (e.g., a favorable setup developing, discovering a fool in the market), not to random chip-flip results.
- **The oil time-spread example** [27:22–28:06]: Losing money daily on a short time spread while it was being marked higher was cause for happiness, because the trader was getting better entry prices for a position he loved.
- **Correlation trading rhythm** [28:06–29:09]: In correlation short strategies, losses during the build-up phase are expected and welcome; the real trade happens on the blowout. Excitement should attach to the setup, not the daily P&L.
- **Stoicism and the sphere of control** [31:22–32:23]: Value what you control (decision quality, process adherence); accept what you don't (outcomes). This is not emotionlessness but rational allocation of mental energy.

### Building a sustainable trading business
- **Start with one edge, expand during slow periods** [35:02–38:27]: Begin with a single profitable strategy. When it enters a quiet regime, use that time to research adjacent opportunities (e.g., vanilla options → Asian options, calendar spreads). Avoid the trap of over-trading a dormant edge.
- **The buffer problem** [40:20–41:50]: Most retail traders fail because they give themselves no financial runway. A bad six-month drawdown should not destroy the business. Build a buffer larger than you think necessary.
- **Avoid forced trading** [38:44–39:16]: Market makers forced to trade natural gas all day will trade at fair value out of boredom. As a discretionary trader, you can walk away. Don't force edge where none exists.
- **Honest accounting for premium selling** [42:57–45:53]: Selling an option for $4 does not mean you made $4. If you believe fair value is $3, you've only booked $1 of edge (the difference between your sale price and your theo). Measure and improve based on honest P&L attribution, not gross premium collected.

### Strategy selection and anomaly hunting
- **Start with well-documented anomalies** [50:33–51:39]: Momentum, mean reversion, and other long-studied effects provide a foundation. There's enough else to get right without betting on an undiscovered edge.
- **Volatility-first approach** [46:48–47:08]: One framework is to evaluate options across surfaces by implied volatility, then trade relative value. Another is to hunt for documented anomalies (e.g., month-end window dressing) and use options as a vehicle.
- **Liquidity-edge tradeoff** [49:44–50:33]: Avoid highly liquid names like SPY; edge is more likely in less-watched, less-liquid instruments where someone has to do something non-economic.
- **Separate trading from investing** [53:05–57:35]: A trader buying at $10, selling at $11, buying at $24, selling at $25 made $3 while the stock appreciated $30. This conflates two different businesses. Compartmentalize: trade for throughput and edge; invest for compounding and long-term growth.

### Personal context and career evolution
- **21 years on the desk, then a pivot** [01:02:16–01:04:23]: After two decades in the same role, the speaker left in 2021 when faced with a choice to scale the business or step back. He chose the latter, lost interest in trading for a couple of years, and has recently found renewed energy via AI/LLM tools and the velocity they enable.
- **Importance of desk culture** [01:04:05–01:04:23]: Working from home on a team lacks the banter, idea velocity, and serendipitous water-cooler insights of a physical desk. The speaker values that environment and would not rule out returning to it.
