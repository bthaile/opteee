# How I Trade Options

## Summary

Eric outlines his comprehensive options trading framework built on process-driven discipline, strong convictions held loosely, and quantifiable edge. He emphasizes that sustainable wealth comes from repeatable systems executed over decades—not from outsized single-trade returns—and demonstrates how he combines fundamental and technical analysis to identify profit mechanisms before selecting strategies.

## Key takeaways

- **Process over outcome** [00:37–01:17]: Focus on building repeatable systems executable over decades, not salvaging individual trades. Deviation from plan compounds losses faster than adherence compounds gains.
- **Three wealth levers** [01:17–02:01]: Saving more money, earning more through better jobs, and improving trading skill. Most traders overweight the third lever and underestimate the first two.
- **Robustness across regimes** [02:19–03:19]: Develop approaches that work in steep declines, stimulus rallies, sideways consolidation, and slow drifts. A strategy that works in one regime only lacks durability.
- **Strong convictions, loosely held** [03:43–05:41]: Use Bayesian analysis—research thoroughly, quantify conviction strength, then update when new information arrives. Example: rotated from long Russell (rate-cut thesis) to NASDAQ/TQQQ when Russell underperformed over reasonable timeframe.
- **Hard edge vs. soft edge** [06:22–07:46]: Hard edge is persistent, quantifiable (e.g., implied volatility overstated vs. realized volatility at earnings). Soft edge is discretionary momentum/breakout trading requiring experience. Both have use cases.
- **Combined analysis** [08:08–08:47]: Use all relevant data—fundamental, technical, macro—weighted by context. Don't label yourself; be a "whatever analyst" aligned to the trade.
- **1% compounds to millions** [09:10–10:01]: Over 30 years, 1% return difference (15% vs. 16%) compounds to ~$2.2M variance. Compounding is invisible for 16 years, then parabolic.
- **Skeptical optimism** [10:01–10:22]: Remain open to new information but screen rigorously for quantifiability and thesis rigor. Markets change; close-mindedness guarantees obsolescence.
- **Top-down portfolio construction** [10:40–11:39]: Start with macro themes (inflation, growth, AI), form disposition, then build portfolio where each position supports the collective thesis—not all positions need to express the same view.
- **Profit mechanism first, strategy second** [11:39–12:18]: Identify the opportunity (e.g., bullish directional move), then choose the structure (long call, short put, etc.) that best fits portfolio risk/return. Structure is just the vehicle.
- **Undefined risk, active management** [12:36–13:12]: Prefer undefined-risk structures (no verticals) and manage mid-trade rather than set-and-forget. Reduces friction and allows adaptation.
- **Leverage and volatility exposure** [13:12–13:32]: Use options for directional leverage and to isolate volatility exposure unavailable in equities. Time frame is secondary to profit mechanism.
- **Bullish bias with reason** [14:07–14:27]: Markets display quantitative bullish drift; trade trend, momentum, breakouts/breakdowns unless strong contrarian thesis exists. Keep strategies simple—"brilliance at the basics."
- **Volatility trading** [15:09–15:30]: Trade variance risk premium, mean reversion of volatility extremes, and relative value/skew across chains and strikes. Use single options and calendars for relative value.
- **Price and volume first** [15:51–16:35]: All indicators (moving averages, standard deviation channels, volume profile) visualize price and volume—the only things that matter. Work with the market, never dictate to it.
