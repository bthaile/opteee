# Euan Sinclair: Can You Predict the Next Big Market Move?

## Summary
Quant options trader Euan Sinclair argues that edge comes from a handful of durable, well-understood risk premia (selling puts, selling vol) plus honest statistical humility — not from clever, hard-to-measure effects like dealer-gamma/GEX or fat-tail "wings" bets. His core thesis on predictability: everything is predictable on *some* timescale and unpredictable on others; the job is to pick the stable regimes and easy problems rather than to forecast surprises. Almost everything a retail trader "knows" (tariffs are bad, a stock looks cheap) is already in the price; real edge requires either a proprietary view or accepting a risk premium and not over-managing it. He is unusually explicit that you can be right about volatility and still lose money, so you must decompose P&L before you can learn anything from it.

## Key takeaways
- **VIX is sticky-low and asymmetric here** [01:28][02:37]. He reads the resting VIX level as a touch too low, *over*reactive on the downside and *under*reactive on the upside (a "hockey stick"). Distinction he draws: low *time-series* (path) volatility but large point-to-point moves — so option sellers who hedge continuously stay fine, and upside calls look cheap.
- **The cheapest systematic thing right now: 40-delta 0DTE / index upside calls** [03:49]. If forced to be short vol, he'd *buy* the meaty ATM-ish call and sell a ~15-delta strangle against it — the opposite of the popular retail "sell 0DTE, buy the wings." He explicitly dislikes selling 0DTE and buying the wings.
- **There is no always-on long-option strategy.** The closest thing to a permanent short strategy is *selling 20-delta puts* — no equivalent exists on the long side; "you've got to pick your spots" [01:28].
- **Leveraged single-stock ETFs are lottery tickets** [08:31]. Mean return scales with leverage exactly as expected, but the **median is vastly lower** — most of the time you bleed on the chop/decay (volatility drag), occasionally you make a ton. TQQQ's past outperformance was just the *path* QQQ took (consecutive moves overpay, alternating moves underpay), not correlation. Shorting them is expensive (you're short an option and paying borrow) — he'd rather sell calls or buy puts.
- **Leveraged-vs-unleveraged pairs trade** [10:54][12:13]. A leveraged ETF is effectively a quadratic/power option; long one, short the underlying (or the inverse) and gamma-scalp the tracking error. Dollar-neutral, rebalance ~monthly, mind transaction/borrow costs. Cited BOIL vs UNG as a candidate. He hasn't traded it — offered as a framework, not a recommendation.
- **Hedge-frequency heuristic** [13:30]: risk is roughly quadratic (∝ vol), costs are linear (∝ stock traded); rebalance at the crossover. High vol or wide beta spread → rebalance *less* (let it move — "that's why you got it"). Not a trend call; "if you know it trends, that's the start of another trade."
- **Separate the vol trade from the directional trade** [14:51]. Mixing hedging decisions with a directional view ("I won't hedge because I think we're going up") means you can never learn why you made or lost money. This is why he dislikes trading options directionally.
- **You can be right about vol and still lose money — routinely, not occasionally** [17:08]. Every option trade has huge variance vs its edge (like a coin that's 51% heads — each flip still looks like noise), so you cannot let P&L drive your theory. Do the work to isolate "what you should have made."
- **Classic market-maker P&L decomposition = the Black-Scholes equation** [18:14]: Vega × change in vol, delta P&L, gamma (≈ half the move), balanced against theta. Vega sits on top since it's not in BS.
- **No edge in the Greeks; skip the higher-order ones** [21:42][19:25]. Delta/gamma/theta/vega tell you what's happening — like a car's instruments. Charm/vanna and higher-order Greeks matter mainly for market makers (e.g., delta drift over a weekend); retail needs to know they *exist*, not track them. Retail wrongly assumes "pros talk in Greeks, so Greeks are the magic."
- **Retail failure modes** [23:44][25:00]: overtrading (delta-hedging a straddle their cost structure can't support); copying pro trades that exist for other reasons (skew RV, 3m-vs-6m); entering trades where everything must go perfectly. A steep skew isn't free money — it's a signal the risk profile *changed*.
- **Dealer-gamma / GEX for retail is "absolute nonsense"** [26:11]→[32:01]. You cannot infer dealer positioning from outside — even bid/offer is ambiguous (dealers cross the spread when overloaded, and pre-hedge). His Amber Data Bitcoin-GEX build took ~35 rules plus phoning market makers to verify, for *one* instrument on Deribit; a website claiming it for all of the S&P 500 is not credible. Even given a dealer's exact position you can't front-run them — an S&P short may be hedging a single-stock long. Market makers have optimized hedging for 50 years; there's no free money on a "call wall" button.
- **Vanna-squeeze / gamma-pinning effects are real but inconsequential now** [34:41][35:44]. It was a big deal in the '90s–early 2000s (few strikes, monthly-only expiries concentrated gamma at strikes); today strikes/expiries are smeared out. Germ of truth, over-weighted by people who love "what the pros are doing."
- **Predictability is timescale-dependent** [38:00][39:00]. Easy: the next S&P tick (if you're Jane Street/Jump) or the S&P in 50 years (higher). Hard: everything in between. Every trade is a prediction (even trend-following). Trade the stable regimes: "VIX is a bit cheap" is one specific, currently-stable bet; selling 20-delta puts should keep working over 10 years because it has for 100. **"We don't get paid for playing on hard mode."**
- **Your knowledge is already in the price** [43:34][44:32]. Sports-betting analogy: great athletes are bad gamblers because what they know, the marginal price-setter already knows. Tariffs are bad → everyone knows → baked in. Public info processed the ordinary way yields no edge.
- **Two drivers of moves: flows vs surprises** [48:13]. Most intraday range (end-of-month drift, post-PPI chop) is *flows* with no new info; genuine surprises (Oracle's ~30% earnings jump) are what markets can't have priced. You cannot predict a surprise — that's why it's a surprise.
- **Earnings-vol premium: accept it, don't cherry-pick** [50:29]→[54:11]. Splitting by industry, P/E, size, past reaction found essentially nothing tradeable. Past earnings reactions have *no* bearing on future ones. Two small edges: the effect is larger in small-cap/low-price names (but hard to trade, so maybe just un-arbitrageable), and a name is a better *sale* if implied vol rose a lot going in (i.e., you should have been long IV first). Start-vs-end of earnings season makes no difference.
- **Long-vol P&L is a stepped plateau** [56:41][57:51]. Occasional big winners, can be flat or down for two years. Last earnings cycle was the worst-ever for premium sellers — the only time a majority of stocks beat their implied move since he began tracking in 2006 — yet the prior cycle was among the biggest winners. The one real drawback of long vol: you accumulate a huge string of positions (10/day for months) that's hard to manage on a retail platform.
- **The wings: not enough data to conclude anything** [58:55]→[01:05:17]. We're terrible at rare-event stats — for a single stock we overestimate a big move; across 500 stocks we underestimate that *something* extreme happens daily. His study: given the kurtosis, proving a 0DTE strategy is statistically profitable would take **~33 to 4,000 years**. So he rejects Taleb's inference that the wings are underpriced (no P&L evidence, no reason a 1-delta option is "magic" vs a 5-delta) — but won't *sell* 1-delta options either ("if you're wrong, you're dead").
- **Statistics are only one input; belief is the meta-judgment** [01:09:02][01:11:16]. A t-stat of 3 is easy to find in-sample and rarely survives out-of-sample. Both his and Lopez de Prado's Sharpe/Kelly sampling-property work assume a *constant* underlying process, which markets violate. Real process: confluence of statistics + a plausible mechanism + stability across time slices + cross-validation (works on S&P → should work on NASDAQ). He wrote a paper on sampling properties of the Kelly ratio, adapting Marcos López de Prado's Sharpe-ratio work.
- **0DTE selling has edge by continuity** [01:09:02]: selling strangles 1-year and 1-month out both carry edge, so 1-DTE almost certainly does too — "it looks an awful lot like selling one year, just happening quicker."
- **Momentum vs reversion by horizon** [01:10:08]: reversion at ~1 month, trending at ~3–6 months (continuation of the *effect*, not price). But long-short momentum is a weak, hard-to-harvest signal (~12%/yr risk-parity weighted) — trade it only on confluence of evidence + belief.
- **Vol/mean-reversion is horizon-specific, not a measurement error** [01:13:34][01:14:40]. Same methodology at 5-minute vs 1-day, but different answers because you're measuring different things (you can't aggregate 5-min vol into 1-day vol). Deck/ant analogy: daily vol is a high-level average; finer sampling sees finer structure. High short-term vol + low long-term vol just says the market has been trending short-term and reverting long-term. Time-series/GARCH vol forecasting is now largely priced out ("everyone's got those models") vs 20 years ago.

## Regime / context
- Recorded 2025-09-14 into a slow grind-up market: index rising while VIX stays sticky/low; upside calls (20–40 delta, incl. 0DTE) unusually cheap.
- Mag-7 dominance driving a wave of levered single-stock ETFs and upside-selling structured products; cheap/easy CBOE listing (~$5k) creating many "zombie" products.
- Recent macro backdrop referenced: April 2025 tariff shock and VIX spike (his fund stayed long through it), ongoing Ukraine war, Middle East risk, CPI/PPI prints — market climbing the "wall of worry."
- Oracle's large (~30%) earnings surprise cited as a fresh, genuinely-unpriceable move.
- Just-completed earnings cycle was the worst-ever for premium sellers (first cycle since 2006 where a majority of stocks beat their implied move) — a cautionary regime marker for short-vol / earnings-straddle sellers.
