# Doomberg: What's Next in the Iran War | The Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Doomberg returns to discuss the escalating Iran–US–Israel conflict as of late March 2026, focusing on how to define "winning" a war and the asymmetric pain-tolerance dynamics at play. The conversation examines Iran's demonstrated precision-strike capability (notably the Ras Laffan LNG facility attack), the fragility of Middle Eastern energy infrastructure, and the risk of boots-on-the-ground escalation. Long-term, the discussion pivots to energy-market bifurcation and the geopolitical realignment of oil and gas flows away from the Western-led petrodollar system.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2026-03-24)

- **War definition and pain tolerance** [01:39–04:03]: A war is won when one side inflicts more pain than the other can tolerate. Historical examples (Vietnam, WWII Germany, Japan) show that air power alone rarely achieves capitulation; pain tolerance and willingness to continue fighting matter more than raw military capability.

- **Iran's precision strike on Ras Laffan** [06:27–07:46]: Iran struck LNG trains 4 and 6 at Qatar's Ras Laffan facility—the only trains jointly owned by Qatar Energy and ExxonMobil—while leaving other trains untouched. This demonstrates surgical precision 19 days into the conflict and signals Iran's ability to target US shareholder interests specifically.

- **LNG supply shock: 20% offline** [32:37–34:34]: Two trains represent ~17% of Ras Laffan's 14-train capacity, taking ~20% of global LNG offline. While LNG is only ~18% of global natural gas (making the impact ~4% of total gas), the disruption is acute for Europe and Asia, which depend heavily on imports. Europe enters summer 2026 with historically low gas stocks after abandoning Russian supply.

- **Oil price resilience under Treasury pressure** [19:57–21:27]: Despite war, oil trades ~$90–100 (Texas/Europe). Treasury Secretary Bessant is executing a strategy to keep oil under $100 domestically and ~$100 overseas by unsanctioning Russian and Iranian oil—a pragmatic reversal of initial strategy to maintain global supply and prevent demand destruction that could trigger recession.

- **Boots-on-the-ground escalation signal** [24:13–25:02]: Trump's 48-hour ultimatum (issued pre-market Monday) coincides with the first batch of Marines entering theater in ~5 days. This timing suggests the decision to deploy ground forces may already be made; Trump is buying time to position assets, mirroring his earlier use of Witkoff and Kushner negotiations while aircraft carriers moved into position.

- **Desalination as a nuclear-equivalent target** [13:34–14:23]: Israel's dependence on desalination plants is a critical vulnerability. Iran has demonstrated precision targeting; destroying even one major facility would be an escalation on par with tactical nuclear weapons and could trigger nuclear retaliation.

- **Fiber-optic cable and Strait of Hormuz threats** [09:32–10:02]: Iran has opened a second escalatory track: targeting undersea fiber-optic cables in the Strait of Hormuz. Even attacks nominally on US interests (e.g., Exxon facilities) harm neighboring countries, complicating regional pain tolerance.

- **Energy lockdowns and "turtling"** [38:18–45:06]: Expect protectionist energy policies ("turtling"): the US, Canada, Russia, and China will prioritize domestic supply, capping prices and restricting exports. This self-fulfilling cascade—panic buying in Australia, empty pumps, theft—mirrors early-COVID frenzy. DEF fluid (urea) shortages could immobilize diesel supply chains.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Defining victory in asymmetric conflict**: Pain inflicted, pain tolerated, and willingness to continue fighting are the true metrics—not military hardware superiority.

- **Precision targeting and escalation ladders**: Modern missile and drone technology allows combatants to signal intent and calibrate escalation (e.g., hitting specific trains, avoiding others) in ways that complicate deterrence.

- **Energy as a geopolitical weapon**: Closure of the Strait of Hormuz, LNG disruption, and desalination-plant targeting are low-cost, high-impact escalatory options for Iran.

- **Petrodollar bifurcation**: The 50-year petrodollar system (since Nixon, 1971) is fragmenting. Energy flows are realigning: China needs Middle Eastern oil; the US can source from the Western Hemisphere. This "punctuated equilibrium" will reshape global trade and financial markets over 30 years.

- **Contested regions**: Australia, Japan, South Korea, Brazil, Africa, and the Arctic will become flashpoints as energy supply chains reorganize.
