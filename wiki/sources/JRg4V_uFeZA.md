# The Nuance Of Implied Volatility In Covered Calls

## Summary

High implied volatility attracts premium sellers, but elevated IV often signals underlying risk or clustering behavior that makes selling at extremes dangerous. The key is measuring IV percentile relative to the security's historical range and avoiding both extremes—sub-10 percentile (undercompensated) and above-90 percentile (potentially underpriced relative to realized risk).

## Key takeaways

- **Avoid IV extremes in covered calls** [00:00–00:23]: High IV attracts premium, but volatility clusters and often has a reason. Measure IV percentile relative to the security's own history, not in absolute terms.
- **Sub-10 IV percentile is a poor sell** [00:23–00:42]: When IV percentile is extremely low, you're collecting minimal premium for the risk you're taking. If a move occurs, you haven't been compensated fairly.
- **Above-90 IV percentile signals caution** [00:42]: When IV percentile exceeds 90, something serious is likely happening. You may be selling underpriced volatility despite the high nominal premium.
- **The core principle: avoid selling underpriced vol** [00:42]: In both extremes, you risk selling volatility that doesn't fairly compensate you for the risk you're assuming.

## Notable quotes

> "The problem is, more frequently than not, there's probably a reason why the volatility is high, and it can cluster high for some time."

> "If the implied volatility percentile is super duper low, like sub 10, then you're not getting any money effectively for what you're selling there."
