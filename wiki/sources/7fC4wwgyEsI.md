# Assessing Your Current Competence as a Trader | Options Trading for Beginners

## Summary

Eric walks through a 10-question self-assessment framework designed to help beginner traders objectively evaluate their current competence across seven core trading functions: education, research, design/execution, risk management, program management, and psychology. The assessment maps responses to five competence levels (unstructured beginner through outlier trader) and provides concrete pathways for progression at each stage, emphasizing that minimizing drawdowns and building consistent process matters far more than short-term outperformance.

## Key takeaways

- **Dunning-Kruger risk in early learning** [07:18] — Beginners often grossly overestimate competence after learning foundational concepts, leading to costly errors; this assessment is designed to prevent that.

- **Seven core trader functions** [13:21] — Education, research, design/execution, risk management, program management, and psychology are the pillars; early focus should be on education and research, with psychology mattering less initially.

- **Competence scale (0–3) per function** [14:40] — 0 = unfamiliar; 1 = basic awareness; 2 = functional with effort; 3 = reliable, intuitive mastery. Most beginners overestimate themselves at 5–6 when realistic self-rating is 3–4.

- **Market structure and participants** [27:07] — Understanding brokers, buy-side vs. sell-side, and liquidity provision is table stakes; applying this to identify arbitrage or market-making opportunities is a level-3 skill.

- **Options fundamentals (calls, puts, Greeks, IV)** [31:01] — A zero means gaps in any single concept; level 1 is fumbling but functional; level 2 is confident application; level 3 is intuitive mastery without conscious thought.

- **Evaluating and building option structures** [32:54] — Level 1: finding an expiration but likely making mistakes. Level 2: logical selection mapped to thesis. Level 3: optimizing structure to thesis and market conditions (e.g., choosing stock vs. derivative).

- **Market effects and profit mechanisms** [34:35] — Level 1: aware but not practical. Level 2: can identify effects with effort and iterate to a mechanism. Level 3: intuitively recognize effects and optimize methodology without heavy research.

- **Thesis clarity and accuracy** [36:35] — Level 0: "stock will go up." Level 1: upside target and downside target with reasoning. Level 2: thesis includes time frame and shows some accuracy in expected-return terms. Level 3: mastery of thesis-building across different profit mechanisms with demonstrable hit rates.

- **Trade management plan** [39:24] — Level 0: no plan. Level 1: profit and loss targets, generally adhered to. Level 2: targets mapped logically to strategy and profit mechanism. Level 3: optimized exits based on real-time regime and underlying behavior, not mechanical rules.

- **Full trading process (generate, test, track, operationalize)** [41:12] — Level 1: familiar but unsure how to cycle. Level 2: can move through but not optimized. Level 3: mastery across all subsections with confidence.

- **After-action review (AAR) process** [42:41] — Level 0: none. Level 1: haphazard. Level 2: structured but room for improvement. Level 3: full loop at portfolio, strategy, and trade level; ability to make novel adjustments based on regime (e.g., zero-DTE adjustments in current market).

- **12-month trading track record** [44:06] — Paper or live; must be ~90% accurate logging of all trades. Eric notes most self-reported "yes" answers likely have gaps; reconcile monthly against broker statements.

- **24-month live track record with progression** [46:30] — Must show 90% accurate logging, monthly review with novel adjustments, and *progression toward* targets (not immediate achievement). Progression = moving in the right direction over time.

- **Five competence levels** [48:34] — 0–5: unstructured beginner. 6–10: aware but inconsistent. 11–16: developing. 17–22: process-oriented. 23+: outlier trader foundations.

- **Progression pathways** [53:59] — Zero to one on options evaluation: paper-trade two variants (e.g., 50-delta vs. 70-delta call). One to two: backtest variants. Two to three: backtest + Monte Carlo simulation on novel data. For thesis: track and backtest; for AAR: just start doing it (level 1 alone drives improvement).

- **Drawdown minimization over outperformance** [09:55] — You don't need to beat the market every year; minimizing big drawdowns places you on a better long-term growth trajectory than most other routes.

- **Learning efficiency vs. trial-and-error** [08:43] — Learning from others' mistakes, paper trading, and structured process can help you skip costly steps; rather than losing money at all 10 steps, skip three and lose less on the remaining seven.

## Notable quotes

> "The defining function of a trader really is not like can you make a ton of money in a year or two. It's like can you do this for 25 years plus and hit the targets that you're trying to hit on average over that period." [15:51]

> "You do not need to outperform the market to the upside every single year in order to outperform it long term. A big part of your return potential comes from your ability to minimize big draw downs." [09:55]
