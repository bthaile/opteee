# Inflation, The Fed & GameStop: What You Need to Know About Today's Markets w/ David Lauer | TOP Ep78

## Summary

David Lauer returns to discuss the current inflation landscape, Fed policy trajectory, and recession probability. The conversation pivots to unusual options activity in VIX calls and a deep dive into Roaring Kitty's historical options trading methodology, market maker hedging dynamics on GameStop, and structural issues in market microstructure including the SEC's recent Reg NMS rule and the case for batch auctions over continuous trading.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2024-10-09)

- **Inflation narrative shift** [02:22]: Headline inflation near 2% but price levels remain elevated; the psychological effect of the inflationary period persists even as growth rates normalize. Wage growth has outpaced price increases on a real basis, though nominal sticker shock remains.
- **Recession probability** [05:47]: Lauer assesses next-6-month recession probability as lower, but next-12-month as ~50/50. Seasonal adjustments in jobs data are breaking down, signaling transition from growth to potential contraction.
- **VIX call positioning** [07:29]–[08:44]: VIX 45 calls (8 DTE) saw open interest spike from ~100k to 300k; volume in 45 strikes at 21k vs. 25 strikes at 32k. Probability of touching 45 is <0.1%. Likely hedging ahead of CPI/PPI data and Fed minutes release.
- **Fed Watch Tool volatility** [14:50]–[17:48]: Probability of no rate cut swung from 0% (one week prior) to 13% in two days—oscillating between certainty and uncertainty. Suggests either normal noise or informed positioning ahead of inflation data.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Inflation vs. price level** [02:22]: Inflation measures the *rate of change* in prices, not absolute price levels. Nominal inflation can appear high while real inflation (adjusted for wage growth) is moderate.
- **Real vs. nominal returns** [03:47]: Psychological impact of price resets persists for years after inflationary episodes, even when real purchasing power has stabilized.
- **Options market maker hedging** [34:39]–[35:49]: Market makers hedge GameStop options immediately upon sale due to high idiosyncratic risk and fat premiums. Hedging is spread across multiple firms; no single firm concentrates the full flow.
- **Out-of-the-money call buying** [39:11]–[40:13]: Retail and tail-risk hedge funds consistently buy far OTM calls on GameStop. Market makers print money on these until a parabolic move occurs; tail-risk funds hold for rare, high-payoff events.
- **Roaring Kitty's investor mindset** [25:28]–[31:11]: Kitty's trade structure (holding low-cost-basis OTM calls while closing ITM calls near expiration) reflects an *investor* rather than *trader* mentality. He expects directional moves and holds leveraged positions with no hard stops or profit targets.
- **Market maker concentration and manipulation** [45:05]–[46:23]: In illiquid names, one or two firms control price completely. Manipulation is possible where regulatory scrutiny is low and complexity obscures intent. Liquidity-first regulatory focus enables this.
- **Batch auctions vs. continuous trading** [47:45]–[49:57]: Frequent batch auctions concentrate real liquidity and improve price discovery vs. phantom liquidity in continuous markets. Adoption is a prisoner's dilemma: switching requires coordination across fragmented venues.

## Notable quotes

- "Inflation is the growth rate, not the price level. Economists focus on how prices are changing, not the absolute price." [02:22]
- "Market makers have learned there is a base level of manipulation they can engage in and withstand regulatory scrutiny because they can overpower regulators with intellect, data, and complexity." [46:23]
