# The Pitfalls of Cheap Options

## Summary

Out-of-the-money options decay rapidly due to theta, eroding extrinsic value at an accelerating rate as expiration approaches. A seemingly cheap OTM option that loses 44 cents of premium in a single day requires increasingly larger underlying moves to recover break-even, making them poor risk-reward vehicles for most traders.

## Key takeaways

- **Theta decay accelerates near expiration** [00:00] — a single day can erase ~44 cents of premium on a cheap OTM option, reducing a 43-cent position to 24 cents.
- **Break-even threshold rises as time passes** [00:33] — after one day of decay, the required move to recover break-even jumps from initial strike distance to $1.90+, forcing you to chase larger and larger moves.
- **OTM options are "hot potatoes"** [00:33] — the longer you hold them, the worse your risk-reward becomes; you need outsized directional conviction to justify the theta bleed.
- **Cheap ≠ good value** [00:00] — low absolute premium masks the brutal percentage decay rate; relative value erodes faster than absolute price suggests.

## Notable quotes

> "You now need a $1.90 move to get back to break even. This is what makes these out-of-the-money options like hot potatoes because the longer you hold on to them, you literally now need larger and larger moves to even get back to break even, let alone to start making money."
