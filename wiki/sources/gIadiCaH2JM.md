# Why Buying Calls is Destroying Your Account | Options Trading for Beginners Ep1

## Summary

This episode launches an eight-part beginner series on common mistakes in options trading, focusing specifically on buying calls. Eric breaks down three major traps: lottery-like plays, cheap options that appear safe but carry poor break-even rates, and the hidden impact of volatility decay and other Greeks. The core message is that buying calls can destroy accounts not because the strategy is inherently flawed, but because traders often misunderstand the mechanics—selecting strikes based on price targets, ignoring probability relationships, and underestimating theta and charm decay on short-dated positions.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Moneyness vs. probability**: A 20-cent OTM call is not automatically safer than a $2 call; the relationship between moneyness and probability of profit is what matters [13:16]
- **Long call win conditions**: For a long call to be profitable, three things must go right besides direction: time, volatility, and velocity (directional move). Liquidity/ability to sell back is more efficient but not required—you can exercise at expiration if ITM [17:54]
- **Probability comparison**: Far OTM weekly calls have lower probability than OTM monthly calls; the term component is a distraction; moneyness determines probability [26:11]
- **Pre-trade definitions**: Before buying a call, define: profit mechanism, asset, exit conditions, target price, break-even, stop loss, risk-to-reward, optimal DTE, and theta horizon. IV is useful but not strictly required [33:01]
- **Strike selection error**: Selecting strikes based on price targets is a "massive mistake"; instead, select based on the quality of the option and desired behavior [43:42]
- **Profitability without hitting strike**: An option does not need to reach its strike price to be profitable; it only needs to be worth more than you paid [44:07]
- **Lottery plays**: Far OTM short-DTE options exhibit a distribution where most trades are small losses, but rare winners can be massive; profitability depends on being directionally correct and having winners large enough to offset frequent losers [50:45]
- **Cheap options trap**: Cheap options are not the same as underpriced options; they exhibit a pattern of perpetual small losses with infrequent winners; break-even rate is what matters, not nominal cost [52:45]
- **Greeks decay on short-dated OTM calls**: Delta decays (charm), gamma drops, theta accelerates as expiration approaches, vega declines; theta as a percentage of remaining premium becomes massive near expiration [55:26]
- **Bid-ask spread impact**: Spreads widen significantly as you move further OTM; relative to option value, the spread becomes worse and worse [58:20]
- **Implied vol vs. realized vol**: In indices like SPY, you typically overpay for vol; in names like MSTR, realized moves sometimes exceed implied vol, creating rare underpriced scenarios [59:33]
- **Fixing the problem**: Apply the [[outlier-strategy-process]] by identifying the profit mechanism first, then structuring long calls accordingly—not defaulting to cheap options [01:02:41]
- **LEAPS and lotto dynamics**: Buying lotto-style calls on LEAPS changes the context; you cannot easily access true lotto deltas on bullish-skewed names like ASTS; the general effect does not change much with respect to time [01:04:42]
- **Weekend volatility anomaly**: There is a documented spike in news events over weekends relative to a 15-year trailing average; volatility expands going through weekends, but much of that vol decays before the weekend arrives [01:16:31]
- **Regime dependency**: Overnight effects and academic research findings are regime-dependent; do not apply them as blanket rules [01:20:48]
- **Research methodology**: Testing should be a gauntlet designed to punch holes in a hypothesis, not cherry-pick examples; overfitting (e.g., running a single ticker from 1990) is a common and critical error [01:23:08]
