# Credit Events w/ Michael Gayed | The Outlier Podcast Ep 55

## Summary

Michael Gayed, founder of Lead-Lag Research and Sphere of ETFs, discusses his thesis on credit events and the lagged effects of the fastest rate-hike cycle in history. While he was early on a November capitulation call, Gayed maintains that the structural conditions for a credit event (VIX spike + widening spreads) remain intact, and argues that being wrong on timing is distinct from being wrong on the thesis. He emphasizes rules-based, long-only hedging via treasuries, gold, and the dollar rather than shorting or cash, and previews 2024 as a potential year for small-cap and dividend-stock outperformance.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2023-12-09)

- **Credit event definition**: A VIX spike (typically >30) paired with widening credit spreads; not a catastrophic event but a market repricing of default risk [03:25]
- **2023 thesis evolution**: Called for "melt-up or crash" in January; got melt-up in large-cap tech only; small caps, Russell 2000, and international equities remain in drawdown [02:00–02:41]
- **Treasury credit event already occurred**: Phase one (treasuries) happened; phase two (corporate credit spreads) has not yet materialized, though long-duration yields now signal Fed overtightening [03:46]
- **November call was early, not wrong**: Predicted circuit breakers early November; instead, treasuries rallied ~4% that week; signal was correct (risk-off), timing was off [04:05–04:23]
- **Real drawdown still in place**: NASDAQ and S&P 500 need ~12% more to reach 2021 peaks in real (inflation-adjusted) terms; Dow's nominal new high is misleading [08:12–08:32]
- **Housing market as catalyst**: Mortgage rates at 5–5.5% will trigger selling even without return to 3%; insurance costs and locked-in rates create compounding pressure [09:31–09:52]
- **Credit card delinquencies rising**: Banks reporting significant credit write-offs; lag effect means consumer spending strain is only now beginning to show [15:43–16:04]
- **Retail and small-cap weakness**: Sideways performance in pre-election year despite "strong consumer" narrative; signals domestic economy strain [17:04–17:24]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Lag concept**: Rate hikes take many months to impact consumer habits, delinquencies, and spending; "slowly then all of a sudden" [16:04–16:22]
- **Imperfect hedges outperform perfect ones**: Shorting or cash require perfect timing; treasuries, gold, dollar, utilities benefit from volatility without being inverse to equities [41:24–43:18]
- **Path matters more than prediction**: Being early or wrong on timing is survivable if the hedge is uncorrelated; being short requires directional precision [11:14–12:18]
- **Mean reversion in strategies and sectors**: Lagging asset classes (small caps, dividend stocks, utilities) historically outperform in election years after pre-election large-cap dominance [51:46–53:39]
- **Anomalies are temporary**: The 2022–2023 period (stocks and bonds both down, credit spreads tight) is a multi-generational outlier; historical data supports treasuries as a flight-to-safety hedge [48:01–48:42]
- **Fed is a follower, not a leader**: Bond market owns the Fed; long-duration yields signal overtightening regardless of Powell's rhetoric [19:54–20:13]
- **Volatility and credit spreads move together**: One-for-one relationship; VIX spike without spread widening is incomplete; spread widening without VIX spike is rare [03:25–03:46]

## Notable quotes

> "The credit event risk has not gone away—maybe my timing has been off, but the thesis has not changed. There are lags. We went through the fastest rate cycle in history. At some point that will manifest, probably in an ugly way. But path matters more than prediction." [04:46–05:26]

> "I don't believe in shorting, I don't believe in going into cash, I don't believe in buying puts. The risk-off expression is still to be exposed to something on the long-only side: gold, treasuries, the dollar, or utilities." [11:14–11:37]

> "The bond market owns the Fed. The fact that the long-duration side is acting like this—yes, it was oversold—but it does seem like the long-duration side is saying the Fed definitely overtightened." [19:54–20:13]

## Regime / context

**Date:** December 9, 2023. Recorded during a period of large-cap tech dominance (Magnificent 7) and small-cap underperformance in a pre-election year. Treasury yields have recently rallied after a multi-year bear market; credit spreads remain tight despite Fed rate-hike cycle completion. Gayed's funds (AX, RORO, JOJO) launched into a period unfavorable to his risk-off thesis but are positioned for mean reversion if a credit event occurs.

**Caveats:** Gayed explicitly acknowledges being early on the November capitulation call and wrong on the timing of a corporate credit event. His thesis relies on lagged effects of the fastest rate-hike cycle in history, which are inherently uncertain and have not yet manifested in equities. The 2022–2023 period (simultaneous stock and bond losses with tight spreads) is a statistical outlier that breaks historical hedging relationships; future mean reversion is not guaranteed.
