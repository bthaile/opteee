# Building a Trade Step by Step | Outlier Options Trading Bootcamp

## Summary

This bootcamp session walks through the systematic process of building an options trade from portfolio assessment through execution. The core framework starts with portfolio-level decisions (market hypothesis, capital allocation), moves to opportunity identification (need-driven vs. opportunistic scanning), and culminates in specific trade construction (thesis definition, duration/delta selection, and management rules). The emphasis is on treating trade-building as a repeatable process rather than ad-hoc decision-making.

## Key takeaways

### Portfolio-level decisions
- **Start with portfolio needs, not ideas** [13:54]. Every trade decision must be contextualized against your current holdings and market outlook. Ask: "What does my portfolio need?"
- **Develop a market hypothesis first** [15:14]. Before scanning for trades, form a view on market direction, volatility regime, sector rotation, and geopolitical risk. This backdrop informs all downstream decisions.
- **Reconcile portfolio alignment** [18:01]. Compare your current holdings against your market hypothesis. If bearish but fully long, you have work to do before adding new positions.
- **Three portfolio outcomes** [18:50]: (1) Do nothing—portfolio already aligned; (2) Opportunistic—capital available, no specific need; (3) Need-driven—portfolio requires rebalancing (delta, expiration, concentration).

### Scanning and opportunity identification
- **Two scanning lenses** [27:48]. Contextual scans (indices, vol, commodities, sector breadth) build market intuition; strategy-based scans filter for specific profit mechanisms aligned to your needs.
- **Need-driven vs. opportunistic** [21:21]. Need-driven: filter strategies that solve your portfolio gap (e.g., "I need short deltas"). Opportunistic: look for dislocations off normal across your watchlist.
- **Build a core watchlist** [27:48]. Maintain a standing list of indices, vol, and commodities to quickly assess market context and identify anomalies.

### Trade thesis and structure
- **Define your thesis before picking strikes** [35:01]. Thesis = what you think will happen, over what timeframe, and how fast. Include directional targets, catalyst timing, and profit-taking phases.
- **Time-bound thesis is critical** [36:45]. Even non-options traders face opportunity cost. Avoid parking capital indefinitely; frame your thesis with a duration expectation.
- **Duration selection balances thesis confidence and margin for error** [50:42]. Shorter expirations require precise timing; longer expirations give room for the move to develop but cost more in extrinsic value. Match duration to how confident you are in *when* the move happens.
- **Volatility influences structure** [51:27]. High vol makes long options expensive; consider synthetic longs or spreads. Low vol may justify paying up for directional exposure.
- **Delta, not strike price, is the decision** [53:30]. Pick delta based on (1) how much you think the underlying will move, (2) how long you'll hold, and (3) acceptable loss if wrong. Avoid the trap of buying the strike you think it will hit.

### Theta and extrinsic value trade-offs
- **Theta decay accelerates near expiration** [46:00]. An 8 April 670 call bought for $975 loses ~$600 by Monday (3 days later) if nothing happens. You need the move *fast*.
- **Longer-dated options preserve extrinsic value** [48:26]. A 42-day 673 call loses only ~$40 on the same $1,000 investment if the market doesn't move, but caps upside if the move does happen.
- **Extrinsic value scales with time and moneyness** [49:46]. Understand the total extrinsic value in your option; it determines how much theta you'll bleed and how many contracts you can afford.

### Decision framework for building a position
1. **Security preference** [34:18]. Do you need options, equity, or futures? Some profit mechanisms (e.g., short vol through earnings) force your hand; others allow flexibility.
2. **Thesis clarity** [35:01]. Directional targets, duration, catalyst, and counterargument.
3. **Options market validation** [41:40]. Is liquidity sufficient? Is vol reasonable relative to your thesis?
4. **Duration selection** [50:42]. Match to thesis confidence and margin-for-error tolerance.
5. **Volatility assessment** [51:27]. Cheap or expensive relative to your outlook? Adjust structure accordingly.
6. **Delta selection** [53:30]. Balance expected move size, holding period, and acceptable loss.
7. **Management rules** [59:25]. Profit targets, stop-loss, thesis invalidation, duration management (from prior session).

### Process over outcome
- **Treat it as a repeatable process** [01:03:30]. Don't optimize for one trade; optimize for consistent decision-making across many trades. Habitual data points (vol opinion, duration logic, delta rationale) matter more than individual outcomes.
- **Acknowledge vol in every options trade** [01:03:56]. Vol is embedded in option prices. Overpaying for vol you don't expect to realize is a common mistake; always have an opinion on vol.
