# $234K GME Call Options

## Summary

A deep dive into an unusual $234,000 block trade in [[gme]] 30-strike calls (19 July 2024 expiration) that printed with inverted bid-ask spreads—a rare arbitrage anomaly. The video explores how different data platforms classify the same trade differently (buy vs. sell) and demonstrates the importance of critical thinking when interpreting order-flow signals from imperfect data sources.

## Key takeaways

- **Bid-ask inversion as arbitrage signal** [01:34–02:33]: The trade printed at $50 when the market was $50 bid / $49 offered—a reversal that normally represents risk-free arbitrage. This inversion is extremely rare because algorithms detect and price it out instantly.
- **Platform classification inconsistency** [02:59–04:38]: Three different data sources (trading platform, Unusual Whales, BarChart) classified the same trade differently—one as a sell (red), one as a buy (green)—because they use different logic (bid-side vs. offer-side thresholds).
- **Size matching as a clue** [05:35–06:46]: The bid-side size (4,688 contracts) matched the trade size exactly, which suggests the trade may have been a sale against the bid, despite the inverted pricing.
- **Data source skepticism** [08:39–09:22]: Even when platforms color-code trades, traders must independently verify logic and cross-reference multiple sources rather than accepting a single platform's classification as definitive.
- **Instinct vs. evidence** [09:42–10:07]: The host's initial instinct (buy, based on volume context) conflicts with the size-matching evidence (sell), illustrating the ambiguity in real-world order-flow analysis without access to full trade details.

## Notable quotes

> "You very rarely will see bids higher than the offers—that is inherently arbitrage."

> "You got to be careful with data sources because they're processing large vats of data that's not perfect. You still have to be a thinking trader out here."
