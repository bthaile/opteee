# GME Coverage Strangle Portfolio Allocation & MOASS Exit Strategy Deep Dive

## Summary

Eric launches a recurring portfolio tracking series using a [[covered-strangle]] allocation on GameStop to demonstrate [[short-premium]] mechanics while maintaining a long equity base. The stream focuses on determining an appropriate allocation size ($10–25K range) and critiques common MOASS exit frameworks, emphasizing [[scaling-out]] at multiple price levels and [[profit-taking]] during moves rather than waiting for extreme price targets. A detailed spreadsheet walkthrough shows how opportunistic selling during upswings can increase position size by 16% without additional capital.

## Key takeaways

- **Covered strangle allocation launch** [00:00–05:09]: Eric announces a recurring video series tracking a [[covered-strangle]] strategy on GameStop, designed to teach [[short-premium]] mechanics while keeping the base position long. The goal is to find a relatable allocation size ($10–25K) that viewers can visualize in their own portfolios.

- **Allocation sizing rationale** [02:36–09:00]: After polling the community, Eric settles on a $10–25K range, noting that $5K is too small to demonstrate meaningful strategy mechanics. The allocation must be large enough to show rolling short calls and position management without becoming a "small stacks" exercise.

- **Broker sell-order limitations** [20:45–29:14]: Review of a DD document listing broker-specific sell-order caps (e.g., TD Ameritrade ~$9,999 max price, Fidelity no price cap but 500% limit). Eric notes that during a squeeze, resting limit orders are unlikely to fill; active monitoring and market orders are necessary.

- **MOASS exit strategy critique** [31:00–37:08]: Eric challenges the "don't sell until $100K" narrative, arguing it sets unrealistic baselines and causes holders to miss significant gains. He emphasizes that prior squeezes were halted by market intervention; waiting for extreme targets risks missing the actual move.

- **Scaling-out framework** [37:08–44:26]: Detailed spreadsheet example: a 1,000-share position at $20 basis scales out at $40 (10%), $80 (20%), $120 (30%), $150 (10%), leaving 30% for residual moves. This approach locks in gains while maintaining upside exposure.

- **Profit realization via opportunistic selling** [44:26–54:20]: Eric demonstrates how selling 50% of profit at $28.50 (a modest 42% move) and redeploying at a lower price can increase position size from 1,304 to 1,512 shares (16% gain) without adding capital. This illustrates the power of [[position-sizing]] discipline.

- **Sell-on-the-way-down risk** [59:49–01:02:20]: Analysis of historical GameStop moves shows that waiting to sell on the downside (e.g., $49 → $40 next day) costs ~$9/share. Scaling out on the way up at predetermined levels prevents this regret and locks in gains before reversals.

- **Marginal utility and wealth mindset** [01:11:01–01:21:44]: Eric shares a personal "money mindset marginal utility exercise" to help identify what spending genuinely adds value to life. He argues against judging others' spending and emphasizes that wealthy individuals spend freely on things they truly value, not on superficial status symbols.

- **Options liquidity during squeeze** [01:32:39–01:34:21]: During a major squeeze, options liquidity evaporates; even though leverage amplifies gains, fills may be suboptimal. Exercise timing (end-of-day only on some brokers) adds friction. Eric prefers options for smaller cycle moves, not MOASS plays.

- **Community poll results** [06:14–16:14]: Poll shows even split between $10K and $15K allocations, with 50% voting for $25K. Eric uses this feedback to finalize the allocation size for the recurring series.
