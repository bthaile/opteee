# 14 Trading Mistakes You NEED to Avoid

## Summary

Eric walks through 14 common trading mistakes accumulated over 16 years of trading experience, spanning foundational errors (skill-gap underestimation, lack of structured learning) through behavioral pitfalls (disposition effect, overconfidence after short success windows) to market-mechanics misunderstandings (confusing methodology with profit mechanism, bargain-hunting in equities). The video emphasizes that sustainable trading success requires acknowledging market complexity, maintaining process discipline, and avoiding narrative over-attachment.

## Key takeaways

### Foundational mistakes

- **Skill-gap illusion** [01:09]: Trading has deceptively low barrier to entry; social media creates false perception that success is easy. Acknowledge the mountain you're climbing—it takes time to develop competence.
- **Lack of structured learning path** [02:14]: Most traders approach learning haphazardly without syllabus, homework, or testing. Recommend *Options as a Strategic Investor* (Lawrence McMillan) as foundational structure.
- **No trading plan or trading log** [03:58]: Markets are multivariate with innumerable inputs. Without written documentation of ideas and decisions, you'll be scattered. Treat it like school notes.
- **Confusing methodology with profit mechanism** [04:17]: Day trading, options trading, equities—these are *methods*, not money-makers. Real profit mechanisms: directional trading, volatility trading, dividend capture, long-term appreciation. Decouple the force from the vehicle.

### Account and structural constraints

- **Undercapitalized account** [05:29]: Small accounts ($3k–$33k) force you into suboptimal structures (verticals, naked-option restrictions, day-trader flag). Understand this is a constraint, not a strategy. Most experienced traders move away from verticals as soon as possible.

### Temporal and sample-size errors

- **Overconfidence after short success window** [06:56]: 13 months of trading is infancy. If you started after 2009, you didn't see a bear market for 11 years—a critical blind spot. Path matters: a strategy returning 39.5% CAGR may include a 79% drawdown en route.
- **Insufficient sample size** [08:06]: Backtesting, forward testing, and live testing are non-negotiable. You cannot accept small samples; you need exposure to multiple market regimes.

### Behavioral and psychological pitfalls

- **Dunning-Kruger effect** [09:06]: Confidence peaks after ~5 years of trading (especially in bull markets), before you've seen a bear market or major drawdown. This is where largest losses often occur. Sustainable growth happens when you recognize how much you don't know.
- **Disposition effect** [12:50]: Cutting winners too short and letting losers run. Study: "Behavior of Individual Investors"—this is what unsuccessful traders do; reverse it.
- **Naive reinforcement** [13:08]: Focusing on good experiences and blocking out bad ones to protect ego. Face the reality of losses.
- **Avoiding responsibility (YOLO/lotto mindset)** [11:39]: If you put a trade on, you expected to win. Losing means you messed up somewhere. Own every trade.

### Market-mechanics and narrative errors

- **Bargain-hunting in equities** [14:35]: Consumer mindset (buy on sale) doesn't translate to markets. Nvidia example: waiting for a pullback because it's "too high" means missing the move, and when it does pull back, there may be a reason (deteriorating fundamentals).
- **Over-reliance on new tools** [15:31]: Options flow, dark pools, algo trading—retail sees these decades after institutions. They're not edge-generators; you still have to build your own edge.
- **Avoiding simple explanations (but not too much)** [15:53]: Markets are complex. Yes, earnings can drive moves, but don't over-narrativize. Price is what matters; develop relationships with products, but never get too wrapped up in your own thesis.

### Identity and flexibility

- **Over-identifying as a trader type** [12:25]: Don't lock yourself into "day trader" or "options trader." Be a trader; use whatever methodology makes sense for the setup.
- **Strong convictions, loosely held** [11:16]: Build conviction through research, but remain receptive to new information. Abandon positions when evidence warrants it; avoid entrenchment.

### Return path and risk management

- **Return path vs. top-line return** [13:54]: Portfolio 1 (7.4% avg return, no big drawdowns) outperformed Portfolio 3 (88.6% avg return, 25% drawdown) in absolute dollars ($142k vs. $139k). Smooth returns compound better than volatile ones.

## Notable quotes

- "If you're going to climb Mount Everest, you have to acknowledge the mountain itself. It's the same thing in trading." [01:54]
