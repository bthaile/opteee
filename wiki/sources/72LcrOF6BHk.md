# Options Trading Common Misconceptions | Options Trading for Beginners Pt8

## Summary

Eric walks through 12 common misconceptions about options trading, emphasizing that success requires proper mindset, realistic expectations, and disciplined process rather than shortcuts. The session covers practical trade examples (including a variance-risk-premium strangle on PATH), explains why structure alone doesn't guarantee profit, and stresses that options are tools whose risk depends entirely on how they're deployed. Key theme: trading is hard upfront but becomes manageable once the foundational work is done.

## Key takeaways

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Misconception #1: "Easy passive income" from covered calls or "$500/week selling calls"** [24:59]: Sets traders up with wrong mindset. Reality: 93% of retail traders quit within 5 years; of those who stay, many underperform the market by 6.5%. The path is hard at first, but becomes easy once all work is done *before* entry.

- **Misconception #2: "The market should have done X"** [48:57]: Implies your view supersedes what actually happened. Market did what it did because the collective decided otherwise. Survivorship bias makes us remember Michael Burry's success but ignore the near-insolvency he faced. Relinquish control; adapt to what is, not what should be.

- **Misconception #3: "Options are too risky"** [51:59]: Options take whatever risk you give them. You can structure them conservatively or aggressively; risk is a design choice, not inherent.

- **Misconception #4: "Stock must reach strike for long call/put to profit"** [02:00:44]: False. Long options profit as long as they move in the correct direction. Example: Tesla 190 calls bought at $0.59 traded at $1.12 one day later without hitting strike, because IV and delta expanded.

- **Misconception #5: "Buying is better than selling (or vice versa)"** [02:04:56]: Both have pros and cons. Theta is not an edge; it's a tool. One is not inherently superior.

- **Misconception #6: "Selling further OTM is safer"** [02:05:48]: Gross dollar loss is smaller, but return-on-risk profile differs. When a big move eventually happens, the smaller premium collected can be wiped out by a single adverse move, negating multiple prior wins.

- **Misconception #7: "Rolling avoids losses"** [02:17:03]: Rolling realizes the P&L of the current trade and opens a new one. It doesn't avoid loss; it resets the opportunity.

- **Misconception #8: "Market is out to get you"** [02:17:32]: Market is competitive and seeks easy money, but no one is targeting you personally. Focus on what you control; take responsibility for outcomes.

- **Delta selection is context-dependent** [01:28:37]: Higher delta (e.g., 36 delta) = higher gamma, more path volatility, higher probability of assignment. Lower delta (e.g., 10 delta) = lower gamma, tighter management, lower premium. No fixed answer; depends on product, portfolio fit, and management style.

- **Variance-risk-premium trade example (PATH)** [01:09:19]: Entered short strangle (13 and 11 strikes) at $0.40 average on 2024-06-14, held to expiration (2024-07-19). Trade was based on persistent, elevated IV vs. realized vol, not price direction. Management was simple: set alerts at predefined P&L targets, let it run. Return on risk ~4% per lot; no earnings risk.

- **Capital allocation and box spreads** [42:08]: Unallocated cash deployed into European-style box spreads (e.g., SPX) for yield. Requires careful attention to early-exercise risk (American options are dangerous in box spreads). Adjust expiration and size based on when capital is needed.

- **Small-account strategy** [01:22:01]: For sub-$100k accounts seeking growth without massive drawdowns, ratio diagonals outperform covered strangles on return; covered strangles provide smoother P&L. Small Stacks series showed 26.85% return over 3 months.

- **Profit mechanism vs. structure** [01:33:35]: A structure (vertical, strangle, etc.) is just a container. The profit mechanism (directional move, volatility crush, theta decay, etc.) is what makes money. Confusing the two is a common error.

- **Greedy trading and drawdown math** [01:57:23]: A 25% loss requires a 33.3% gain to recover. One bad year can derail years of compounding. Develop a long-term plan (e.g., $115k at 21 → $1M by 35 with 15% annual return + $1.1k/month savings) and commit to it. Monk mode in your 20s pays off in your 30s.

- **Mechanical vs. discretionary rules** [01:47:27]: Mechanical strategies (e.g., 50% profit-take) need positive expected value baked in. Discretionary strategies (e.g., ratio diagonals) benefit from individualized management. Don't blindly apply one rule to all strategies.

- **Entry timing** [01:43:38]: Typically enter new positions toward end of day to avoid morning volatility rush and illiquidity. Management trades can happen anytime.

- **Reductive bias in options** [01:26:40]: Oversimplifying (e.g., "always use 30 delta") loses nuance. Options' power lies in variability; structure them carefully to match thesis and portfolio needs.
