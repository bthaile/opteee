# The BIG Problem with Vertical Spreads

## Summary

Eric dissects why vertical credit spreads—despite their popularity among retail traders—are structurally unprofitable due to negative expectation, high friction costs, and unfavorable pricing. The strategy wins frequently but in small amounts, making it a "death by a thousand paper cuts" approach that underperforms alternatives for expressing directional or volatility views.

## Key takeaways

- **Risk-defined appeal masks negative expectancy** [03:22]: Vertical spreads cap losses and margin requirements, making them attractive to new traders, but backtesting from 2015–2023 shows net negative P&L even before commissions.

- **Neutral credit threshold vs. actual credit received** [06:39]: A $1-wide put spread at 35 DTE requires ~31¢ credit for zero-sum expectancy; typical market pricing offers only 23¢, guaranteeing a losing strategy over time.

- **Friction costs are devastating** [04:39]: At 65¢ per leg (two legs = $1.30 entry cost), a $23 max profit loses 5.7% to commissions alone, before exit slippage.

- **High win rate ≠ profitability** [08:29]: The strategy wins frequently but in tiny amounts; the cumulative effect of commissions and bid-ask spread erosion outweighs small gains.

- **Inefficient expression of directional thesis** [05:04]: Selling credit spreads when IV is high contradicts the premise (you're buying expensive volatility while claiming to sell it); directional bets are better expressed via covered strangles, ratio call diagonals, or long calls.

- **Alternatives for bullish positioning** [08:50]: Covered strangles, ratio covered calls, ratio call diagonals, or outright share purchases offer better expectancy, though with higher upfront cost and lower capital efficiency.

- **Alternatives for bearish positioning** [09:35]: Ratio put diagonals, long puts, or short straddles/strangles (with far OTM wings to preserve credit) capture volatility more efficiently.

## Notable quotes

> "Death by a thousand paper cuts—you're not going to blow up on any single trade, which is great, but you're not really going to make any money either."

> "There's no free lunch here. Just because vertical credit spreads are accessible to you doesn't mean they're good."
