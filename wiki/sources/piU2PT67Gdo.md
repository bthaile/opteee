# Why You Shouldn't Chase Cheap OTM Options

## Summary

Far out-of-the-money (OTM) options with low delta and short time-to-expiration appear cheap but are dangerous on both the buy and sell side. The implied volatility on these tail strikes is inflated relative to at-the-money options, and the fat-tail distribution of large-cap stocks like Tesla means a single losing trade can erase dozens of winners.

## Key takeaways

- **Relative IV inflation on OTM strikes** [00:49–01:14]: A 335 call on Tesla (0.02 delta) trades at 99.4% IV vs. 77.6% IV on the 280 call. The counter-side demands higher premium because the profit potential is limited while the loss severity is extreme.
- **Buying cheap OTM is a sucker's trade** [00:26–00:49]: A 13–14 cent call looks attractive, but the probability of hitting 335 within 3 days is ~0.2%—a one standard deviation move.
- **Selling cheap OTM is equally dangerous** [01:42–02:06]: For volatile stocks, a single losing trade from the short side can wipe out dozens of winners because fat tails are fatter than normal distribution assumes.
- **Fat-tail risk dominates the math** [02:06–02:32]: Even if you win repeatedly on short OTM premium, the tail event that does occur can reverse all gains. The probability model underestimates the severity of outlier moves.

## Notable quotes

> "On a relative basis these are really expensive to buy… but the issue is one losing trade from the cell side can wipe out not even like a couple winners but we're talking dozens of winners."
