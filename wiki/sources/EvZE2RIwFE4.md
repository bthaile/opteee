# Prop Firm to Options - @optionswithdavis | The Outlier Podcast Ep76

## Summary

Options with Davis shares his journey from retail trader blowup during the 2008 subprime crisis, through four years at a proprietary equity trading firm, to building a diversified multi-strategy options portfolio generating ~20–24% annualized returns. His core philosophy centers on **risk-first position sizing**, **price-level-based entry**, and **portfolio-level regime coverage** rather than single-strategy reliance.

## Key takeaways

### Early career & foundational lessons
- **[01:02–02:19]** Bought trading signal software as a university student; system repainted in live trading, teaching him that backtested performance ≠ real-world edge.
- **[02:38–03:23]** Discovered options via *Get Rich with Options*; opened thinkorswim account and negotiated per-leg commissions directly with a founder.
- **[04:20–05:52]** First major loss: 99%-win-rate OEX strangle during 2008 subprime crisis; panicked exit cost him the account despite position expiring worthless. Learned that overconfidence + tail risk = ruin.

### Proprietary trading firm (4 years)
- **[07:32–08:41]** Joined prop firm to learn professional risk discipline; witnessed senior profitable trader fired for letting a loss exceed daily limit—boss enforced hard stops.
- **[12:04–12:46]** Hit his own loss limit early; proactively reported to boss instead of hiding it; received mentorship and permission to watch a movie rather than punishment—positive reinforcement for hard decisions.
- **[16:00–17:19]** Observed trader hiding a losing stock from risk monitor; fired for dishonesty. Learned: **honesty + risk management = foundation**.
- **[19:11–19:34]** Prop firm used **price-level-based trading** (no technical analysis), market-maker scalping, and queue management—not options.

### Transition to independent options trading
- **[18:03–18:42]** Moved to self-directed trading; built capital gradually; maintained side income to reduce psychological pressure of trading-only income.
- **[20:39–21:29]** Portfolio split: **60%+ growth** (LEAPs, covered calls held for upside) vs. **40% income** (strangles, broken-wing butterflies, ratio spreads).

### The wheel & covered-strangle strategy
- **[22:34–23:56]** Selects **fundamentally sound, large-cap stocks** (survived 2008, pandemic); uses conservative valuation; **staggers short-put entries** below valuation in tranches to avoid all-in assignment.
- **[23:56–24:28]** Waits for stock to recover above valuation before selling covered calls; staggers call exits on the way up to capture capital gains.
- **[24:51–25:29]** Holds underwater positions long-term if thesis unchanged; only exits if company structure changes materially.
- **[26:36–26:57]** Parks unused capital in **fixed-income securities** (T-bills, money-market funds) or **box spreads** to earn excess over risk-free rate (~0.71% above SOFR in current example).

### Portfolio construction & risk management
- **[30:28–31:11]** Income strategies (strangles, broken-wing butterflies): shorter cycle time; exited at 21 DTE or 50% profit.
- **[31:33–32:25]** LEAPs: 1+ year DTE; wheel: 1-week DTE (to increase assignment probability).
- **[38:24–39:05]** Evolved from single-strategy to **multi-regime portfolio**: bullish (positive delta), neutral (strangles), bearish (put butterflies, ratio spreads) to cover all market conditions.
- **[39:27–39:53]** Maintains **30–50 positions** at any time; most are income trades.
- **[43:31–44:34]** **Risk cap: max 50% capital deployed** at any time; exits losing trades at 21 DTE to limit drawdown; relies on tastyworks research showing 21 DTE is inflection point for probability.
- **[44:56–45:44]** Current holdings: Google, Meta, Adobe, Amazon, Palantir; avoids dividend stocks (tax inefficiency outside US); prefers tech for volatility.

### Notable quotes

- **[05:37–05:52]** "I didn't accept the risk at the point of time, which is a very big mistake... if I held to expiration it would have expired worthless, but it could easily work out the other way as well."
- **[13:03–13:22]** "I'm glad you came. I was actually wondering when you would come to me... managing it like this right now will help you in the long term."
