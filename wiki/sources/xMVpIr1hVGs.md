# Cars, Options, Leverage, and Volatility with Euan Sinclair

## Summary

Eric and Euan Sinclair discuss 2022 market conditions, volatility regimes, and the mechanics of options trading for retail traders. Sinclair emphasizes that successful trading requires identifying predictable profit drivers (volatility, not direction), proper P&L attribution across Greeks, and realistic position sizing given liquidity constraints. The conversation covers hedging philosophy, the role of carry costs, and practical advice for traders with small accounts.

## Key takeaways

### Market regime and 2022 reflection
- **Process over market regime** [13:08]: Don't change your process based on whether markets are up or down—you can't predict that in advance. Trade what's predictable: volatility, not direction.
- **Elevated VIX with low volatility-of-volatility** [15:25]: VIX stayed elevated in 2022, but the volatility of VIX itself was unusually low—a regime shift worth monitoring in your process.
- **Bonds and equities correlation is regime-dependent** [17:46]: The correlation between stocks and bonds flips based on whether the market fears inflation or recession; avoid macro-based hedging strategies.

### Options mechanics and Greeks
- **P&L attribution is critical** [19:38]: Separate your delta P&L from volatility P&L. Many traders don't realize their profits come from delta, not vol—if so, trade the underlying instead.
- **Skew and variance risk premium** [25:41]: The 15–20 delta range typically offers the best risk-reward for selling options in indices; skew is a distinct risk factor from variance, not just a volatility premium.
- **Interest rates and carry costs** [33:56]: For most retail traders, interest rates are negligible; borrow rates (60%+ on biotech) matter far more. Theta decay overwhelms rho.

### Liquidity and position construction
- **Liquidity is the binding constraint** [42:05]: Only ~800 products trade >1,000 options/day; most have <2,000/day. This forces professionals toward front-month options and holding to expiration.
- **Expiration risk is manageable** [46:36]: Pin risk and assignment happen, but liquidity improves near expiration; Sinclair holds ~80% of positions to expiration due to liquidity constraints.
- **Hedging should match the underlying** [30:20]: Hedge stocks with stock options or futures, not cross-products (e.g., don't hedge VXX with VIX options). Correlations break in crises.

### Volatility trading and regime shifts
- **Vol can stay low for years** [32:28]: Just because VIX is at 12 doesn't mean it's cheap; it can stay there for 3+ years. Timing is critical; carry costs will drain you.
- **Volatility of volatility matters** [16:15]: High VIX + low vol-of-vol is a different regime than high VIX + high vol-of-vol. Adjust your process accordingly.

### Advice for small-account traders (2–5 years in)
- **Avoid day trading; focus on pairs trades** [55:40]: Transaction costs kill day traders. Instead, identify small mispricings (e.g., Coke vs. Pepsi spreads) that take 2–3 weeks to resolve.
- **Use Delta-one for pairs; avoid over-leveraging** [48:17]: Buy 70-delta calls, not 20-delta, if you must use options for leverage. Don't over-leverage just because you can.
- **Find identifiable, repeatable edges** [54:00]: Stop betting on macro (Ukraine → potato futures). Look for structural mispricings you can explain and trade mathematically.
- **Read Pairs Trading by Ganapathy Vimuthy** [57:34]: Foundational for understanding statistical arbitrage and position construction.

## Regime / context

**Date:** January 2023. Sinclair reflects on 2022 as the third down year in the S&P 500 since Eric began trading in 2007. VIX remained elevated but with unusually low volatility-of-volatility—a regime shift worth monitoring.

**Liquidity regime:** Only ~800 products trade >1,000 options/day; this structural constraint forces professionals to trade front-month options and hold to expiration, limiting the theoretical ideal of 3–6 month positions with gamma reduction.

**Retail trader context:** Discussion addresses traders 2–5 years into their tenure, many of whom started in 2020 during stimulus and are now unprofitable. Sinclair advises focusing on pairs trading and identifiable mispricings rather than directional day trading or macro bets.
