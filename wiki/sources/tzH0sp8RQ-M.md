# An Inside Look at How SIG Trains Traders | The Options Trench

## Summary

Eric interviews a former Susquehanna International Group (SIG) trader about the firm's systematic approach to training traders over a 4–5 decade pipeline. The discussion covers the progression from clerk to full trader—including on-the-job training, evening classes, a two-week boot camp in Pennsylvania, and 10 weeks of intensive classroom and mock-trading—and emphasizes decision-making, expected value, and mental discipline as the core skills that transcend any single market or product.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2026-06-20)
- SIG's reputation has shifted dramatically: in 2000, market-making was not a status career; today it is widely known as a lucrative path, attracting candidates who explicitly cite "providing liquidity" as a childhood dream [02:26].

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Hiring criteria are not finance-specific** [08:22]: SIG recruited from top colleges looking for facility with numbers, competitiveness, and curiosity—not prior investing knowledge. The first formal class covered "What is an interest rate?" [09:08].

- **Immersion in language and mechanics** [13:26]: New clerks spend the first month learning the absolute basics—bid/offer, calls/puts, vertical spreads, exchange rules—described as "immersion in a new language." Fluency typically takes a couple of months [14:05].

- **Dual-track learning: OJT + evening classes** [15:14]: Clerks worked on the trading floor (e.g., SPY market-making on the American Stock Exchange) during the day, then attended 2–3 hours of formal trading classes each evening. This separation allowed learning concepts (options, synthetics) not directly encountered in their day job [16:35].

- **Cohort-based progression and competitive gates** [15:54]: New hires were organized into cohorts. After ~3 weeks, all cohorts traveled to SIG's Pennsylvania headquarters for a two-week intensive classroom boot camp covering pricing models, Black-Scholes, binomial trees, and synthetic relationships [17:40].

- **Mock trading as deliberate practice** [18:23]: Back in the office, instructors ran whiteboard-based open-outcry simulations with multiple simultaneous orders (call spreads, put spreads, straddles, synthetics). Trainees had to spot arbitrage, calculate synthetic relationships, and execute hedges. Instructors would "debug" every decision, similar to a poker coach [31:36].

- **Speed drills on mental math** [21:36]: During slow market hours, trainees raced through put-call parity problems (15 questions in ~15–25 seconds) to build the mental speed needed to compete in mock trading.

- **Competitive selection for boot camp** [22:26]: Only 4–5 trainees per cohort of ~20 were accepted to the formal 10-week boot camp each quarter. Acceptance came with a raise; completion brought another raise and a trading license ("badge"). Fastest trainees qualified in <6 months; median was ~12 months [23:26].

- **Boot camp structure: 4 weeks classroom + 6 weeks mock trading** [26:04]: The first four weeks covered model-building (convertible bonds, binomial trees, index pricing) and Black-Scholes derivation (8 hours over two days). Trainees also logged 100+ hours of limit seven-card-stud poker, with the highest Sharpe ratio winner earning prestige within the firm [27:13].

- **Poker as a training tool** [26:51]: Poker was mandatory during boot camp—played during breaks, after hours (until midnight), and even with SIG partners. It trained decision-making under uncertainty and was tracked via Sharpe ratio, not just win rate [27:55].

- **Mock trading with live simulation** [30:58]: The second six weeks used a full internal exchange simulation with laptops/tablets, live bid-ask screens, and stock/option order routing. Instructors continuously questioned every decision [31:36].

- **Guest lectures from business leaders** [32:12]: Each afternoon of the final six weeks, heads of SIG business units (risk, NASDAQ desk, special situations) and founder Jeff Yaz spoke about decision-making, strategy, and independent thinking (e.g., Yaz discussed NFL coaches making mathematically suboptimal decisions) [32:32].

- **Expected value as the foundation** [37:20]: The core interview question—"A fair die pays $1–$6; how much would you pay to play?"—tests whether candidates understand that trading is about consistent positive-expectation decisions, not individual outcomes. The answer is any amount <$3.50 [36:56].

- **Option pricing via decision trees** [39:46]: A follow-up question—"What if you can re-roll once?"—introduces the concept of pricing optionality (the re-roll is worth ~$0.75, making the game worth $4.25 instead of $3.50) without using the word "option" [40:36].

- **Backgammon doubling cube as a risk-decision framework** [47:07]: A third interview question asks: "If you're in a game for $10 and your opponent offers to double to $20 or you forfeit the $10, what minimum win probability justifies accepting?" This mirrors real trading decisions under uncertainty [48:04].

- **Applicability to retail traders** [25:21]: Although modern retail traders won't trade on a physical pit floor, the principles—language immersion, deliberate practice, competitive drills, decision-tree thinking, and expected-value reasoning—are directly transferable via podcasts, AI tutoring (e.g., ChatGPT), and mock-trading platforms.

- **Culture and lineage matter** [01:36]: SIG's 26-year pipeline (as of the interview) produces consistent traders because the firm treats training as a multi-phase, competitive, and culturally embedded process. Partners participate in training; winners are celebrated; the bar is high but not arbitrary.

## Regime / context

This is a retrospective account of SIG's training pipeline as it existed in 2000–2008 (when the guest worked there). The firm's hiring and training practices have evolved significantly since then; the guest notes that in 2026, candidates are expected to arrive with much more pre-existing knowledge of trading and finance, making the interview process more competitive and less forgiving of gaps [44:28]. The core principles—expected value, decision-tree thinking, deliberate practice, and competitive selection—remain evergreen and applicable to modern retail traders, though the delivery mechanism (physical trading floor, open-outcry pits, mandatory poker) has been replaced by electronic platforms and simulation software.
