# Ignoring Delta In Short Puts Is Killing Your Gains

## Summary

Short puts are often treated as neutral-to-bullish strategies, but ignoring delta exposure leaves significant performance on the table. When the underlying trends upward, a short put seller with low delta exposure misses substantial gains per dollar of underlying movement—a critical blind spot in position management.

## Key takeaways

- **Delta as directional exposure** [00:28]: A 30-delta short put means you capture only 30 cents of profit for every dollar the underlying rises; you miss 70 cents per dollar of upside.
- **Sideways movement is slow money** [00:00]: If the underlying trends sideways, short put P&L decays slowly via theta alone—not the ideal scenario.
- **Ideal scenario is upward drift** [00:00]: The best outcome for a short put is for the underlying to trickle upward, but most traders fail to optimize for this.
- **Delta management is underemphasized** [00:00]: Underlying movement and delta exposure are rarely discussed in short-put management, despite being critical to performance.
