# Earnings Beats Don't Always Mean Stock Goes Up: Nvidia, Chewy, GameStop Analysis

## Summary

A deep analysis of three earnings releases—Nvidia, Chewy, and GameStop—demonstrating that beating earnings expectations does not guarantee a stock price increase. The video explores the structural incentives that cause analyst estimates to systematically underestimate earnings, and how market pricing already incorporates expected growth trajectories, requiring "near-perfect" execution from mega-cap stocks to avoid disappointment.

## Key takeaways

- **Nvidia earnings paradox** [00:00–07:10]: Nvidia beat EPS ($0.68 vs. $0.59 expected), crushed year-over-year and quarter-over-quarter metrics, yet closed down ~$10 (~8%) to $125.61. The market had priced in an unsustainable growth trajectory; even strong results trigger consolidation when they fail to exceed the parabolic expectations already baked in.

- **Analyst incentive misalignment** [02:18–04:40]: Over the past 10 years, S&P 500 earnings overwhelmingly beat expectations—not because forecasting is impossible, but because analysts are structurally incentivized to underestimate (conflicts of interest, herding behavior, no penalty for inaccuracy). Accuracy is punished; groupthink is rewarded.

- **Chewy's massive beat, muted move** [08:18–10:44]: Chewy beat by ~20 cents (expected 3¢, delivered 24¢)—a larger absolute beat than a prior quarter's 31¢ beat—yet only rallied 11%. Of that 11% volume, 43% was sell-side, indicating profit-taking despite the huge surprise. Demonstrates that beat magnitude alone does not drive proportional price moves.

- **GameStop technical breakdown** [12:53–14:00]: Broke below key support; volume at 5.1M (slightly below 20-day average of 5.3M), 98% sell volume. Expected drift toward $18 support unless a catalyst emerges. Meme stocks can pivot rapidly on news; prior 9→65 move in ~1 month shows asymmetric upside potential.

- **Forward guidance > beat magnitude** [04:40–05:45]: What matters most in earnings is forward guidance (new information about future cash flows), not whether the company beat by 1¢ or 20¢. Investors discount future value; past-quarter beats are already known.

## Notable quotes

> "A lot of retail traders mistakenly believe that if a company beats earnings, it should go up. And that's not at all the case."
