# Futures Trading Explained for Beginners!

## Summary

This video introduces futures contracts as a leveraged derivative instrument with superior liquidity and market access compared to options. Eric demonstrates practical use cases—including a real trade on the Iran news event (June 22)—and walks through contract mechanics: tick sizes, notional values, margin requirements, settlement types, and the simplified delta-1 nature of outright futures. The core thesis is that futures are best used as a portfolio add-on for hedging, overnight/weekend moves, and direct commodity access rather than as a primary vehicle for small accounts.

## Key takeaways

- **Why futures exist:** Originally designed to stabilize agricultural prices; today used by commercials (airlines, farmers) to hedge operational risk via the Commitment of Traders report [02:39–03:43].
- **Real-world edge: Sunday/overnight access.** Futures trade 23 hours/day (closed 1 hour daily; Sunday 6 PM ET to Friday afternoon for indices). On June 22 (Sunday), news of military action was tradable in ES when stock market was closed; Eric shorted ES at ~5959.88, covered at ~5959.65 for ~$1,150 profit per contract in under 60 seconds [00:00–02:39].
- **Leverage is massive but dangerous.** E-mini S&P 500 (ES) requires ~$21,537 maintenance margin to control $312,500 notional value = 14.5× leverage. This is not suitable for small accounts building bad habits; better as a portfolio complement [06:11–07:20].
- **Simplicity vs. options.** Futures are delta-1 instruments (no gamma, theta, vega complications). You trade direction only (long/short), similar to stock, but with leverage and tax benefits (Section 1256: 60/40 long/short capital gains) [07:20–08:27].
- **Contract anatomy:** Underlying, contract value, tick size & value, expiration (coded by letter: U=Sept, Z=Dec, H=March), settlement type (cash or physical), margin requirements, carry cost [08:27–09:38].
- **Tick size confusion.** ES moves in 0.25 increments; each 0.25 tick = $12.50 P&L. One full point (dollar equivalent) = $50. Micro ES (MES) is 1/10th the notional size, making it more palatable for smaller accounts [11:55–14:31].
- **Contract multiplier math.** SPY up $1 = $1 P&L. ES up 1 point = $50 P&L. MES up 1 point = $5 P&L. Screenshot the cheat sheet for quick reference [13:09–14:31].
- **Volume concentrates in front contract.** All liquidity is in the nearest expiration; volume shifts to next contract within days of expiration. Roll yield is a separate trade [17:08].
- **Seasonality matters for commodities.** Corn, soybeans, wheat, cattle, hogs have production-cycle seasonalities; equities and indices do not [18:18].
- **Practical use cases:** Hedge portfolio overnight/weekend moves; offset earnings volatility in correlated indices (e.g., short ES to hedge long NVDA earnings risk); gain direct access to commodities (gold, oil, natural gas) without ETF drag [18:18–19:28].
