# Momentum Masterclass with Wes Grey | The Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Wes Grey discusses the mechanics and robustness of momentum as a trading factor, emphasizing that pure momentum strategies work best when kept simple and uncorrupted by value or quality overlays. He explores lookback windows, the relationship between momentum and volatility, the challenges of applying machine learning to lower-frequency momentum systems, and why momentum persists as an edge despite decades of academic scrutiny—largely because it requires long holding periods and discipline that most institutional capital cannot sustain.

## Key takeaways

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Momentum purity** [01:01–02:21]: Adding valuation or quality filters to momentum systems increases correlation with fundamental investing and destroys the diversification benefit. Pure momentum is "animal spirits"—it doesn't care about fundamentals, only price action.

- **Lookback window hierarchy** [14:42–15:42]: Short lookback windows (1–4 weeks) lead to reversal; intermediate windows (3–18 months) produce continuation; long-term windows (5+ years) revert again. This architecture has remained robust across decades of research.

- **Volatility as a momentum dampener** [16:52–17:56]: High volatility environments (e.g., Trump tariff reversals, crypto swings) are hostile to momentum continuation strategies. Reducing capital deployment during elevated VIX or realized volatility improves risk-adjusted returns.

- **Trailing stops and trend filters** [05:54–07:03]: Momentum can be improved by adding momentum-derived signals like trailing stops (exit if a position drops 10% before rebalance) or market trend filters (don't trade momentum if overall market trend is weak). These don't corrupt the factor because they're price-action-based.

- **Lookback window robustness** [14:42]: No new research has negated the basic architecture of short/intermediate/long lookback windows. The effect persists across time periods and geographies.

- **Machine learning hasn't beaten simple momentum** [26:58–29:07]: A PhD machine-learning researcher tested complex neural networks and AI systems against a brain-dead 12-month momentum sort-and-hold strategy using full data access. The simple strategy matched or beat the complex systems. This suggests that in lower-frequency (monthly rebalance) domains, complexity doesn't add value.

- **Why momentum persists despite arbitrage** [31:36–35:02]: Momentum and value require long holding periods and discipline; most institutional capital (hedge funds, prop shops) cannot sustain them because they need quick, hedgeable trades to justify their cost of capital. Warren Buffett-style arbitrage is rare; most smart money chases easier, faster edges. This capital scarcity preserves momentum as an evergreen factor.

- **Post-earnings drift vs. momentum divergence** [30:14–31:36]: Post-earnings drift has waned in large caps as frictional costs dropped and execution became easier. Momentum persists because it requires longer horizons and higher idiosyncratic risk—capital cannot easily arbitrage it away.

- **Retail edge in momentum** [17:56–21:09]: Retail traders have minimal structural advantage in momentum (unlike value, where small-cap illiquidity favors small players). Liquid large-cap names have momentum as good as small caps. However, retail can execute faster than ETFs (no compliance delays) and compete against retail YOLO traders, though they face competition from nimble hedge funds and prop traders.

- **Data quality limits options-momentum research** [23:28–25:44]: Options data is poor quality and expensive (Bloomberg charges millions for clean feeds). This is why there's little academic research on momentum applied to derivatives—the data infrastructure doesn't exist at accessible cost.

- **Simplicity and peer review as guardrails** [09:17–11:21]: Preferring strategies vetted by thousands of peer-reviewed papers over novel ideas reduces the risk of overfitting and data mining. Academics have different incentives than product vendors—they want to publish robust findings, not sell a system.

- **Communication and complexity trade-off** [12:25–13:28]: In intermediated asset management, overly complex strategies fail when they underperform because clients don't understand them and withdraw capital at the worst time. Simplicity and transparency are necessary to retain discipline through drawdowns.

- **Noise from retail and ETF rebalancing** [15:42–16:52]: Increased retail participation and ETF rebalancing add noise to markets, potentially making momentum lookback windows noisier. However, the basic architecture (short reversal, intermediate continuation, long reversal) remains intact.

## Notable quotes

> "Momentum fundamentally has to be about buying what's winning, you know, in like a investing sense."

> "The minute people start getting too cute with momentum, they're like, 'All right, well, what if we do momentum, but let's start considering valuations or quality or whatever.' Well, guess what you're doing now? You're making it look more and more like fundamental investing like kind of the old methods, which is fine, but it's becoming more and more correlated, and at some point, you might as well just buy the market again, right?"

> "If you solve that [options-momentum data problem], you'd be a gazillionaire because you would sell it to literally every hedge fund, every, you know, prop trader, every academic on the planet."
