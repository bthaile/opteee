# The Ultimate Guide for Using Stops in Options Trading

## Summary

Eric walks through a systematic framework for setting stops in options trading, emphasizing that stops must be strategy-specific rather than arbitrary. The core principle: define where you're wrong, quantify the risk at that point, and ensure your stop placement aligns with your system's long-term expected return. He demonstrates the process using a live breakout trade example with specific position-sizing calculations.

## Key takeaways

- **Mental vs. resting stops** [01:02–02:03]: Mental stops are predetermined decision points; resting stops are market orders. Avoid stop-limit orders because they may fail to execute when you need them most, defeating the purpose of risk management.

- **Two-element stop equation** [02:56–03:58]: (1) Define where you're wrong based on the strategy (e.g., below a consolidation level for breakouts); (2) Confirm the dollar risk at that point is satisfactory relative to your system's average loss.

- **Strategy-level framework** [05:00–07:12]: Build a profit mechanism first, identify signals that confirm you're wrong, track win rate and loss rate, then ensure stops guarantee positive long-term expected return. This prevents random stop placement.

- **Live trade example: BIL breakout** [08:20–11:54]: Entered 21 Feb 85 calls at ~$1,150; defined wrong point at 83.18 (below consolidation). Sized down from 25 to 17 lots so the loss at stop ($5,185) matched the system's average loss ($5,140). Profit target eyeballed at 92.

- **Required win rate calculation** [11:54]: Divide average loss by (average win + average loss) to find the minimum win rate needed. In the example: $5,181 ÷ ($4,050 + $5,181) = 55.6% required; system's 60% win rate exceeds this, validating the trade structure.

- **Profit-taking and trailing stops** [13:04]: Once in profit, move stops up to new consolidation lows rather than taking the full position off. This is strategy-specific and cannot be copied blindly across different approaches.

- **Portfolio vs. strategy-level risk** [01:02]: General guidelines (e.g., "don't risk >5% per trade") are useful for portfolio management but must be overridden by strategy-specific risk calculations at the individual trade level.
