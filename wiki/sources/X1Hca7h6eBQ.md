# GameStop Dark Pool Activity & Options Positioning Analysis

## Summary

Eric analyzes recent GameStop dark pool transaction patterns and options positioning, noting that 5,000-share block trades are not necessarily attributable to a single entity despite retail speculation. The options market shows a shift toward longer-dated 2025 expirations with lighter front-month positioning, while implied volatility percentile has compressed to 38%, signaling a quiet period ahead of the next catalyst.

## Key takeaways

- **Dark pool 5,000-share blocks are not a reliable signal** [00:00–04:33]: The 5,000-share block trades visible in dark pool feeds began appearing after mid-May movement, correlating with price action rather than indicating a specific trader's "calling card." Eric distinguishes between 5,000-lot *options* trades (which represent larger notional exposure due to liquidity constraints) and 5,000-share *stock* trades (which are relatively small and a common round number).

- **Options positioning has shifted to longer-dated expirations** [05:47–06:50]: Open interest shows 630,000 calls vs. sub-300,000 puts (notable call skew), but the distribution has changed: 2025 expirations (Jan 17, Oct 18) now carry significant open interest, while front-month positioning is lighter than historical norms (167,000 of 630,000 calls, ~26%).

- **Call selling dominance on sideways price action** [06:50–08:08]: 40% of the 101,000 calls traded today hit the bid or below, indicating sellers. GameStop has trended sideways since early August with no significant directional conviction post-earnings.

- **Volume and volatility compression signal a quiet period** [08:08–09:19]: Post-earnings volume declined from 28.9M to 5.9M shares daily. Implied volatility percentile stands at 38% (low for GME despite raw IV of ~90%), suggesting reduced near-term event risk and waiting for the next catalyst (Roaring Kitty news or GameStop leadership announcements).

- **Retail options activity remains concentrated in out-of-the-money strikes** [09:19–10:19]: Smaller position sizes across the board; continued preference for far OTM calls (1-delta to 3-delta) despite Eric's view that smarter entry points exist for retail traders.
