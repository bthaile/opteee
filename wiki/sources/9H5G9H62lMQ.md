# Are Options Zero Sum?

## Summary

Individual option contracts are mathematically zero-sum—one side's gain is the other's loss—but options *trading as a practice* is not. The critical distinction lies in understanding that sellers often hedge their positions, hold offsetting stock, or operate in low-friction environments where they capture spread and delta-hedging profits, decoupling the contract outcome from the trader's total P&L.

## Key takeaways

- **Individual contracts are zero-sum; the practice is not** [00:00–02:46]. A single option contract has a winner and loser, but that doesn't mean the seller is betting against you directionally.
- **Delta hedging breaks the zero-sum illusion** [02:46–04:01]. A market maker or seller who immediately buys delta-equivalent stock can profit even if the option loses, by capturing bid-ask spread and managing gamma.
- **Context matters: covered calls and broader systems** [02:46–03:30]. Selling a call against 200 long shares (covered call or ratio write) means the seller profits if the stock rises, despite the short call losing. The option contract is zero-sum; the portfolio is not.
- **Avoid the "counterparty knows something" trap** [01:20–02:46]. The zero-sum framing tempts you to assume the other side has an information edge. Reality: they may be hedged, retail, or operating on spread capture—not directional conviction.
- **Market makers operate in low-friction environments** [04:01]. Transaction costs, bid-ask spreads, and delta-hedging efficiency allow them to profit even when the option itself loses money.

## Notable quotes

> "The reality is options as a practice, options trading as a practice, is not zero sum."

> "The implication of a zero-sum system is that the counterparty knows something that we don't and we might be stepping in front of a moving train."
