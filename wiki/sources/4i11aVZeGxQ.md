# nobody knows

## Summary

A critique of contrarian positioning and appeal-to-authority bias in trading. The video examines a market participant with two decades of industry experience who bought puts on the S&P 500 during a sustained uptrend, betting on a crash within 3–6 months. The host argues that following price action and momentum—rather than fighting the market based on conviction about what "should" happen—is a more efficient approach, and that industry experience does not guarantee better market timing.

## Key takeaways

- **Appeal to authority is a retail trading trap** [01:03]. Industry experience and information access do not guarantee superior market timing or directional conviction.
- **Follow price action, not conviction** [01:20]. When the market is rising, ride the momentum upward; when it flips to downside severity, ride it down. Fighting the trend because you believe it "shouldn't" be happening is inefficient.
- **Buying puts into strength is stepping in front of the train** [01:40]. Even if the eventual downside thesis is correct, the trader overpays for beta (implied volatility is high on puts), and must recoup that cost before breaking even—a costly tax on being early.
- **IV crush compounds the cost of early contrarian bets** [01:58]. High implied volatility on puts means the trader must wait for both directional move *and* volatility compression to recover losses.
- **Market context (June 2025)**: S&P 500 has rallied ~1 month straight; no rate cuts are coming; tariffs are paused, not cancelled. Backdrop for the contrarian's bearish thesis, but price action is bullish.

## Notable quotes

> "If you learn to focus more on what the market is doing and less on what you think it should do, you're way better off."

> "By buying puts as it's going up because we just don't think that it should be going up even though it is, we're stepping in front of the moving train."
