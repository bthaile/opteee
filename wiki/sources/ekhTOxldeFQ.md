# DON'T Make These 3 Common Beginner Mistakes | Options Trading for Beginners Pt15

## Summary

Eric identifies and addresses the three most common mistakes beginner options traders make: **lack of structure** in learning, **broken foundational understanding** of profit mechanisms and signals, and **failure to conduct self-assessment** through trading logs and cognitive bias analysis. Rather than focusing on tactical-level decisions (specific strategies, Greeks, entry/exit rules), he emphasizes that strategic and operational foundations—philosophy, self-awareness, habits, and a clear understanding of *why* a trade makes money—must precede any options-specific knowledge.

## Key takeaways

### Mistake #1: Lack of Structure [00:28–19:00]

- **The problem**: New traders jump directly into tactical decisions (which options to buy, when to exit) without building a learning framework. They lack the equivalent of a curriculum or progression.
- **The analogy**: Teaching a non-native English speaker requires starting with fundamentals (alphabet, basic words) before complex grammar. Trading requires the same scaffolding.
- **The solution**: Create a **monthly and weekly learning roadmap** [49:10]. Define financial goals, risk tolerance, and market fundamentals *before* studying options mechanics.
- **Action item**: Use the provided syllabus (or build your own) to structure 3 months of focused learning, starting with market basics, then organizational habits, then profit mechanisms.

### Mistake #2: Broken Foundation (Skipping Profit Mechanisms) [19:00–50:47]

- **The problem**: Traders immediately adopt specific strategies (e.g., "buy 7-day, 15-delta calls; exit at +$2") without understanding *what makes money*. They confuse signals (news, volume) with profit mechanisms (price movement, time decay, volatility change).
- **The Big Four Trade Process** [36:28]: Every trade must follow:
  1. **Identify the profit mechanism** — the specific thing that generates profit (e.g., price direction up, volatility expansion, theta decay).
  2. **Create a signal** — measure when that mechanism exists (e.g., stock crosses 50-day MA, earnings in 2 weeks).
  3. **Fit a structure** — choose an options strategy that aligns with the mechanism.
  4. **Manage execution** — apply risk management and trade hygiene.
- **Key insight** [37:37]: Price-up profit mechanisms are *not all the same*. Positive drift, momentum, and breakouts are distinct subcategories requiring different signals.
- **Nike example** [43:27–44:06]: A gap-up on CEO news doesn't automatically make money. The profit mechanism is the *movement itself*; the signal is the news. Volatility expanded into earnings (a separate mechanism), not because of the gap.
- **Action item**: Open a trading plan document and Excel log. List all profit mechanisms you can identify for your target securities, then subcategorize them (e.g., "price up via momentum," "price up via breakout"). Tie specific signals to each.

### Mistake #3: No Self-Assessment (Skipping the Trading Log) [50:47–02:09:00]

- **The problem**: ~73% of traders don't maintain a comprehensive trading log [01:45:02]. Even those who do often can't answer basic questions: "Which strategy performs best in a bull market?" or "What is my win rate by strategy over 52 weeks?"
- **Cognitive dissonance**: Traders know a log is valuable but don't maintain one. This gap between perception and reality is a primary driver of failure.
- **What to track** [01:47:27–01:48:31]:
  - Total number of trades per strategy.
  - Winning vs. losing trades.
  - Average winner, max winner, smallest winner.
  - Average loser, max loser.
  - Wins and losses per week.
  - **Portfolio-level risk**: Worst overnight gap for each security you trade (over 10 years of history). This informs position sizing and reveals tail-risk exposure.
- **Cognitive biases to audit** [01:51:19–01:56:32]: Disposition effect, anchoring, recency bias, confirmation bias, overconfidence, and 23 others. Identify which ones affect your trading.
- **Action item**: Start a trading log immediately. Even if you've already begun trading, pivot now. Track every trade with profit mechanism, signal, structure, and outcome. Review monthly to identify patterns and biases.

### Why Options Don't Matter Yet [33:43–38:02]

- Eric emphasizes: **90 minutes into a video about "options trader mistakes," he hasn't mentioned options once.** This is intentional.
- Options are just vehicles. If you don't understand profit mechanisms and signals, the vehicle doesn't matter—you'll fail regardless.
- Only after mastering the Big Four should you learn options mechanics (Greeks, IV, theta decay, etc.).

### Bloom's Taxonomy Integration [02:11:05–02:13:32]

- A viewer introduced **Bloom's Taxonomy** (remember → understand → apply → analyze → evaluate → create), which Eric found revelatory for structuring learning progressions.
- This framework maps perfectly onto trading education: start with remembering basic concepts, progress to understanding relationships, then apply to real charts, analyze patterns, evaluate trade setups, and finally create original strategies.

## Regime / context

**Date**: 2024-09-21 (live session).

This is the 15th installment in the **Beginner Lab** series and explicitly frames itself as a capstone or pivot point: Eric notes this may be the final dedicated "options trading for beginners" session before shifting to more open-ended live formats. The session is heavily pedagogical, introducing a learning framework and the "Big Four Trade Process" as foundational concepts for all subsequent trading education.

The emphasis on structure, profit mechanisms, and self-assessment reflects Eric's philosophy that most beginner failure stems not from tactical mistakes (wrong Greeks, bad timing) but from skipped foundational work. This video is a meta-lesson on *how to learn to trade*, not a lesson on trading itself.
