# 5 Option Trading Strategies for ALL Markets

## Summary

Eric presents five core option strategies he uses to adapt to all market conditions: covered ratio strangles (bullish, core allocation), ratio call diagonals (directional with unlimited profit), short single options (directional volatility plays), short straddles/strangles (pure volatility trades), and long single options (speculative directional exposure). Each strategy has distinct volatility preferences and risk/reward profiles that allow traders to form-fit their approach to current market regimes.

## Key takeaways

- **Covered ratio strangle** [00:37–03:46]: Short puts with long stock and covered calls; bullish core allocation that avoids capped upside by using ratios; prefers implied volatility percentile ~30–40% to balance premium collection with adjustment frequency; volatility-indifferent because the long stock component is neutral to vol changes.

- **Ratio call diagonal** [05:10–07:56]: Long deep-in-the-money calls (180+ days) with short near-term calls; ratio structure unlocks unlimited profit vs. regular diagonals which cap gains; works for both bull (calls) and bear (puts) markets; volatility-indifferent due to offsetting vega preferences (long option prefers low vol, short option prefers high vol).

- **Short single options** [07:56–09:06]: Short calls (bearish) or short puts (bullish) in high-volatility trending environments; speculative, margin-traded; requires high implied volatility percentile because volatility movement is a major part of the compensation; higher probability of adjustment needed.

- **Short straddles and strangles** [09:06–11:28]: Sell call + put in same expiration; straddles use same strike (peaky payoff), strangles use staggered strikes (flat payoff); pure volatility plays requiring implied volatility > realized volatility; neutral directional bias unless skewed; speculative allocation.

- **Long single options** [11:46–14:01]: Long calls or puts for directional speculation with defined risk; rarely used pure; often converted to diagonals; still speculate on volatility even when directional intent; prefer low implied volatility and low variance between IV and realized vol to avoid overpaying.

- **Portfolio integration** [14:01–14:27]: Covered strangles run continuously as core allocation; volatility trading (via straddles/strangles) intensifies around earnings, FOMC, and economic announcements; ratio strategies provide flexibility across market regimes.
