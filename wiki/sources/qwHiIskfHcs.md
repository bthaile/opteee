# Covered Strangle Options Strategy Explained in DEPTH

## Summary

The covered strangle is a multi-leg options strategy that combines a cash-secured put, potential assignment and covered-call sale, and an additional cash-secured put. Eric presents this as his primary trading strategy (~70% of his trades), emphasizing that edge comes from disciplined management and trade structure rather than directional prediction. The strategy works across liquid index ETFs and S&P 500 stocks, with careful attention to entry delta, implied volatility percentile, technical setup, and scaling mechanics.

## Key takeaways

- **Core structure** [01:10–01:47]: Start with a cash-secured put; if assigned, sell covered calls against the long stock; add another cash-secured put on top. The strategy recycles through these legs based on market movement.

- **Mindset requirement** [02:01–02:43]: You must genuinely be willing to hold the underlying long-term. Saying "I don't mind ownership" but panicking on a 15% drop is self-deception; assignment means the stock trades below your strike, so you're buying at a premium to the then-current price.

- **Management over prediction** [00:46–01:10]: Edge is defined by well-structured trades and option pricing advantage, not stock-picking ability. This is a management-intensive strategy.

- **Waiting is critical** [05:11–06:20]: If assigned at a loss (e.g., basis $10, stock at $6), do not immediately sell covered calls. Wait for the stock to recover so you can sell calls above your basis with capital gains, not at a 40% discount.

- **Covered-call ratio** [19:55–20:19]: If long 500 shares, sell 2–3 covered calls to preserve unlimited upside potential. Never sell short calls at the same strike as your long stock; allow for capital gains.

- **Entry delta** [13:01–13:27]: Sell cash-secured puts at 0.30–0.40 delta. Higher delta = higher probability of management but more premium; balance is key.

- **Setup criteria** [06:41–08:50]:
  - Trade liquid products (sector/index ETFs preferred; IWM is Eric's go-to).
  - Implied volatility percentile > 25% (avoid low-IV environments).
  - Price and trend: prefer uptrending on short/mid/long-term moving averages; price above long-term and mid-term MAs.
  - For individual stocks: growing free cash flow, sales, and P/E at or below industry average.
  - Minimum 1% return on invested capital for the short put.

- **Allocation and outlay** [11:41–14:30]:
  - Index ETFs: allocate freely (willing to hold 100% of portfolio if assigned).
  - Individual stocks: max 8–10% of account (typically 2–5%).
  - Outlay (initial capital deployed) depends on distance from all-time highs: >40% off highs → use 90–100% of allocation; closer to highs → use smaller outlay to preserve capital for scaling.

- **Scaling mechanics** [15:24–15:46]: Reserve capital to add cash-secured puts at lower strikes as the underlying drops. This keeps you sensitive to basis and allows continued profit realization during downturns.

- **Management timeline** [16:10–16:55]:
  - Manage at 60% of credit received, or 30–50% max profit if within a week.
  - Around 20 days to expiration, gamma risk rises sharply; offer trades at 30–50% max profit.
  - Within 18 days, delta moves very quickly; monitor closely.

- **Rolling vs. assignment** [17:22–18:19]:
  - Rolling is the first preference when challenged (in-the-money).
  - Best time to roll is when stock price equals strike (maximum extrinsic value and flexibility).
  - If rolling becomes difficult (insufficient extrinsic value, capital constraints), take assignment.

- **Risk-based scaling** [18:44–19:13]: Add 8–10% of allocation for each 10% drop in the underlying. This systematically increases exposure during downturns.

- **Early assignment risk** [19:35–19:55]: Monitor extrinsic value in short puts; <$0.10 signals early assignment risk. Early assignment is manageable if you have capital to unwind.

- **Liquidity and rolling** [21:02–21:20]: Liquidity is critical, especially for rolling. Prefer rolling out minimal time while collecting credit and adjusting strike, rather than rolling far out in time.
