# Inside Rolling Options

## Summary

Rolling options does not eliminate losses—it merely defers them and ties capital to open positions. A rolling transaction is fundamentally a sequence of separate trades whose accounting is linked; losses are realized when you close the initial leg, and any credit from the new leg becomes trapped in an open position that carries its own loss risk.

## Key takeaways

- **Rolling is multiple trades, not loss avoidance** [00:00] — Rolling options is a sequence of distinct trades with linked accounting; it does not prevent losses from occurring.
- **Losses are realized on the close leg** [00:00–00:28] — When you buy to close a short position at a loss (e.g., sold at $1, bought back at $2), you realize that loss immediately, regardless of whether you simultaneously open a new trade.
- **New credit is trapped in open risk** [00:28] — Any credit received from rolling into a new position (e.g., $3 received) becomes capital at risk in that new open trade, which can itself move into loss.
- **Capital efficiency illusion** [00:28] — The appearance of a net credit masks the fact that unrealized losses from the closed leg plus the new open position create compounded risk exposure.
