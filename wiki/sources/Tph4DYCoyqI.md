# Managing Options Positions & Expected Return

## Summary

This video addresses how retail traders sabotage themselves through poor position management and emotional decision-making. Eric walks through a systematic framework for managing options positions based on profit/loss targets, portfolio alignment, time decay, and upcoming events—then demonstrates how to calculate expected return to evaluate trade quality before entry, using a GameStop call example to show why win rate alone doesn't guarantee profitability.

## Key takeaways

- **Disposition effect & naive reinforcement** [01:45–03:04]: Retail traders tend to sell winners too quickly (ego boost, "nobody goes broke taking profits") and avoid closing losers or doing deep analysis (painful process). This creates a system-level drag that erodes long-term returns, even after years of winning streaks.

- **Decision cycle for position management** [04:07–05:36]: Manage positions based on: (1) profit/loss targets hit; (2) portfolio alignment (does this position still fit your broader thesis?); (3) time in trade (theta decay accelerating, or thesis disproven); (4) upcoming events (earnings, macro shifts, news).

- **Expected return framework** [08:47–16:28]: A 95% win rate with $10 average wins and $190 average losses yields zero profit across 200 trades. Calculate expected return by: defining upside/downside targets via technical analysis, estimating probability of touch (from options chain or risk profile), and computing weighted outcome. Example: GME 30 calls at $4.35 with 64% prob of $350 profit and 63% prob of $265 loss yields +$45.99 expected return and 1.29 R multiple—positive edge.

- **Probability of touch vs. delta** [13:39–15:46]: Use probability of touch (not just probability of expiring ITM) because you'll manage at target levels before expiration. Delta ≈ 2× probability of touch in normal, less-volatile products; breaks down in high-vol names like GME and over longer time horizons.

- **Pre-trade decision-making** [07:41–08:26]: Define profit/loss targets and management rules *before* entering, not while in the trade. Once risk is on, subconscious bias and survival-based psychology distort your perception of risk/reward.

- **System over single trade** [17:23–18:07]: Codify your process (e.g., "buy 60+ DTE calls, not 31 DTE") so you can evaluate expected return across a *system* of trades, not just one. This is how you build a durable edge.
