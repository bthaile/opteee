# Overpriced Skew in SPY #optionstrading

## Summary

This video examines the structural overpricing of put options relative to calls in SPY, driven by demand for crash protection. The presenter demonstrates a ~10-point implied volatility spread between 30-delta puts and calls, illustrating the put-skew premium that sellers can capture through short-put strategies. The mechanics tie to variance risk premium and the long-term upward bias of equity markets.

## Key takeaways

- **Put skew mechanics** [00:26]: 30-delta SPY puts trade at ~27.66% IV while corresponding 30-delta calls trade at ~18.23% IV—a ~9.4-point skew reflecting demand for downside protection.
- **Variance risk premium component** [00:00]: Selling puts in indices captures both the variance risk premium and the put-skew premium, a documented and persistent effect.
- **Win-rate vs. tail-risk tradeoff** [00:47]: Strategies exploiting put skew tend to win frequently because markets trend upward, but losses can be severe when they occur—a classic short-volatility profile.
- **Demand-driven premium** [00:26]: Sellers of puts demand a premium to compensate for bearing crash-protection demand, creating the observable skew.

## Notable quotes

- "The 30 delta put has an implied vol of 27.66%. The implied vol for the corresponding out of the money call is 18.23%. There's almost 10 points of variance between these. That's put skew." [00:26]
- "If we think in general, the market tends to go up. So, what that means is with this kind of strategy, you can expect to typically win, and when you lose, it's probably going to suck." [00:47]
