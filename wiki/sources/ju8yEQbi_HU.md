# IBIT Covered Strangle Trade Management

## Summary

Eric walks through live trade management of a covered strangle on IBIT (Bitcoin spot ETF) during a ~30% drawdown from entry. The position, initiated in December 2024 and adjusted multiple times through January–February 2025, demonstrates how to methodically reduce basis, scale into weakness, and navigate expiration liquidity while maintaining long-term portfolio allocation to the underlying asset.

## Key takeaways

- **Covered strangle vs. the wheel** [01:16]: Unlike the wheel, a covered strangle does not cap upside by selling calls at a ratio; short puts and long equity (or pure short puts) coexist, allowing full upside participation.

- **Allocation vs. utilization** [02:31]: Assign a fixed capital allocation to the trade (e.g., $150k), then manage utilization (% deployed) to preserve dry powder for scaling in during declines.

- **First adjustment: Jan 16 entry** [02:31]: Sold 8× Feb 20 51-delta puts at $1.37 credit (~60–80% utilization); top-ticked a minor rally before a 16.5% sell-off.

- **Jan 26 pivot: exit equity, convert to short puts** [04:54]: Closed long shares at a loss ($8,051 realized loss), bought back the 51 puts ($264 loss), then sold 20× March 6 52-delta puts for $3.70 ($7,400 credit). This flattens the position to pure short puts, which are easier to adjust without adding capital.

- **Feb 9 scale-in: add size, grind basis down** [08:44]: Increased from 20 to 24 contracts on March 13 50-delta puts, adjusting basis from 52 to 50 strike. Kept expiration tight (44 DTE) to preserve upside optionality if a recovery occurs.

- **Expiration liquidity trade-off** [10:13]: Front-month expirations are 2–7 days apart; beyond ~44 DTE, gaps widen to 7+ days. Staying closer to expiration (higher delta, less extrinsic value) preserves recovery upside but increases early-assignment risk.

- **Current state (Feb 11)** [11:28]: 24 contracts on March 13 50-delta puts (now deep ITM, ~$1.11 extrinsic value). Basis has improved from original ~$56 to $50, but underlying is in the high $30s, so further adjustments likely needed.

- **Capital-constrained alternative** [07:33]: In a smaller account, exiting the entire trade and re-entering later is often preferable to grinding through prolonged drawdowns; the opportunity cost of capital may outweigh the recovery upside.

- **Realized vs. unrealized P&L tracking** [06:17]: Short call rolls contributed +$871; total P&L across all adjustments is +$220 (modest), but the trade remains net positive and positioned for recovery.

- **Don't chase losses** [13:45]: Avoid over-committing to a losing trade just to recoup losses; exit and redeploy elsewhere unless the position is a core long-term allocation.

## Notable quotes

> "The way to think about the covered strangle in a nutshell is it's kind of like the wheel, but I do not cap my upside profit by selling the short calls at a ratio, and I almost always have short puts out along with long equity versus one or the other." [01:16]

> "What I'm doing is I'm slowly grinding my way down." [08:44]
