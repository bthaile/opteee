# Secrets to Trader Performance | The Outlier Podcast Ep47

## Summary

Eric (host, 16-year retail trader and Marine officer) and David Jaffe (Best Stock Strategy) discuss the psychology, mechanics, and discipline required for consistent trading performance. They emphasize that success stems from stoicism, work ethic, and risk management via position sizing rather than hedging complexity. Both traders reject the notion that discretion can be removed from profitable trading and stress that accepting small losses quickly—rather than rolling or hoping for recovery—is essential to long-term profitability.

## Key takeaways

### Mindset & discipline
- **Stoicism and control** [07:14]: Focus on what you can control; accept circumstance and perform your best. "Do what you can with what you have where you are."
- **Work ethic over intelligence** [20:35]: Intelligence is vastly overrated; consistent work ethic and learning a skill (trade, HVAC, construction) can create wealth regardless of formal education.
- **Mentorship and receptivity** [08:30]: Being open to input from mentors who give freely is transformative; good mentors and a supportive community ("it takes a village") compound opportunity.
- **Habits define character** [53:30]: Undisciplined traders are undisciplined in other life areas; habits are who you are.

### Trading strategy & allocation
- **Core vs. speculative allocation** [24:38]: Run a core allocation (covered strangles, box spreads, cash-secured puts) to hit annual return targets; deploy speculative allocation (ratio diagonals, variance risk premium trades) opportunistically during earnings or high-volatility regimes.
- **Covered strangle mechanics** [25:00]: Short calls at a ratio to long deltas; double-dip with portfolio margin by running box spreads on excess cash (currently ~5.4% annualized).
- **Ratio diagonals for directional leverage** [30:39]: Long leg 90+ DTE (often 180+), short leg ~20 DTE; significant driver of outperformance in 2023 (~30% YTD).
- **Index vs. individual names** [26:28]: Box spreads in SPX (European options for safety); covered strangles in IWM; speculative trades in individual names unless variance risk premium.
- **Discretionary entry criteria** [34:28]: Vary DTE based on IV rank, distance from highs, and regime; 0–5 DTE for variance premium; 2–30 days for covered strangles; 20 DTE shorts on 90+ DTE longs for diagonals.

### Risk management & hedging
- **Size over hedging** [37:02]: Control risk via position sizing, not complex hedges. Naked leverage is acceptable if you monitor and adjust quickly when drawdowns occur.
- **Long-dated SPX or VIX puts for tail protection** [35:16]: Buy 2–3 year SPX puts when VIX is low (12–15); alternatively, use VIX calls (more efficient because VIX has a floor ~12 and doesn't decay as far out-of-the-money as SPX).
- **VIX call ratio spreads** [42:20]: Buy deep ITM VIX calls (80+ delta), sell shorter-dated OTM calls at a ratio to subsidize cost while maintaining upside.
- **Avoid over-hedging** [37:25]: Over-hedging and fear of variance can be detrimental; leverage is fine if you manage it actively.

### Loss aversion & rolling
- **Rolling is a psychological crutch** [01:07:55]: Rolling simply closes one trade and opens another; it does not change realized P&L. Retail traders use it to avoid admitting losses (loss aversion).
- **Be loss-seeking** [01:09:22]: Embrace small losses quickly; a 98% win rate with unmanaged 2% losses can be unprofitable. Successful traders often win ~50% but manage risk well.
- **Realized vs. unrealized P&L** [01:08:40]: Understand the difference; rolling does not change realized losses, only accounting presentation.

### Zero DTE & market microstructure
- **Zero DTE is a retail trap** [01:04:18]: Retail traders were net profitable for ~4 months after zero DTE launch (May 2021/22), then turned negative. Slippage and commissions dominate losses.
- **Slime options (10 lots or less)** [01:07:04]: Retail traders collectively lose ~$133k/day in zero DTE via slim options; it is a minority allocation (5–10% of volume) only.

### Discretion & automation
- **Discretion is where alpha lives** [01:00:24]: No retail-available automated system produces reliable alpha. Professionals have leverage and lower friction that retail cannot match.
- **Automation as a filter, not a decision-maker** [01:15:51]: Use scans and rules to narrow opportunities (1000 → 20–30), then apply pattern recognition and judgment to select the best 3–4 trades.
- **Pattern recognition is subconscious** [01:16:24]: After thousands of charts, your mind overlays patterns subconsciously; "gut feeling" is actually sophisticated data processing, not emotion.

### Market conditions & allocation shifts
- **Adjust allocation based on breadth** [32:29]: Recently rotated from covered strangle to ratio put diagonal in IWM because market breadth deteriorated; net short deltas is rare but appropriate when trend changes.
- **Earnings cycles increase speculative allocation** [29:53]: More variance risk premium trades during earnings because you need more occurrences to withstand drawdowns.

### Practical examples
- **David's strategy** [34:28]: 10–15 stock watchlist; sell call spreads when overbought, put spreads when oversold; load up on long-dated SPX puts when VIX < 15; outperformed market by ~5% during August 2023 downturn.
- **BlackRock example** [58:29]: Sold 520/460 put spread; willing to take ownership at 460–480 (50% below prior highs); acceptable risk/reward despite tail risk.

## Notable quotes

> "Do what you can with what you have where you are." — Eric, on stoicism and the power of now.

> "I would rather win 51% of my trades but manage risk well and have a good return than win 90% of the trades and after friction have no money to show for it." — Eric, on win rate vs. profitability.

> "Rolling is a fugazi. It doesn't mean anything. Retail traders deploy it as a way to avert losses." — Eric, on loss aversion and rolling.
