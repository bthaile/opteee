# Trading the Covered Strangle Options Strategy in Bear Markets

## Summary

The covered strangle is a neutral-to-bullish strategy that can survive bear markets through disciplined money management, cash reserves, and strategic scaling. While not designed for bearish conditions, careful position sizing and rolling adjustments allow traders to exit profitably even during significant market declines, as demonstrated by a trade that exited at a ~40-point advantage despite a major drawdown.

## Key takeaways

- **Covered strangle is neutral-to-bullish, not bearish** [00:42–01:08]: The strategy should not be deployed specifically to time bear markets; instead, continuous deployment with proper allocation management allows survival through downturns.

- **Portfolio allocation splits core and speculative** [03:26–03:55]: Core holdings focus on covered strangles for steady returns; speculative allocation captures directional trends. This dual approach prevents being sidelined during market stress.

- **Maintain cash reserves and scale carefully** [05:43–06:32]: Add to losing trades only on significant moves (e.g., 10-point declines); avoid exhausting capital early. Scaling in acts as cost-averaging while preserving dry powder for extended bear markets.

- **Average bear market lasts ~290–300 days** [07:42–08:05]: Plan for the long haul; scaling too fast leaves no capital for subsequent opportunities. Maintain price sensitivity by reserving capital throughout the drawdown.

- **Trade only index ETFs for covered strangles** [08:52–10:10]: Use [[spy]], [[iwm]], [[qs]] because they historically recover and surpass previous highs. Individual stocks carry structural risk and may never recover; index ETFs provide confidence to hold through assignment.

- **Risk inflow/outflow: scale out on bounces** [10:52–11:41]: During volatile downturns, eagerly reduce size on any accommodation in the market. Taking profits at scratch or small gains removes risk and increases sensitivity for true bear markets.

- **Accepting unrealized losses is psychological** [12:21–13:32]: Paper-trade to test emotional resilience; most traders underestimate how they'll react to 40–50% drawdowns. Index ETFs reduce this emotional burden compared to individual stocks.

- **Manage overall portfolio risk via allocation** [14:13–15:05]: Even while managing large losses in core holdings, deploy speculative bearish strategies to hedge and maintain market engagement without exhausting capital.
