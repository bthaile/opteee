# Stock Market Melt Up is On w/ David Hunter | The Outlier Podcast Ep64

## Summary

David Hunter, a 50-year veteran of Wall Street, argues that despite recent market consolidation and inflation concerns, a "melt up" rally is imminent, driven by a wall of worry that has been rebuilt with minimal correction. He forecasts an S&P 500 move from ~5,000 to 7,000 within months, followed by a severe "global bust" driven by excessive leverage (320 trillion in global debt, quadrillions in derivatives notional value) and delayed central bank response. The near-term catalyst is capitulation from investors who missed the 3,500-to-5,260 rally and will chase aggressively once the market breaks above 5,200.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2024-04-17)

- **Inflation stickiness reshaping rate expectations** [01:08]–[01:28]: Market had priced six Fed cuts at year-start; now down to zero-to-two as inflation ticked up three months running. Powell's comment that recent data "clearly not given us greater confidence" confirmed the street's pivot away from premature cuts.
- **S&P correction shallow despite stress** [01:50]: Only ~3.8% pullback from all-time highs despite Middle East tensions, inflation surprise, and rate backup. Hunter interprets this as bullish—the wall of worry is rebuilt without heavy damage.
- **Housing data shows spring bounce dampening** [20:57]: Housing starts came in at 1.32M (vs. 1.15M forecast), permits at 1.15M (vs. 1.51M forecast). Rate backup is crimping the expected spring bounce; Hunter sees housing peaked 18–24 months ago with 30%+ downside ahead.
- **Sector rotation underway but not exclusive** [31:11]: Tech consolidating after 18-month run; defensive sectors (utilities, staples, healthcare) rebounding; industrials (steel, copper, XLI) and financials (XLF, broker-dealers) showing strength. Hunter sees broadening, not rotation away from tech.
- **Metals breaking out after years of range-trading** [41:12]: Gold broke above 2,100 resistance after triple-top; Hunter targets 3,000 by year-end. Silver lagging but poised for 60–75 by year-end; miners have doubled/tripled potential. Dollar weakness and rate rollover are catalysts.
- **Bond market consolidating, not rolling over** [20:08]: 10-year backed up from 3.8% to 4.65% (near 4.70% today), but Hunter sees this as consolidation of the 120 bps drop from Q4 2023. Still targets 3% by summer.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Market as discounting mechanism vs. smart money** [07:32]–[13:42]: Hunter distinguishes between "smart money" (professional investors, analysts) and "the market in aggregate" (all participants, retail + institutional). The aggregate market is a better predictor of future outcomes via law of large numbers. Smart money fought the rally from 3,500 until 4,800+; the market itself kept going. Powell's attempt to jawbone the market removes the stock market as a leading indicator.
- **Leverage and derivatives as systemic risk** [01:04:16]–[01:05:33]: 320 trillion in global debt + quadrillions in notional derivatives create massive leverage far beyond 2008 levels. When the bust hits, central banks will be forced to print despite recent rhetoric against QE, but the lag in response will exacerbate the downturn.
- **Melt-up mechanics: capitulation and buying stampede** [54:37]–[55:43]: Investors who missed the move from 3,500 to 5,260 are waiting for a 10–15% correction that won't come. Once the market breaks above 5,200, FOMO will trigger a "buying stampede"—all defensive/sidelined money rotating offensive simultaneously over 2–3 months. Illiquidity on the way up (few sellers) drives parabolic price action: S&P 5,000 → 7,000 possible.
- **Bust catalyst: policy error and leverage unwinding** [01:05:33]–[01:06:52]: Central banks, scarred by 2008 and post-2020 criticism, will be slow to respond to the next crisis. That vacuum—the window between systemic stress and emergency printing—is when damage compounds. Accompanying the bust: 80% bear market.
- **Sector leadership and cross-market signals** [48:10]–[49:50]: Broker-dealers, junk bonds (HYG, JNK), and industrial/commodity stocks (copper, steel, XLI) are Hunter's tells for a bull market ahead. If these are rising, equities are not heading south. Investment-grade bonds and treasuries look oversold and ready to reverse.
- **Housing and commercial real estate divergence** [22:13]–[24:18]: Commercial office is already in crisis (St. Louis skyscraper sold for $3M vs. $40–50M valuation two years ago). Residential housing peaked 18–24 months ago; expect 30%+ declines from peak, with some markets (Phoenix, parts of the West) already hit. Repurposing (office-to-residential, malls-to-mixed-use) is underway but slow; industrial and reshoring are the bright spots.
- **Global fragility beneath surface calm** [59:20]–[01:02:30]: Europe treading water post-Ukraine energy crisis; consumption weak vs. US. Canada facing affordability crisis (Trudeau policies blamed). Japan is the "wild card"—massive central bank bond holdings and zero rates create tail risk if yields break out. China in trouble. Hunter speculates on a potential Plaza Accord-style currency coordination to weaken the dollar.

## Notable quotes

- "The market in the aggregate, it's law of large numbers—the market in the aggregate generally is a better predictor of the future than any individual, no matter how smart we are." [11:19]
- "I think we have the potential for what I would call a buying stampede... all this money on the sidelines or money that's in defensive areas becoming more offensive... all of them try to do it in a fairly short two or three month time horizon... all coming in at the same time... if they all kind of jump in and in a fairly concentrated period of time, the market's pretty liquid... on the way up everybody's trying to buy and there aren't a lot of sellers and what gives then is price." [54:37]–[55:23]

## Regime / context

Recorded 2024-04-17, during a period of inflation surprise (three consecutive months of CPI ticking up vs. expectations), Fed pivot away from rate cuts, and Middle East tensions. S&P 500 had rallied ~30% from October 2023 lows (3,500 → 5,260) and was consolidating near all-time highs with only ~3.8% pullback. Hunter's "melt up" thesis is high-conviction but explicitly non-time-specific; he emphasizes the next several months as the window but acknowledges it could extend into September–October. The "global bust" is positioned as a separate, later phase (possibly late 2024 or 2025) with 80% bear market accompanying it. This is part of Hunter's multi-year narrative arc; see prior Outlier Podcast appearances for earlier iterations of the melt-up/bust thesis.
