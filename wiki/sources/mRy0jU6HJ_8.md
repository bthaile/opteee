# How to Size Options Trades

## Summary

This video provides a systematic framework for sizing options trades within a portfolio context. Rather than using fixed percentages or intuition, Eric walks through a three-step process: defining portfolio objectives, allocating capital by strategy and hypothesis, and validating positions against overall portfolio correlation and risk tolerance. The core insight is that position sizing must balance capital deployment to meet return targets with diversification and drawdown management.

## Key takeaways

- **Why sizing matters [00:44–01:30]**: Haphazard sizing (1%, 6%, 2% across trades) prevents rational performance projection and can lead to drawdowns that force you to stop trading. Consistent methodology is non-negotiable.

- **Three-step sizing process [01:52–04:07]**: (1) Define portfolio objectives (aggressive growth vs. variance reduction); (2) Allocate capital to strategy, then split into initial outlay and residual scaling capital; (3) Validate the position fits your disposition and portfolio correlation.

- **Core vs. speculative allocation [04:48–06:39]**: Split a $100k portfolio into ~70% core (up to 70% in a single position) and ~30% speculative. Adjust the split based on whether core positions are meeting return targets. This gives flexibility to scale up speculative exposure if core underperforms.

- **Initial outlay and scaling [07:03–07:51]**: If allocating $70k to a trade, deploy 50% upfront ($35k) and reserve 50% ($35k) for scaling on 5–10% moves. Deploying 100% upfront removes the ability to scale and dollar-cost-average into weakness.

- **Speculative-only sizing [08:31–08:55]**: If not splitting core/speculative, cap individual speculative positions at ~10% (not exceeding 15%). This is a generality; adjust based on your strategy and margin vs. value-at-risk.

- **Correlation and diversification [09:39–10:47]**: Tesla and Nvidia are highly correlated. If you already have a bullish Tesla position, don't size a bullish Nvidia position at maximum allocation unless you want that concentrated exposure. Diversify to avoid having all positions behave like one.

- **Options vs. stock sizing [11:13–12:08]**: Buying 100 shares of Apple at $187 = $18,739 capital. A deep ITM 91-delta call at $155 controls $15,500 notional for $3,500 capital. Use actual capital committed, not notional, for sizing decisions.

- **Undefined-risk trades (short puts) [12:08–13:34]**: A short put has margin requirement (e.g., $1,800) but undefined max loss. Define your risk by either (a) committing cash to buy 100 shares at strike, or (b) setting a hard exit loss level before entry. This gives you true value-at-risk.

- **Expected return and system performance [13:53–14:33]**: Balance individual trade risk with long-run system returns. One oversized loss can erase an account even if the system is profitable long-term. Project expected return over time to validate sizing.

- **Sequence of returns [14:54–15:13]**: Use a probability chart to estimate consecutive losses (e.g., 1% chance of 5 losses in a row with 60% win rate). Calculate max drawdown from that scenario to ensure you can withstand it.

- **Risk-per-trade vs. return target [15:36–16:42]**: If you size all positions at 1% and manage them at 50% profit, you need ~50+ trades to hit a 15% annual return on a $100k account. This creates tiny $45 trades over 20 days—unmotivating and unsustainable. Size must align with return objectives.

- **Tight stops vs. trend-following [16:01–16:22]**: Tight-stop strategies allow larger position sizing (constrained value-at-risk). Trend-following strategies require smaller positions (more variance). Match sizing to your strategy's risk profile.

## Notable quotes

> "If it is just a shotgun blast of sizing there's nothing that you can logically project the performance."

> "You have to strike that balance: you need enough money working in order to meet your portfolio objectives but you also need to diversify your risk profile to the degree that you want."

> "Think carefully about the way you size your positions: give them money but not too much money."
