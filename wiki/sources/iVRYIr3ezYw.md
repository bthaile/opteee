# GameStop Update & Market Manipulation | Meme Stock Watch Ep55

## Summary

This episode examines market manipulation allegations against Citadel and Virtue Capital in a class-action lawsuit filed by Genius Group Limited (GNS), detailing spoofing and naked short-selling schemes. The host provides detailed technical analysis of GameStop's current price action near support levels, explains the mechanics of options market manipulation through order-book spoofing, and walks through his current position management strategy—including covered calls, short puts, and rolling decisions—demonstrating how to maintain profitability in a declining stock through volatility capture.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2025-11-16)

- **GNS class-action lawsuit details** [04:26–06:00]: Genius Group Limited filed suit alleging Citadel and Virtue engaged in thousands of spoofing trades (2022–2025) designed to create false impressions of supply/demand and volatility, with damages sought at ~$250M. The lawsuit provides a potential playbook for other companies (including GameStop) to pursue similar claims.
- **Citadel penalty context** [20:00–29:16]: Citadel paid $1M fine in the last two years for consolidated audit trail violations—representing 0.03% of quarterly trading revenue (~$3.4B). Largest historical penalty was $97M to Chinese regulators (2020) for malicious short selling. All penalties remain <1% of annual revenue, creating minimal deterrent.
- **GameStop price action & support levels** [42:17–45:05]: GME trading near $26.60, with prior support at $22 (held), $15 (next major level). Linear regression channels and volume profile suggest next support around $18.77. Host considers this a critical juncture for position management.
- **30-day implied volatility** [47:40–48:00]: IV hovering ~70% for ~1 month; recent uptick from 50% occurred ~1 month prior. Call skew remains elevated relative to put skew, reflecting squeeze potential and meme-stock anomaly.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Spoofing in options markets** [07:06–18:52]: Market makers and large traders place and cancel orders to extract volatility forecasts from competitors' resting orders. By observing bid/ask size and price, competitors can infer implied volatility assumptions and adjust their own pricing. This is a "smaller element of spoofing" distinct from direct market manipulation but still impacts retail traders as a by-product.
- **Implied volatility extraction** [14:10–17:13]: Options premium encodes volatility forecasts. By observing a resting order (e.g., $1.00 bid × 100 contracts), competitors can reverse-engineer the implied volatility and either refine their own models or deliberately misprice to mislead. This is why market makers often quote slightly off-market prices.
- **Delta as probability approximation** [57:17–01:02:14]: Delta approximates probability of in-the-money only under two conditions: low-volatility stock and <60 days to expiration. GameStop violates both. Probability of touch = 2× delta. The relationship stems from shared components in delta (ND1) and true probability calculations, but they are not identical.
- **Call skew in meme stocks** [54:42–55:54]: GameStop exhibits call skew (calls more expensive relative to puts) due to squeeze potential and retail engagement. This differs from typical put skew in other names. As a stock approaches a perceived floor, call skew often increases because the market assigns low probability to further downside.
- **Covered-call management at ratio** [01:21:06–01:23:55]: Sell calls at a ratio (e.g., 6 calls against 800 shares = 200 uncovered shares) to retain upside participation in a squeeze. Early assignment risk is low but present; avoid selling calls if you cannot accept assignment. Roll out and up to increase effective strike and preserve shares.
- **Short-put rolling for basis adjustment** [01:31:02–01:33:56]: When rolling short puts downward (e.g., from $25 to $22 strike), prioritize basis reduction over premium collection. This allows better covered-call premium when eventually assigned. Rolling to in-the-money strikes constrains future management options.
- **Profit-taking and re-accumulation** [01:22:10–01:23:55]: Exit portions of a position during rallies (e.g., sell 500 shares at $60 after buying at $15 = $22.5K profit). Redeploy capital to buy more shares at lower prices, increasing total inventory and future profit potential. This active trading approach outperforms buy-and-hold in volatile names.
- **Position sizing and capital allocation** [01:05:35–01:07:03]: Host currently holds 76% of allocated GameStop capital, with only 27.5% in stock (rest in options). This "light stock" posture reflects downward price action; will add shares on stabilization or rally. Allows flexibility to shift between equity and premium strategies.
