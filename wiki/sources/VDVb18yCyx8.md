# Probability of Profit and Expected Return Explained

## Summary

Expected return (expectancy) is the true measure of strategy profitability, not probability of profit alone. A high win rate can coexist with a losing strategy if average losses exceed average wins. The expectancy formula—(win rate × average win) + (loss rate × average loss)—quantifies long-term performance and enables data-driven optimization.

## Key takeaways

- **Expectancy formula** [01:54]: (Win rate % × Average win) + (Loss rate % × Average loss) = Expected return per trade. This single metric captures the full picture of strategy performance.
- **High win rate ≠ profitability** [02:20]: An 85% win rate with $30 average wins and $170 average losses yields zero expectancy—a losing strategy despite the ego-satisfying win rate.
- **Three optimization levers** [09:29]:
  1. Increase win probability (better timing, selectivity, IV percentile filters)
  2. Increase average win size (let winners run longer)
  3. Decrease average loss size (identify and eliminate outlier losses, avoid holding through earnings)
- **Trading plan + trading log are mandatory** [03:25]: Without documented trades and rules, you cannot calculate expectancy or optimize anything; you're just "slinging trades."
- **Historic vs. implied forecasting** [08:23]: Use historical data to calculate actual expectancy; scale sample sizes to forecast future performance under the same conditions, but remain sensitive to regime changes.
- **Strategy rotation by expectancy** [06:01]: Run expected return by strategy to identify which systems work in current market conditions; rotate away from negative-expectancy strategies in real time.
- **Outlier loss management** [10:43]: Examine your two largest losses on a strategy; if they are anomalies (e.g., held through earnings), codify a rule to avoid that scenario and recalculate average loss.

## Notable quotes
