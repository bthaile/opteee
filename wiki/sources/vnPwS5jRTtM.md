# A Look Inside the Mind of a Trader with Jared Tendler | The Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Jared Tendler, a performance psychologist who has worked with professional golfers, poker players, esports athletes, and traders, discusses the foundational role of psychology in trading execution. The conversation emphasizes that while edge and strategy are prerequisites, psychology determines whether a trader can execute that edge consistently under pressure. Tendler argues that superficial self-diagnosis (e.g., "I got greedy") masks deeper performance flaws, and introduces practical frameworks—like data-collection worksheets—for traders to identify true causal factors in their mistakes.

## Key takeaways

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Edge first, psychology second** [01:00–02:08]: You need a profitable system to anchor yourself; psychology cannot create edge, only help execute it. Early traders often conflate emotional swings with lack of competency rather than actual psychological weakness.

- **Confidence is not causal** [06:39]: Confidence must be grounded in actual competency. A newer trader with high confidence is not a better bet than a 30-year veteran with lower confidence. Overconfidence is prevalent (~70% of people rate themselves above average), and familiarity with the Dunning-Kruger effect does not prevent falling into it.

- **Imprecise language blocks correction** [22:08–23:08]: Terms like "FOMO" and "greed" are too vague to act on. FOMO can stem from anger, overconfidence, lack of confidence, or fear—each requiring different fixes. "Greed" is not inherently bad; it becomes a problem only when execution exceeds sound strategy.

- **Data collection worksheet** [27:32]: Capture triggers, thoughts, emotions, physical sensations, and behavioral changes in real time during trading mistakes. Review daily, look for patterns, and cross-reference with frameworks (e.g., Tendler's *The Mental Game of Trading*) to diagnose root causes rather than surface symptoms.

- **Desperation as overlooked destructive behavior** [48:35]: A compulsion to violate rules (leverage up, remove stops, ignore daily loss limits) to sustain hope of recovery. Often feels justified in the moment and lacks obvious emotional charge, making it harder to detect than revenge trading.

- **Passion and work ethic are non-negotiable** [49:55]: Successful traders love trading and obsess over it. This translates into the work required to improve. Passion cannot be faked; infatuation is not enough for greatness.

- **Aspirations without emotional attachment** [37:17–39:34]: Set big goals to guide effort and demand higher standards, but do not become emotionally attached to outcomes. Use results to validate or adjust process. Results and process fit together; predetermined destinations provide feedback on whether your process is sufficient.

- **Soft language signals self-deception** [41:55–43:10]: When writing goals and their "whys," watch for vague, waffly, or over-qualified language (e.g., "I'm completely comfortable"). Superlatives and hedging adjectives often indicate you are trying to convince yourself rather than stating truth.

- **Look to other performance domains for weakness patterns** [44:05–45:02]: Examine hobbies, tests, presentations, and competitive environments outside trading. Patterns of losing aversion, pressure sensitivity, procrastination, or low work ethic will amplify in live trading and should be addressed early.

- **Simulation does not inoculate to live market** [02:08]: Early traders learn in the live market because no simulator fully replicates emotional pressure. Expect a funnel effect: knowledge from study or sim does not transfer 1:1 to live trading. Faulty expectations from over-preparation are damaging.

- **Humility and clear strategy are prerequisites** [03:18]: Overconfidence in your knowledge is dangerous. Establish clear technical and strategic understanding first, then tease out where emotion actually affects execution. Without this anchor, frustration and fear are indistinguishable from incompetence.
