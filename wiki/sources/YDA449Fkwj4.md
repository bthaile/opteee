# Find Edge and Trade Volatility with Euan Sinclair | the Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Euan Sinclair, options trader and author, discusses the variance risk premium as a sustainable retail edge, the misconceptions retail traders hold about institutional market makers, and why most retail traders fail—not because of unfair competition but because they chase hot strategies instead of specializing. He emphasizes that selling volatility works only when you correctly price it; theta decay alone is not an edge, and hedging costs money that often erases expected value.

## Key takeaways

- **Institutional vs. retail is a different game, not an unfair one** [08:18–09:39]. Professionals specialize in one narrow task (e.g., equity options market making) with massive capital and low costs; retail must jump between strategies. Retail's difficulty is reinvention, not market manipulation.

- **Market makers cannot manipulate; they are at the mercy of order flow** [11:40]. The misconception that market makers hunt stops or rig expiration is largely outdated; they are the last people with control over price.

- **The variance risk premium is the most accessible retail edge** [21:22–22:41]. Options are systematically overpriced; floor traders knew this intuitively. Retail can specialize in selling volatility when it is high relative to realized volatility, rather than chasing meme stocks or crypto.

- **IV vs. HV comparison is 90% of what you need** [36:28–38:09]. Comparing implied volatility to historical volatility over a rolling window (e.g., 30-day IV vs. 60-day HV) gives you broad regime detection without expensive time-series models. Exact numbers matter less than relative positioning.

- **Theta is not an edge; volatility mispricing is** [40:39–41:11]. The equation "theta profit = gamma loss" holds unless you have volatility edge. Selling strangles gives false feedback because you can be wrong about volatility and still profit for many trades before a tail event reveals negative expectancy.

- **Strangles are dangerous feedback loops** [42:07–42:31]. A strangle can show 10 winning trades in a row while you are actually short negative-edge volatility; you haven't yet seen the disastrous case. Use a t-test on realized vs. expected P&L to detect this.

- **Spreads and wings destroy edge** [43:48–44:41]. Selling an SPY straddle with 600 dollars of expected value, then buying far-OTM wings to hedge, reduces edge to 260 dollars while keeping all the losses. Hedges are the most expensive thing you can do; size and diversification are better risk controls.

- **Zero-DTE strategies have three common pitfalls** [47:18–50:06]. (1) Selling strangles instead of straddles (bad feedback); (2) buying expensive wings (halves edge); (3) arbitrary risk-reward ratios (2:1, 3:1) with no statistical basis. Stops assume a trending process; in mean-reverting markets, they lock in losses.

- **Sizing is the only robust retail risk management** [51:20–53:01]. Trade enough positions that a single loss is a "paper cut" in a large portfolio. If you can only trade one thing, you must size so small that it may not be worthwhile. Diversification across 300+ positions lets you absorb outliers.

- **Look for volatility edge in indices, short-dated options, and steep skew** [33:40–34:59]. Indices have the largest variance premium; short-dated options (days to expiration) have more edge than long-dated; steep volatility skew (puts or calls overpriced) signals opportunity. Two-year options have minimal risk premium.

- **Everything in volatility is mean-reverting** [39:23–39:46]. Term structure, skew, variance premium, and implied vol all revert to historical ranges. Use this as a rule of thumb: if something is far from its historical range, it is likely to revert.

- **Buying volatility requires a catalyst; selling does not** [39:46–40:10]. It is dangerous to buy cheap volatility without a reason (e.g., an event) because it can stay cheap for years. Selling volatility can be justified on statistics alone.

- **Specialization beats hot-chasing** [22:41–23:30]. Retail can either jump between strategies (meme stocks, crypto, etc.) or become an expert in one edge (e.g., variance premium). Sinclair chose the latter; both can work, but few people can do both.

## Regime / context

Recorded November 2022. Sinclair emphasizes that the variance risk premium has worked for over a century and should not be abandoned based on recent anomalies (e.g., 2020–2022 volatility regime). He advocates weighing 100 years of evidence over 2 years of deviation unless a fundamental shift is identified.
