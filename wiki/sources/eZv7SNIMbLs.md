# Sizing Options Trades

## Summary

Directional options traders should size positions by first defining upside risk levels (where the thesis breaks), then calculating the cumulative loss if price reaches that level, and finally sizing the position so the loss represents 1–3% of portfolio capital. For directional trades, you can further refine sizing by comparing the probability of hitting your stop versus your profit target to measure expected return.

## Key takeaways

- **Define your stop first, not your position size** [00:23]: Identify the price level where your directional thesis is wrong (e.g., "if GM pops above 48.75, I'm wrong"), then work backward to size.
- **Calculate cumulative loss at your stop level** [00:48]: Determine what your total loss would be if the underlying trades at your predefined risk level, accounting for all legs of the position (long calls, short puts, spreads, etc.).
- **Risk 1–3% per trade, max 5%** [01:09]: Size the position so that hitting your stop represents a 1–3% portfolio loss; 5% is the absolute ceiling for directional trades.
- **Measure expected value for directional plays** [01:29]: Compare the probability of reaching your profit target (e.g., 43.5) against the probability of hitting your stop, then calculate expected return to validate the bet.
- **Short options require a different framework** [01:29]: The speaker notes that short-premium sizing follows different rules (not detailed in this clip).
