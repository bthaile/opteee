# Selling Covered Calls for Income

## Summary

Selling covered calls marketed as "income" is fundamentally flawed because premium received is a liability, not income—realized P&L is income. The strategy systematically caps upside on appreciating assets, underperforms buy-and-hold benchmarks, and creates capital lock-up risk. Proper execution requires selective strike selection (20–35 delta), regime awareness, and avoiding mechanical systematic selling.

## Key takeaways

- **Premium ≠ income** [01:51–03:48]: Collecting a credit creates an open liability (the short call). You cannot safely withdraw it as income without either accepting assignment or rolling perpetually. Realized P&L (closed trades) is the only true income.

- **Covered call ETFs systematically underperform** [06:49–07:15]: S&P 500 and Russell 2000 covered call variants lag their underlying indices because selling calls caps upside on assets that tend to appreciate.

- **Overemphasizing the short leg** [04:14–09:24]: The equity position is the primary driver; the short call is secondary. If you expect upside, selling calls at 1:1 ratio surrenders that right tail for minimal premium. Use ratio calls (e.g., 1.5 calls per 100 shares) to preserve upside.

- **High IV ≠ better premium** [04:39–10:32]: High implied volatility often signals elevated risk or clustering volatility. Avoid extremes: IV percentile <10 means you're undercompensated; >90 suggests something serious is happening. Target IV percentile in the 30–70 range.

- **Strike selection and delta balance** [12:58–13:57]: Aim for 20–35 delta short calls (or covered strangles) to balance theta decay, gamma exposure, and probability of assignment. This range typically captures risk premiums without excessive gamma risk.

- **Avoid mechanical/systematic selling** [10:56–11:44]: Market makers front-run predictable covered call ETF selling. Discretionary strike and timing selection—picking shots when IV looks rich—outperforms rigid schedules.

- **Loss of control** [05:49–06:11]: Once you sell a call, you surrender control of your shares. Early assignment is possible (especially near dividends or if extrinsic value is exceeded), and you cannot prevent it.

- **Regime and risk premium awareness** [12:08–12:34]: Selling calls during periods of elevated risk premium (e.g., when puts are relatively expensive vs. calls) improves odds of keeping the underlying while profiting from the short call.
