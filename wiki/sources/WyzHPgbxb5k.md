# Covered Strangle Option Strategy in TQQQ | Live Trade Update

## Summary

Eric walks through live management of a covered strangle strategy deployed across leveraged ETFs ([[tqqq]], [[sso]]) during a sharp market drawdown (TQQQ down ~28% from entry). The core lesson: by converting equity positions into short puts with mobile basis, traders can actively manage downside exposure, realize losses strategically, and improve cost basis through rolling—turning a passive buy-and-hold loss into near-breakeven or small profit via disciplined options adjustments.

## Key takeaways

- **Portfolio structure** [00:00–01:02]: Model $600K portfolio split across covered strangles in leveraged ETFs; TQQQ allocation $150K (75% utilization), SSO $275K (77% utilization). TQQQ down 28% from $75 entry to $53.86 as of video date.

- **Market thesis & volatility context** [02:14–03:23]: Bearish near-term outlook driven by tariff uncertainty and news-driven volatility (VIX spiked above 60 on April 7). Large speculators not buying the dip (COT data). SSO and TQQQ showing early reversal signals but insufficient to buck broader downtrend.

- **Core adjustment: equity → short puts** [04:30–05:39]: On April 7, unwound 800 TQQQ shares at $41.95 (basis $64, loss $17,880). Immediately sold 82-delta in-the-money puts at $63 strike for $21 credit ($16,800 total), reducing effective basis from $64 to $63 and converting static equity loss into mobile options position.

- **Rolling & basis management** [07:07–08:21]: Continuously rolled short puts lower as market rallied, capturing opportunities to improve basis from $63 down to $60s. Current TQQQ position: 10-lot at 40-delta (out-of-the-money, June 20 expiry) + 10-lot at $60 strike. Realized loss $14,500 but net realized P&L +$382; if options expire worthless, total P&L +0.34% vs. –28% buy-and-hold.

- **Adaptability & scaling** [09:45]: Unlike static equity holding, covered strangle allows real-time adjustments based on market moves and thesis changes. Plan to scale down position size if able to close existing trades at full profit, rather than re-deploying capital into new short puts at current utilization levels.

- **Why this works in downturns** [08:21–09:45]: Covered strangle converts unrealized equity loss into a series of realized and near-realized credits, improving basis incrementally. The strategy's edge lies in optionality: hold shares for recovery upside, or actively manage via rolling to reduce cost basis faster than passive waiting.
