# CANSLIM Technical Analysis & Options with Matt Caruso | The Outlier Podcast Ep58

## Summary

Matt Caruso, a professional trend-following trader and former market maker, discusses his approach to combining CANSLIM technical analysis with options strategies. The conversation covers position-building discipline, earnings risk management via protective hedges, and the critical importance of broad market environment assessment (net highs/lows) before initiating directional trades. Caruso emphasizes that even the best stock thesis requires a conducive market regime to execute profitably.

## Key takeaways

### Market environment & regime
- **Net highs/lows as primary regime filter** [56:22–01:02:00]: Track the number of stocks making 52-week highs vs. lows on the NASDAQ. Sustained periods with 50+ net highs indicate healthy accumulation; surges in net lows signal distribution and breakdown risk. This is more responsive than traditional distribution-day counts.
- **Broad market health precedes individual stock moves** [56:02–57:16]: Even strong-thesis stocks (SMCI, Meta, Shopify) stall or reverse when the market environment turns sour. Conversely, mediocre stocks can rally in healthy regimes. Always assess regime first.
- **Overlay net highs/lows on individual charts** [59:05–01:00:16]: Visualizing the colored bars (green for net highs, red for net lows) directly on stock charts reveals that major advances occur during green periods and sharp reversals during red periods. This applies to mega-cap winners (Amazon, Monster Beverage) and growth stocks alike.

### Position building & sizing
- **Dollar-stop discipline over percentage stops** [17:23–17:56]: Set a fixed maximum dollar loss per trade (adjusted quarterly with account growth). This psychological anchor allows aggressive position entry without fear paralysis. Gut responds to dollars, not percentages.
- **Scaling into positions with profit cushion** [13:09–13:39]: Build concentrated positions (30–50% of portfolio) gradually, adding only after 4–5% gains on the initial tranche. This ensures each add is protected by prior profits, reducing overnight gap risk. Inspired by Jesse Livermore's wheat/corn example in *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator*.
- **Consistent position sizing on adds** [19:00–19:17]: Once you establish an initial 10% allocation, add similar-sized tranches at each 4–5% milestone. Consistency prevents over-concentration in a single price range and ensures you're protected before scaling.

### Earnings & event risk hedging
- **Protective puts as insurance, not panic hedge** [23:07–24:47]: Buy puts only when you have sizable unrealized gains, elevated market conditions, and concentrated positions. The cost is justified by asymmetric payoff: if the market corrects 25–30%, the hedge pays for itself; if it rallies, you lose the premium but keep the upside.
- **Hedge at-the-money or slightly OTM for 3–4 week windows** [25:32–26:24]: Target the 50-day moving average as your protection level. A 35% gain costing 4% to hedge for 3–4 weeks is sound risk management. Avoid full delta hedges; instead, buy puts matching your position size.
- **Combine near-term and far-OTM puts** [33:43–34:04]: Use 60–90 day at-the-money puts for steady protection (lower theta bleed) and buy far-OTM puts cheaply to capitalize on panic selling at support levels. Manage the hedge actively; don't hold to expiration.
- **SMCI earnings example** [02:11–04:36]: Held a 40–50% portfolio position up 50% (cost ~$240, pre-earnings ~$340). Gap-down 25–30% to $230 on earnings. Exited near breakeven to avoid emotional drain. Now hedges earnings with protective puts to avoid repeating the experience.

### Options vs. equity leverage
- **Ratio call diagonals for directional leverage** [44:24–45:48]: Buy 90–180 day, deep-in-the-money calls (90 delta) to control 100 shares for ~$2,500 instead of $6,700 in equity. Minimal theta decay on far-dated options; mostly intrinsic value. Allows 2× sizing with lower capital outlay.
- **Deep-in-the-money puts preserve upside better than ATM** [29:10–30:22]: Going deeper ITM (e.g., $115 puts on $64 cost basis Shopify) costs more upfront (~$3,700 vs. $465) but only ~$210 is extrinsic value. If no accommodating move occurs, you lose less overall. Trade-off: caps upside more than ATM puts.
- **Theta decay accelerates below 60 days** [28:47–29:10]: ATM puts lose 17¢/day within 60 days; 168-day ATM puts lose only 3¢/day. For hedges, go further out in time to reduce daily bleed unless you plan to actively manage and roll.

### CANSLIM & trend identification
- **Stocks with prior big moves are more likely to repeat** [07:35–08:07]: Momentum begets momentum. Stocks that have already shown extreme strength are statistically more likely to exhibit big moves again. Avoid chasing beaten-down stocks expecting mean reversion; focus on relative strength.
- **Management under-promise/over-deliver cycles** [08:26–08:44]: SMCI management historically scared markets with conservative guidance, then delivered surprises. This creates whipsaw risk. Understand management communication style before earnings.
- **Liquidity & market cap constraints on options** [53:30–53:44]: Small-cap CANSLIM stocks often lack liquid options. Larger-cap growth stocks (now $300+) have better option liquidity, enabling hedging and leverage strategies that weren't viable in earlier eras.

### 2023 performance & lessons
- **Shopify & SMCI earnings blowups** [01:54–02:29]: Two major positions (Shopify down 25–30%, SMCI down 25–30% in August) turned a "phenomenal" year into "mediocre." Learned to implement hedging strategies to survive earnings volatility.
- **Market-maker background informs risk aversion** [13:22–13:39]: Years trading miners and overnight financing risk taught Caruso to build positions slowly with profit cushion, avoiding gap-down disasters.
- **2024 outlook & protective puts** [20:37–21:36]: Markets frothy at year-end 2023. Bought protective puts before vacation (both ATM and OTM) to allow aggressive positioning while away. Paid insurance cost but preserved positions through January volatility (Arc Innovation down 13–14%, Russell down 8%).

### Equity curve management & hedging philosophy
- **Hedging as business decision, not emotional response** [22:30–23:07]: Hedge when you have large gains, concentrated positions, and elevated market risk. Not to avoid seeing red. Cost of insurance (4% of 35% gain) is rational if downside risk is 25–30%.
- **Avoid over-hedging; focus on key inflection points** [24:13–24:47]: Don't hedge constantly; you'll bleed premium. Hedge at specific moments when asymmetry is clear: big gains, concentrated position, market frothy, and pullback likely to 50-day MA.
- **Manage the hedge actively** [39:17–41:00]: Once the market corrects and you hit your target (e.g., 50-day MA on SQ), close the hedge and use the gain to lower cost basis. Don't hold hedges to expiration; they decay and waste value.

### Mindset & consistency
- **Optimize for your personality, not the industry standard** [51:28–52:30]: Bill O'Neal held 200% of wealth in Amgen; Caruso is comfortable at 30–50%. Both are successful. Find your max concentration level and stick to it. Consistency beats heroic sizing.
- **Continuous learning & strategy rotation** [43:11–43:45]: Caruso tries new strategies yearly (2024 focus: equity curve management). Each teaches something. Market-maker background (theta focus) vs. trend trader (momentum focus) are different mindsets but both valid.
- **Process over outcome** [51:07–51:28]: After trying many approaches, commit to what aligns with your psychology. Caruso is a stock trader first, using options tactically for leverage and hedging, not as primary vehicle.

## Regime / context

**Date:** February 2024. Markets were frothy at year-end 2023; January 2024 saw significant volatility in growth/innovation stocks (Arc Innovation down ~13%, Russell 2000 down ~8%) while QQQ remained near all-time highs. This disparity—large-cap tech strength vs. broad-market weakness—is historically anomalous and creates regime challenges for trend-following traders.

**Key backdrop:** The S&P 500 at all-time highs while the Russell 2000 remains in a bear market (down ~20%) is unprecedented. This concentration in mega-cap tech (Nvidia, Meta, Apple) has made active management difficult for traders not focused on the "Magnificent Seven." Caruso's net highs/lows framework helps navigate this by measuring breadth independent of market-cap weighting.

**Prior context:** 2023 was a difficult year for Caruso despite good timing on entries. Two major earnings gaps (Shopify, SMCI) turned a strong year into mediocre returns, prompting a shift toward earnings hedging strategies in 2024.
