# End of Year Melt Up w/ David Hunter | The Outlier Podcast Ep70

## Summary

David Hunter returns to discuss the market's recent capitulation and his long-standing "melt up" thesis. With the Fed likely to begin rate cuts in September (or possibly July), Hunter expects a parabolic rally into August–September, targeting S&P 7,000 (~25% upside), Russell 3,300 (~50%), and NASDAQ 24,000 (~30%). He frames this as the final stage of an 80-year credit super-cycle, followed by a "global bust"—a severe recession with financial crisis and 80% bear market—likely in late 2024 or early 2025, driven by extreme leverage and policy missteps.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2024-07-17)

- **Fed rate-cut probability**: July meeting shows ~6.7% odds of a cut; September shows ~80–90% (or higher). Hunter would not be surprised by a July cut, though the street expects September. [03:49]
- **Bond market leading the Fed**: 10-year yields have fallen from 5% (October 2023) to 4.17% (current), with Hunter expecting sub-3.8% before the election. Bond markets are already pricing in economic slowdown and Fed easing. [17:40]
- **Russell 2000 breakout**: After weeks of skepticism, the Russell rallied ~10% this week. Hunter's 3,300 target (50% upside) remains on track; he attributes the move to technical setup and broadening breadth, not solely Trump-related sentiment. [45:30]
- **Melt-up timeline**: Parabolic stage expected August–September; Hunter has been forecasting this since October 2023. [16:23]
- **Commodity divergence**: Gold breaking above 2,100 (target 3,000 near-term); silver target raised to 75; copper target 6 (from current ~4.50). Grains (wheat, corn, soybeans) remain weak and deflationary. Oil bearish at $60 target (vs. current ~$80). [49:59]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Why the PCE deflator matters**: The Fed prefers PCE over CPI because it is less volatile and more representative of true inflation trends. [06:17]
- **Bond market as efficient discounter**: The aggregate bond market (law of large numbers across all institutional players) is a better predictor of future rates than individual economists or the Fed itself. When bonds rally, they are discounting economic slowdown and future Fed easing. [20:02]
- **Leads and lags in policy**: Central banks react too slowly to economic inflection points. By the time policy adjusts, momentum has already built. Waiting too long to ease risks a hard landing and overshooting stimulus, which triggers the next inflation cycle. [08:04]
- **Leverage as severity multiplier**: Extreme leverage (320 trillion in global debt, quadrillions in notional derivatives) does not cause the bust but magnifies its severity. A policy mistake in an overleveraged system produces outsized damage. [33:08]
- **Melt-up vs. bust distinction**: The melt-up is a market phenomenon (parabolic rally); the bust is economic (recession + financial crisis). The bust drives the bear market, not vice versa. [38:55]

## Notable quotes

## Regime / context

**Date context**: July 2024. The Fed has just signaled potential rate cuts; inflation (PCE) is near 2.6%, down from earlier highs. Economic data has softened in June–July, raising recession concerns. The Russell 2000 has just broken out after weeks of weakness.

**Hunter's macro thesis**: This is the final stage of a multi-decade credit expansion. The melt-up (August–September 2024) is the last bull leg before a "global bust" (late 2024–early 2025) characterized by severe recession, financial crisis, and 80% bear market. Post-bust, Hunter forecasts deflation (2025), followed by massive stimulus-driven inflation (15–25% by 2028), commodity super-cycle, and eventual systemic collapse in the 2030s.

**Key caveat**: Hunter emphasizes he does not forecast the next few months with high confidence; his edge is in long-term cycle analysis. Near-term catalysts (policy errors, geopolitical shocks, financial accidents) remain unpredictable. The melt-up thesis is robust to timing uncertainty within a 2–3 month window.
