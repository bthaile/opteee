# Chris from projectfinance Pt3 | Money Talks

## Summary

Chris from projectfinance returns for a deep discussion on inflation dynamics, Bitcoin versus gold as inflation hedges, the mechanics of futures-based ETFs like BITO, long-dated call strategies, portfolio hedging with options, and the counterintuitive edge of selling premium in low implied-volatility environments. The conversation emphasizes that option prices scale with realized volatility, making lower-IV selling more profitable than high-IV selling despite the superficial appeal of higher premiums.

## Key takeaways

### Inflation & asset allocation
- **Base effect distortion** [01:48]: When inflation was very low a year ago and prices normalize now, the year-over-year comparison gets distorted; CPI around 5% forces investors into higher-risk assets (stocks, real estate, crypto) to outpace inflation.
- **Gold vs. Bitcoin as hedges** [03:02]: Gold down ~8% YTD despite being the traditional inflation hedge; Bitcoin up ~100%. Bitcoin's superior divisibility, portability, and perfectly inelastic supply (difficulty adjustment) make it a better store of value than gold, which can increase supply when prices rise.
- **Personal inflation rates** [12:26]: Everyone has a different inflation rate depending on what they're trying to acquire (S&P 500, real estate, Bitcoin). If you're holding cash and the S&P is up 23%, your effective inflation is 23% because you can now buy less of it.

### Futures-based ETFs & tracking error
- **BITO as trading vehicle only** [24:29]: Futures-based Bitcoin ETF suitable for trading but not long-term holding due to roll decay. When futures trade at a premium to spot Bitcoin and the asset price stagnates, the ETF decays toward the underlying.
- **Futures decay mechanics** [26:09]: A futures contract with zero days to expiration equals the asset price. Longer-dated futures are less sensitive to asset price changes than shorter-dated ones. If Bitcoin stays flat, premium futures decay toward spot, creating drag.
- **Comparison to VXX** [27:39]: VXX exhibits predictable decay because VIX futures trade at a premium in normal markets (VIX 15–20). During flat periods, those futures decay toward the index, creating consistent losses for long VXX holders.

### Long-dated call strategy
- **Core approach** [42:28]: Buy deep out-of-the-money call options with 1+ year to expiration (e.g., Tesla 750 call for $10k). Once the stock rips higher, short a higher strike call (e.g., 800 call) for the same premium, creating a risk-free spread.
- **Waiting for conviction** [45:10]: The prerequisite is being right about a big directional move and having the discipline to wait. Shorting the higher strike too early caps upside; waiting 180+ days allows the stock to move parabolic and IV to explode, making the short call premium much richer.
- **Wide spreads preserve optionality** [49:45]: A 900–1500 call spread on Tesla (450 days remaining) gives flexibility. If Tesla rallies further, you can resell or adjust; if it pulls back, the short call decays in your favor.
- **Ratio diagonals** [52:31]: Instead of selling 10 calls against 10 long calls, sell 8 to recoup most cost while preserving upside. This balances capital efficiency with time for the trade to work.

### Hedging with options
- **Buy insurance when you don't need it** [01:11:41]: Hedge when VIX is low (~17) rather than when it spikes (~25). Buying spy puts at low IV is far cheaper than during a crash.
- **Avoid perpetual hedges** [01:13:10]: Continuous hedging is expensive and eats returns. Better to control position size and risk management as the primary hedge.
- **Collar on profitable positions** [01:15:02]: If you own 100+ shares at a profit, buy a put and sell a call at a net credit (or scratch). This locks in a minimum profit while capping upside—Mark Cuban used this strategy during his Yahoo lockup period.
- **Hedge largest positions first** [01:14:21]: If two positions represent 70% of portfolio risk, hedge those directly rather than buying broad index puts.

### Selling premium in low vs. high IV
- **Realized vs. implied volatility** [01:25:41]: Option prices scale with realized volatility. A 15 VIX environment with calm markets has lower realized volatility than a 30 VIX environment with large swings. Selling options in low IV (15–17) has higher edge than selling in high IV (30+), despite lower premiums.
- **Euan Sinclair's research** [01:27:51]: *Volatility Trading* shows that selling options in lower IV environments had higher expectancy than in higher IV. Extreme volatility (VIX >50) can flip to negative expectancy.
- **Risk scales with volatility** [01:31:30]: Selling premium in low IV means same directional risk but lower volatility risk. In high IV, you collect more premium but face larger expected moves and volatility expansion risk.
- **No holy grail** [01:30:22]: Selling options in high IV is not a no-brainer; larger premiums reflect larger expected moves. The edge comes from the volatility risk premium, which is environment-dependent.

### Small account trading
- **Minimum viable capital** [59:26]: Accounts under $10–15k have severe flexibility constraints. With $2k, position sizing forces compromises; a single cash-secured put can exhaust the account.
- **Pattern day trader rule** [01:00:56]: Accounts under $25k can only make a limited number of round-trip trades in a rolling 5-day period. Staying above $25k is critical for active trading.
- **Leverage as necessity** [01:04:56]: To outperform the market, leverage is often required. Without it, beating the S&P 500 is very difficult unless you have exceptional directional accuracy.

### Conviction & research
- **Trade ideas from deep conviction** [01:06:23]: Chris's MicroStrategy position stems from 18 months of Bitcoin research and podcast listening, not a stock screener. Conviction must come from somewhere—fundamentals, sector knowledge, or macro thesis.
- **Avoid forced trades** [01:07:10]: If you don't have a good idea, don't force it. Static positions (index ETFs) are better than speculative guesses.
