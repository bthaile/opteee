# Solving the Right Problems as an Options Trader

## Summary

New options traders often jump directly to tactical questions (which expiration? which delta?) without first identifying the underlying profit mechanism they're trying to capture. This video demonstrates how to reverse-engineer position structure from market outlook: if you expect minimal movement, selling premium (e.g., short calls or call spreads) aligns with that thesis far better than buying long options, regardless of expiration choice.

## Key takeaways

- **Identify profit mechanism first** [00:26–00:56]: Before selecting a strategy or expiration, ask "what is the profit mechanism I'm trying to trade?" and "what structure fits that best?" This is the foundational question, not which delta or expiration to use.
- **Match structure to outlook** [00:56–01:46]: If you expect minimal movement (e.g., NVDA trading sideways between 110–114), buying long options loses money in both near-term and longer-dated expirations. Selling premium (short calls, call spreads) aligns with that thesis.
- **High IV context matters** [01:46–02:11]: When implied volatility is elevated (~88%), buying premium is expensive. If your outlook doesn't justify that cost, you're simply choosing which long option loses money faster.
- **Solve the hierarchy, not the details** [02:11–02:35]: Delta selection (50 vs. 51) is a second-order detail. First solve for profit mechanism and position structure; tactical Greeks optimization comes later.

## Notable quotes

> "Solve the right problems as a trader. It typically starts with clearly identifying the profit mechanism that you're trying to trade and then building the best possible position you can around that—not trying to solve for trivial details like should I do a 50 delta or a 51 delta."
