# Buying vs Selling Options | Options Trading Basics

## Summary

This live educational stream breaks down the fundamental differences between buying and selling options, using an AI-generated quiz to test community knowledge and then diving into the mechanics, risk profiles, and decision-making frameworks. The core message: neither buying nor selling premium is inherently superior; the choice depends entirely on portfolio fit and profit mechanism. Win rates are a misleading metric—expected return and risk-adjusted performance matter far more.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2025-08-30)
- SPX 5-year range: ~3,465–5,800; VIX 5-year range: ~16–23 (mean reversion in volatility is far tighter than price drift) [38:40]
- Long-term VIX average ~18.50 (inception 1986); demonstrates volatility's magnetic pull to mean vs. price's directional drift [40:52]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Break-even has two reference points:** today vs. at expiration. Newer traders often confuse the at-expiration break-even (strike + premium for long calls) with the current mark-to-market break-even, which includes extrinsic value [11:45]
- **Long options (buying premium):** defined risk (max loss = premium paid), unlimited profit potential, benefit from volatility increases and directional moves [14:02]
- **Short options (selling premium):** undefined/unlimited risk, defined profit (max gain = premium collected), benefit from theta decay and volatility decreases, higher probability of profit but lower average win size [30:54]
- **Volatility behavior differs from price:** volatility exhibits strong mean reversion (pulls back to long-term average); price exhibits positive drift (upward bias in equities). This is why selling calls against long stock (buy-write) caps upside and underperforms buy-and-hold over long periods [45:49]
- **Win rate ≠ profitability:** A 46% win rate can coexist with positive P&L and positive expected return if average winners exceed average losers. Sellers often fall into the trap of celebrating high win rates (e.g., 70%+) while missing that their average loss per loser exceeds their average gain per winner [49:33]
- **Risk-adjusted metrics matter:** Sharpe ratio penalizes all volatility (upside and downside); Sortino ratio only penalizes downside P&L volatility. Sortino is more relevant for traders [51:27]
- **Portfolio fit first, volatility level second:** The primary decision factor is what profit mechanism fits your outlook (bullish → long calls; neutral/slightly bullish → short puts). Buying when IV is low or selling when IV is high are secondary optimizations; they don't override the need for directional alignment [43:27]
- **No structural advantage:** If selling premium were objectively better, no rational trader would buy, and vice versa. The market's two-sided nature ensures both sides have utility [34:07]
- **Covered call underperformance:** The BXM index (S&P 500 buy-write) historically underperforms SPX because it caps upside gains while the underlying exhibits positive drift [45:49]

## Notable quotes

> "There's nothing that makes selling inherently better than buying. Which is why when we talk about trading options so much, you always hear me talk about these kinds of things in the context of what they are." [34:38]

> "If the game was won by simply selling premium on different stocks, who wouldn't do that? It would be completely illogical to not do that. Yet, you'll find most professionals don't do that." [48:14]

> "Don't be a one-trick pony. One is not better than the other. They all have their uses. The main thing is to do your absolute best as a trader to determine what fits the best for what you're trying to do." [54:55]
