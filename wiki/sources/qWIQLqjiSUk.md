# The (Few) Advantages of Retail Option Traders

## Summary

Retail options traders operate at a structural disadvantage against institutions in latency, custom derivatives, volatility pricing, and information access. However, they possess genuine edges in position sizing, discretionary activity levels, entry timing via institutional momentum, portfolio flexibility, and freedom from performance evaluation constraints—advantages that must be actively exploited rather than passively assumed.

## Key takeaways

- **Retail disadvantages (no edge):**
  - High-frequency trading and sub-millisecond latency [00:00–01:16]: institutions execute at speeds retail cannot match; scalping in the pure sense is uncompetitive.
  - Custom derivatives and swaps [01:16–02:26]: institutions can build bespoke products (e.g., credit default swaps on mortgage bonds) to isolate specific risk premiums; retail is forced into imperfect proxies like short straddles with delta hedging.
  - Volatility pricing [02:26–03:42]: institutions employ armies of PhDs to forecast volatility; retail must extract implied volatility from the market and accept it as given.
  - Information availability and processing [03:42–05:57]: Bloomberg terminals cost ~$24–27K/year per user; institutions have closed-channel relationships and early access to dispersed information.

- **Retail advantages (exploit these):**
  - Trade size flexibility [05:57–07:11]: small position sizing (1–20 lots) does not move the market; access to illiquid options with low open interest that institutions cannot fill.
  - No mandatory trading activity [07:11–08:26]: retail can abstain for days or weeks; market makers must continuously make markets and are forced to hold unwanted inventory.
  - Entry timing via institutional momentum [08:26–09:28]: follow large institutional order flow and trend rather than picking tops/bottoms; ride coattails of billion-dollar position builds.
  - Portfolio flexibility [09:28–10:42]: no investment policy statement constraints; can pivot from long to short, or change strategy entirely without investor approval.
  - No evaluation criteria [10:42–13:04]: no Sharpe ratio mandate, no quarterly performance briefings; can tolerate ugly drawdowns (e.g., buying Nvidia at the top) and hold through multi-year declines without client pressure.

## Notable quotes

> "Trading is all about exploiting your strengths."

> "The best asset of a retail trader is the ability to do absolutely nothing."

> "You do not have to pick the top or bottom, which is really difficult to do if you've ever tried it. We get to just follow the trend, follow the waves produced by the large institutions."
