# Inflation & Trend Following with Matt Caruso - The Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Matt Caruso, a trend-following trader and founder of Caruso Insights, discusses navigating choppy, range-bound markets in 2023, the mechanics of market breadth as a leading indicator, and the macroeconomic backdrop of persistent inflation and rising rates. He emphasizes the importance of avoiding drawdowns during trendless periods, maintaining discipline to stay engaged when conditions improve, and using technical guardrails to prevent missing major moves. The conversation covers sector rotation, the banking crisis, earnings expectations, and the Fed's policy dilemma.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2023-05-27)

- **Choppy, range-bound environment since February**: Markets have been stuck in a flat-line pattern after a strong January, making trend-following difficult [00:21–00:43]
- **Cash as a position**: Caruso has been mostly in cash for ~1 month to avoid forced trades and drawdowns in sideways markets; cash decay is real but preferable to grinding losses [03:42–04:24]
- **Sector leaders visible but fleeting**: AI (via Nvidia as "shovel seller"), affordable dining (Chipotle, Wingstop), and housing/building stocks show strength, but rotations are violent and brief in choppy conditions [09:48–11:23]
- **FRC volatility spike and halt risk**: A high-volatility financial stock exemplifies the danger of directionally-neutral volatility capture in unstable products; halts and realized-vol spikes can force early exits [16:35–17:33]
- **Earnings mixed, not terrible**: Large-cap companies showing pricing power; smaller firms under pressure from higher rates; too early to assess full impact as rate resets are still rolling through [41:32–42:08]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Trend-following in choppy markets**: The worst environment for trend traders is a flat line; the best strategy is to avoid trouble, stay in cash, and wait for the "fat pitch" [00:21–00:43, 09:27]
- **Never fully trust a tool**: Always verify that signals represent the actual environment; systems that worked can stop working; healthy skepticism is essential [08:25–08:42]
- **Market breadth as a leading indicator**: Net highs/lows and advance/decline line reveal participation and cycle maturity; breadth divergences signal fatigue before price breaks [27:25–31:28]
- **Guardrails prevent catastrophic mistakes**: Build rules that force you to stay engaged (e.g., buying on green signals) so you don't lose your touch or miss major trends [05:41–06:37]
- **Sizing and volatility**: Higher volatility means bigger moves; directionally-neutral strategies (e.g., short straddles) require careful position sizing and awareness of gap risk, especially in volatile products [14:22–17:33]
- **Inflation is a confidence game**: Once inflation expectations become entrenched, breaking them requires a long, painful process; the Fed must act aggressively early to prevent this [38:36–40:05]
- **Rate hikes and debt reset**: The longer rates stay elevated, the more debt products (mortgages, leases, credit cards) reset at higher rates, amplifying economic stress over time [42:08–42:23]
- **Fed policy dilemma**: The Fed prefers to overshoot (break things) and then cut aggressively rather than undershoot and let inflation entrench; this typically leads to a sharp, quick rate-cut cycle once pain is visible [45:00–45:33]

## Regime / context

**Date:** May 2023. Markets are in a choppy, range-bound regime following a strong January and February. The Fed has raised rates aggressively from near-zero to ~5% over the prior 12–18 months; inflation remains sticky on core measures. Regional banking stress (SVB, FRC) is ongoing. Earnings season is underway with mixed results. This is an unusually long period of sideways action for a trend-following trader; Caruso notes it resembles the dot-com crash era in character (long chop with occasional violent rotations) but is rare in recent decades.
