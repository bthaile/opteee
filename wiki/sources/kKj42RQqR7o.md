# Using Paper Trading to Make More Money

## Summary

Paper trading is a critical but underutilized tool for developing trading skill and testing strategies in a risk-free environment. The host argues that paper trading serves two distinct purposes: for beginners, it's a 3–6 month learning phase to build a trading checklist and plan before risking real capital; for experienced traders, it's a data-collection mechanism to test variations and sharpen edge. Proper tracking and clear goal-setting transform paper trading from "fake money" into a legitimate practice environment that accelerates skill development.

## Key takeaways

### Beginner-level paper trading (learning phase)
- **Minimum 3 months, ideally 6 months** before committing real capital [05:52]
- Primary objectives: develop a solid trading checklist, trading plan, and trade log template [07:10]
- Weed out costly mistakes (e.g., selling strangles on high-IV stocks with unrealized earnings) without real losses [06:28]
- Paper trading captures experience that backtesting cannot: subconscious pattern recognition and decision-making under uncertainty [04:24]

### Experienced-trader paper trading (sharpening)
- Host has 2,100+ paper trades accumulated over 17 years [07:46]
- Split portfolio approach: one "live" paper portfolio trading the intended strategy; separate "data" portfolios testing variations (e.g., 25Δ, 20Δ, 15Δ, 10Δ puts) [12:39]
- Collect large datasets to analyze strategy performance retrospectively [08:05]
- Test multiple profit mechanisms simultaneously (e.g., directional call vs. short put on the same thesis) [13:18]

### Testing hierarchy
- **Backtesting** → broad-brush efficiency; bad backtest = probably bad idea; good backtest ≠ future success [03:48]
- **Forward testing** (Monte Carlo, synthetic data) → project forward [04:05]
- **Paper trading (live testing)** → purest expression of an idea; no historical bias; unknown outcome [04:47]
- Paper trading is time-consuming; use it after backtesting to isolate and validate specific ideas [05:29]

### Tracking and data ownership
- Manual trade logging (not automated) builds deeper understanding of what details matter [09:01]
- Create summary tabs (e.g., "C2C" command-and-control) with high-level portfolio metrics [09:01]
- Build strategy-specific tabs (e.g., ratio-call-diagonal analysis) to aggregate and analyze all trades of that type [09:17]
- Without tracking, you lose all position details on reset; memory alone is unreliable [11:25]

### Mindset shift
- Paper trading is practice for "creating wealth and freedom," not "fake money" [12:00]
- Live testing reveals your ability to synthesize profit mechanisms and interpret indicators—backtesting never captures this [13:36]
- Paper trading will never fully replace live money, but it eliminates "stupid details" before risking capital [14:55]

## Notable quotes

> "If you think about a skill or a sport that you have a big skill set in, something that you've developed over years, you probably attained it through going through prolonged periods of working on and isolating small pieces of it in lower risk environments." [02:00]

> "Paper trading is kind of like the last step of this process: I start with backtesting, then I may or may not forward test, and then paper trading is when I get really specific and I want to test specific ideas in a pure environment that I have no context about what's going to happen." [05:11]
