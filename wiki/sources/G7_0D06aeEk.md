# Trading Market News & Information Integration

## Summary

This session deconstructs how to systematically trade news and information integration into equity prices. Rather than applying blanket rules ("short the acquirer, buy the target"), the host emphasizes breaking down each news event into its profit mechanism, time frame, and market context. The core thesis: not all news is created equal, and understanding *how* information gets priced in—and *how long* it takes—is the edge.

## Key takeaways

- **Profit mechanism taxonomy for news trading** [20:55–22:37]: News trading is fundamentally about *information integration*, not drift. Classify the news type first (earnings surprise, tariff announcement, M&A, conference), then match your time frame to how fast that specific information typically gets priced in.

- **Information discreteness matters** [24:27–25:48]: Highly covered stocks (NVDA, AAPL) integrate news almost instantly; less-covered names give retail traders more time to react. This is where opportunity lies for retail.

- **Two time frames for the same profit mechanism** [25:48–27:16]: You can trade the *immediate* integration of news (minutes to days) or the *longer-term* thematic impact (weeks to months). Trump tariffs example: short-term is the announcement shock; long-term is the infrastructure buildout and deal-making with companies seeking exemptions.

- **Post-earnings announcement drift (PEAD) as a case study** [29:55–31:43]: PEAD is a well-researched, repeatable information-integration effect. Conditions that matter: earnings surprise magnitude, analyst coverage, market cap, stock characteristics (value vs. growth), and investor attention. Use historical data to identify which conditions predict drift.

- **Deconstructing the Trump agenda into tradeable tenants** [34:55–38:43]: Break macro themes into discrete, actionable ideas: trade wars (tariffs → auto/tech pain), deregulation, "drill baby drill" (oil supply → crude-oil fade), tax cuts (sector rotation). Each has different time frames and affected securities.

- **Crude oil as a fade trade** [37:17–38:43]: Increased domestic US production + OPEC production = supply glut. Oil breaks support; this is "the easiest trade on earth" if you pay attention to the information flow.

- **M&A trading requires nuance, not blanket rules** [44:46–52:00]: The standard "short acquirer, long target" fails when deal probability is low, payment is stock (dilution), or the target price is below market. Analyze: deal size relative to acquirer cash, payment type, antitrust risk, expected close date. Use options markets (probability cone, implied moves) to validate your thesis.

- **Using options markets to define positive expected value** [53:52–55:14]: Options prices embed the market's probability estimates. Compare the option market's probability cone to your historical analysis of similar events. If your edge says the move is bigger than what options price in, you have positive expected value.

- **Data-driven validation process** [59:11–01:10:30]: Don't guess. For any news effect (e.g., "bad employment data tanks consumer discretionary"), download historical price data, get the release dates from BLS or ChatGPT, and analyze the actual price behavior around those dates. Use at least 2 years of data; weight recent events more heavily.

- **Tools and platforms** [13:59–14:00]: Thinkorswim (Schwab) is the host's primary platform; Fidelity Pro and other major brokers' platforms are comparable. For news scanning: Benzinga, BarChart. For data: BLS (unemployment), ChatGPT (event lists—but always fact-check).

- **Pairs trading on correlation distortions** [00:00–00:13]: Short-term (3-day, 5-day) correlations between sectors (e.g., comm services vs. tech) spike and normalize; these distortions create pairs-trade opportunities.

## Notable quotes

> "Our job as a trader is to find different market effects—profit mechanisms—that we can monetize. The way we do that as a retail trader looks a lot different than the way an institution can approach it."

> "If you are not using [AI/ChatGPT] to help with everything you're doing as a trader, you literally are falling behind at a rapid rate."

> "Trading is a game of nuance. If you play the markets from the lens of 'if this then that,' very rarely will it actually work out in that way. That's what leads to a lot of opportunities existing in markets."
