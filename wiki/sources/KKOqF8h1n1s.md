# Outlier Options Trading Basics — Session 1: Wealth Building, Market Structure & Options Fundamentals

## Summary

This foundational session covers three core pillars: (1) **wealth-building mechanics** — why investing (not just saving) is essential, the three levers of wealth (saving, investing, income growth), and compound interest; (2) **market structure and participants** — buy-side vs. sell-side, exchanges, market makers, the OCC, and dark pools; and (3) **options fundamentals** — why options matter (leverage, hedging, volatility access, structural alpha), option components (intrinsic/extrinsic value, Greeks), pricing models, moneyness, and settlement mechanics. The session includes a 10-question quiz and live market commentary.

## Key takeaways

### Wealth & Investing
- **Financial freedom unlocks autonomy** [00:00–15:44]: Wealth is not about not working; it's about choice. W2 income is "sloppy" (highest tax bracket, no control), so investing is essential to avoid 60 years of forced labor.
- **Three levers to wealth** [21:11–24:56]: (1) Save first (creates basis for investing); (2) Invest the savings (compound interest is exponential, not linear); (3) Grow income (you can only cut costs so far). Saving alone won't build wealth—$500/month for 30 years at 0% return = $180k; at 8% = $680k.
- **Compound interest is the engine** [22:30–23:39]: Demonstrated via calculator: $100 starting capital + $500/month savings over 30 years yields $180k (0% return) vs. $680k (8% return). This is why investing beats saving alone.

### Market Structure & Participants
- **Exchanges facilitate price discovery, not price-setting** [28:05–29:28]: Buyers and sellers determine price through consensus. Exchanges provide the venue; market makers provide liquidity.
- **Market makers are primary liquidity providers** [51:49–52:00]: Their entire function is to provide two-sided quotes. Brokers can internalize orders but are not the primary liquidity source.
- **Dark pools serve institutional order flow** [01:15:24–01:22:12]: When a large order (e.g., $100M) hits the open market, it gets front-run and moves the price. Dark pools allow institutions to negotiate fills off-exchange without market impact, then report to the consolidated tape with a slight delay. This does slow price discovery but prevents massive slippage.
- **OCC clears all options trades** [01:01:41]: The Options Clearing Corporation guarantees every trade; this is non-negotiable.

### Options Fundamentals
- **Why trade options** [30:43–33:24]: Options provide (1) leverage, (2) hedging, (3) ability to trade all directions (including sideways/range-bound), (4) direct volatility access, (5) structural alpha (e.g., variance risk premium—puts priced richer than calls), (6) income generation or equity enhancement.
- **Options are derivatives** [34:34–36:03]: Their value derives from an underlying asset. Buyer has the right; seller has the obligation (and collects premium upfront). Two types: calls (appreciate when underlying rises) and puts (appreciate when underlying falls).
- **Option value = intrinsic + extrinsic** [40:38–41:52]: Extrinsic value = time value + volatility. As expiration approaches, time value decays (theta). Volatility is the only unknown input in pricing models (Black-Scholes, binomial trees); this is where edge is made.
- **Implied volatility (IV) is forward-looking; realized volatility is backward-looking** [41:52–44:17]: IV percentile is preferred over IV rank because rank is skew-prone (uses 1-year high, which can be distorted by recent spikes). Example: TQQQ at 62% IV, 65% IV percentile, but only 26.6% IV rank—the rank is misleading.
- **Moneyness determines intrinsic value** [39:27–40:38]: For calls, ITM when strike < underlying price; for puts, ITM when strike > underlying price. OTM options have zero intrinsic value (only extrinsic).
- **Settlement methods** [38:18–39:27]: (1) Sell to close (most common); (2) Exercise (rarely optimal—forfeits remaining extrinsic value); (3) Expire worthless (if OTM at expiration). American options can be exercised anytime; European only at expiration.

### Quiz Insights
- **Exchanges execute trades; markets set prices** [48:25–50:05]: Price discovery is consensus-driven, not exchange-mandated.
- **Retail disadvantage: transaction costs (friction)** [56:22–58:51]: Commissions, regulatory fees, bid-ask spread, order bundling for PFOF rebates—all add up. Retail always pays the tail end.
- **Compound interest differs from simple interest** [01:00:22]: Interest on interest; growth is geometric, not linear.
- **Smaller position sizes are a retail advantage** [01:07:46–01:09:28]: Retail can place max trade size without moving the market; institutions need dark pools to avoid front-running.
