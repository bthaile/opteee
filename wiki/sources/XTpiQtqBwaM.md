# Spot Price Sensitivity - Be Careful on Put Assignment

## Summary

When selling puts, assignment risk forces a critical decision: accepting shares at a strike far removed from current spot price can create inventory misalignment and constrain future call-selling opportunities. The impact depends on existing inventory levels and long-term conviction, but assignment at unfavorable prices may force you below cost basis to collect premium on covered calls.

## Key takeaways

- **Assignment mechanics & inventory impact** [00:19–00:38]: If assigned on a short put at 56 while spot trades in the low 40s, you inherit shares at a significant premium to market. This creates a cost-basis problem: selling calls above 56 yields minimal premium (e.g., 6 cents), forcing you to sell calls *below* your basis to collect anything meaningful—opening new risks.
- **Inventory context matters** [00:38–00:59]: If you already hold substantial inventory in the low 40s, a small assignment at 56 may be manageable and not materially worsen your average cost. But if you have no existing position, assignment at 56 when spot is in the 40s creates a large gap between your entry and current market price.
- **Spot-price sensitivity as a position-sizing filter** [00:00–00:19]: Before selling puts, ask yourself: *How far from spot am I willing to be assigned?* This question should inform strike selection and contract size, preventing forced assignment into unfavorable inventory.
