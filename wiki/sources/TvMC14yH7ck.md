# Vertical Spreads, Strategy Process, and Building a Well-Rounded Toolbox

## Summary

This stream addresses the foundational question of when to exit a vertical spread, emphasizing that the answer depends entirely on the underlying strategy and profit mechanism rather than the structure itself. Eric walks through the Outlier Strategy Process and Outlier Trade Process frameworks, discusses why options traders should rarely sit in cash despite market uncertainty, and explores the mental and practical barriers that prevent traders from developing a well-rounded toolkit. The session includes detailed examples using SPX and Palantir, plus discussion of backtesting solutions and the upcoming Outlier Options Trading Foundations mini-series.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2024-01-18)

- **Current volatility environment is favorable for options traders** [23:45–24:15]: Options traders should view the current market as ideal because volatility is the lifeblood of options trading; this condition is infrequent and should be exploited rather than sitting in cash.
- **Sitting in cash is a disease for options traders** [18:10–19:11]: Unlike stock traders who can benefit from moving to cash, options traders have fewer periods where cash is the optimal position; the instinct to sit in cash often signals gaps in strategy development.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Exit decisions depend on strategy, not structure** [00:00–15:54]: Whether you're trading a vertical spread, horizontal spread, or any multi-leg structure, the exit decision must be driven by the underlying strategy's profit mechanism and behavior, not the spread type itself.
- **Define profit mechanism first** [12:01–13:20]: Before choosing delta or sizing, identify whether your trade is directional, volatility-based, or time-decay focused; this determines how you manage the position.
- **Behavior of the profit mechanism matters** [13:20–14:44]: Two downward moves of the same magnitude can have different patterns (aggressive vs. gradual); your strategy must account for these behavioral differences.
- **Close before expiration for non-index options** [14:44–15:54]: For individual stock options, close before expiration to avoid assignment risk; for index options (cash-settled), you can hold into expiration; for zero-DTE strategies, you may let them expire.
- **Use spread width and sizing for risk management** [17:07–18:10]: Rather than actively managing multi-leg spreads, use the strike width and position size to define max loss; this reduces slippage and fees from frequent adjustments.
- **Paper hands stem from lack of strategy** [24:15–25:36]: Exiting trades too quickly is not a discipline issue but a symptom of not having a clearly defined, tested strategy; build the strategy first, then trust it.
- **Understand intraday range and reversals** [26:49–28:53]: For short-premium zero-DTE trades, knowing the typical daily range and frequency of reversals prevents panic exits during normal drawdowns; this knowledge comes from studying the strategy's historical behavior.
- **Delta selection for ratio call diagonals** [47:00–52:54]: Choose short-call delta based on (1) your directional outlook for the underlying and (2) where you are in the position lifecycle; if long options are deep ITM and need rolling, you can be more aggressive on the short calls.
- **Backtesting is expensive but necessary** [33:26–42:20]: Quality backtesting data and infrastructure cost significant money; free solutions have severe limitations; paid solutions (O Rats, Options Omega, thinkorswim) offer trade-offs between cost, data quality, and feature set.
- **Imperfect information is acceptable** [37:46–40:10]: Don't over-optimize delta selection (30 vs. 33 delta) or exit rules (50% vs. 55% max profit); focus on identifying the profit mechanism and signal correctly; the rest is secondary.
- **Specialization vs. well-rounded toolkit** [29:59–32:08]: Trading one stock or one strategy creates periods of disadvantage; building multiple profit mechanisms and strategies allows rotation when one approach underperforms; this requires accepting the trade-off of complexity.
- **Hobby traders prioritize convenience over performance** [31:07–33:17]: Choosing simplicity (one stock, one strategy) over developing a broader toolkit comes with the trade-off of sitting idle during unfavorable periods; professional traders accept the work required to stay active.
- **Sharpe ratio is institutional, not optimal for retail** [44:24–45:42]: While risk-adjusted returns matter for scalability, Sharpe ratio is a fund-manager metric; focus instead on whether your strategy is scalable given its volatility profile.
- **Kelly criterion optimizes for growth at the cost of comfort** [45:42–46:00]: Kelly sizing maximizes long-term growth but produces extreme position sizes; use fractional Kelly (half, quarter) to balance growth with psychological tolerance.
