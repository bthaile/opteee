# Selling Options is Not Just Picking High IV

## Summary

Selling premium is fundamentally about trading the relationship between implied volatility (IV) and realized volatility (RV), not IV in isolation. High IV alone does not guarantee profitability; directional moves can overwhelm the premium collected if realized volatility exceeds implied volatility or if price action moves against the position. The variance risk premium—the spread between implied and realized—is the true edge, and it can flip negative even during periods of elevated IV.

## Key takeaways

- **IV vs. RV is the real trade** [00:00] When selling options, you are trading the *relationship* between implied volatility (expected move) and realized volatility (actual move), not IV directly.
- **High IV ≠ automatic profit** [00:30] Even during periods of elevated IV, if realized volatility exceeds implied or price action moves sharply against your position, the variance risk premium can turn negative and losses can exceed premium collected.
- **Variance risk premium can flip** [00:30] The premium (purple lines in the example) that exists when IV stays elevated relative to realized can reverse, turning negative and causing losses despite favorable IV conditions.
- **Directional risk is the killer** [00:30] Selling premium without accounting for directional exposure means you can "get ran over by direction" and lose money even when volatility conditions appear favorable.

## Notable quotes
