- **Rolling options framework** [47:34–01:16:16]: Rolling is **not** a mechanical rule (e.g., "always roll when challenged"). Instead:
  - **Best opportunity to roll losing positions**: when stock price equals strike (maximum extrinsic value, zero intrinsic value). Rolling earlier avoids buying back intrinsic value; rolling later forces deeper ITM buyback.
  - **Most common rolls are actually winners**: rebalancing deltas after favorable moves, not necessarily rolling out—often rolling same expiration, up (puts) or down (calls) to recapture target delta.
  - **Decision framework**: analyze available rolls (strike, expiration, credit/debit) and only roll if comfortable with outcomes. If uncomfortable with roll options, act immediately. If comfortable, can wait and monitor.
  - **Example (IBIT 51 puts at-the-money)**: sold for $1.37, now at $2.20 close. Rolling to 58 DTE 47 puts ($1.60) nets $0.77 credit, moves strike down 11.7% (51→47). If allowed to fall deep ITM (e.g., 58 puts at $7.20), rolling to 56 puts only nets $5.83 credit—much smaller basis adjustment.
  - **Key insight**: you do NOT need net credit on a roll; track total P&L math. Confidence in rolling comes from testing different management approaches via backtesting or on-demand paper trading.
