# $0 Commissions are Expensive w/ Dave Lauer | The Outlier Podcast Ep45

## Summary

Dave Lauer, CEO of Irvin and co-founder of We The Investors, discusses the hidden costs of "free" trading and the structural problems in U.S. equity markets. The conversation covers inflation's real-world impact versus statistical measures, the SEC's modernization efforts around market structure (Rule 605 and Reg NMS updates), and how payment for order flow and extreme market concentration have widened spreads by ~30% while reducing execution quality for retail and institutional investors alike.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2023-08-12)

- **Inflation narrative shift** [07:16–10:01]: Core inflation cooling, but food inflation remains elevated in Canada and the U.S.; consumer spending and wage growth still elevated; unemployment low; soft landing increasingly debated as possible (though not consensus).
- **Economic data snapshot** [17:21–18:05]: Durable goods orders up ~4.7% (vs. ~1% expected); ISM Manufacturing PMI still expansionary (>50); JOLTS job openings released same day; mixed signals on recession timing.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **The "free trading" illusion** [25:11–26:41]: Zero-commission brokers don't eliminate costs—they shift them. Brokers send retail orders to wholesalers (Citadel, Virtu) who internalize them, offer small price improvements, but widen on-exchange spreads by ~30% to compensate for toxic order flow.
- **Payment for order flow (PFOF) and market structure** [22:40–23:42]: Retail orders almost universally routed to one of two firms; this concentration destroys on-exchange liquidity, widens spreads, and increases market fragility. Institutional investors (pension funds, mutual funds) also pay higher transaction costs as a result.
- **SEC modernization efforts** [19:05–21:19]: Rule 605 updates extend execution-quality disclosure to large brokers; Reg NMS tick-size proposals aim to modernize 2005-era rules. Industry broadly supports modernization; Lauer's advocacy focuses on shrinking tick sizes for liquid stocks and widening them for less-liquid ones to improve overall execution.
- **Competition as the solution** [21:44–30:32]: Lauer argues the U.S. market is not "the best in the world" (contra industry claims); international markets without PFOF and extreme fragmentation show better execution quality. Open, competitive order routing—not prescriptive regulation—is the goal.
- **Zero-DTE options and market fragility** [31:17–34:48]: Most zero-DTE options expire worthless; retail participation is akin to gambling. Market makers (Citadel, Susquehanna) collect premium and benefit greatly. Research (JP Morgan) suggests convexity effects and increased volatility, though Lauer notes limited deep analysis. Professional traders increasingly participate alongside retail.
- **Irvin platform update** [35:27–38:53]: Beta launched ~1 month prior; 12,000 waitlist at launch, ~14,000 users by interview date, 120,000+ on waitlist. High data quality, news feed, research tools, community features. Alignment of incentives: retail investor is the customer, not the product.

## Regime / context

Recorded August 2023, during a period of elevated debate over whether the Fed could achieve a soft landing after aggressive rate hikes. Inflation had cooled from ~9% but remained sticky in food and services. SEC was actively proposing modernizations to equity market structure (Rule 605, Reg NMS tick-size updates) in response to decades of PFOF-driven concentration. Irvin platform was in early beta with strong user growth. This episode reflects Lauer's ongoing advocacy for competitive market structure and transparency in retail order routing.
