# Why Hold a Losing Position? #optionstrading

## Summary

Eric discusses the rationale for holding underwater positions rather than immediately cutting losses and reallocating capital. The key insight is that losing trades are a normal part of long-term strategy execution, and premature exit decisions driven by discomfort often reflect poor portfolio segmentation and unrealistic expectations about trading outcomes.

## Key takeaways

- **Opportunity cost is real, but context-dependent** [00:00] — The question "why not just take the loss and move elsewhere?" is valid, but only applies when capital is truly better deployed. If a position is part of a long-term strategy allocation, enduring sub-optimal periods is part of the plan.
- **Segment your portfolio by strategy and time horizon** [00:26] — Allocate a smaller, predetermined portion to long-term strategies specifically to absorb drawdowns without forcing panic decisions. This pre-commitment removes emotion from the exit decision.
- **Retail expectations are broken** [00:49] — Social media and survivorship bias create a false narrative of smooth, consistent gains. Losing trades and adverse moves are inevitable; traders who accept this have a psychological edge over those who don't.
- **Distinguish between "sub-optimal" and "broken"** [00:26] — A position going against you temporarily is not a signal to abandon the strategy; it's a test of whether you actually committed to the plan.

## Notable quotes

> "People on the internet really don't like to talk about losing trades or things going against them, which I think gives retail traders this really, really broken expectation of what this thing looks like, aka smooth sailing all the time, making tons of money."
