# Live Covered Strangle Trade: What to Do in Bear Markets

## Summary

Eric walks through active management of a deep in-the-money covered strangle position in IBIT during a 44% bear market drawdown from 52-week highs. The core strategy involves rolling short puts progressively lower to reduce basis, maintain capital efficiency, and regain spot price sensitivity while accepting small realized losses as the cost of basis adjustment. The position demonstrates how options allow traders to move risk without necessarily adding capital, and how to navigate losing trades through disciplined rebalancing rather than capitulation.

## Key takeaways

- **Bear market context** [00:29]: IBIT down 44% from 52-week highs; position hit a floor around 36 and is grinding higher with small higher lows and higher highs; trader remains tenuous on direction.

- **Rolling mechanics for basis reduction** [02:27]: By rolling short puts lower (e.g., from 50 to 48 strike), you can add contracts while keeping total capital requirement flat or nearly flat, effectively lowering your average basis without adding proportional risk.

- **Deep ITM puts and extrinsic value** [09:38]: 45-strike puts with 25 days to expiration are 82 delta and deeply in the money; spot at 40.05 means ~$4.95 intrinsic value, leaving only ~$0.30–0.40 extrinsic value to work with.

- **Spot price sensitivity trade-off** [11:53]: By rolling down and adding size, you sacrifice some max profit potential but regain the ability to monetize upside moves via short calls above your new basis; this is preferable to sitting idle in high-strike positions.

- **Capital allocation and portfolio context** [12:44]: This position is a minority allocation within a larger portfolio; the strategy is long-term in nature, so suboptimal interim points are expected and acceptable; other portfolio segments are generating returns to offset this drawdown.

- **Year-to-date performance vs. underlying** [13:58]: Realized P&L down 14.4% YTD; underlying down 20.5%; outperformance of 6% despite losses, with unrealized gains providing additional cushion.

- **Modeling basis adjustments** [17:18]: Simulate passage of time by checking the same strike in a nearer expiration cycle (e.g., 45-strike in 6 April vs. 17 April) to estimate theta decay; then check roll prices in a further cycle to model the cost of rolling out and down.

- **Two paths forward** [25:28]: (1) Continue rolling down (e.g., 45 → 41 strike, adding 7 contracts, increasing capital requirement from 126k to 143k) to maximize basis adjustment and regain spot sensitivity faster; (2) Take half the position down for a loss and redeploy capital to sell OTM puts sooner, sacrificing delta recovery but reducing wait time.

- **Annualized return on short calls** [16:26]: Selling 45-strike calls for ~$0.53 on a 25-day trade yields ~1.1% return on invested capital, which annualizes to a respectable rate; this is the payoff once spot price sensitivity is regained.

- **Realized loss acceptance** [24:39]: Accepting a net 6.2% loss on the allocation now (via basis adjustment) is justified because it unlocks the ability to sell 35-delta calls and scale profitably; the loss is a cost of repositioning, not a failure.

- **No shares held** [07:45]: Equity position was closed at a loss; the current position is pure short puts (covered strangle with no long stock), which simplifies management and capital requirements.
