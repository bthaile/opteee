# GME Options Positioning, Market Structure & Dave Lauer on Regulatory Reform

## Summary

Live stream analyzing GameStop's 17 January 2025 options expiration (829,000 calls remaining, 485,000 expiring this week) and comparing current price action to 2021 squeeze setup using technical analysis. Second half features interview with Dave Lauer (IEX, Urvin Finance) discussing market structure issues: spoofing/layering, payment for order flow, dark pools vs. internalization, and regulatory challenges. Host emphasizes pattern-seeking bias and the importance of volume/volatility confirmation over cycle theories.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2025-01-11)

- **17 Jan expiration is "spicy"** [00:00–01:00]: 829,000 calls remain; 485,000 expire this coming week. High concentration of options activity expected to exacerbate moves.
- **Reject 2021 comparison via chart overlay** [17:07–35:15]: Viewer claim that current setup mirrors 2021 squeeze does not hold under objective comparison. Slope, volatility, volume profile, and average daily range all differ materially. Host demonstrates confirmation bias risk by overlaying 1-year daily charts and highlighting missing peaks/dips.
- **Volume and volatility are the real signals** [36:49–38:08]: Squeezes present consistent traits: price breakout, massive volume spikes, expanding average daily range. These are observable *now* without relying on settlement cycles or hype dates. November 8, 2024 entry was triggered by rally + breakout on volume, not prediction.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Hype dates are statistically meaningless** [13:28–14:29]: Web scraper analysis of Superstank hype date predictions shows only "lots of people guessing lots of dates." Asymmetric incentive structure (99 wrong guesses forgotten, 1 right one remembered) makes tracking legitimacy impossible.
- **Confirmation bias is universal** [18:46–31:58]: Humans naturally seek patterns matching preconceived ideas. Mitigation requires deliberate chart comparison (apples-to-apples, same timeframe) and focus on what price/volume/volatility *actually show*, not what we want to see.
- **Order book structure and spoofing** [01:06:17–01:20:27]: Level 2 order books show bid/ask at each price level. Spoofing = posting large orders with no intent to execute, to deceive HFT systems about demand. Layering = similar tactic to push price into hidden sell orders. Both are market manipulation but difficult to detect at scale.
- **Dark pools vs. internalization** [01:00:06–01:02:54]: Dark pools use lit exchange BBO as reference but allow larger fills without moving the market; they still hit consolidated tape (with ~6-second delay). Internalized orders do *not* hit tape immediately, harming price discovery. Market makers disincentivized to improve bids if they know Citadel will execute on their price without visibility.
- **Payment for order flow (PFOF) and retail order flow** [01:23:05–01:26:07]: Retail orders are "uninformed" (unaware of next 50ms moves). Market makers profit by trading against them, so they pay brokers for flow. Recent paper shows PFOF impact on spreads can be ~25% or more. IEX offers free retail trading and no rebates; users can manually route to IEX via thinkorswim.
- **Regulatory fines are performative** [01:29:49–01:32:36]: Citadel $1M fine for FINRA violations = 0.0016% of assets; equivalent to $1.56 fine on a $100k net-worth person. Enforcement lacks teeth; systemic change requires slow legislative/regulatory process.

## Notable quotes

> "The only way you short circuit [confirmation bias] is by knowing our brains want to do that and then doing something like this." [31:58] — on deliberate chart comparison to counter pattern-seeking.

> "I think that spoofing and layering in markets is a big problem. I can say from the experience of constantly looking at market data for those patterns and seeing them relatively regularly." [01:06:17] — Dave Lauer on market manipulation prevalence.
