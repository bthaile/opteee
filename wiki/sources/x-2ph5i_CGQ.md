# Comprehensive Guide to Rolling Options

## Summary

Rolling options is the process of closing one option position and opening another while tying the accounting together. The optimal time to roll occurs when stock price equals strike price with minimal extrinsic value remaining—a "Goldilocks scenario" rarely achieved in practice. The decision to roll should be informed by market disposition, portfolio context, trade thesis integrity, assignment risk, days to expiration, upcoming announcements, and profit potential rather than ego-driven defense of losing positions.

## Key takeaways

- **Market disposition first** [00:19] — Before considering any roll, assess whether your market outlook has materially changed since entry and how the position fits into your broader portfolio.

- **Question whether to stay in the trade** [01:47] — Ask yourself if you're defending a loser out of ego or if there's a logical reason. The best move may be to exit and redeploy capital rather than roll.

- **Optimal roll timing: stock = strike** [03:10] — When stock price equals strike price, you have maximum strike selection choices and the option carries peak extrinsic value, though you'll pay the most to buy it back.

- **Early assignment risk** [07:01] — Short options with less than a dime of extrinsic value (especially sub-nickel) face elevated assignment risk and may require immediate action.

- **Inventory preference drives decisions** [09:44] — Decide upfront whether you want to take assignment on shares; this choice cascades through all subsequent roll mechanics.

- **Expiration liquidity matters** [10:02] — Near-term expirations (within 60 days) offer dense weekly choices; beyond 60 days, gaps widen dramatically. Fewer choices = less flexibility to adjust.

- **Spot price sensitivity vs. basis adjustment tradeoff** [11:08] — Rolling far out in time (e.g., 10 DTE to 45 DTE) yields larger basis adjustments but locks you into the position if the underlying continues moving against you. Rolling minimal time out (10 to 16 DTE) preserves flexibility.

- **Theta decay accelerates near-term** [14:07] — Options at 10 DTE decay ~10¢/day; at 38 DTE only ~6¢/day. Rolling minimal time out captures the steepest theta curve while maintaining re-roll optionality.

- **Profit potential vs. basis adjustment** [12:06] — Collecting maximum extrinsic value (staying at-the-money) yields more theta profit but worse basis. Rolling down and out sacrifices theta income to improve basis.

- **Decision tree framework** [05:33] — (1) Market & portfolio context, (2) Trade thesis intact?, (3) Assignment risk?, (4) Days to expiration?, (5) Announcements?, (6) Inventory preference, (7) Basis adjustment aggressiveness, (8) Profit potential.
