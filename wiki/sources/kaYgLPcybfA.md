# Diagnosing Common Options Trading Mistakes | Options Trading Basics

## Summary

Eric diagnoses the most common behavioral, process, and knowledge gaps that plague beginner options traders by analyzing real Reddit posts. He categorizes trading failures into three buckets—knowledge (overconfidence/underconfidence), process (lack of structure, poor risk management), and behavior (disposition effect, impatience, rose-colored glasses)—then walks through practical examples of how these manifest in real trading decisions. The episode emphasizes that sustainable options trading requires deliberate process-building, not luck, and that most retail traders are fooled by randomness in their early results.

## Key takeaways

- **Three diagnostic buckets for trading failure** [07:50]: Knowledge (overconfidence, underconfidence, not knowing what you don't know), Process (lack of one, risk/portfolio/scanning gaps), and Behavior (disposition effect, impatience, rose-colored glasses). These collapse into two parent themes: discipline and humility.

- **Overconfidence from credentials is a red flag** [18:39]: Citing an economics degree or "a few years investing" to justify options trading success is a sign you're attributing randomness to skill. Early wins are often luck, not edge.

- **No process = no feedback loop** [22:59]: Randomly putting on covered calls and long calls without a defined strategy means you learn nothing from wins or losses. Individual trade outcomes don't matter; only systematic process matters.

- **Desperation kills decision-making** [26:53]: When you need the money (e.g., switching careers after one profitable month), your brain suppresses logical red flags. This cognitive bias leads to poor position-sizing and risk management.

- **Overpaying for options via high IV is systematic** [31:47]: Buying options when IV is at the top of its range (not understanding IV percentile) guarantees constrained profit potential. You make money *despite* your selection, not because of it.

- **Futures options liquidity is a hidden cost** [35:27]: Claiming 60% of a portfolio in futures options signals inexperience. Bid-ask spreads and open interest on E-mini options are orders of magnitude worse than equity options; slippage will kill returns.

- **"Repeatable small wins" misses expected return** [39:00]: Selling cash-secured puts for 3.5% monthly (51% annualized) while claiming it's "not bad to start" shows no understanding of expected return or what constitutes edge.

- **IV percentile > 50 is backwards** [43:50]: Selling when IV is "high" and buying when it's "low" is a common mantra that is fundamentally wrong. High IV percentile can mean cheap relative to recent history, not expensive.

- **Greeks matter even on long-dated positions** [59:36]: A 5-lot LEAP long call losing 3% of premium to theta over 30 days (14% annualized) is a real cost. Ratio diagonals (selling near-term calls against long calls) can offset this drag while preserving convexity.

- **Leverage via options requires volatility awareness** [59:36]: Buying a LEAP at 68 delta vs. 51 delta means different vega exposure. A 5-point IV drop can wipe out gains even if spot moves in your favor. Always model vega impact.

- **Synthetic long vs. ratio diagonal trade-off** [01:15:34]: A synthetic long (long call + short put) is a pure leveraged play; a ratio diagonal (long LEAP + short near-term call) subsidizes theta but caps upside. Choose based on conviction level.

- **Position sizing by price is a trap** [40:40]: Limiting cash-secured puts to stocks under $20 because "I can't afford expensive stuff" is arbitrary. Liquidity and volatility matter far more than absolute price.

- **All-time highs are where markets spend most time** [01:34:19]: Sitting out near all-time highs means missing the majority of tradable opportunities. Discomfort with ATHs is a sign of incomplete market understanding, not prudence.
