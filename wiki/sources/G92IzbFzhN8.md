# 5 Questions Every Options Trader Must Answer Before Entering a Trade (Trade Management Basics)

## Summary

This video outlines a five-question framework for options trade management: profit management (what to do when right), loss management (what to do when wrong), thesis management (has the core idea been invalidated?), duration management (how long should the trade take?), and catalyst management (what external events might change the thesis?). The core principle is that robust trade management flows from clearly defining your profit mechanism and signal overlay, then tailoring exit and scaling decisions to that mechanism rather than arbitrary percentage targets.

## Key takeaways

- **Five core questions before entry** [00:24]: Profit management, loss management, thesis management, duration management, and catalyst management form the backbone of a repeatable trade plan.

- **Disposition effect trap** [01:18]: Traders tend to take winners too quickly and hold losers too long. Avoid this by anchoring exits to your thesis and profit mechanism, not emotional comfort.

- **Thesis vs. P&L targets** [01:48]: Your idea can be disproven even if you haven't hit your profit or loss target. If the underlying reason for the trade no longer holds, exit regardless of P&L.

- **Duration and delta decay** [03:07]: Every options contract expires. As time passes, delta changes (charm effect): out-of-the-money options lose delta, in-the-money options gain delta. This is especially pronounced in high-volatility names.

- **Delta example with SPY puts** [03:54]: A 40-delta put becomes 37-delta after 62 days with no price movement, then 34-delta at 9 days to expiration—showing the impact of time decay on your directional exposure.

- **Catalyst as a separate bucket** [05:54]: Earnings moves, industry news, or other horizon events can invalidate your thesis. Build catalyst checks into your standardized process.

- **Profit mechanism drives decisions** [07:10]: Use the outlier strategy process: define your profit mechanism (e.g., momentum continuation below 200-day MA), then overlay signals. This naturally answers "what do I do when right/wrong?"

- **Exit logic from thesis, not percentages** [08:28]: If your thesis is price below the 200-day MA, exit when price breaks above it—not when you've lost 5% or gained 10%. The option's value moves from many factors; the profit mechanism is what matters.

- **Post-earnings drift example** [10:37]: Scaling in on post-earnings momentum (e.g., gap up + two green days → expect 5% up over five days) requires large sample-size validation to avoid overfitting to individual stocks.

- **All work happens before entry** [12:01]: Trading (clicking the button) is easy. The hard work is answering these five questions logically and then following the plan without deviation.

## Notable quotes
