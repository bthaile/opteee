# Trade EVERYTHING w/ Tom Sosnoff | The Outlier Podcast Ep59

## Summary

Tom Sosnoff discusses his contrarian positioning on [[nvda|Nvidia]] using [[vertical-spreads|vertical spreads]] rather than naked options due to extreme volatility and upside skew. He outlines a macro thesis favoring [[long-dated-calls|long bonds]] and [[short-premium|short stocks]], particularly [[russell-2000|small caps]] over large caps, while addressing the disconnect between equity and bond markets. The conversation covers the evolution of retail trading, [[capital-efficiency|capital efficiency]] as the defining shift, and the future of [[event-driven|event-based trading]] and [[tokenization|tokenized markets]].

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2024-02-24)

- **Nvidia positioning**: Short [[vertical-spreads|vertical spreads]] around 655–660 strike, roughly $60 below market at ~720, due to perceived overvaluation and uncontrollable upside skew on naked shorts [00:26–02:53]
- **Macro thesis**: Long [[tlt|long bonds]], short [[spx|S&P 500]] and [[nasdaq|NASDAQ]] futures; favors [[russell-2000|Russell 2000]] (small cap) over large cap due to recent bond selloff and rate repricing [08:34–10:22]
- **Fed rate expectations**: Bond price declines (125 → 118 on long bond; 113 → 109 on 10-year) have pushed rate-cut expectations from March to June or later; no cuts priced in if bonds remain at current levels [07:30–08:34]
- **Tech earnings**: Meta was the biggest surprise and most difficult to trade; most other earnings (Microsoft, Tesla, Coinbase) showed immediate follow-through but then reversed; no consistent [[post-earnings-drift|post-earnings drift]] in large cap [14:08–18:05]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Vertical spreads vs. naked options**: Prefer naked positions 85%+ of the time, but use [[vertical-spreads|verticals]] when [[gamma-exposure|gamma exposure]] is uncontrollable (e.g., 50-point daily moves); verticals move ~5¢ per $10 move, providing wiggle room [02:11–03:08]
- **Capital efficiency revolution**: Stock trading is obsolete for retail due to high share prices (e.g., $720 Nvidia = $72k for 100 shares); shift to [[capital-efficiency|capital-efficient]] [[vertical-spreads|spreads]] and [[zero-dte|zero-DTE]] products [19:31–20:14]
- **Product-agnostic trading**: Modern traders must apply the same [[edge|edge]] and [[risk-management|risk management]] across stocks, [[futures-vs-options|futures]], crypto, commodities, and indices; [[market-maker|market-making]] efficiency has leveled the playing field [20:14–20:56]
- **Event-based contracts**: Currently binary and less interesting to [[theta|theta]] traders, but regulatory constraints will eventually lift; no fundamental difference between trading Nvidia earnings (implied move ~10%) and macro events (unemployment, PPI) [23:47–25:57]
- **Sizing kills**: The timeless mistake; [[position-sizing|position sizing]] remains the primary risk control, more important than any other factor [27:08–28:15]
- **Learning curve acceleration**: Free content and tight, efficient markets have shortened the path from novice to competent trader, but this does not eliminate [[risk-tolerance|risk tolerance]] and [[emotional-discipline|emotional discipline]] challenges [29:26–30:12]

## Notable quotes

## Regime / context

Recorded 2024-02-24 during a period of elevated tech concentration (Nvidia, Microsoft, Apple dominating indices), rising bond yields (10-year ~4.3%), and debate over Fed rate-cut timing. Sosnoff's macro thesis reflects a view that equity valuations (especially AI-related) are disconnected from bond repricing and that mean reversion is overdue. His preference for [[russell-2000|small-cap]] outperformance assumes bond yields stabilize or decline, which would reduce the relative drag on lower-duration equities. The discussion of [[event-driven|event-based contracts]] and [[tokenization|tokenized trading]] is forward-looking and reflects regulatory uncertainty (SEC/Gary Gensler mentioned as a blocker).
