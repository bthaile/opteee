# GME Options Max Pain

## Summary

This video explains the concept of maximum pain—the strike price where the greatest notional value of options would expire worthless at expiration—and its relevance to GameStop's options market. The host demonstrates how to identify maximum pain using tools like maximumpain.com and thinkorswim, discusses the mechanics of market-maker hedging behavior, and reviews academic evidence suggesting that maximum pain effects are statistically significant, particularly in smaller, less-liquid stocks like GME.

## Key takeaways

- **Maximum pain definition** [02:39]: The strike price where the greatest value of options in an expiration cycle would expire worthless; assumes market makers are net sellers hedging their positions and incentivized to move price toward this level.
- **GME close at max pain** [00:00]: On the expiration date, GME closed at $24.97 with maximum pain calculated at $25—a suspicious proximity that warrants attention.
- **Volatility collapse** [00:56]: IV contracted sharply from above 200 to the high 100s, reflecting general market quiet and broad rotation out of top performers.
- **Market breadth deterioration** [01:23]: Net highs zeroed out; gross highs fell from 361 (two days prior) to 224 (previous day) to 55 (current), indicating broad market rotation into flight-to-safety assets.
- **How to find max pain** [05:01]: Use maximumpain.com or thinkorswim's intrinsic-value view; identify the strike where the lowest point of the open-interest curve sits.
- **Max pain is dynamic** [06:04]: The figure moves intraday as traders adjust positions; focus on max pain calculations on the day of expiration for best accuracy.
- **Effect strongest in small-cap, illiquid stocks** [06:51]: GME is a prime candidate because smaller markets require less capital to manipulate price; effect is harder to execute in large-cap products like SPY.
- **Academic evidence** [08:14]: A referenced study finds that a spread portfolio buying high-max-pain stocks and selling low-max-pain stocks generates statistically significant positive alpha; returns reverse after expiration week.
- **Manual calculation method** [09:23]: Multiply each strike's open interest by the strike price to find intrinsic value at expiration; the strike with the highest total intrinsic value is max pain.
- **Objectivity caveat** [04:21]: Avoid dismissing all price action as "rigged" or claiming "everything is priced perfectly"; identify gray areas where natural hedging behavior and incentive alignment both influence price.
