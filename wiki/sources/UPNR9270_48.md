# 0DTE Strategies for SMALL Trading Accounts

## Summary

Zero-DTE (same-day expiration) options offer a documented variance risk premium—the tendency for implied volatility to exceed realized volatility—but applying this edge to accounts under $110k requires careful position sizing and structural discipline. Eric walks through backtested entry rules, spread widths, and the critical trade-off between capturing the profit mechanism and managing max loss per trade without excessive discretion.

## Key takeaways

- **What zero-DTE is** [00:39]: Options expiring the same day they are traded; the profit mechanism is variance risk premium (IV > HV), not "fast easy money."

- **The signal: IV/HV ratio matters more than absolute IV level** [03:47]: Selling volatility when "it's high" is a common misconception. What matters is the *relationship* between implied and historic volatility; you can have high IV with even higher realized volatility and still lose.

- **Entry timing and robustness** [06:49]: Backtests show 13:30–14:00 ET entries tend to outperform; testing a 30-minute window demonstrates robustness around the time frame.

- **Wider spreads outperform in backtests** [08:03]: A 10-point iron condor (e.g., 20 Delta / 5 Delta) significantly outperforms a 5-point version (15 Delta / 5 Delta) in frictionless conditions, but margin requirements scale accordingly.

- **Margin and account sizing constraints** [09:03]: A 15 Delta / 5 Delta iron condor on SPX requires ~$4,000 margin; on a $5,000 account, this consumes the entire account and risks $3,265 per trade—far too much. Smaller accounts must use tighter deltas (10 Delta / 5 Delta) or accept suboptimal returns.

- **Fees and spreads destroy backtest performance** [09:43]: All backtested results are frictionless. Real-world transaction costs and bid-ask spreads will meaningfully contract returns; minimize legs and touches to the trade.

- **Path dependence and volatility clustering** [10:49]: When realized volatility breaches implied, it tends to stay elevated for several days. Discretionary skipping of trades during volatility expansion is acceptable *if carefully tracked*, because the strategy's edge depends on sufficient occurrences.

- **Win rate vs. loss magnitude** [14:18]: A 10 Delta short strangle averages ~$200 win / $465 loss; one loss wipes out two wins. A 15 Delta / 5 Delta spread averages $220 win / $714 loss—much harder to overcome without sufficient runway and occurrences.

- **European-style options required for expiration holds** [13:37]: Assignment risk on American-style options creates problems if you intend to let trades run to expiration. SPX (European) is preferable; XSP (tenth the size) is viable but has worse spreads.

- **Tax advantage: Section 1256** [13:57]: SPX and XSP qualify for 60/40 long-term/short-term treatment, improving after-tax returns.

- **Small-account viability** [12:15]: Zero-DTE *can* work for accounts under $110k, but requires accepting suboptimal (tighter) spreads to keep max loss per trade reasonable (~$200–$300 on a $5k–$10k account), which reduces the edge captured.
