# Why I Trade IWM vs SPY

## Summary

Eric compares the Russell 2000 ETF (IWM) to the S&P 500 ETF (SPY) and the Russell 2000 Index (RUT) across liquidity, dividend yield, diversification, capital efficiency, and underlying structure. He demonstrates why IWM is superior for his covered-strangle strategy: better sector diversification (2,000+ small-cap holdings vs. 500 large-cap), higher capital efficiency (~2.5% return on cash-secured puts vs. 2.04% for SPY), and the ability to take assignment on shares, which RUT (cash-settled) cannot provide.

## Key takeaways

- **Liquidity comparison** [01:38–02:24]: SPY trades 100M+ shares daily with penny-wide bid-ask spreads and extensive option expirations (weeklies, quarterlies). IWM is also highly liquid but slightly less so than SPY; both are pragmatically equivalent for active trading.

- **Dividend yield** [02:50–03:15]: SPY pays $1.64/share; IWM pays $0.67/share. For covered-strangle strategies that hold underlying, dividend income matters for total return.

- **Diversification: the core advantage** [07:51–12:10]: SPY's top 10 holdings comprise ~25% of the index, with Apple alone at 7.12%. IWM's top 10 holdings comprise only ~4%, with the largest at 0.38%. SPY is heavily concentrated in information technology (26% of index); IWM distributes more evenly across sectors (healthcare, industrials, tech, consumer discretionary all ~equal weight). This reduces unsystematic risk significantly.

- **Capital efficiency** [03:59–04:49, 12:31]: For a 42-DTE, 30-delta short put: SPY yields 2.04% return on cash-secured capital; IWM yields ~2.5%. Over time, this 0.5% difference compounds meaningfully across position sizing.

- **Why not RUT?** [05:17–06:31]: RUT is cash-settled (no underlying shares), so assignment cannot occur—incompatible with covered-strangle mechanics. RUT does offer Section 1256 tax benefits (60/40 long-term/short-term treatment), making it preferable for naked short puts/calls when not holding stock.

- **Core strategy alignment** [13:00–13:25]: IWM is one of Eric's largest portfolio contributors because it combines liquidity, dividend income, true diversification, and capital efficiency—all essential for systematic covered-strangle deployment.
