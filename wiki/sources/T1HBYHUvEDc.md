# 5 Options Selling Myths Destroying Your Returns

## Summary

This video deconstructs five widespread misconceptions about options selling that systematically destroy trader returns: selling when IV is high, treating selling as an inherent edge, using spreads to reduce risk, believing rolling avoids losses, and conflating high win rates with profitability. The host emphasizes that successful premium selling depends on capturing the variance risk premium (the spread between implied and realized volatility), directional accuracy, and positive expected value—not on volatility levels or mechanical rules.

## Key takeaways

### Myth 1: Sell options when volatility is high [00:00–05:13]
- **Volatility clustering** means IV can remain elevated for extended periods before mean-reverting; a 30–45 day strategy may not capture the reversion [01:12]
- The dominant force in short-premium strategies is **direction**, not vega; IV collapse doesn't save a position blown through by price movement [01:12]
- You trade **variance risk premium** (implied vs. realized volatility), not absolute IV levels [02:36]
- The "sweet spot" for selling is when IV is *low* but realized volatility is *even lower*—not at IV extremes [05:13]
- Avoid both extremes of the IV thermometer; the best selling opportunities often occur during low-IV regimes [05:13]

### Myth 2: Selling options is an inherent edge [05:13–06:35]
- The claim "90% of options expire worthless" is false; OCC data shows most options are traded before expiration [05:13]
- Selling is *not* magic; it requires an additional edge (e.g., directional skill or volatility forecasting) [06:35]
- **Premium chasing** (selling high-IV names for outsized returns) often forces you into volatile underlyings that move outside your range [06:35]

### Myth 3: Spreads reduce risk and improve profitability [06:35–09:48]
- Adding a short strike cap (vertical spread) massively decreases your edge; a 70% win-rate short put spread can still have negative expected value [07:44–08:44]
- Spreads technically reduce per-trade risk but *destroy* long-term profitability unless you have directional skill [08:44]
- If you're good at picking direction, **uncapped strategies** (long calls, naked shorts) often outperform spreads because they don't cap your winners [09:48]

### Myth 4: Rolling avoids losses [11:03–13:31]
- Rolling is simply multiple trades with linked accounting; it does *not* erase losses [11:03]
- A realized loss on the first leg is still a loss, even if the roll-to trade is profitable [11:03–12:21]
- The net P&L of a rolling campaign may appear positive, but the unrealized position can still be a loss; you often would have been better off closing and redeploying capital [12:21–13:31]

### Myth 5: High win rate = great strategy [13:31–15:53]
- A 70% win-rate strategy with small wins and large losses is net-negative over time [14:35]
- A 35% win-rate strategy with large wins and small losses is massively profitable [14:35]
- **Expected value** (average win × win rate − average loss × loss rate) is the only metric that matters [14:35]
- Beware traders claiming 90% win rates; it signals either dishonesty or lack of understanding [14:35]
- Selling strategies over-emphasize win rate; focus on positive expected return instead [15:53]

## Notable quotes

- "The dominant force in your strategy is still direction. It doesn't matter if IV collapses here and your Vega P&L looks good, but then the underlying just blasted right through your short strikes. You're still loose." [01:12]
- "Rolling is literally just multiple trades that the accounting is tied together." [11:03]
- "If you see some sort of guru, a YouTuber or somebody else that's like, hey, I have a 90% win rate. You immediately know something's wrong with that person." [14:35]
