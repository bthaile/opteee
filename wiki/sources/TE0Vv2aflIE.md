# Truth about rolling options

## Summary

Rolling options for a net credit does not avoid a loss—it merely defers accounting reality. Each roll closes one trade (realizing a loss) and opens a new one (creating unrealized P&L). Traders must abandon mental accounting gymnastics and accept that losing positions are a normal part of trading; the key is whether future trades can offset past realized losses.

## Key takeaways

- **Rolling ≠ avoiding loss** [00:00–00:21]: Rolling an option that is losing always realizes a loss on the closed leg, regardless of net credit received. The mental framing of "no loss if rolled for credit" is accounting fiction.
- **Two separate trades, one accounting** [00:21–00:48]: A roll is literally closing one trade (realized loss) and opening another (unrealized P&L). Traders conflate them mentally to avoid discomfort.
- **Accept losing positions as normal** [00:48–01:00]: Losing trades happen to all traders. The goal is to make money on future trades; past realized losses remain realized and cannot be retroactively erased by future performance.
- **Discomfort drives poor mental accounting** [00:21–00:48]: Traders avoid facing realized losses because it is psychologically uncomfortable, but this avoidance does not change the economic reality.

## Notable quotes

> "If you roll for a net credit you're avoiding a loss or you don't have a loss—the reality is you unbelievably are taking a loss every single time."

> "The sooner that traders can stop playing these stupid mental gymnastics in their head and get over the fact that there's a losing trade as part of what they're trying to do, it's literally just looking at reality as what it is."

This is a foundational psychology lesson applicable across all market regimes. The principle holds regardless of underlying volatility, market direction, or time frame.
