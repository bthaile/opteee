# GameStop Analysis & Update | Meme Stock Watch Ep47

## Summary

This episode provides a detailed technical and options analysis of GameStop following its recent earnings and debt offering. The host examines price action, implied volatility metrics, and the performance of a covered strangle position, while addressing viewer questions about option Greeks and their real-world behavior. The stock remains range-bound with low implied volatility percentile despite elevated raw IV, presenting a favorable environment for premium-selling strategies.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2025-07-13)

- **Price structure post-earnings** [03:29]–[05:26]: GameStop has entered a tight, rangebound phase following earnings and the debt offering. The floor near $22.15 is holding, but the stock lacks conviction for a sustained recovery rally. Recent oscillation shows a recovery move followed by a pullback that is "too far" for a healthy rebound pattern.

- **Correlation breakdown** [02:57]–[03:29]: GameStop is not moving in parallel with small-cap indices (Russell 2000), which is noteworthy given typical correlation patterns. This independence suggests idiosyncratic factors are driving price action.

- **Volume context** [01:04:08]–[01:04:32]: Current volume (8–12M shares) is elevated relative to lighter periods but insufficient to drive parabolic upside moves. This constrains near-term momentum.

- **Options chain positioning** [01:05:06]–[01:07:02]: Heavy concentration of open interest in August 1 calls, particularly at the $24 strike (134k contracts), suggests targeted positioning for a specific upside level. Most strikes cluster near-the-money ($20–$30 range).

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Implied volatility percentile vs. raw IV** [15:36]–[17:07]: GameStop's IV percentile is at zero (lowest in one year) while raw IV sits at ~60%, nearly double that of stable names like GE (38%) or TripleM (32.6%). This disconnect reveals that GameStop's volatility is structurally elevated but currently at cyclical lows—a key signal for premium-selling strategies.

- **Average Daily Range (ADR) and Average True Range (ATR) compression** [17:55]–[19:25]: ADR over 90 days is 4.8%, declining to 3.8% (30-day) and 3.2% (5-day), showing recent moves are "little tiny baby moves" compared to historical volatility. This compression reflects the post-earnings quiet period.

- **Covered strangle performance in sideways markets** [12:50]–[15:03]: The covered strangle is "absolutely smashing" buy-and-hold equivalents in rangebound environments. Current position (300 shares + 2 short $25 calls + 6 short $23 puts, 15 AUG expiry) is up 16.3% total return, 19.5% return on invested capital, with 75% capital utilization. This strategy captures volatility premium while limiting downside.

- **Delta, gamma, and theta interaction** [23:42]–[37:27]: When an underlying moves, option premium changes reflect multiple Greeks simultaneously. A $0.90 move in GME with a 0.56 delta call should yield ~$0.50 gain, but theta decay (daily loss of ~$0.013 per contract) and vega changes (IV compression) reduce realized gains. This is not a market failure but correct option pricing.

- **Gamma acceleration in out-of-the-money calls** [46:23]–[51:25]: Lower-delta calls (e.g., $48 calls at 0.21 delta) gain deltas more slowly initially but accelerate faster as the underlying rises due to higher gamma. At-the-money calls (0.55 delta) gain deltas faster early but cap at 100 deltas. For large expected moves, OTM calls can outperform ATM calls; for modest moves, ATM calls dominate. Position structure must match expected move magnitude and timing.

- **Ratio call diagonal strategy** [51:25]–[52:12]: Blends long higher-delta calls (base position) with short near-term lower-delta calls and long OTM "kicker" calls to balance payoff across move scenarios. This approach optimizes for uncertainty about move timing and magnitude.

- **Golden cross historical performance** [01:16:58]–[01:18:10]: Analysis of 19 historical golden cross instances in GME shows median 10-day return of 0.31%, 20-day return of −7.36%, and 60-day return of −6.21%. Average returns are skewed by one outlier (likely May 2024 squeeze). Conclusion: golden crosses show no reliable edge in GME.

- **Variance risk premium as an edge** [01:31:00]–[01:38:47]: Options themselves carry no inherent edge, but traders with an edge in directional prediction can amplify that edge via options. Variance risk premium (the tendency for realized volatility to underperform implied volatility) is a documented, monetizable market effect that can only be traded via options. A breakout strategy using 5 long calls ($2,700 each, $14.2k total) on a 60% move yields $26,675 profit vs. $8,520 for 100 shares—a 3× amplification.
