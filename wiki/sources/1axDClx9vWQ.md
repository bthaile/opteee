# Maximize Your Gains: TSLA Ratio Call Diagonal Explained

## Summary

Eric walks through a live ratio call diagonal trade in Tesla that generated approximately $30k profit over two days, emphasizing the process and mistakes rather than the outcome. The trade was built on a technical breakout with elevated volume, using September long calls paired with July short calls to capture upside while managing gap risk. The video focuses on identifying confluence signals, position management, and the drawbacks of the ratio structure in fast-moving markets.

## Key takeaways

- **Confluence over single signals** [02:02–02:25]: Opportunity identification relies on multiple converging factors—not one indicator. In TSLA, the trigger was a range breakout on elevated buying volume, followed by aggressive moving-average slopes and strong volume support.

- **Technical setup** [02:53–03:35]: TSLA broke above a range on the 26th with above-average volume; the next days showed sideways consolidation, then heavy volume with a break above the 200-day moving average. The 1st saw a powerful move with early-day high volume, signaling entry.

- **Initial position structure** [07:04–07:24]: Opened 25 September 200 calls (split into tester + scale), then sold 19 July 215 calls against them. This is a ratio call diagonal designed to generate credit upfront while maintaining long-dated upside.

- **Profit target and stop placement** [07:44–09:04]: Initial target was above the +2 standard deviation channel; downside stops were the low of the day (first) and the gap top (second). After the strong move on the 1st, the stop was tightened, converting it to a lower-risk trade.

- **Management plan: flatten, not scale** [09:26–09:49]: Original intent was to exit the entire position if the move hit, not to scale in/out. This is because Tesla is gappy and can reverse sharply; taking profits early was preferred over holding for a runner.

- **The ratio call diagonal headwind** [11:17–12:11]: Closed the short 215 calls for a $6k loss when the market gapped up with strength. This was the cost of the ratio structure—the short calls became a drag. However, the shorts also provided portfolio protection by dampening early drops and extending trade duration.

- **Exit and P&L** [12:33–12:56]: Closed the 200 calls for a $40 credit (vs. $26 debit entry), netting ~$27k realized P&L on a 41% return on invested capital. The trade was a textbook breakout/trend-follow win.

- **Post-trade assessment** [13:21–13:41]: Once the move became overextended, Eric exited and moved on. Overextended price tends to normalize; holding for further upside would require a higher-probability stop, making the risk/reward unfavorable.

## Notable quotes

> "I'm not very good at identifying opportunities. You can't look at just the good ones and say how did you find that good one because there's thousands of bad ones I come across."

> "You can't assess trades like that. You have to assess the performance of the process and the system over the long run. You can't overemphasize any one single trade."
