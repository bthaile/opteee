# Technical Analysis for Trading Options | Guide for Beginners

## Summary

Eric presents a systematic two-part framework for applying technical analysis to options trading, emphasizing efficiency, applicability, and confluence of evidence over individual indicators. The methodology prioritizes price and volume as the foundation for probabilistic decision-making, using moving averages, linear regression channels, volume profile, support/resistance, and market breadth to build a multi-timeframe picture rather than relying on any single tool.

## Key takeaways

### Methodology principles
- **Efficiency first** [01:22]: Avoid deep dives into individual candlestick patterns; focus on tools with proven efficacy across thousands of hours of research and 5,000+ products.
- **Applicability matters** [02:06]: Align technical analysis to the thesis duration—intraday charts are irrelevant for a one-year thesis; adjust tools to match strategy timeframe.
- **Context before precision** [02:30]: Start zoomed out on broad market behavior (price and volume), then zoom in to pinpoint specific areas of interest for profit/loss targets.
- **Confluence over single signals** [03:54]: Combine multiple tools (moving averages, momentum, volume) to form a complete picture; no single indicator is the "end all be all."
- **Say no 98% of the time** [04:37]: Successful traders reject most opportunities and only take trades with compelling risk/reward; shift from forcing trades to finding great trades.

### Technical tools

**Moving averages** [05:23]
- Long-term: 200 or 150-day
- Mid-term: 50-day
- Short-term: 21 and 9-day (adjustable to 7, 11, or 22)
- Key metrics: width between averages (strength), slope (momentum), orientation (9 > 21 > 50 > 200 > price = bullish), crossovers aligned to strategy duration

**Linear regression channels** [08:17]
- 50% and 100% bands (analogous to ±1σ and ±2σ standard deviation channels)
- Assess trend direction (positive/flat/negative slope) across multiple timeframes
- Price extension beyond 50% band suggests overextension and potential reversion (probabilistic, not deterministic)

**Volume profile and average volume** [10:31]
- Point of control (highest transaction frequency) acts as dynamic support/resistance
- Nodes (congestion zones) indicate areas where price may congest; gaps indicate areas of rapid movement
- Volume average (30-day typical): rallies on low volume are untrustworthy; rallies on outsized volume suggest support formation
- Price can fall under its own weight; upside moves require outsized volume

**Support and resistance** [13:43]
- Identify congestion zones from volume profile and moving averages
- Recency matters: recent congestion is more powerful than old levels
- Number of touches amplifies strength; horizontal price levels more useful than trend lines for context

**Oscillators (secondary)** [15:40]
- Money flow index or MACD: watch for flattening, divergence (price rallying while oscillator declining = suspicious), and turning points
- Less concerned with overbought/oversold extremes; more interested in divergence signals

**Market breadth (net highs/lows)** [16:38]
- NYSE-wide indicator: compare stocks making new highs vs. lows
- Add 10-day and 50-day moving averages for context
- Bullish when net highs positive; bearish when negative
- Assess across multiple timeframes to avoid false signals from short-term noise

**IV/HV plot** [18:06]
- For options traders: visualize implied vs. historic volatility gap
- Prefer implied > historic (variance risk premium environment)
- Critical context for earnings and volatility premium strategies

### Process and validation
- **Backtesting framework** [19:27]: Create Excel sheet with random stocks, apply technical analysis columns (moving averages, volume profile, etc.), record bullish/bearish/neutral bias, track accuracy to identify what works for your style
