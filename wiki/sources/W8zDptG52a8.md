# Russell 2000 Fed Rate Cut Trade Breakdown

## Summary

Eric walks through a directional futures trade on the Russell 2000 executed around the December 10 Fed rate decision. Using a probability discrepancy (88.6% cut vs. 11.4% no-cut) as the primary signal, he entered 15 Russell futures contracts at ~2332 before the announcement, targeting asymmetric upside from under-positioned market participants. The trade yielded ~$12K profit (240.9 points) in under two hours, exiting at 2572.9 via short-term moving average stops.

## Key takeaways

- **Probability discrepancy as edge** [01:14]: The market was still split 88.6% / 11.4% on the rate cut despite it being widely expected, signaling incomplete positioning and potential for a sharp move if the cut occurred.
- **Small-cap rate sensitivity** [00:00]: Russell 2000 outperformed other indices (+1.4%) on the day because small caps are more rate-sensitive than large caps.
- **Pre-catalyst entry** [03:58]: Entered ~1055 Pacific (before the 12:00 statement release) to capture the initial move and front-run early information flow; used futures for pure directional leverage.
- **Tight risk architecture** [03:58]: Placed stop at 2328.5 (total risk ~$2,625) despite $220K margin deployed, because the trade was binary—either the idea plays out (no downside) or it doesn't (quick exit).
- **Breathing room vs. tight stops** [05:15]: Positioned stop below recent consolidation to allow the trade room to work, not directly at entry.
- **Profit management via moving averages** [06:29]: Slid stops up using a mix of short-term moving averages on the 5-minute chart, exiting when price reversed below the consolidation zone on the 15-minute chart.
- **Return on invested capital** [07:46]: ~10% return in under 2 hours (~$12K on $220K margin); the key metric is capital efficiency and time-to-execution, not absolute dollar size.
- **Leverage as brute force** [09:06]: Expected only a 1–2% move in Russell, so used futures leverage to amplify a modest directional edge into meaningful P&L.

## Notable quotes

> "If I was right and my idea played out, it should not go down. Literally at all. It should just go up. If my idea didn't pan out and let's say it had a down tick and then it ran up, I would have gotten stopped out, but that wouldn't have been my idea."
