# GameStop Technical Analysis, Options Positioning & Market Structure Deep Dive

## Summary

Eric conducts a detailed technical and options analysis of GameStop's ~10% unexplained intraday decline on January 14, 2025, examining price action, insider transactions, institutional positioning, and options chain structure. He argues that GameStop violates standard technical analysis norms due to structural market factors, then reviews a Carl Hagberg presentation on naked short selling, phantom shares, and voting dilution as systemic issues affecting the stock.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2025-01-17)

- **Unexplained 10% decline [00:00–24:31]**: GameStop dropped ~10% on January 14 with no clear fundamental catalyst; insider transactions (Daniel Moore, Haymond) on January 6 were modest; institutional ownership showed expansion, not contraction; store closures (150 locations) occurred in late 2024, not on the 14th.
- **GME does not follow standard TA [14:33–15:53]**: While GME often respects supply-demand relationships, its most pivotal moves violate standard technical analysis norms. The January 14 drop had no predictive posts or conjecture beforehand, unlike typical catalyst-driven moves.
- **Options chain is mellow, not front-loaded [32:02–34:51]**: Call OI ~459K, put OI ~157K; massive expiration cycle just cleared (~500K calls). Next dominant expiration is February 21 (73K calls). February 7 shows elevated put skew and downside volatility pricing, but no clear catalyst identified.
- **February 7 week pricing something unknown [34:51–36:25]**: Market is pricing elevated volatility for the week of February 7, particularly on the put side (downside skew), but no earnings or announced event explains it.
- **Institutional buying likely reflects optionality [39:59–41:14]**: Institutions may be accumulating GME because downside is priced in (cash pile, loyal base) while upside optionality (e.g., strategic pivot, Bitcoin purchase) remains uncapped.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Pattern-seeking bias in retail [17:31–20:15]**: Human brains are innately programmed to identify patterns; confirmation bias leads traders to onboard data supporting their thesis while discarding contradictory signals. This is especially dangerous in GME, where retail often lacks rigorous backtesting.
- **Technical analysis limitations on GME [19:01–20:15]**: Eric ran comprehensive TA studies (moving averages, standard deviation channels, RSI, MFI, stochastic, MACD, candle patterns) and found poor predictive value on GME compared to other equities. Selection bias in data interpretation hurts traders more than it helps.
- **Naked short selling and phantom shares [57:27–01:08:34]**: When shorts borrow and resell shares, the original lender retains voting rights; excessive short selling creates "phantom shares" and phantom votes, decoupling debits from credits. This inflates apparent liquidity and can dilute voting power 2–3× the actual share count.
- **Margin account voting suppression [01:09:57–01:11:11]**: Retail investors with margin accounts often lose voting rights without compensation, because brokers lend their shares to short sellers. Institutional investors and mutual funds profit from this lending while individual shareholders remain in the dark.
- **Infinite liquidity cheat code [58:25–59:34]**: Unfettered securities lending allows shorts to keep borrowing against borrowed shares, creating an infinite supply of tradeable shares at lower prices. This is a structural market problem, not a mystery—the math is clear.
