# Building a Trading Approach - Outlier Options Trading Foundations

## Summary

This foundational session establishes the three pillars of professional trading: identifying profit mechanisms (the specific market effects you exploit), managing risk across portfolio/strategy/trade levels, and integrating them into portfolio management. Eric walks through concrete examples—earnings trades, breakouts, covered strangles—to show how casual traders conflate strategy structure with profitability, and how professional traders decompose market behavior into tradable conditions and size risk accordingly.

## Key takeaways

### Profit mechanisms
- **Profit mechanisms are not strategy structures.** Selling credit spreads is a structure; it does not inherently make money. You must identify the *market effect* you're capturing: price direction, volatility contraction, drift, momentum, etc. [01:17–02:41]
- **Price direction is not monolithic.** A 16% move in SPY over 19 trading days behaves differently from an 87% move in Palantir over the same period. Catalyst-driven gaps differ from drift. You must double-click into the specific condition. [10:28–13:32]
- **Earnings creates four distinct tradable conditions:** IV expansion (2 weeks pre-earnings), IV contraction (post-earnings), near-term price catalyst, and post-earnings drift. Each requires different strategy. [37:57–39:37]
- **Theta is a profit mechanism, but not an edge by itself.** Volatility contraction, directional drift, and momentum are others. Your first job is brainstorming which market effects you can reliably identify and trade. [08:44]
- **Going further out in time reduces vega sensitivity** to IV crush while maintaining directional exposure—a key trade-off when buying calls into earnings. [30:05–31:48]

### Risk management (three levels)
- **Portfolio level:** Define your maximum acceptable drawdown (e.g., 25% or 50%) and institute circuit breakers. Balance risk tolerance against return objectives. [39:06–42:52]
- **Strategy level:** Paper-trade your strategy to establish average max drawdown per contract, then use that as a planning factor for position sizing. [48:10–50:44]
- **Trade level:** Set profit targets and stop-loss levels *specific to the strategy*, not via fixed risk-to-reward ratios. A breakout strategy (50% win rate, large average win) differs from a short-put strategy (high win rate, small average loss). [50:44–59:56]
- **Risk-to-reward ratios are strategy-dependent.** The "2x risk-to-reward" rule from day trading does not apply universally. Misapplying it to the wrong strategy causes you to exit profitable trades prematurely. [55:06–59:56]

### Portfolio management
- **Segment your annual return goal into monthly targets.** If you want 10% annual return on a $100k account, that's ~$833/month. Deploy minimum viable risk to hit that target; scale up only if market conditions are favorable. [42:52–45:33]
- **Core + speculative allocation.** Eric runs a covered strangle core (long beta, leveraged sometimes) and rotates speculative positions based on market conditions. His core contributed only 1% YTD; other profit mechanisms made up the difference. [19:23–22:26]
- **Avoid hypercollinear risk.** Selling naked puts on Nvidia, Meta, and Amazon simultaneously creates correlated drawdown risk; a market crash hits all three at once. [45:33–47:01]

### Execution and expectations
- **First two years: target greater than zero.** Your goal is to develop a solid process, not to make a fortune. Treat it like learning jiu-jitsu: master the fundamentals before expecting mastery. [01:06:20–01:08:54]
- **Manage emotions, don't eliminate them.** Emotions are unavoidable; focus on managing them through a disciplined process. [01:10:13]
- **Paper-trade before risking capital.** Everything up to live trading requires zero dollars. [59:56–01:01:35]
- **Use alerts, not limit orders.** Resting limit orders on options get filled at terrible prices. Set price alerts and enter manually when conditions are met. [01:15:38]
