# 13 Options Trading Lies You've Been Told

## Summary

This video debunks 13 commonly repeated misconceptions about options trading, ranging from "90% of options expire worthless" to "you can't go broke taking profits." The host clarifies the distinction between technically true statements and their misleading implications, emphasizing that understanding the *why* behind each claim is critical for sound trading decisions. Key themes include the relationship between implied and realized volatility, the psychology of position management, and the dangers of clickbait trading narratives.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **90% of options expire worthless is false** [00:00]: ~55–60% of opened contracts close before expiration; only ~30–35% expire worthless. The implication—that selling is inherently superior—is the real misconception.

- **Selling [[theta]] is not inherently an edge** [02:28]: [[theta]] is merely time decay measurement. Edge exists only when [[implied-volatility]] exceeds [[realized-volatility]] over the same timeframe. When realized vol is higher, selling can lose money despite positive theta.

- **"Sell puts to buy stock at a discount" conflates two separate ideas** [05:02]: Selling a 30-delta [[short-put]] gives defined profit but undefined downside risk. If assigned after a sharp drop, you buy at the strike (e.g., $592) when spot is lower ($570), paying a premium to current price. To genuinely capture a discount, sell higher-delta (50-delta) puts to increase assignment probability and collect more extrinsic value.

- **Long options profit before strike price is reached** [08:19]: You do not need the underlying to reach your strike at expiration to make money. As soon as the underlying appreciates (isolating [[delta]]), the call gains value. Theta and volatility changes are the trade-off; buying further out in time (84 days vs. 21 days) reduces theta drag.

- **"You can't go broke taking profits" is false** [10:29]: If you take 9 winning trades at $50 each ($450) but lose $475 on one loss, your net is negative. [[disposition-effect]] (taking winners too fast, letting losers run) is reinforced by this saying. Manage trades by [[expected-return]], not aphorisms.

- **Buying vs. selling: neither is inherently better** [11:38]: Both have use cases and pros/cons. Context determines which is appropriate.

- **Options are zero-sum, but the inference is misleading** [12:42]: While one side's gain is the other's loss, the counterparty is typically a [[market-maker]] earning money from [[bid-ask-spread]] and liquidity provision, not from directional conviction. A short call seller may be bullish and collecting premium on long shares, not betting against you.

- **Emulating institutions is a trap** [14:58]: Institutions (market makers, hedge funds) play a different game—they profit from liquidity provision and risk management, not speculation. Retail traders cannot replicate their access, infrastructure, or mandate. Adopt their *process* (rigor, checks, training) but not their strategies.

- **Institutions are not "out to get" retail** [16:04]: Institutions compete with each other; retail is incidental. Adopting a victim mindset is self-defeating.

- **Clickbait "$1,000/day in 10 minutes" sets false expectations** [17:13]: Such claims are designed to attract clicks, not reflect repeatable reality. High trading failure rates exist precisely because this narrative is false.

- **Rolling does not avoid losses** [19:24]: Rolling is closing one trade and opening another. Whatever [[realized-vs-unrealized-pnl]] was realized on the first trade is locked in; the new trade may offset it, but rolling creates no "free money."

- **Trading is hard overall, but execution is easy** [20:37]: The difficulty lies in [[process-over-outcome]]—building strategies, planning, and discipline. Once a plan is defined and trusted, the act of clicking the button is simple execution, like a well-practiced athlete in competition.
