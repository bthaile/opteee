# The Melt Up has Started w/ David Hunter | The Outlier Podcast Ep54

## Summary

David Hunter, a macro strategist, argues that the S&P 500 has entered a parabolic "melt up" phase following a symmetrical inverted head-and-shoulders bottom at ~4,100 in late 2023. He projects the index reaching 6,000 with high odds of 7,000 by Q1 2024—a ~50% move in months—before a deflationary bust and 80% drawdown in 2024 driven by central bank policy error, excessive leverage, and a rolling-over economy. The melt up is underpinned by the Fed's pivot from tightening to pause/potential cuts, falling rates, and contracting oil and dollar; the bust will be triggered by credit contraction and financial stress as the economy weakens faster than policymakers recognize.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2023-12-01)

- **Melt up underway** [01:51–02:31]: After a perfect retrace to 4,100 (inverted H&S), the market has bounced off trend support and is rallying toward 4,600 with steep momentum. Hunter expects minimal 8–10% corrections until the top; 3–5% pullbacks are possible but unlikely to derail the move.
- **Fed pivot is the catalyst** [05:20–06:28]: The street has shifted from expecting more hikes to pricing in a pause and potential cuts. Fed officials are now entertaining rate cuts if inflation moderates or the economy weakens—a dramatic headwind-to-tailwind reversal in weeks.
- **Rate environment collapsing** [07:19–07:47]: The 10-year yield has fallen ~75 bps in one month (from ~5% to ~4.25%), signaling the street's view that the economy has "stopped on a dime." Q3 GDP was revised up to 5.2%, but forward indicators (auto sales, housing) are deteriorating rapidly.
- **Recession risk underestimated** [08:12–09:05]: Auto sales and housing sales are "pretty crummy"; credit card delinquencies are at 2008–2009 levels despite no official recession. Hunter warns that recessions can happen "fast when they happen"—citing Sept 2008 when economists called soft landing weeks before Lehman.
- **Credit card delinquencies spike** [16:23–18:28]: Delinquency rates are worse than 2008–2009, reflecting a "have and have-not" economy. Lower-income consumers are exhausting pandemic-era savings and government aid; credit card usage for groceries signals budget stress. This is a precursor to the bust.
- **Oil and dollar rolling over** [23:41–24:54]: Crude has fallen from ~95 to mid-70s; Hunter expects low 60s or 60 before a bounce to 70s, then a drop toward 30 in the bust. Energy stocks (XLE) are lagging; he targets XLE at 20 or below in the bust (vs. 83 now).
- **Sector leadership in melt up** [28:11–31:32]: Russell 2000 (target 3,000), semiconductors/SMH (target 300, ~2x from 162), miners, industrials, and financials will lead. Defensive sectors (healthcare, staples, utilities) will lag. Home builders will outperform despite housing peak due to rate-buy-down margins.
- **Metals poised to run** [53:19–57:34]: Gold is near all-time highs and will break above 2,100 to 3,000 within 6–7 months; silver will run from ~20 to 60–75 in the same window. Copper targets $6 pre-bust (vs. ~3.70 now). Miners have thin liquidity and will see sharp moves once institutions allocate.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Inverted head-and-shoulders pattern** [01:26–01:51]: A symmetrical reversal pattern that marked the bottom; the market bounced off trend support and is now rallying toward the neckline (4,600).
- **Melt up definition** [02:31–03:17]: A parabolic, very steep rally covering "an awful lot of ground in a matter of months"—not a straight line but steep with minor 3–5% pullbacks.
- **Fed policy error and lags** [09:54–10:44]: Central banks tighten until they see results, but with 6–9 month lags, they end up "many months too late" recognizing they went too far. The market discounts this faster than economists.
- **Market as predictor vs. consensus** [10:44–11:34]: The market in aggregate (law of large numbers) is a better predictor of economic rollover than Wall Street strategist consensus. It's already pricing in dollar weakness, rate cuts, and economic slowdown.
- **Backward-looking GDP vs. forward indicators** [12:27–14:05]: GDP is a lagging indicator; the economy can shift quarter-to-quarter. Auto sales, housing, credit delinquencies, and consumer spending patterns are real-time signals of margin deterioration.
- **Deflationary bust mechanics** [45:11–46:15]: A deflationary bust gives central banks "almost infinite ability to print money" because deflation lags inflation by ~2 years. They will prioritize saving the system over long-term inflation risk, printing trillions.
- **Central bank response scale** [48:12–49:26]: Hunter expects a ~$20 trillion Fed balance sheet expansion (vs. ~$5 trillion in 2020), matched by ~$20 trillion in new government debt/stimulus programs. This will be rolled out reluctantly, month by month, as crises emerge.
- **Leverage and fragility** [36:37–37:23]: Pandemic-era savings, government aid, student-debt moratoriums, and supply-chain disruptions created fragility. As these unwind (savings depleted, moratorium ended), the system becomes vulnerable to a "brick wall" moment.
- **Credit contraction as trigger** [39:15–40:57]: Bank lending is dropping, reverse repos are down (banks hoarding liquidity), and credit spreads remain narrow—precursors to a credit event. Hunter expects Q2 2024 (or sooner) for visible stress.
- **Bust vs. bare market** [35:00–35:25]: A "bust" refers to the economy and financial system; a "bear market" is stock-specific. Hunter is calling for both: an 80% stock drawdown and a systemic financial crisis.
- **Long-term super-cycle** [51:00–52:11]: We are at the end of an 80–90 year super-cycle between two depressions. The melt up, bust, and subsequent inflation cycle (25% inflation by decade's end) are part of a structural reset.

## Regime / context

**Date:** Early December 2023. The S&P 500 has just bounced from ~4,100 (late October 2023) and is rallying toward 4,600. The 10-year yield has fallen ~75 bps in one month; Fed funds futures are pricing near-zero probability of further hikes and rising odds of cuts in 2024. This is Hunter's seventh or eighth appearance on the Outlier Podcast; he and Eric catch up quarterly to track macro thesis evolution.

**Caveats:** Hunter's 2024 bust forecast is extreme (80% drawdown, $20 trillion Fed expansion, 25% inflation by 2030). He acknowledges he could be wrong on magnitude and timing (Q2 2024 is a guess, not a hard call). The melt up is underway, but the bust is still months away; precursors (credit delinquencies, lending contraction) are visible but not yet acute. Traders should size risk accordingly and not assume the bust will arrive on schedule.
