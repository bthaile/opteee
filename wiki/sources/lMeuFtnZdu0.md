# Hedging and Regret | Live Stock Market Analysis

## Summary

Eric explores the psychological roots of loss aversion and how they distort portfolio construction, then dissects three common hedging mistakes: buying long-dated puts that drift out-of-the-money as markets rise, overhedging with short deltas that bleed off in bull markets, and selling covered calls that cap upside far more than they protect downside. The core thesis: hedges should be viewed as drag, sized selectively, and never expected to profit.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2026-03-14)

- **Market structure**: SPY spends ~70% of time within 5% of all-time highs; forward returns remain positive even at deeper drawdowns [29:43]
- **Regime context**: Long-term uptrend dominates; markets "take the elevator down and stairs up" [25:22]

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Loss aversion is hardwired**: Humans fear losses below a survival threshold far more than equivalent gains above it; this bias persists even in surplus [11:09–17:44]
- **Portfolio-level hedging**: Define the *purpose* and *tenor* of the hedge first; then choose instruments (direct: puts/calls on index; indirect: uncorrelated long positions) [32:24–37:34]
- **Trade-level hedging**: Hedge specific risks (e.g., earnings) with matching expiration; avoid perpetual hedges—they signal oversizing [38:46–39:32]
- **Direct vs. indirect hedges**: Direct hedges (long puts) are expensive and should lose; indirect hedges (e.g., long crude oil offsetting long tech) are preferable and align with other trading ideas [33:18–36:39]
- **The deductible problem**: Long-dated puts bought at 50-delta become 06-delta as markets grind higher; the "deductible" (% drop needed to profit) widens from ~5% to ~25%, requiring constant rebalancing [45:22–49:15]
- **Covered-call trap**: Selling calls saves ~$800 on a 5% down move but costs ~$1,300 on a 5% up move—asymmetric risk-reward that caps the compensation for downside risk [55:00–01:02:38]
- **Hedge management discipline**: If you close a profitable hedge, you lose protection exactly when you need it; treat hedge + base position as a pairs trade to avoid premature exit [52:26–53:44]
- **Oversizing and hedging correlation**: Constant hedging signals the portfolio is too large; reduce position size instead of layering expensive hedges [39:53–40:18]
- **Realistic return expectations**: 30% returns with 15% max drawdown is not a linear trade-off; stacking risk-reward requires accepting volatility [21:56–23:30]
