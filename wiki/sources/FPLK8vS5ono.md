# Options Quiz | Options Trading for Beginners Pt14

## Summary

Eric conducts an interactive quiz covering foundational and intermediate options concepts: covered vs. naked options, defined vs. undefined risk, intrinsic value, delta mechanics, implied volatility, volatility surfaces, market-maker function, and psychological biases like the disposition effect. The session emphasizes the distinction between theoretical and practical risk limits, the role of supply-and-demand in price discovery, and the critical importance of process-over-outcome thinking in trading.

## Key takeaways

### Covered vs. naked options [03:17–06:03]
- A **covered option** is a short option paired with something that reduces total risk (e.g., short call + long shares = covered call).
- A **naked option** is a short option standing alone with no offsetting position.
- Context determines coverage, not the number of legs; multiple legs can all be naked (e.g., short strangle).

### Defined vs. undefined risk [07:46–17:04]
- **Naked short puts** are colloquially called "undefined risk" but are actually defined: a stock can only fall to zero, so the floor is the strike price (or zero for the stock).
- **Naked short calls** are theoretically unlimited but practically capped by broker margin requirements and liquidation thresholds.
- Brokers enforce defined risk limits; your account will be liquidated before losses truly become infinite.

### Intrinsic value calculation [23:09–25:35]
- Intrinsic value = how far in the money an option is.
- For calls: stock price − strike (if positive).
- For puts: strike − stock price (if positive).
- Out-of-the-money options have zero intrinsic value.

### Delta and leverage [26:25–30:31]
- On a 1% move, a 50-delta option always outperforms a 10-delta option in absolute dollars.
- The 10-delta shows higher *percentage* return because the premium paid is lower.
- Traders must balance gross dollar moves, percent returns, and capital outlay when sizing positions.

### Market inefficiencies vs. mispricings [32:40–39:01]
- **Mispricing**: a temporary, one-off price error (e.g., a $20 bill on the sidewalk).
- **Inefficiency**: a persistent, known anomaly with compensatory risk (e.g., volatility risk premium, post-earnings drift, window dressing).
- Inefficiencies are tradable but competitive; mispricings are rare and fleeting.

### Implied volatility [44:07–48:36]
- IV is backed out from market option prices using models like Black-Scholes; it is not the last unknown but the variable we solve for.
- **Forecast volatility**: your own estimate of what IV should be, based on a model or methodology.
- **Volatility term structure**: IV across different expirations at a fixed strike.
- **Volatility smile**: IV across different strikes within a single expiration.
- **Volatility surface**: IV across both strikes and expirations (2D or 3D visualization).

### Market-maker function [58:10–01:09:15]
- Market makers' primary job is to **provide liquidity**, not set prices.
- Prices are set by supply and demand (buyers and sellers); market makers are forced to price competitively by other participants.
- If a market maker prices too wide, competitors will undercut and capture volume.
- Market makers manage inventory risk by adjusting bid/ask sizes and spreads based on their book.
- Retail traders typically *consume* liquidity (hit the bid/lift the offer); resting orders *provide* liquidity.

### Disposition effect [01:16:22–01:21:57]
- The tendency to cut winners too short and let losers run—driven by fear of loss, desire to lock in gains, and overconfidence.
- This is a documented cognitive bias that undermines long-term returns.
- **Solution**: focus on process and expected returns over individual trade outcomes; adopt Bayesian thinking (update priors with new data).

### Bayesian thinking and conviction [01:27:54–01:30:46]
- **Strong convictions, loosely held**: develop a thesis rigorously, then update it when new evidence contradicts it.
- Traders often conflate changing their mind with being wrong, leading to attachment to losing ideas.
- Updating a thesis based on new data is intellectual strength, not weakness.

### Leverage and volatility drag [01:54:11–02:02:06]
- Leveraged ETFs (e.g., TQQQ, SSO, QLD) incur daily rebalancing costs (~4% annually) and volatility drag.
- TQQQ has returned ~34% CAGR but with 58% standard deviation; SPY has lower returns but much lower volatility.
- Two-times leverage (SSO, QLD) offers a better risk-adjusted profile for long-term holds than three-times.
- Traders must be prepared to hold through severe drawdowns (e.g., 70–80%) if using leveraged products.

## Notable quotes

> "Nobody's ever gone broke taking profits—but if you're only taking profits, how would you functionally go broke? The problem is, if you're in the process of taking profits, I guarantee you're booking losses as well." [01:19:05]

> "The process of making money trading is literally easy once you understand how it works. What's difficult is making sure we're measuring things objectively and not just focusing on outcomes." [01:21:27]
