# Implied Vol and Realized Vol when Selling Options

## Summary

When selling options, the critical edge is not betting on implied volatility mean reversion in isolation, but rather exploiting the *spread* between implied volatility (what you sold) and realized volatility (what actually occurs). Even if implied vol declines favorably, if realized vol trends directionally and breaches your short strike, losses can still materialize—a crucial distinction for short-volatility traders.

## Key takeaways

- **The real trade is the IV/RV spread, not IV mean reversion alone** [00:27] — selling options profits when implied vol exceeds realized vol, not simply when IV reverts downward.
- **Realized vol can trend directionally despite IV decline** [00:00] — even with a favorable 35% → 30% IV move, if realized vol trends in one direction and violates your short strike, you lose money.
- **Short-volatility traders must monitor both components** — implied vol behavior and realized vol behavior are distinct; conflating them leads to false confidence in short premium positions.

## Notable quotes

> "Remember what you're actually trading, which more often than not is not actually the mean reverting component of implied volatility, but you're typically trading the relative relationship between implied V and realized."
