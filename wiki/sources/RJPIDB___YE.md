# Option Liquidity and Bid Ask Spread Explained: A Complete Guide

## Summary

Option liquidity is the ability to transact at a fair price when you want. This video provides a practical framework for assessing liquidity across different underlyings, strikes, and expirations, emphasizing that most retail traders focus on entry liquidity but neglect exit liquidity—a critical mistake that can erode profits. The bid-ask spread, expiration cycles, open interest, underlying price, volatility, and moneyness all materially impact your ability to exit a position at a reasonable price.

## Key takeaways

- **Liquidity definition and entry vs. exit [00:20–01:56]**: Liquidity is the ability to transact at a fair price. Most traders analyze entry liquidity well but fail to conceptualize exit liquidity, leading to forced price concessions when closing profitable positions.

- **Expiration cycles as a liquidity filter [02:13–02:58]**: Products with weekly expiration cycles (roughly 550 underlyings) are typically more liquid than those with only standard third-Friday expirations. Weekly availability signals sufficient volume and demand.

- **Bid-ask spread mechanics [03:44–04:39]**: The bid is the highest price someone will pay; the offer is the lowest price someone will sell. Market makers capture this spread. Smart traders can sometimes fill at the mid-price, but instant fills require hitting the bid or offer.

- **Underlying price impact on spreads [05:31–06:20]**: More expensive underlyings (e.g., MSTR at ~$1,500) naturally have wider bid-ask spreads in absolute points. Cheaper underlyings (e.g., QLD at ~$86) may appear tighter but often have fewer expiration cycles and lower total open interest, making them less liquid overall.

- **Strike type and round-number bias [07:33–08:25]**: Standard strike prices and round numbers (20, 25, 30) attract more open interest and tighter spreads due to human psychology. Half-strikes and non-round prices have less liquidity.

- **Moneyness and time decay effects [08:49–09:34]**: At-the-money options are more liquid than deep in-the-money or far out-of-the-money options. Options within 30 days to expiration see more activity. Standard expirations (third Friday) are more liquid than weekly expirations.

- **Volatility expansion widens spreads [11:02–11:30]**: As implied volatility rises, bid-ask spreads widen, acting as a natural brake on runaway markets. This is critical for exit planning: if you expect higher volatility at exit, budget for wider spreads.

- **Benchmarking liquidity across underlyings [11:50–13:11]**: Create a bid-ask ratio by dividing the spread by the strike price. Compare the same delta and DTE across your target underlying and a liquid benchmark (e.g., SPY). Spreads under 1% are generally acceptable; above 1% warrants caution.

- **Liquidity checklist [13:52–14:35]**: Prefer underlyings above $5; use weekly expirations when available; compare bid-ask ratios to SPY. Adjust liquidity requirements based on transaction frequency: covered calls need less liquidity (one exit); ratio diagonals and zero-DTE need much more.

- **GameStop case study: volatility impact [14:57–17:16]**: A modest 11-to-16 move in GME on May 7 widened bid-ask spreads dramatically (e.g., 0.07 to 0.29 on a 0.5-delta option) due to volatility expansion, even though the move was not extreme. Plan exits conservatively for volatile underlyings.

## Notable quotes

> "There are two key components of an options trade: the entry and the exit. Traders are really good at analyzing liquidity when we're trying to get in. Most traders are not good at conceptualizing liquidity when we're looking to exit the trade."

> "The more you transact, the more liquid you need the underlying to be."
