# Roth IRA Backdoor Contribution Explained

## Summary

A backdoor Roth IRA contribution is a legal method for high-income earners to fund a Roth account when direct contributions are prohibited by income limits. The process involves making a non-deductible contribution to a traditional IRA, then converting it to a Roth IRA, with careful attention to the pro-rata rule and proper tax reporting via IRS Form 8606 and Form 1099-R.

## Key takeaways

- **Why Roth over Traditional:** Roth contributions are taxed upfront but withdrawals are tax-free; beneficial if you expect higher future income [00:00]
- **Income limits block direct contributions:** Single filers over ~$161k and married filing jointly over ~$240k cannot directly contribute to a Roth IRA [00:00]
- **Step 1 — Non-deductible traditional contribution:** Make a contribution to a traditional IRA that you cannot deduct (typically required if single income exceeds ~$89k) [01:16]
- **Step 2 — Optional holding period:** Some practitioners hold the traditional IRA briefly before conversion, though this is not necessary and risks triggering the pro-rata rule if gains accrue [02:34]
- **Step 3 — Convert to Roth:** Once funds settle (typically a few days), initiate a Roth conversion; if no gains exist, there is no tax on the conversion [02:34]
- **Pro-rata rule caveat:** Any gains accrued in the traditional IRA before conversion are taxable; there is no penalty, but it increases tax liability unnecessarily [02:34]
- **Tax reporting:** File IRS Form 8606 (non-deductible contributions) and receive Form 1099-R (conversion notice) from your broker [03:57]
- **Broker implementation (Schwab example):** Initiate a transfer, select "complete Roth conversion," choose complete (not partial) to move all funds [05:03]
- **Applies broadly:** This process works for any account holder seeking to fund a Roth IRA, not limited to options traders [05:03]
