# Is Selling Options Trading Volatility Going Down

## Summary

Selling options is commonly misunderstood as a directional bet that implied volatility will decline. In reality, short-option sellers are primarily trading the difference between implied volatility (IV) at entry and realized volatility (RV) over the holding period. A temporary rise in IV hurts mark-to-market P&L but does not damage the position if realized volatility remains contained; the true risk materializes only when realized volatility exceeds the implied volatility sold.

## Key takeaways

- **Selling options ≠ betting IV goes down** [00:00] — the core misconception. Short sellers are trading IV vs. RV spread, not directional IV movement.
- **IV rise hurts mark-to-market, not the edge** [00:15] — if you sell a straddle at 30% IV and IV rises to 35%, unrealized P&L declines immediately, but this is temporary pain, not permanent loss.
- **Realized volatility is the killer** [00:30] — the position survives IV expansion if realized volatility stays below the IV sold. The true loss occurs when realized volatility breaches the short strikes or exceeds the sold IV level.
- **Range-bound markets reward short premium** [00:25] — even with rising IV, if the underlying stays rangebound and doesn't realize the higher volatility, theta decay and the IV-RV spread work in the seller's favor.

## Notable quotes

> "The thing that kills you proverbially is when realized V exceeds whatever the implied vault was that you sold." [00:30]
