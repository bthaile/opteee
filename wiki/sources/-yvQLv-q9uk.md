# Short Interest Float Explained

## Summary

Short interest (or short float) measures the total number of shares sold short in a stock, typically expressed as a percentage of shares outstanding. It serves as a sentiment indicator reflecting institutional bearish positioning. The video explains how to interpret short interest metrics—including the days-to-cover ratio—and how short squeezes can create trading opportunities when shorts are forced to cover at higher prices.

## Key takeaways

- **Short interest definition** [00:26]: Short interest, short float, and short shares are synonymous terms describing how many shares have been borrowed and sold short.
- **Short selling mechanics** [00:50]: Sellers borrow shares from a broker, immediately sell them at a high price, then buy them back at a lower price to return to the broker—the inverse of buy-low-sell-high.
- **Sentiment indicator** [02:10]: High short interest can signal institutional bearish conviction, though it should be used as one tool in a broader toolkit rather than a standalone signal.
- **Percentage expression** [02:55]: Short interest is most useful when expressed as a percentage: divide short shares by shares outstanding (e.g., 20% of outstanding shares are short).
- **Days-to-cover ratio** [03:37]: Also called the short interest ratio; calculated as short interest divided by average daily volume. Shows how many days it would take shorts to cover if they all exited simultaneously.
- **Volatility from covering** [04:26]: High days-to-cover ratios can create volatility if the stock rallies and shorts are forced to buy back shares, increasing demand.
- **Short squeeze mechanics** [05:04]: When shorts are forced to cover at losses (e.g., sold at $50, now at $70), they buy back shares, driving price higher temporarily until the market normalizes.
- **Contrarian opportunity** [06:14]: High short interest can paradoxically signal a bullish opportunity if the stock trends upward, as forced short covering creates additional buying pressure.
- **Tool, not signal** [07:44]: Short interest should inform trade generation ideas but never be the sole basis for a trade.
