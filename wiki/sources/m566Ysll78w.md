# Precious Metals, Macro Imbalance, & Russian Invasion with Tavi from Crescat Capital | Money Talks

## Summary

Tavi Costa, portfolio manager at Crescat Capital, discusses the firm's macro-focused investment approach and bullish thesis on precious metals and commodities amid three simultaneous macro imbalances: GDP constraints (1940s), inflation (1970s), and speculative excess (late 1990s). He argues that central banks will eventually need to increase gold reserves to restore currency credibility, driving a multi-year bull market in gold, silver, and exploration assets—particularly as major miners have chronically underinvested in exploration. The geopolitical shock of Russia's invasion of Ukraine reinforces deglobalization trends and commodity scarcity, creating structural tailwinds for tangible assets over long-duration growth equities.

## Key takeaways

- **Three simultaneous macro imbalances** [29:00]: The U.S. faces GDP constraints (1940s), inflation (1970s), and speculative valuations (late 1990s) all at once—an unprecedented setup that constrains Fed policy and creates political pressure.
- **Capital rotation from growth to value** [31:06]: Technology (~40% of S&P 500) and bonds dominate portfolios; inflation and rising cost of capital will drive outflows into natural resources, energy, and profitable businesses with low debt.
- **Gold as currency anchor** [39:04]: Central banks will likely buy gold over the next 5–10 years to restore credibility to fiat currencies, especially as crypto libertarian movements challenge the dollar's anchor.
- **Exploration assets > major miners** [35:46]: Major miners have underinvested in exploration for decades and now hold record cash; exploration companies offer leveraged exposure to gold/silver prices without the wage/energy cost squeeze hitting large producers.
- **Geopolitical commodity scarcity** [45:06]: Russia–Ukraine conflict accelerates military spending, fiscal imbalance, and deglobalization; countries controlling oil, food, and minerals gain strategic advantage.
- **Quantitative model evolution** [23:59]: Backtesting quant factors is critical but difficult in inflationary regimes; historical data from the 1970s is sparse, forcing reliance on macro views to validate factor selection.
- **Long-term fund structure** [21:22]: Crescat's precious metals and macro funds use 1-year lock-ups to attract long-term capital; most macro themes play out over 3–5 years, with sub-themes turning over in 6–12 months.
- **Avoid China and Russia** [49:34]: Despite valuations, Crescat avoids investing in China and Russia on principle; short Chinese currency due to overvaluation and banking-to-GDP imbalance.

**Date:** March 3, 2022 — one week after Russia's invasion of Ukraine (Feb 24, 2022). Oil prices spiking (~$107/bbl mentioned), sanctions on Russia imminent, geopolitical risk premium elevated. This is an early-stage macro call on commodity supercycle and capital rotation away from growth equities.
