# Taxes for Traders & Money Mindset w/ CPA Bob Wheeler | Money Talks

## Summary

CPA Bob Wheeler discusses tax strategy, money psychology, and practical tax planning for traders and entrepreneurs. The conversation covers wash-sale rules, short-term vs. long-term capital gains, capital-loss harvesting mechanics, real-estate integration into tax planning, and the psychological barriers that cause self-sabotage around money and financial decisions.

## Key takeaways

### Tax mechanics & trader pitfalls
- **Wash-sale rule**: Selling a stock at a loss and repurchasing within 30 days disallows the loss deduction. Many traders unknowingly accumulate hundreds of thousands in disallowed wash sales [12:06–12:46].
- **Short-term vs. long-term capital gains**: Holding for more than one year triggers long-term rates (typically 15% federal); under one year is taxed as ordinary income. Waiting even two days can save significant tax [14:02–15:49].
- **Capital-loss limitation**: You can only deduct $3,000 of net capital losses per year against ordinary income; excess losses carry forward indefinitely. A $100k crypto loss yields only $3k deduction in year one [16:10–16:49].
- **Passive-loss mechanics**: Real-estate losses suspend until you sell the property or income drops below $150k threshold; they don't disappear [22:50–24:06].

### Real-estate integration
- Real estate creates a "bucket" of passive losses that can offset future gains or income, providing multi-year tax deferral [22:50–23:26].
- Hiring a property manager or investing in REITs avoids the burden of direct management while preserving tax benefits [22:08–22:25].
- Coordinate with your CPA before selling property to ensure passive losses are released; missing this can cost tens of thousands [24:06–24:22].

### Money psychology & self-sabotage
- Many high-income earners unconsciously sabotage their finances because of deep beliefs (e.g., "I don't deserve this," "Mom will rescue me if I fail") [41:18–42:35].
- Limiting beliefs ("I'm not good at math," "I can't pass the CPA exam") become self-fulfilling prophecies until consciously rejected [44:27–45:40].
- Tax harvesting should not be used as psychological permission to celebrate losses; focus on the fact that you lost money, not the tax benefit [19:15–20:17].

### Choosing a CPA
- Look for a CPA with hands-on experience in your specific area (real estate, options, K-1 income, etc.) [36:42–37:03].
- Seek someone willing to push the gray area legally and advocate for you, not someone who stays 10 feet from every line [37:25–37:41].
- A good CPA reviews returns multiple times, sleeps on decisions, and catches missed deductions (e.g., passive-loss carryovers) [38:33–38:48].
- Avoid CPAs who shame clients or take a one-and-done approach; relationship and continuous learning matter [38:16–39:04].

### Broader financial patterns
- Successful clients typically delegate portfolio management (paying 1–2% AUM) rather than self-manage, freeing time for business growth [26:27–27:06].
- Many successful people invest in real estate or managed portfolios; few take on high risk in their 80s (though one memorable client did) [26:27–31:51].
- Quarterly or more-frequent communication with your CPA prevents year-end surprises and allows proactive planning [18:23–18:45].
