# Meme Stock Updates: PLTR, GME, ASTS | Meme Stock Watch Ep49

## Summary

Eric reviews three positions held in his portfolio: Palantir (PLTR), which is running at all-time highs ahead of earnings on August 4th; Axiom Space (ASTS), which experienced a sharp 9.8% pulldown but shows mixed options flow; and GameStop (GME), which remains quiet with historically compressed volatility. The stream emphasizes how options flow becomes harder to interpret in well-developed, large-cap names with diverse trader motivations, and demonstrates the importance of using volatility surfaces and term structures alongside flow analysis. A detailed discussion of UNH earnings follows, exploring how to trade earnings through volatility mechanics rather than directional conviction.

## Key takeaways

### Dated market read (2025-07-27)

- **PLTR momentum and earnings setup** [06:56–10:43]: PLTR has rallied from ~146 to 159 over the past week, up 496% over the last year. Earnings slated for August 4th after close. Stock shows net long call premiums and net short puts (cash-secured put selling), suggesting bullish positioning. Eric is trading a [[ratio-call-diagonal]] and expects continued strength, though the parabolic move warrants caution.
- **ASTS pullback and volatility anomaly** [12:48–16:28]: ASTS dropped 9.8% (a ~2.5 standard deviation move), the largest in six months. Despite the sharp move, options flow shows heavy call premium before the drop and continued accumulation, suggesting traders may be rolling positions out in time rather than capitulating. Earnings on August 11th. Volatility surface shows backwardation from 56–84 DTE (abnormal) and calls more expensive than puts (unusual skew).
- **GME: quiet consolidation** [20:40–24:29]: GME is flat with historically compressed [[implied-volatility]] (sub-80, exceptionally low for the name). Open interest is skewed to calls but not heavily, and notably absent from the front expiration—a sign of low retail/meme activity. This is the opposite of typical GME behavior during active periods.
- **Dark pools and market structure clarification** [22:21–24:29]: Eric clarifies that dark pools are not nefarious; they exist to prevent front-running of large trades. Dark pool volume still hits the consolidated tape with a 1–2 second delay. XRT is a fixed-mandate ETF, not a vehicle for moving shares via rehypothecation. Market makers (e.g., Citadel's market-making arm) are distinct from hedge funds and banks; failures to deliver relate to market makers, not hedge funds.
- **UNH earnings trade modeling** [40:14–01:05:50]: UNH has collapsed 49% over 12 months following CEO departure and DOJ investigation into Medicare billing. Healthcare sector (XLV) is down sharply. Options market prices ~7.5% expected move; front [[implied-volatility]] is 79% vs. 60% for mid-term (20-point spread). Event volatility calculated at ~148.6% for the earnings day. Call/put ratio is 72k/42k (bullish skew), unusual for a large-cap with hedging activity. Eric's historical UNH earnings record: 40 trades, 74% win rate, but average losses are extreme. Prior moves often exceed expected move. Eric would prefer to see more front-end volatility before committing, and suggests trading volatility mechanics rather than direction.

### Evergreen mechanics

- **Options flow complexity in large-cap names** [07:31–08:23]: As stocks become "well-developed" (large equity positions, many traders), options flow becomes ambiguous. Put buying may reflect carry hedges, not directional bearishness. [[Implied-volatility]] surface and [[volatility-term-structure]] become more useful than raw flow for signal extraction.
- **Volatility surface interpretation** [16:28–17:34]: Backwardation (front cheaper than back) and contango (front more expensive) reveal term structure expectations. Skew (calls vs. puts at same strike) reflects directional bias. Abnormal configurations (e.g., calls more expensive than puts in a down market) warrant investigation.
- **Earnings volatility mechanics** [48:04–01:03:26]: Earnings create discrete trading opportunities: pre-earnings [[implied-volatility]] buildup, [[iv-crush]] post-earnings, intraday catalyst trading, and post-earnings drift. [[Implied-volatility]] typically contracts post-earnings (new information removes uncertainty), but abnormal expansions (like UNH's prior release) can be traded. [[Straddle]] pricing (average of ATM call + put) is a better proxy for expected move than term volatility, which gets skewed by OTM options.
- **Covered call vs. cash-secured put equivalence** [25:03–26:43]: At-the-money covered calls and at-the-money short puts have nearly identical P&L profiles on directional moves (e.g., ±15%), but differ in capital efficiency and cash requirements.
- **Market structure: dark pools, market makers, and DTCC** [22:21–39:04]: Dark pools prevent front-running of large trades but create artificial supply/demand matching. Market makers (sell-side intermediaries, sometimes part of larger conglomerates like Citadel) are distinct from hedge funds and banks. DTCC is user-owned by member institutions (banks, brokers, funds) and regulated by the SEC; it is fully aware of the distinction between market makers and banks. Failures to deliver relate to market-making activity, not hedge fund positioning.
