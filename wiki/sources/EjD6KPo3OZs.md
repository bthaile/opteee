# Euan Sinclair on Retail vs Institutional Trading | The Outlier Podcast

## Summary

Euan Sinclair, who has traded both institutionally and as a retail trader, dissects the fundamental differences between how professionals and retail traders operate. The conversation focuses on the structural advantages institutions enjoy (size, franchise value, team specialization) and the critical mistakes retail traders make when attempting to emulate professional techniques without understanding their context. Key themes include transaction-cost awareness, proper risk sizing, volatility mechanics, and the psychological discipline required to execute a trading plan consistently.

## Key takeaways

### Institutional vs. retail structure
- **Institutional traders operate within a production line**: they are often one piece of a larger machine (market-making, sales decomposition, customer flow). Removing them from that context destroys their edge. [02:24–06:15]
- **Franchise value is real**: much of an institutional trader's P&L comes from the firm's customer relationships and seat value, not personal skill. A Goldman Sachs equity options trader loses customer flow if moved to a smaller firm. [05:01–07:22]
- **Retail has no franchise value but owns their full upside**: this is the structural advantage retail should lean into, not fight against. [07:22]

### Hedging misconception
- **Professionals hedge to isolate edge, not to prevent losses**: Jane Street and Susquehanna hedge away unwanted risks so they can concentrate on the one thing they do want. Retail often hedges to stop catastrophic loss, which is risk management, not hedging. [08:31–09:48]
- **Hedged options are a single instrument**: you cannot think of a hedge separately from the underlying option; they form one combined payoff. [09:48]

### Transaction costs and trading frequency
- **Every keyboard touch costs money**: spreads, commissions, and slippage are insidious because they are constant regardless of outcome. A $50 spread cost is always paid whether you make $5,000 or lose $5,000. [11:03–12:13]
- **Professionals obsess over half-penny improvements in commissions** because it compounds. Retail typically underestimates transaction-cost drag. [12:13]
- **Commission-free trading is a myth**: the cost is paid elsewhere (CBOE, market makers, order internalization). [12:13]
- **Retail trades too much**: professionals treat trading like a business with detailed clearing statements, account reconciliation, and disciplined execution. [12:13–13:36]

### Psychology and discipline
- **Professional traders execute regardless of emotion**: "trader's block" or hesitation is not tolerated; you come to work, do your job, and go home. [13:36–14:43]
- **Most trading psychology problems vanish if you adopt a professional mindset**: treat it as a job, not a heroic endeavor. [14:43]
- **Don't attach self-worth to short-term outcomes**: daily P&L swings are noise; focus on the long-term process. [25:05]

### Volatility and Greeks—a trap task
- **Greeks, implied vol, skew, and sticky-strike are irrelevant for retail**: these matter if you are a volatility arbitrageur trading variance swaps against VIX futures. For a one-week SPY strangle, they do not drive profitability. [16:04]
- **Gamma feedback and similar narratives are performative**: they make traders feel professional but do not generate edge for retail. [17:23]

### Risk acceptance vs. risk avoidance
- **Professionals accept intrinsic risk-reward profiles**: if a strategy wins $1,000/week on average but loses $5,000 every couple of months, that is the deal. Retail often tries to cap losses, which hedges away the paid-for risk. [17:23–18:43]
- **Never risk bankruptcy, but accept normal drawdowns**: the distinction is between catastrophic ruin and the noise of the game. [18:43]
- **Use Monte Carlo simulation to understand your strategy's loss distribution**: ask ChatGPT to help if you cannot code. See how many days per year you lose money. [18:43–20:02]

### Recency bias and sample-size errors
- **Retail massively overweight recent experience vs. backtested statistics**: they backtest 5 years of data, then trade for 2 weeks and abandon the strategy. [22:32–23:48]
- **Short-term losses feel catastrophic but are statistically meaningless**: if your strategy is sound, a bad week is noise. [23:48–25:05]
- **Communities amplify this**: well-meaning traders overlay additional rules based on tiny sample sizes (e.g., "this doesn't work on Thursdays"). [23:48]

### Psychological tricks for loss acceptance
- **Pre-loss acceptance**: before opening a trade you know will lose, convince yourself the loss will be worse than it likely is. When you open the screen and see a smaller loss, it feels like a win. [27:26–28:34]
- **Don't monitor P&L intraday**: focus on process, not daily equity swings. [22:32]
- **Size so losses don't hurt**: if you cannot emotionally handle the loss, reduce size. If you cannot size small enough to be comfortable, trading may not be for you. [29:42]

### Position sizing and the intrinsic risk-reward profile
- **You cannot change a strategy's risk profile by adding stops or hedges**: you can only accept or reject the market's offer. [32:05–33:16]
- **Yield hunting kills accounts**: if IV is 5%, you might sell 1 option for $1,000 profit. If IV is 30%, you might need to sell 20 options for the same $1,000. The catastrophe risk scales with notional short options, not Greeks. [33:16–34:29]
- **LKM hedge fund example**: they lost ~$1 billion in 10 minutes by chasing yield—selling more options as IV fell to maintain constant premium. [34:29]

### Price sensitivity and edge location
- **Your edge is not in the structure; it is in the price**: you should not be price-insensitive to iron condors just because you believe in the structure. There is always a price where you should buy instead of sell. [35:44–36:44]
- **Zero-DTE options**: premiums are tiny, costs are fixed in dollars (not vol-dependent), so cost-to-premium ratio is terrible. Only trade zero-DTE if IV is above 20%; below 7–8% IV, the math does not work. [36:44–37:05]
- **Why retail loves zero-DTE**: daily dopamine hit and the illusion of activity. Everything you can do in zero-DTE you can do in weeklies or monthlies without the cost drag. [37:05]

### Volatility rank vs. volatility level
- **Cross-sectional IV rank (high-IV stocks vs. low-IV stocks) has edge**: sell the highest-IV names, buy the lowest. This edge has compressed as tools democratized but still exists. [38:30–39:38]
- **Time-series IV rank (a stock's IV vs. its own history) is a trap**: selling when IV rank is high is often wrong. When IV is high, realized vol often exceeds implied vol, so the variance premium is low or negative. [39:38–40:55]
- **Implied vol is the price of the straddle**: focus on straddle price, not the vol number. If a straddle is $5 and normally $2, you are betting the stock will not move more than $5. Vol changing just affects buyback price. [46:10]

### Variance premium timing
- **The variance premium is hard to time**: it looks easy when you look at VIX spikes, but the actual premium has little pattern. [47:21]
- **Default to a core short-vol position unless something is crazy**: VIX 120, term structure inverted, 9-day VIX spiking—these are "crazy" signals. VIX 85 vs. 88 is not. [47:21–48:50]
- **Sell vol when VIX is 15–25**: below 15, premiums are too small to cover costs; above 25, variance premium is minimal. [48:50–50:01]
- **Stick to indices**: they have consistent volatility premiums. Stocks are much harder to predict. [50:01]

### Bubble trades and put selling
- **Selling calls in a bubble (GME, AMC) is insane**: once a stock is unmoored from reality, stupidity knows no bounds. It can keep going up. [51:11–52:30]
- **Selling puts in a bubble is the edge**: when IV is 1,000+ and the stock is at $500, sell 40-delta puts at $200 strike. If the stock crashes to $200, IV collapses to normal levels, and the vega gain offsets the delta loss. [52:30–53:47]
- **You cannot force bubble trades**: they happen every 3–4 years. Wait for them; do not hunt for them. [53:47]

### Earnings and event volatility
- **Options before earnings are systematically overpriced**: sell them. Same for biotech before FDA announcements, zero-DTE before non-farm payroll. [55:02–56:27]
- **Biotech has a distinct volatility premium**: if you sell vol because it is risky, biotech is riskier, so the premium is higher. Logical conclusion: sell biotech vol. [56:27]
- **Size earnings trades small and do many**: you cannot pick which earnings will blow through. On 100 earnings trades, you might get a 10-SD loss on one; your portfolio risk is ~2 SD. Size based on the portfolio, not the individual trade. [57:48–58:59]
- **Do not buy wings**: they are overpriced and rarely save you. [59:48]
- **Calendar spreads for earnings**: sell front, buy back (2+ months out). If you blow through, you get stock and are long gamma. Wide bid-ask on deep ITM long options means you hold to expiration, but you are hedged. [59:48–01:00:18]

### The underserved educational gap
- **Retail education is missing legit, non-condescending, true information**: you can trade vol without Black-Scholes or Greeks; payoff diagrams suffice. The market needs a "Guy Fieri" approach—genuine, entertaining, serious, and true. [01:02:42]

## Notable quotes

> "Most institutions are not taking the sort of risks that retail always thinks you need to take to make money. Because they can do it with much, much bigger size in much, much smaller edges."

> "Every time you touch the keyboard it costs you money, and it's really insidious, right? Because you're thinking, oh, I might make 5000 bucks on this trade. Why do I care that I paid 50 bucks in spreads? Then it's because it's always 50 bucks."

> "You've got to accept that every strategy you've got has a sort of an intrinsic risk reward profile. You know, and if you're winning on average, $1,000 a week, look, legitimately winning that on average, well, if it takes you a $5,000 loser every couple of months, just deal with it."

## Regime / context

Recorded November 12, 2025. This is a foundational interview on the structural and psychological differences between institutional and retail trading. Sinclair draws on 15+ years of professional experience (both sell-side market-making and personal account trading) to debunk common retail misconceptions about volatility, hedging, Greeks, and risk management. The discussion is evergreen for traders at all levels but especially valuable for retail traders attempting to adopt institutional techniques without understanding their context.
