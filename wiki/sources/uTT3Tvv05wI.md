# I've Been Options Trading for 10 Years, and These Are My 5 Biggest Tips

## Summary

Eric reviews five foundational options trading principles, offering nuanced critiques and expansions on each. He emphasizes understanding the Greeks and volatility mechanics over mechanical button-clicking, challenges the binary buy-vs-sell framing, defends earnings plays as strategically viable despite individual event randomness, and stresses the importance of pre-planned exits and disciplined risk management over prediction-based trading.

## Key takeaways

- **Understand what you're trading, not just the mechanics** [00:00–01:06]: Options are fundamentally volatility and time instruments, not just leveraged stocks. The Greeks are table stakes for understanding position behavior; [[implied-volatility]] crush and [[theta-decay]] are non-negotiable concepts. Many traders lose money on directionally correct trades due to [[iv-crush]].

- **Never YOLO undefined-risk size; define your risk before entry** [02:25–03:27]: Weekly out-of-the-money naked positions are lottery tickets, not strategies. However, even undefined-risk strategies (e.g., naked calls, naked puts) can be profitable if you pre-define your personal risk limit and stick to it—the key is discipline, not the structure itself.

- **Theta decay is not a reason to avoid buying options** [03:27–05:44]: [[Theta-decay]] is relentless but varies dramatically by moneyness and expiration. Far out-of-the-money and longer-dated options have much lower theta bleed. If you're skilled at directional trading, buying options can be far more profitable than selling; the choice depends on your [[profit-mechanism]], not a universal rule.

- **Earnings plays have edge when treated as a strategy, not individual coin flips** [06:00–09:26]: Each single earnings event is indeed a 50/50 directional bet, but peer-reviewed research shows predictable patterns around earnings announcements. Strategies exploiting [[implied-volatility]] expansion pre-earnings and contraction post-earnings, or analyzing [[order-flow]], can generate consistent edge. Dismissing earnings entirely leaves money on the table.

- **Always plan your exit before entry** [09:26–10:39]: A defined exit plan before trade entry is non-negotiable for long-term profitability. This applies regardless of strategy type or profit mechanism.

- **Options trading is about discipline and risk management, not genius prediction** [10:39–11:40]: Understanding the Greeks and building simple models (even spreadsheets) is foundational, not optional. Treating trading as a serious discipline rather than a hobby is the prerequisite for consistent returns.
