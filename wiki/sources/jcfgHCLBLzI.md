# Inside Profit Mechanisms and AARs | The Outlier Trading Podcast Unfiltered Ep3

## Summary

Eric and Shray J explore the foundational concept of **profit mechanisms**—the actual drivers of trading returns—and demonstrate why understanding them is essential before selecting option structures or management rules. The episode then shifts to **after-action reviews (AARs)**, a systematic framework for analyzing portfolio, strategy, and trade-level performance to inform next-year planning. Eric shares his 2025 performance context and philosophy around return targets, capital allocation, and the role of experience in execution.

## Key takeaways

### Profit mechanisms & execution gaps

- **[06:03]** The gap between theoretical understanding and practical execution is common; confidence comes from demonstrated ability over time, not just research.
- **[08:32]** Option structures themselves provide zero edge—the profit mechanism is what actually makes money. Theta is not an edge; it has a counterparty (gamma).
- **[10:05]** Using TQQQ as an example: buying shares, selling puts, synthetic long, buying calls, or buying LEAPs all made money at one entry point and all lost money at another. The structure doesn't matter; the profit mechanism does.
- **[11:32]** Profit mechanisms for TQQQ include: (1) levered long position in a trending asset, (2) trend-following to avoid drawdowns, (3) covered-strangle risk premium capture (~4.8% ROIC over 39 days on a 30-delta put).
- **[14:08]** Selling options for theta is not an edge because gamma is the counterparty. The real profit mechanism must be identified through deeper research.
- **[16:30]** Profit mechanisms define sizing and management rules; they answer "how do I want to structure and manage this trade?"

### Building confidence through systematic research

- **[17:31]** The Outlier Strategy Process: (1) Profile profit mechanism, (2) Identify signals, (3) Define management, (4) Choose structures. Options structures come last, not first.
- **[18:35]** Without the first two steps, management rules (50% max profit, 20-day MA stop) are random. Your subconscious will call bullshit if you haven't done the foundational work.
- **[20:41]** Analogy: Steph Curry doesn't question his free-throw mechanics at the line because he's done thousands of repetitions. Similarly, traders must iron out profit mechanisms in the background before live execution.
- **[24:32]** Use SSRN (Social Science Research Network) to research profit mechanisms like momentum; academic papers reveal lookback periods and signal definitions.
- **[27:09]** Cross-sectional momentum example: rank S&P 500 sectors by 12-month return weekly/monthly, buy the strongest, track performance without worrying about option structures yet.
- **[31:12]** Once you understand the profit mechanism's average move (~1.8% for sector rotation), you can make informed decisions about strike selection, time decay, and structure.

### After-action reviews (AARs) framework

- **[38:36]** AARs ask: What did you expect? What occurred? What do you sustain? What do you improve? Apply at three levels: portfolio, strategy, trade.
- **[39:54]** Portfolio-level metrics: starting/ending value, savings rate, total trades, commissions, execution quality, P&L volatility, max drawdown, cumulative returns.
- **[41:14]** Savings rate is critical early in a trading career; a $1,000 monthly save on a $10K portfolio = 10% return. This diminishes as capital grows but compounds powerfully.
- **[42:31]** Execution quality: request broker documentation on order routing; every button press incurs cost. This becomes more relevant as account size grows.
- **[44:05]** Strategy-level analysis: number of trades, average win/loss, cumulative P&L per strategy. Intraday scalping often underperforms after commissions and execution costs.
- **[45:31]** Trade-level review: spot-check outliers (both good and bad) to identify recreatable wins or avoidable losses.
- **[47:11]** Regime analysis: overlay market conditions (up/down) to see which strategies perform best in each regime; use this to weight strategies next year.
- **[49:20]** Follow-on research: if intraday scalping shows poor average P&L per trade, test modifications (e.g., reduce frequency) rather than abandoning the strategy.

### Eric's 2025 performance & philosophy

- **[50:37]** Volatility clustered at highs for shorter durations, at lows for longer durations; transitory periods more aggressive than typical.
- **[52:05]** Covered-strangle segment underperformed (up 13.8% YTD vs. SPX +16%, QQQ +21%, TQQQ +38%), but with 1/3 the P&L volatility of the broader market.
- **[53:33]** Despite not pursuing aggressive returns, 2025 is tracking as Eric's second or third best year; minimum 70% return expected.
- **[55:04]** Psychological insight: people below a comfort baseline are risk-seeking; above it, they're risk-averse. Eric has lowered return targets as capital scaled because lower % returns generate sufficient gross dollars.
- **[57:31]** Crude oil example (June 2025): shorted at ~76, risking 6 points to target 14–15 points (down to ~60). Risk/reward was too good to pass up.
- **[58:55]** Mature execution approach and expanded learned experience have tightened return distribution; returns are more consistent and predictable.
- **[01:00:14]** Lower return imperative + continually expanding experience + mature execution = successful year.

### Goal-setting & capital allocation

- **[01:10:48]** Eric targets a base return to achieve gross dollars needed, not a percentage. Anything above base is upside; he doesn't chase beyond that.
- **[01:11:57]** Money mindset exercise: define thresholds ($1M, $10M, $25M+) and what each enables. At $10M, half a million in annual cash flow is achievable; material difference beyond $25M is fuzzy.
- **[01:14:25]** Eric works more now than in the Marine Corps or consulting; financial freedom enables more meaningful work, not escapism.
- **[01:16:47]** Capital buckets: personal needs, mother's care, brother's future security (Marine Corps injury), mentor's backup. These inform target-setting.
- **[01:19:08]** At $5M, you're "free" (not lean-fire); at $10M, you're "chubby fire" and can do almost anything.

### Shray J's execution challenges

- **[01:02:59]** Shray's performance is volatile and non-uniform; he focuses on targets rather than managing the path to them.
- **[01:04:12]** Within long calls, multiple profit mechanisms exist (sequential breakouts, consolidation breakouts, etc.); mixing management techniques creates noise.
- **[01:05:41]** Solution: define the specific profit mechanism before entry, then plan the entire trade lifecycle (entry, management, exit) before execution.
- **[01:08:22]** Backtest first (historical data), then live-test with simulations (fake tickers, Monte Carlo), then live-test with real market data. This mirrors Steph Curry's practice repetitions.
