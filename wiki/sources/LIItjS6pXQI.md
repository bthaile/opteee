# My Top 4 Option Trading Strategies

## Summary

Eric presents a framework for building option strategies based on three core profit mechanisms: positive drift (long-term market growth), volatility risk premium (overpriced implied volatility), and momentum. Rather than listing isolated strategies, he demonstrates how to identify measurable market effects and match them to appropriate structures—covered strangles, ratio call diagonals, and short strangles—with live examples from his trading portfolio.

## Key takeaways

- **Profit mechanism first** [00:00–01:15]: All option strategies must be anchored to a measurable market effect (positive drift, volatility risk premium, momentum). Strategy selection follows from the profit mechanism, not the reverse.

- **Positive drift via covered strangles** [08:32–12:03]: Sell cash-secured puts (20–30 delta, <60 DTE) on ETFs; add ratio covered calls and additional short puts to capture upside while harvesting put skew premium. Example: SSO and TEQ positions showing uncapped upside with positive theta slope.

- **Momentum via ratio call diagonals** [12:03–14:36]: Buy long-dated calls (>90 DTE, 50–80 delta) and sell shorter-dated calls at higher strikes as price moves. Allows premium collection without capping upside; roll or unwind short calls when challenged. Example: Palantir multi-leg diagonal with phased entry.

- **Volatility risk premium via short strangles** [14:36–17:00]: Sell strangles at 10–35 delta (typically ~25 delta) to capture overpriced implied volatility. Add wings in smaller accounts to define risk. Zero-DTE and earnings-release versions available; requires volatility sampling and careful delta selection.

- **Simplicity and consistency** [17:00–18:17]: The majority of trading activity consolidates into these three strategies. Consistent performance depends on momentum-following entry/exit discipline, especially with leveraged ETFs.

## Notable quotes

> "It does not matter if you use a put vertical spread, a call credit spread, a long straddle, like none of that matters unless you can match it to a market effect that you can measure and make money from." [00:45]

> "Simplicity is truly the ultimate form of sophistication. If you could do the basics really well, you can make a lot of money." [06:07]
