# How to ACTUALLY Make Money Trading Options - 3 Profit Mechanisms

## Summary

This video deconstructs the three fundamental profit mechanisms that actually generate returns in trading: **direction** (buy low, sell high), **risk assumption** (capturing structural alpha and risk premiums), and **inefficiencies** (exploiting mispricings, value dissonance, and post-earnings drift). Eric argues that traders often confuse trading activities (day trading, swing trading, options) with the underlying functions that produce profit, and that clarity on these mechanisms is essential for optimization and consistent returns.

## Key takeaways

### Direction
- **Buy low, sell high** is the oldest profit mechanism; works with stocks, volatility, and directional bets [04:28]
- Win rate and average win size are decoupled; you can profit with 25% win rate if winners are large enough, or with high win rate and small wins [05:11]
- Success requires both directional accuracy *and* effective money management (position sizing, profit-taking discipline) [05:34]

### Risk Assumption (Structural Alpha)
- **Put-call skew** creates structural alpha: 34-delta puts on SPY trade at a premium vs. equivalent-delta calls due to demand for downside protection [06:18]
- Pension funds and institutional players overpay for protection to avoid drawdowns; retail traders can capture this inefficiency [07:20]
- **Variance risk premium** is baked into puts because implied volatility tends to price above realized volatility [07:41]
- Selling cash-secured puts is a concrete example of assuming risk for others [07:41]

### Inefficiencies
- **Mispricing**: Market maker quote errors are extremely rare and nearly impossible for retail to exploit before algos [08:18]
- **Cross-exchange arbitrage**: Buy cheap on one exchange, sell expensive on another; normalizes prices and provides liquidity [09:01]
- **Value dissonance**: When price breaks sharply from perceived value (e.g., GameStop short squeeze); can trade either direction when move is overdone [09:23]
- **Post-earnings drift (PED)**: Stocks that beat earnings significantly tend to outperform over the next quarter; market inefficiency in digesting new information [10:21]

### Building a Strategy
- Start with a hypothesis tied to one profit mechanism (e.g., "technical analysis predicts direction 51% of the time") [11:18]
- Test the hypothesis, tools, and money management; compare alternatives [11:36]
- Select the combination with greatest opportunity, live-test, and optimize [11:56]
- Trading is intentionally difficult; low barrier to entry means opportunity exists for those who do the work [11:56]

## Notable quotes

> "You can lose money day trading. You can buy and hold a stock that goes down. You can swing trade options and not make money. These are just products—none of those inherently lead to making money." [01:34–01:59]

> "The barrier to entry is so low anybody can start. Imagine if it was so easy to make money—there would be nothing left to pick up off the ground." [11:56]
