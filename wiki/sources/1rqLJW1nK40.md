# Strategy Comparison in GameStop | Meme Stock Watch Ep54

## Summary
A Meme Stock Watch livestream that head-to-head compares four ways to deploy $25k into GameStop: 100% long equity (buy-and-hold), long calls, cash-secured short puts, and the host's favored "coverage triangle" (his branded covered strangle = long stock + short puts + ratio short calls). Using an on-demand replay from 12 June, he tracks each position's P&L through GME's months-long sideways chop. The takeaway: in GME's prolonged consolidations, long calls bleed from theta and overpriced vol, and 100% equity only "works" if you have a timing protocol to actually sell — while the coverage triangle grinds realized income, keeps a capital reserve to scale in on dips, and books profit you can redeploy into more shares. His live GME coverage triangle is +13.5% vs. a −25.86% buy-and-hold from a $29 entry, ~39% of outperformance on the same ticker over the same window.

## Key takeaways
- Never deploy 100% of capital into GME up front. He targets ~80% utilization (~$20k of $25k), reserving dry powder because GME can crack a "floor" and grind sideways for a full quarter — using 100% leaves you handcuffed with no capital left to adjust your basis. [13:14]
- Estimate a price floor before sizing. He eyeballs relative ranges (GME up ~115% from pre-squeeze lows), notes it recently cracked below the ~$22 handle to a ~$20.50 floor, and cross-checks intrinsic-value/DCF estimates (dismissing single-digit models; his gut is high-teens/low-20s) to decide how much capital to hold in reserve for lower scale-in levels. [11:37]
- Trader vs. investor framing: equity only pays when price rises. If GME chops sideways you tie up capital for a quarter for a poor per-month return — even top-ticking a move was only ~19% over ~5 months. Investors should just buy the dip; traders want to monetize the chop. [17:30]
- Capital split of the ~$20k: coverage triangle (base position, long stock + short puts) ~$15–18k, long calls ~$2–5k — but he wouldn't deploy the long-call sleeve here. Long calls are only for aggressive moves, and in a squeeze they're hard to exit (vol crush + market makers charging a premium for liquidity risk), so he prefers liquid equity during a squeeze. [20:11]
- Scaling in = "building units." Dumping remaining capital too fast (adds at $20, then $19) barely moves your basis and leaves no reserve. Deeper dips buy more shares per dollar, giving a more meaningful basis adjustment — so save capital for genuinely advantageous levels. [24:13]
- Use the options chain's probability-of-touch to set scale-in triggers. On ~69 DTE, ~50% chance of touching $18, ~43% at $20, <~10% at $10 (a blown-out bid/ask distorts the tail). He sets the scale-in level where prob-of-touch is just over 50% (the $21 handle). [29:07]
- Don't sell calls below your basis. If a short call's strike is under your stock basis, you risk locking in a loss — which is why driving the basis down via disciplined scaling matters before writing calls. [33:21]
- Backtest setup (replay from 12 June, spot ~$22.53, $25k): 100% equity = 1109 shares; long calls = 64x 15-Aug 21 calls @ $3.88; short puts = ~11x 30-delta 21 puts @ ~$1 cash-secured (~$1,100 credit); coverage triangle = ~$10k stock (443 sh) + ratio short calls + short puts, 80% utilized. [38:33]
- Early P&L snapshot: short puts +$1,045 (~4.5% on capital), 100% equity +$1,567, long calls −$192, coverage triangle +$1,260. The coverage triangle made the same ~6.3% ROIC as the others while using less money and keeping a reserve. [53:35]
- Long calls die in the chop: "you almost always overpay for vol" on GME calls, and in a quiet period you both overpaid AND suffer heightened theta decay — calls go anemic. This is where the long-call buyer throws in the towel and takes the loss. [1:03:01]
- 100% equity's flaw is monetization timing: it can be up ~19% at $27 but you don't get the money until you sell, and holding to $20.95 leaves nothing to show. A firm close below support (~$25.40) as a sell rule realizes ~$3,182 → giving you ~$28k to redeploy into more shares. [1:04:30]
- Risk framing (Marine Corps MLCOA/MDCOA): most-dangerous case = insolvency/delisting (accept it, size accordingly, low probability); most-likely bad case = drift to high-teens intrinsic value. Manage via allocation + utilization + basis-adjustable short puts + short calls that profit on the way down = layered management. [1:16:15]
- Upside stress test: on a ~20% up-move the coverage triangle underperforms 100% equity by only ~$1,200 (~1% of capital) — but the equity gain is unrealized (open) while the triangle mixes realized (~$1,742) and unrealized (~$1,935). Only realized profit can buy more shares. [1:27:15]
- The 45-DTE convention is a "tastytrade artifact." In high-vol names you get paid for fighting gamma, but the coverage triangle has a defined playbook (take assignment or roll), so gamma isn't the concern. Selling ~20–21 DTE annualizes to a much higher return than 41–45 DTE despite a smaller absolute credit. [1:33:53]
- Delta selection is inventory-first, price-second: downtrend ~20–30 delta (don't want shares), rally 35–50 delta (more aggressive), sideways ~35 delta. [1:42:58]
- Ratio covered calls beat call credit spreads for the short-call leg: a covered call can be rolled (deferring/avoiding a realized loss), whereas a credit spread must be closed and re-established for tiny credit with skewed risk/reward (risking ~$178 to make ~$0.22). His worked rolling example ended net positive (~+$6) where the spread path ended ~−$156. [1:44:14]
